    I can't speak for the author; I'm just sharing my take on the issue, based on training in the three major vehicles of Buddhism.

    When it's time to meditate, just do it. Bring your wakefulness to every situation, unconditionally. This is the way to freedom, as it trains you to show up without depending on outside circumstances.

    You aren't "in a permanent state of strong dullness". It comes and goes. Even when dull, that dullness becomes thicker and thinner. If you are present and mindful of the dullness, is it really dullness? This is an interesting question.

    On the one hand, it is beneficial to take care of your life so that you can be healthy and sharp and go far in meditation practice. On the other hand, if you never practice wakefulness in less than ideal circumstances, how will you ever be able to apply it when things are not going your way? How will you show up for yourself and others if every time demands are placed on you, you check out and avoid being present?

    I believe the instruction may mean that outside of scheduled practice, don't push yourself so far that you linger in dullness. Likewise, while traditional practice is quite rigorous by modern western standards, it is also important not to go overboard with asceticism â€” don't push so hard that you become rigid, resentful, incapacitated.

    Find the Middle Way between laxity and unnecessary asceticism, and between dullness and anxiety. The correct balance will depend on your capacity and where you are in your practice. The ox can take a greater burden than the goat.

    Usually we have poor perspective on our own capacity. Our preference and habit loom large. A good teacher can help bring out the best in you.

    In time, your capacity will become greater and greater; you will be able to apply wakefulness in all kinds of circumstances. I know one lay-woman who used to regularly do all-night practice and then drive her meditation group for excursions to other cities the next morning. I've also been around monastics who take only a couple of hours of sleep, or no lying-down sleep at all, and yet are among the brightest, most energetic people I've ever encountered. It's the power of consistent practice, and the freedom from the inner tension of self-concern: once a breakthrough awakening is reached, tremendous energy becomes available to you.

    Upasaka Culadasa (the author of The Mind Illuminated) will be doing an AMA on Reddit tomorrow; so you can ask the question directly.
