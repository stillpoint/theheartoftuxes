    >For some reason I tend to stop midway...

    It's not 'for some reason'; it's because you allow yourself to stop before the time is up. If you don't give yourself that 'out' you can continue and always finish.

    Checking the time is a bit like looking down when you're climbing on a precipice. It rips you out of the moment and hurtles you into the mind that thinks about future possibilities. At the edge of a cliff, you envision and think about falling. When you check the time you think about how far you've come, how much effort and discomfort lie ahead, whether it's 'worth it', and so on. Checking the time tends to make you think about time rather than be momentary.

    One easy solution is to use a timer rather than just a watch or clock. Then you don't have to check; you only end practice when the timer rings. There are meditation timer apps for smartphone, in addition to basic alarm apps that work just as well. You can pick up a kitchen timer for a buck or so at a dollar store. If you want to spend more you can buy a dedicated meditation timer, but the basic function is the same: to do the timing for you so you don't have to check.

    If you are momentary, everything is no problem. Comfort is truth; discomfort is truth. There is no time, there is only what's happening. Within the moment, you have integrity. But when you step out of the moment and check your condition and judge how things are going, that's already a losing game; you're already outside of meditation, fighting things.

    If you decide on a time for a session, finish the time. Don't allow yourself to hedge or give up. As soon as you allow that to happen it becomes easier and easier to give up on yourself. Then giving up becomes a habit, which makes it even harder to overcome.

    If the time is too long, practice for a shorter time to start. Forcing yourself will cause resentment. But if you choose a time that is do-able, you will get not only the benefit of completing your meditation sessions, you will also get the benefit of knowing you can see it through. That joy and confidence will grow and become the basis of future progress. So it is much better to do 5 minutes every day without fail, and to perform the meditation wholeheartedly and completely, than to do 15 minutes one day, 7 minutes the next, maybe skip a day, and never trust yourself to finish. Step by step is much better.

    Next, it is necessary to be free of doubt when meditating. A moment of doubt or hesitation opens the door for speculation, dithering, tinkering, evading, and all those kinds of trains of thought. You should cut off all trains of thought and return to the method. That in itself will take you through to the end. When the thought "should I stop?" begins to arise, cut off your connection to it and return to the method. But if you are not very clear on what exactly you're supposed to be doing with body and mind at any given time, doubt will arise and you will be susceptible to trains of thought.

    In order to be free of doubt in your practice, you need to have confidence in the method. Therefore you need a proper, proven, traditional method; and you need to learn it from a proper, attained source (ideally a live teacher); and you need to understand very well the 'why' and 'how' of the method; and finally, you need to apply the method completely, without messing about.

    Finally, many (most?) of us are incapable of holding the container of practice for ourselves. Our habit mind is powerful and our understanding and motivation are deficient. That is why we use good external circumstances to help us. You use one external circumstance, a timed session, but you don't give yourself to it completely. So you need more structure. Here are some possibilities:

    • A practice journal.

    • A practice schedule and routine/ritual.

    • A practice friend to be accountable to.

    • A teacher and practice community (because often 'friends' can also make tacit agreements to let things slide; and because having the example of others' fiery determination is so valuable).

    • A place of practice — where the environment is supportive, where there's nowhere to run away to, and where everyone is doing the same practice for the same duration.

    • A course of training that builds step by step in the right order, from a very solid basis.
