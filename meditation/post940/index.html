    How does Tai Chi practice compare to Sitting practice?

    >Sitting practice is hard because when you are still and eyes closed your mind goes even more hard-out than it usually does.

    That's why it develops a more profound concentration: you have to learn to deal with the movement of your mind, by way of your own resources, not because something outside is occupying your mind.

    (Not eyes closed, though! Keep your eyes open! They mind drifts too much with closed eyes, and the great majority of people meditating with eyes closed have some level of dreaminess in their practice, whether they're aware of it or not.)

    It's like asking whether you will get more strength just swinging your arms, or swinging your arms with heavy weights attached. The weights are harder, and sometimes you have to build up to them, but there's little question that the difficulty helps you produce greater results.

    Having said that, the focus required in Taiji can certainly help you, and the energetic aspect can also help bring your mind to relative clarity. It just will likely not have the depth and stability of concentration developed through sitting.

    You also have to keep up the practice -- you have to apply what you've gained consistently through your daily life. This amounts to mindfulness practice -- engaged awareness through the activities of your life.

    But mindfulness is not the same as sitting concentration meditation. In sitting practice you develop a deep focus, like a laser -- which is not always necessary in life. You learn to let go of the mind that has a hold on you. You let it go and you return to the object of meditation. Each time the mind moves, you return as soon as you realize it. Returning again, again, again, your connection with the object becomes deeper and deeper. In this way you not only develop expertise in letting go of disturbances, you also come closer to your original mind, which is already perfectly concentrated.

    If you practice in this way you may reach a point of effortless clear concentration, and you may then get a taste of your true self (your original mind without the layer of thinking upon it). This is an extremely beneficial experience to have; it can show you your correct direction in life, organizing all your priorities.

    So concentration is not the same as mindfulness, though mindfulness requires some beginning level of concentration. If your intention is just to calm an overactive mind (which is a beneficial intention), I think it's quite appropriate to use a body-based practice to help you, as well as a mindfulness or momentary calming practice throughout the day. Calming the mind for half an hour won't help much if you spend another 15 hours making the mind busy and distressed.

    Once you learn to settle and relax in Taiji or Qigong you can apply it in a moment, letting go of whatever is gripping you at that time and allowing the mind and energy to flow more smoothly. So this could possibly be something you practice 50 times or more per day, just settling for a second or two. Even without learning any technique you can do something like this.

    But I also want you to consider an intent beyond just relieving stress. Please consider offering your presence and peace to others, and to the whole world. This is possible to have as an intent; many people live that way. It's a happy life path,a meaningful one. If you only try to get things for yourself, your mind stays small and lacks flow, and you can't be happy. If you offer yourself to others, your mind becomes wide, and this wide mind has a peace, stability and flow not attainable otherwise.
