    It is quite possible to enjoy silence, and some are more inclined toward silence than others, either through their bent of nature — their karmic patterning — or through deepening practice. But if, resting in silence, one perceives the movements of the thinking mind one will see the very ingrained habitual movement away from emptiness, boundlessness, formlessness, and silence. Humans are form-seeking machines. It doesn't mean that silence isn't our birthright and our original nature, but our human situation innately means that we have (as Zen Patriarch Seng Chao said) "anxiety about emptiness". It can be ignored and disowned; it can be perceived and overcome; but it is a force to be reckoned with nonetheless.

    In more advanced practitioners, the habitual filling-in of emptiness may be expressed in more subtle ways, such as the enjoyment of the feeling of stillness, or inclination toward certain states of concentrative absorption. Utter openhandedness and complete, unmoving embrace of all states is exceedingly rare, and it doesn't happen automatically but only through extremely devoted practice. For example, history is filled with the tales of practitioners who had profound awakenings and yet harbored lingering attachments: they awoke to great truths but, whether they were aware of it or not, still failed to completely embrace emptiness or boundlessness. The fortunate ones either perceived their lingering attachment on their own or were shaken out of their delusion by a master.

    The four levels of dhyana or concentrative absorption (as described in Buddhism) show us the the progressive relinquishment of traces within relative silence. Even after attaining samadhi, which is a radically brilliant and effortless concentration, one is still only in the first level of dhyana. Further letting-go of conception and discernment, and then of the joy of dhyana, and then even of the neutral sense of well-being, are necessary before true unwavering, unchoosing stillness can be said to be attained.

    The movement to respond, to voice disagreement, to speak of "I" and "my situation", to seek out teachers and teachings, and to formulate concepts and realizations are all movements to fill in or cover what is originally silent and empty. If one is very satisfied with silence, no need to even open the mouth to respond. Neither agreement nor disagreement are necessary.

    Mahasi's and Soryu Forall's instructions are lovely. It's important not to make a "thing" of Unknowing. There is actually no such thing as confusion; there is only the mind as it is. There is actually no person who accomplishes "just doesn't know". When the moment hits you so deeply that your mind can't move anywhere, your don't-know and the mind of the Universe are not separate.
