    > Obviously a teacher is great for this, but I can't imagine anything that could be more valuable in my life than a connection with the divine.

    In some traditions the two are not in the slightest bit separate. And in fact, the master/guru can be the most direct and accessible connection, because she/he is right here with you.

    One should always approach these traditions with appropriate care, patience, and research rather than diving in blindly or superstitiously, out of desire for attainment.

    When appropriate trust is established, an awakened teacher is the divine. And this reveals the critical point as far as the student is concerned: that we can likewise become the divine; we can become what we, in essence, already are.

    ~

    It is often the case that what is established by a luminary gets diluted or even perverted by his/her successors. Even with the best intent, misconception can grow, institutional politics can influence the unfolding of the organization, 'good ideas' for spreading the work can have a hidden shadow, personal preferences can be disguised as teachings, cultural influences can veil parts of the work, and group dynamics can take on a life of their own. It doesn't necessarily mean there are sinister aims at work (though it may); it means that every path has its shadow, and we need to proceed with awareness and self-responsibility. It's the people who grasp at fancy experiences and special powers who are most easily fooled.
