    What are some good practices to suffer consciously?

    Another very skillful way to deal with suffering is to use it to grow in compassion for others. One realizes that this suffering is not merely one's own. Despite the hero/martyr scenarios the ego projects, one is not isolated in the situation of suffering; it is but a taste of the suffering shared by all beings.

    This recognition quickly liberates a person from the narrow self-centered view that exacerbates the suffering, and brings purpose to the situation: "May I liberate all beings from their suffering."

    Then 'my' suffering no longer takes the position at the front of the stage. The picture widens.

    We have suffering built into our habit. Our energy habitually turns around on itself, both self-fascinated and self-irritated. We are constantly making 'second arrows'. You know? When the Buddha was asked if he experiences pain like regular people, he replied that of course he feels pain, but he doesn't receive the 'second arrow' â€” the Buddha doesn't for a moment fall into 'poor me' thinking. There is only the reality, no added drama, no resistance, no revisiting and brooding, no magnification, not the slightest trace of a 'me' experiencing the pain.

    But our self-centered habit is to keep turning our attention back to our situation. "How am I doing now? Is it better... worse...? How do I compare? What's a better situation? Oh, now this is going wrong too! How bad can things get?" Turning the attention around to I, my, me is the classic navel-gazing energy; it puts you and your problems ahead of everything else in the universe.

    The correct movement of your energy is radiance. When you are centered, your mind shines without hindrance. You reach out to others, because the obstacle of self-centered thinking doesn't appear. The idea of not having enough doesn't arise. Original mind has enough; original mind is already everything. So the individual centered in original mind can offer, can be generous.

    The heart that is self-concerned suffers. The heart that extends to others has happiness. Even connecting to the suffering of others is a kind of happiness; when you are open to the pain of the world, that is a kind of joy. Compassion springs from your center.

    In certain practices this radiant relationship has been formalized into a training. Tonglen (exchange of suffering) and Brahmavihara ('divine abidings') practices are like this, calling on the fathomless source of compassion within you.
