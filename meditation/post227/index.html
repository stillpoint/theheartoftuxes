    After one year of meditation, I think I'm starting to lose the grip.

    It seems very strange to me that those at a zen priory would recommend closing the eyes. I've visited a lot of zen centers and temples in a lot of different schools, and none of them recommended closed eyes. But it's interesting how we find our own pathways and our own unique experiences.

    I wonder if the recommendation you encountered was either a one-time instruction for the sake of calming down, with the intent to later open the eyes; or if it was an instruction to half-close the eyes, which corresponds to the teachings passed down from antiquity.

    If you look at a Buddha image, you will see that the eyes are gently half-closed, half-open. This expresses a gentle, balanced relationship with the world. Our human habit is to attach to objects: we either open our eyes wide and grasp at things outside, trying to possess and control them, or we close our eyes completely and indulge in inner objects like fantasies and sensations. The half-open eyes reflect a mind that has a non-grasping relationship with objects â€” it doesn't fly out and it doesn't get lost within. In fact, this clear mind doesn't create subject-object separation at all. It has complete integrity.

    But keeping the eyes open has perhaps a more immediate beneficial effect on our practice: it keeps us awake and engaged with the present reality. Over longer meditation sessions, and certainly when sitting for hours throughout the day, you may need all the help you can get to keep from wafting into dreaminess, distraction, sluggishness, or sleep. Seeing something clear and stable in front of you is a great help, a firm anchor to the present moment.

    It is said that the floor (or wall, if you face a wall during meditation) is one of your main teachers. When the floor is present clearly, your meditation is going well. When the floor disappears, you know you've gone off somewhere.

    So are your senses clear? Can you see, smell, taste, touch, and hear clearly; or is something else invading your clear mind? Clear mind reflects clearly; things are allowed to be what they truly are. That has the same quality as enlightenment or awakening; it is not different. So practicing awakening has some literal meaning: you stay awake and aware with what is. The wall is the wall, the floor is the floor. The wall is white; the floor is brown. Sugar is sweet; salt is salty. Clear, as-is.
