    Why do some meditation techniques involve holding your breath after breathing in and out?

    It's more properly called pranayama or breath practice than meditation. Though the two fields are closely related, true meditation doesn't try to control the breath.

    There are many layers of teaching on the breath and on the meaning of the various stages of the breath cycle. If you sense it yourself, you can feel the in-breath informing your presence, the out-breath informing release, and retention informing stillness.

    The breath is sometimes related to the phrase "soham" in yoga pranayama. Soham is both the phrase "I am that" (= I am the universal, the absolute, the divine) as well as the actual sound of breath (soooo... haaaaaam... sooooo... haaaaam....). So the inhalation is said to establish "I", the exhalation is said to establish "am that", and the suspended space or retention at the turning of the breath is said to open to the ineffable mind space.

    In connecting to the still space at the turning of breath, one can merge with spacious mind. It is at this phase where much of the actual retention and cultivation of energy (prana) is performed. When one is purified of worldly afflictions and then fully merges with this space it is understood to be a divine nourishment â€” one is fed by the divine energy of that spacious mind rather than by physical breath. Thus one sign of mastery in some traditions is that the practitioner can be in the breathless state at will, and indefinitely.

    On a physical level, the phases of breath are adjusted to bring balance. Inhalation is stimulating; retention at the turn between inhalation and exhalation is warming and nourishing; and exhalation is cooling. (I don't remember what is said to be the function of the turn between exhalation and inhalation.)

    Some psychotherapeutic techniques use phases of the breath to control the mind and calm the nervous system. 'Square' breathing, which is almost as you describe, is sometimes prescribed for anxiety attacks, for instance. But it is apparent that many practitioners don't really understand the breath and what it does; many prescribe techniques just because they've been told to, or because someone else does it, not because they know the actual effects.

    Since the breath is so tied to the balance of thought, emotion, energy, tension and relaxation, and nervous response, one would think it's a good idea to know precisely what one is trying to bring to balance, and what breath pattern will do so. But there's a lot of 'tinkering' out there â€” people guessing and experimenting based on authority rather than real knowledge.

    While proper meditation can be practiced in its basic form from a book or soundtrack, pranayama/breathing practice is much more volatile, and can lead to negative effects much more easily; so breathing practice should be learned from an expert teacher and practiced under her/his watchful eye.

    In any case, though it can be very beneficial within its own context, and can be a very good partner practice, it is not proper meditation. Meditation does not try to control processes; it lets them be to find their own natural pathways. Reality is allowed to be reality, without assuming to impose one's individual will and view upon it.

    Forced breathing also has some support in Japanese Zen, although thankfully it is not as popular as true meditation. Some lineages and teachers instruct students to push the exhalation forcefully. If you try it, you can feel a sort of vitality and power. It is this feeling that so easily becomes an attachment, and which has more to do with ego enhancement than real power. In any case, the forced breath is said to trigger the same survival response that being choked or holding the breath provokes. The mind responds with extreme alertness. (The mind can also respond with manic obsession and control-freak attitude, but they don't mention that in the training.) So this is another possible function of the held breath: it provides a moment of near-death that tonifies the attention.
