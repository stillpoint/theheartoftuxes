    title: New to meditation: zafu cushion? summary: 1.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2015-06-02 16:52:26 some_url:

    The color of zafu chosen tends to be either based on preference, style, or the colors used in the particular culture or lineage the practice is from. There's no particular experience or power one derives from a different color, except for maybe some slight psychological influence (as in feng shui). This influence would be akin to wearing bright colors when you're feeling joyful or dark colors when you're feeling purposeful. You can still be joyful in dark colors or purposeful in light colors.

    • Zen temples and practice centers tend to use black or brown zafus. The aesthetic is earthy and dark. This goes along with the practice room etiquette of refraining from wearing bright or vividly patterned clothing, gaudy jewelry, or items with words printed on them. It's mainly to provide a very low-key, non-stimulating environment so there's nothing in particular for the mind to grasp at on the outside.

    • Japanese Zen tends to be quite stark. Black is usually the chosen color for both zafus and robes. It is evocative of the depth of Unknowing.

    • Korean and Chinese Zen tend to be more earthy. Brown is often used for zafus in these practice places, reminiscent of the Earth â€” pointing to both your physical basis and your vow of supporting all beings (the whole world). Grey is used for Korean Zen robes (coming from the Chinese Taoist robes), symbolic of emptiness. Grey is the undefined area between black and white, and as such suggest ungraspability and emptiness.

    • Sometimes zafus or other cushions or benches are used in Chinese temples, in ocher or mustard color. This hearkens to the ocher robes of the forest monks of ancient Buddhism.

    • Tibetan Buddhist temples almost invariably use brighter colors, most frequently gold and burgundy, symbolic of the dual attainments of wisdom and compassion. These practice places may use zafus or large, usually rectangular, cushions called gomden. Tibetan decor, like the practice tradition, tends to be quite lush and rich.

    • Yoga centers vary widely in their choice of colors, some opting for light colors that convey purity, others using the vividly contrasting colors of India, and yet others keeping to whatever color scheme they feel goes with their practice style or corporate image.

    I would think that a color that matches your decor is as good as any, but with a couple of caveats: if it's too eye-catching (say, hot pink), if it has pictures or words on it, or if you become too enamored of it, it could be distracting.

    The rule of thumb is to respect your seat as the place of your enlightened practice, which means it's the seat of your already awakened nature, equal to a Buddha. Some traditions are more formal than others, but in general you might want to treat your zafu with a bit more mindfulness than you might habitually use with other cushions or furniture; don't kick it with your feet, for example. Eventually in your practice tradition you may come to be respectful and mindful of every object in your life. Besides that, don't make special or fancy thinking; your true mind doesn't depend on any contraptions.

    ~

    Actually, far more important than color is the configuration and composition of the cushion. It should be firm enough and high enough to support your sit-bones, so your knees can make good contact with the mat, providing you with a firm triangular base. If you can go shopping with someone familiar with meditation postures, or if the salesperson is knowledgable, it would be good to actually try out the cushion and see if it works for you.

    Kapok or buckwheat filled cushions are usually adjustable: you can remove material to make them lower/looser.

    If your knees don't touch the floor even when you use a zafu, you may need to use other support cushions under them until your flexibility develops; or a chair or bench might work for you.
