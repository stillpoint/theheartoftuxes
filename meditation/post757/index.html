    A lot of people have 'great things to say' about a lot of things. How much of this comes from wisdom, and how much from opinion, attachment, habit, preference, and limited view?

    In general, people don't want to do what it takes to face their minds; rather, they want to find the easy way, the shortcut, the magic pill. In general, people don't want to hear that the process depends on consistent simple effort and gradual accumulation; rather, they hope a short-term heroic effort or careless abandon will do it. Young men seem especially susceptible to this kind of thinking. They have yet to develop the wisdom that comes through patient, gradual progress. The countless masters and advanced practitioners I've met did not have any secret to success other than doing the hard and often unspectacular work of facing their minds without gimmicks and crutches.

    Your plan is not only misguided, but severely misguided. First develop stability. First cultivate concentration â€” the ability to relinquish trains of thought and other distractions, and return to what's real. Create the foundation, the basis, of consistent non-avoidance, non-manipulation. Develop the practice-habit, which is letting go of all planning, making, controlling, and hoping. These are all different ways of saying the same thing. That foundation is essential. It is to be established first, in a way that is solid and unwavering.

    Going into the content of the mind is incorrect. That is not meditation. Meditation means keeping your unmoving awake mind, regardless of content. But you not only intend to get embroiled with content, but want to do it while high. Many children play at cooking with mudpies or pretend to make magic potions by combining a bit of dad's cologne with some peroxide from the cupboard, and so on. They are very serious about it, mystifying themselves. There is a very similar quality to this kind of practice. It's a glee in seeking magic outcomes by tinkering and manipulating. That sort of self-mystifying glee is a sign of the ego content involved. It's not a surrender to proper method, but a way of keeping your hands in the stew, a way of maintaining a sense of control. Although your intent may be very sincere (to go beyond insecurity and fear), the plan is based on a big mistake. It is an inherently reckless and manipulative approach, seeking to play with images and feelings and gain control of them. It is in fact an ego-based controlling motivation, and is in opposition to the correct direction of meditation.

    In meditation, you develop freedom not by boosting the ego's control but by practicing freedom â€” practicing non-grasping, non-attachment, non-manipulation. Then whatever appears, whether pleasurable or uncomfortable, whether uplifting or fearsome, has no power to sway the mind. When you practice non-attachment, your power of non-attachment develops.

    But when you practice attachment and manipulation, your ego's habit of engaging with the content of the mind becomes stronger. This fascination with prompting a certain kind of (say, intense or supposedly more meaningful) experience is just more of the same self-absorbed ego habit that causes the insecurity and fear in the first place. The more need there is to grasp control over an inherently fluid and ungraspable reality, the more insecurity and fear one experiences.

    All meditators will experience strange events; and all meditators will experience periods of extreme plainness and boredom. The essential nature of these is the same. They are both mental content. It is like going through vastly different emotions and situations in your dream: when you wake up, you realize that it was all dream material; it all had the same essential dream substance. So all content is equal. None is more special. But attachment makes 'special'. Meditation does not mess about with the content; it maintains openhandedness regardless of content. This is called equanimity. You must develop a mind of equanimity rather than trying to control your situation and make something happen. That is the difference between mature and immature in meditation.

    So fire and flame and bubbles, scary or fantastic or profound â€” none of this is significant. I'll say it again so it can sink in: none of that is significant. What is significant is if you cultivate your non-attachment to any of that. That is what will free you from insecurity and fear. You will gradually wake up from the dream of self upon which the insecurity and fear are based.
