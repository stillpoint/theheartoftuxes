    If you're clear on the method of your meditation, only return to the method. During meditation time, nothing is more important than meditation; otherwise, you wouldn't be doing meditation. You are practicing the ability to disengage and return: disengage from distraction, including thoughts, and return to the method. Every time, just this time disengage and return, disengage and return. Then your returning will become stronger and more frequent, and your going-away will become weaker and less frequent. Eventually you will be stable in reality, and wandering thoughts will not control your actions and reactions.

    In learning how things go, many new meditators then try to use that knowledge to manipulate their experience. This is not correct practice. Don't check "Have I really let go?" and similar such assessments. Only be involved with the method. Only be involved with the method. Don't check.

    One way of doing this is to say 'later' when something grabs your attention. "Not now; later. Now's the time to return to the method." When your returning becomes strong, you can use it at appropriate times in life: "Not now. Now's the time to take care of my child." (Or "...my work", "...eating", etc.)

    When it's useful, you can consider things and try to resolve them. But often thinking isn't the helpful thing. We only have the strong thinking habit, so we try to apply it everywhere.

    >When all you have is a hammer, everything starts looking like a nail.

    Sometimes it's necessary to think. Sometimes it's necessary to give space and let realizations well up from the depths, or let things play out until the situation becomes clear.

    And sometimes it's necessary to take action; even if you don't see the outcome, sometimes it's necessary to at least take a step, in good faith — improve your position, so to speak. When the US Marines are in a seemingly untenable position, they will still do whatever they can to improve their situation. They may make improvements to their trenches; they may have a meal and hot drink. Taking care of each next thing, they leave no room for self-defeat.

    The aim isn't to abandon thinking; the aim is to not let thinking control you. When it's time to think, you want to be clear about it. Efficient, creative. You want your thoughts to have space to move effectively, and you don't want to be reactive to them, pushed and pulled around into reckless behavior or useless emotion. (Useful emotion is sincere, and fits the situation: appropriate fear when one needs to be aware of risk; anger when justice needs to be upheld; grief in times of loss; joy when there's cause for it; love when there's opportunity for it.) When you've developed a strong ability to return in meditation, it makes your mind less reactive and more spacious. This is like a mirror that reflects objects rather than a dog that chases objects. It is like the sky that allows things to come and go clearly.

    So before you sit down to figure out an issue (if it's even possible to figure it out — often it's not, and we just have to do our best and 'let it all come out in the wash'), it's essential to be able to re-set, to return to neutral. That is why we practice not only when the shit has hit the fan, but also when things are going well. We need to be clear at all times, to direct our lives well; and we need to develop the returning ability in preparation for difficult times. So when it's time to think, think. But first return to zero; return to the before-thinking mind, which is non-reactive, and only reflects what it's facing.

    Finally, I'll leave you with this question: what makes something a 'stressor'? Is it in the nature of the event itself, or is does it have to do with your own perspective? The Chinese word weiji (crisis) teaches us about this question. It is composed of two separate characters: wei, or danger; and ji or 'crucial moment', or 'emergence', or 'point of change'. There is a risk to crisis situations, but there is also a powerful potential for change in them. Depending on your relationship — whether you're functioning from reactivity, or whether you're functioning from your clear before-thinking mind — this may make the situation a stressor or a profoundly lively and rich experience.

    For example, it is the tradition of Tibetan Buddhists to welcome difficult people and situations, because they are seen as the best way to develop stability and compassion. If we didn't have difficulty, our spiritual practice would be weak.
