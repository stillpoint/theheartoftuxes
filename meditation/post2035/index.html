    One of my masters would ask "Why do you eat every day?" This is a good question. Every day we eat, we drink, we go do our work or studies, we arrange our lives... but why? Is it just going through the motions, just repeating a pattern and sort of unconsciously just clinging to life; or is there a knowledge of what we're on about?

    What is this day for — what is the food and effort and time going toward?

    We could ask the same of our good tools and personal development: we may, if we are determined, actually gain some insight and self-control; but so what if we do — what will we use them for?

    This is what I mean when I talk about clarifying our direction in life.

    >To be honest, I wouldn't really know where to start to look for one [teacher]. I'm thinking that maybe finding someone online would be my best bet. That way, at least teachers experience can be validated.

    Your response reveals some fear and mistrust. Where does that come from?

    When we don't have direct experience, the imagination takes over, and we can depart further and further from the way things actually are. This is like having a long-distance girlfriend or boyfriend: the longer you're apart, the more your thinking tends to replace the reality of the person and your togetherness. Your idea and image of the other person tends to come to the forefront, and the many subtle, complex, unaccounted, and unpredictable realities fade. Then you are left relating more to the story in your head than to a real person — until the next time you talk or meet, and you get a shot of reality, a dose of the whole being that you could never make up with thought alone.

    This is something like the relationship most people have with spiritual teaching before they actually meet a teacher. The relationship is all in their heads, oversimplified and skewed, perhaps shaped by scandalous stories or utopian legends or comical pop-culture references. The longer you spend imagining without direct experience, the more imagination takes over. Imagination and speculation then become your reality, and they actually steer your decisions and limit your possibilities. You become the product of your limited thought rather than opening to something new.

    Yes, it is wise to be sure of a teacher's validity, both in their credentials and in your personal experience of them. That is why you take steps to research, and meet, and try some of their offerings, and go step by step. No one requires you to surrender yourself without very good reason and having already worked together for a long time. You go step by step, little by little; and when you yourself see that the teachings are embodied in this person, and that their action matches their words, and that their instruction has panned out in your experience — then you take further steps of commitment. Commitment matches experience; but first you need at least some kind of experience, you need to try something.

    If you step outside of your fear and withdrawal, then you really do know where to start to look for a teacher. For example, you are here on this forum, where there are many other meditators; and at least a few of the people who post here will have real-life experience with a teacher. You can start by asking here.

    You also already know other things you can try. The critical point is to do it, to actually try.

    I'm not saying you must. This is your life, and it's up to you to live it as you see fit. But we fool ourselves so easily, and close doors on ourselves. It's very easy to say "I can't"; and maybe you have reasons why that is working for you at this time. Maybe you need to see yourself as incapable for a while. But when you're ready to move out of that stance, you can take on "I can..." and "I will..." and you can actually start taking steps.
