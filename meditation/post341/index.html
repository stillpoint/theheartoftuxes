    TheHeartofTuxes

    If you draw a shape — say, a Chinese character — into the ground with a stick, one rainfall might wash it away. If it's only drawn very lightly, maybe just a breeze will erase it. But if you draw it heavily or many times, it will take more time and more washing before it is erased.

    Our karma is like this. Although we have come through with a refreshing rain, the underlying patterns may remain. Some things we have only done lightly; other things we have ingrained deeply. What can we do? Our practice is the same: wash.

    Besides the issue of patterns, there is reverberation. When you speak into a cave, there is an echo. Even after the speaking is finished, an echo comes back. When you shout loudly the echo will reverberate for quite a while. So even if there is no significant attachment 'in your subconscious', the body still has echoes. It's like when you've been startled when watching a movie: even after the scene has ended there is still agitation, excitement. The body response is still playing out.

    For some of us this becomes problematic, as the body response can trigger new thoughts: "Oh, this is bad. What's going on now? I'm losing control! Is my heart okay? Now I don't even know where this feeling is coming from! It might be some secret danger. Maybe there's something wrong with me...." And so on, prompting a vicious cycle of thought and response.

    You already know how to fully let it go; but maybe you confuse your letting go with needing it to be gone. Those are two different things. Letting it go means open your hands, detach from the thought, and return to your method of meditation (or return to your theme of mindfulness).

    That is your job: release and return. Your job has nothing to do with checking whether anxiety has gone, or how much it has gone, or if it's returning. Your job isn't checking; your job is letting go and returning. Leave the rest to itself.

    So anxiety is okay. Anxious thoughts don't hook you in. Fast-beating heart is just as it is. You don't need to generate further responses. Only return to the method. (Do you have a proper method? Have you learned it from a single, proper source? Have you learned it well? Do you follow it meticulously?)

    Earlier this week there was a post of a video on regulating physical response through breathing. You might find that interesting and helpful. The gist of the talk was that maintaining even rhythm of breath causes physical response to become regular, which then prompts chemical, emotional, and mental processes to become regular and balanced. In this issue, fast or slow breathing doesn't matter so much as evenness.

    While that can be a helpful technique, take care that you don't get into a controlling relationship with yourself, trying to force certain feelings and responses to appear or disappear. Again, your job isn't to check your situation or condition. Your job is to let go of self-manipulation, let go of attachment, let things be what they are... and apply yourself to the task at hand.

    If anxiety is an ongoing issue, don't expect to cure it with meditation alone. Seek the assistance of someone expert in dealing with anxiety, so you can address the cause, not only wrestle with the aftermath.
