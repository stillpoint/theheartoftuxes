    What is the best form of meditation for someone who suffers from social anxiety?

    There is no "best"; there is only what works well. You'll get a lot of different answers to your question here, based on the particular experience that people have, and what they themselves have found useful. It may be the case that many or all of the suggestions would be helpful, or it may be the case that different suggestions will contribute to confusion.

    The first question is why you want to meditate. Do you want to meditate in order to overcome social anxiety, or do you want to overcome social anxiety so that you can meditate effectively and share the benefit of your meditation?

    We all have some attachment, some problem that besets us. Social anxiety, like other problems, comes from the thinking. The outside situation is the same for all of us — we walk the same streets, negotiate our way around the same people and environment, experience the same weather — but our thoughts shape our experience and our relationship to the situation. So you're right to think that finding some space in your thinking can help relieve your anxiety. And if you develop the ability to find that space quickly and easily, you will have a very useful tool.

    Most meditations will train you in disengaging from the thinking. Some involve the thinking, but most involve some object of attention to which you repeatedly return, letting go of the thinking. This is an ability you get by practicing it — letting go of this thought just now, and returning to the method of meditation.

    Some meditations, though, tend to involve more mental processing than others. The "energy stays up in the head". The method that some people here recommend, vipassana, is extremely helpful because it overturns our attachments at their roots; you see the nature of things, seeing through the illusion of the things you grasp or fear. But vipassana does require a certain level of calmness and concentration to even perform it; so if you're starting from a place of agitation you'll likely need another practice to help you regain your center first.

    I might tend to recommend a meditation that is (A) very simple and (B) body-based. Getting into the body can help you get out of the head, rather than trying to arrange your thoughts with your thoughts. There are a few options I could suggest if you're interested — practices for letting go of the thinking and returning to neutral via the body.

    I would also suggest vigorous movement and exercise in general, as it will almost certainly help you feel better about yourself and the situation, will help circulate the pent-up emotional energy, and will help energy come down from its place stuck up in the head where it's making problematic thinking. Running, rowing, biking, bowing practice, and free dance are all good possibilities, among others. You don't have to fight yourself, just enjoy the movement.
