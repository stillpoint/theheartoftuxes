    Exercise and Meditation

    Physical cultivation has been part of traditional spiritual training for millennia, including bowing, work practice, and movement meditations. People only reading about spiritual practice in books and online may tend to have a lopsided, philosophical view; and our society's sedentary ways may cause us to miss out on balanced practice. The fact that sitting meditation itself is a physical embodiment of universal qualities is often missed.

    I've recommended physical practice for a long time. Here are a couple of excerpts from my posts in other threads:

    >…I would most recommend a movement practice for you, something that involves the body, the breath, the mind, and the emotional heart all together. Free dance/ecstatic dance/conscious dance and other wholistic movement practices are very, very effective for your situation. By their very nature they encourage integrity rather than separation: instead of feeling (or not feeling) something, thinking about something else to do with it, and reflecting something else altogether with the body, these movement practices bring all parts of you into alignment. In dancing your own dance you learn to trust what moves through you and harmonize with it. Everything becomes grist for the mill: you can experience and express the most delicate and the most raging emotions. And then you can move on to the next thing. That is very wonderful, to be able to move on to new life.

    >For a young guy, I can hardly think of anything that contributes to emotional intelligence and flow better than free-dance practice. [By free dance, I mean dance that comes from within you rather than trying to follow steps given to you by someone else.] It has the added benefits that it is fantastic aerobic exercise (thus addressing some possible physical causes of your difficulties), it deepens the brain-body connection, it immediately helps emotions and energies flow, and it is awesome FUN!!

    >Each of the following movement practices is wonderful, and they are all quite different from each other:

    > Body-Mind Centering > 5Rhythms >* Authentic Movement

    >You can also check out your local ecstatic dance jam or "barefoot boogie" if you want to dip your toes in the water. Just be aware that practices like 5Rhythms have very pertinent teachings and very expert teachers that a dance jam can't come close to duplicating.

    >…many people find their mind too active and out of control to even begin meditating correctly. In that case, you are right — just sitting through suffering without any transformation is pointless. There are very good alternatives. In these situations, a physical practice is very useful.

    >A rhythmic, whole-body practice like bowing is very, very useful to bring the focus down out of your agitated mind and into a more stable place in your body. Other possibilities are running, swimming, Taiji, and — highly recommended — free dance. Bowing is the traditional meditative "emergency method" when someone is unable to sit. In addition, many traditions include bowing as part of the meditative practice (in addition to chanting, sitting, and walking meditations); the combination of practices work together to train the body, speech, and mind.

    >If you try some vigorous whole-body-and-breath movement, I'm sure you'll experience its settling effect. The body gets tired (in a good way) and is ready to sit down; the breath invigorates and clears the mind. It's like going for a long hike and then naturally being very ready to sit down quietly at the lake: the energy has come down, and the mind's waves have settled just as the lakes ripples settle when the winds subside.
