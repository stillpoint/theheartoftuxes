    I still see no "buy-in" to Meditation.

    These factors are very important, and in fact decisive as to the results of meditation:

    • Intention
    • Consistency
    • Method
    • Training

    Intention

    It is quite incredible how influential intention is upon the fruitfulness of meditation, and quite amazing how easily it may be overlooked. If you enter with the wrong idea, however innocently and reasonably, you could end up spinning your wheels for a long time.

    For example, the aim to quiet the mind is fine, but it can create both a limiting mindset and an improper relationship with one's psyche. A self-centered direction that seeks only "for myself" makes it impossible to surrender, impossible to move beyond the ego and thus experience deeper states of absorption. And the expected result of a quiet mind misses the point of accepting and harmonizing with all mind-states, and so fails to develop the deep compassion and self-trust that are felt as peace, satisfaction, and joy.

    For Tibetan Buddhists, for example, compassionate and open-handed intent (called Bodhicitta, "the mind of awakening") is seen as all-important, and is cultivated in the beginning, middle, and end of practice. The practice of intent and the eventual enlightenment are seen as non-separate.

    Consistency

    On and off practice gets on and off results. Cause and effect are clear. No meditation ever goes to waste, but consider 10 minutes once a day for a few months in view of the other influences that you engage in and that have bombarded you for the other 1430 minues a day, for months on end, all the years of your life. An indistinct, indifferent effort produces vague and undefinable results. If you want an extraordinary result then you have to generate an extraordinary cause.

    Method

    You have made no mention of your method, other than to say it involves mindfulness. Again, no moment of mindfulness is ever wasted; but method and result are inextricably tied. There are thousands of "meditation" methods, but many of them fall short or are downright misleading; and lack of clear method can be just as much of a hindrance as following a bad or low-class method.

    Your method of meditation should be clear, it should be effective, and it should be followed meticulously. If any of these points is missed, your meditation will miss the mark. So have you studied a method proven to be effective — one with a wide and open-hearted intent, and one with very clear instructions? Have you become completely clear on what to do with your mind and body during meditation? And have you followed this method scrupulously, moment-to-moment?

    Training

    The personal ego is a slippery, shifty, impish con artist. It can co-opt almost any spiritual strategy so that it — the ego, the self-identity — will always have the upper hand, and will never be dissolved in surrender, unity, openness. Even the best instruction can be subtly twisted into self-aggrandizement, indulgence, denial, or delusion by our own tricky mind. That is why we are seldom qualified to lead our own training, and why the presence of a guide with the ability to rise above his or her own ego is so invaluable.

    "The doctor who treats himself has a fool for a doctor." If we lead our own practice, we tend to remain blind to our habits and pitfalls until they've produced enough suffering to wake us up. With a good teacher and controlled practice environment, not only can our suffering be more efficiently aimed at awakening, in some cases we can avoid those repeated cycles altogether and progress very quickly.

    Once you have an actual taste of stillness you will not only know its value, you will also see how difficult it is to preserve it, how many things have to go right and how many conditions have to be met, both internal and external. It is really only when you have your first profound experience that you start to become aware of the true benefit of training; before that, you are just operating from a relative viewpoint where things are either better or worse but never pure and complete in themselves.

    Training means not only sitting meditation, but keeping correct relationship with the mind and with the situation moment to moment. It means entering into relationship with others under a spiritual context — something that is usually more rigorous than we are able to muster on our own. Often only connection with a teacher and a community of practitioners gives us the wherewithal to move beyond our usual pleasure-seeking and discomfort-avoiding habits, to a place of stability and equanimity.
