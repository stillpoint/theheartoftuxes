    Meditating in a cold room

    Most zen centers and many other meditation centers I've visited tend to keep their room temperature colder than usual. I think this goes along with conservation of resources as well as encouraging practitioners to be rigorous, vigorous, and self-reliant.

    Physical work and vigorous exercise should be part of your training, and this can be performed just before sittings so your body will be more comfortable and you will appreciate the cooler conditions. Bowing, Sun Salutations, Hindu Squats, Taiji, running, or other exercises that unify whole-body movement with breath are extremely supportive to a meditation practice. At many centers and temples 108 bows (full prostrations) are performed every morning before sitting practice, and practitioners wanting to advance quickly will practice 500, or 1,000, or more bows daily.

    Zhan zhuang standing practice in Qigong can heat your body up very quickly, and can train your ability to allow flow within minutes; and flow means circulation, which means warmth. This is possible within a few weeks or months of standing practice, depending on your body and aptitude.

    Some yoga meditation centers will keep their rooms warmer. They tend to focus more on relaxation than on vigor, although the distinction is not black-and-white, and you'll find variations among all styles.

    One great teaching you may receive if you persevere is that complete comfort is not necessary; and in fact, happiness is not dependent on comfort! A bit of cold, a bit of pain is not only okay but it keeps you awake and engaged. Newcomers may find it easier when they sit with others. In a group setting, the effort of others helps you to stay with it, and even pride keeps you going where otherwise you may give up.

    The cold will not have any negative side-effects on your meditation, not at all. But when sitting in the cold you should protect the vulnerable areas that can be easily penetrated by cold:

    • Make sure to cover your upper back and the back of your neck, up to the base of your skull. This spot is notorious in Chinese Medicine as a place where wind and cold easily enter the body and create sickness.

    • Keep your lower back well protected. Even if you don't want to wear many layers on the rest of your body, the kidney area just below the ribs should be kept warm and protected against drafts. Although cold may not penetrate as easily there, the repercussions are more serious if it does. That area is seen as the deep energy storage for the body, and its weakness is cold; so protecting it will help prevent the kind of deep exhaustion that otherwise can occur in winter.

    • Cover your knees or wear thicker pants or warmers. The knees are another gateway where cold can enter, and they are connected to the deep storage area around the kidneys. As large joints they also don't have as much blood supply circulating through them, so the joints themselves become vulnerable to injury.

    • If it's cold enough to warrant it, you may want to wear a hat, as some monks do.

    Your meditation practice will actually generate energy. No need to think about that or try to do anything special; the act of letting go of distraction and returning to your object of meditation will help energy flow in your body, which will gradually build energy. The important thing is not to be tense about it — don't try to get or make anything with your practice, only relax and do it. Grasping at mind states creates a tension that hinders circulation (in fact, grasping is the lack of flow).

    If you find your body becoming cold through the course of the days and weeks you can add warmth and energy to it. Here are a few strategies that can help you through a cold season:

    • hot baths
    • hot water bottle placed on the lower back, or back of the knees, or bottom of the feet (can be used as you work on computer, for instance, or when you go to bed)
    • soups with root vegetables
    • tea made with slices of fresh ginger (drink throughout the day; add honey if you wish)
    • moxibustion: using the ember from a burning stick of mugwort or charcoal on the lower back, all around the knees, and the bottom of the feet
    • stay active outdoors; the body adapts to cold
    • repeatedly exposing the body briefly to deep cold enourages cellular energy generation (mitochondria activity); lumberjacks may plunge their hands, feet, and face into snow to develop long-term resistance to cold, and some folks do regular plunges in cold baths or even icy lakes.

    You may also look into how you can adapt your living space — a bit of insulating foam/weatherstripping/plastic sheeting, perhaps?
