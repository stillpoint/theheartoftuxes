    Why try to see the mind as 'something' or 'nothing'? Why try to force an image or perspective on yourself?

    If you want to find out, find out. That means that there will be a period of not knowing. Not knowing is appropriate.

    Whether someone says mind is 'something' or whether they say it is 'nothing', what it actually is remains the same. So if it's important to you to know mind (and it is — very important!), then you ought to find a proper teacher of a proper traditional method of investigation, and follow the method, and receive the benefit of ongoing support and instructions, so that all doubt can be removed from the process. That is, so that you can continue looking and not-knowing, but you will have no doubt about how to do that.

    Trying to figure it out on your own builds your own misconceptions and doubts into the practice; so they continue to block the way despite your efforts.

    There are many styles and methods of meditation, including the category of self-inquiry or finding the nature of one's own mind. If we're talking about this category of practice, it doesn't start with some imagined or imposed description. It is open: you just look for your self, for your mind. Very soon you come to realize that you can't find it; there's just not-knowing. You experience the fact that there's nothing to hang onto, and that the witness can't witness itself. Then you rest in that not-knowing, without making anything up.

    It's very important to refrain from making anything up. Don't cling to ideas or images, because ideas and images aren't your mind; they only appear to or in your mind. You are looking for the one that everything happens for, the one that experiences everything. All else is to be let go.

    But some — perhaps many — people become quite agitated by having nothing to hold onto. This is our shared human anxiety with true nature: we fear and avoid and resist openness and boundlessness. Even though it is what we really are, and even though it is what we actually seek behind all of our efforts and goals, still we have ongoing anxiety about openness and boundlessness.

    The Third Patriarch of Zen, a very great master, recognized this:
    > Our shared human sickness is anxiety about emptiness.

    And for some meditators, this anxiety proves too overwhelming. They can't seem to continue without getting wrapped up in the need to cling to something!

    In a skillful teaching setting, the anxiety can be addressed. The teacher can put you at ease and help you face the task in the right, balanced way. But without access to this kind of support, you may want to try another kind of meditation in the meantime — something that will prepare you for later self-inquiry practice.

    This means choosing an object-based (usually concentration-style) meditation. In this style, you use one object to relieve you from attachment to all other objects. You stay with only that object, letting it become more and more clear in your attention. When your mind wanders, you return again and again to the one object.

    So this style of meditation has the benefit of offering you something to hold onto at first. But eventually, as you come closer and closer to the object, you merge with it. This is complete concentration (dhyana); and when it is effortless and spontaneous it is called samadhi. At these levels, subject and object are one. In that state, you are openness and boundlessness; and there is no agitation (there is no 'subject' to be agitated).

    The great master Sri Ramana Maharshi taught and recommended self-inquiry; but for those who were too anxious to practice it, he recommended concentration practice. If done properly, with proper instruction and proper mental approach, both ways lead to the same point. Also, concentration style can prepare you to undertake self-inquiry style at a later time.
