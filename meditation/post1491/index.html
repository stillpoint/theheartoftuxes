    Need help sorting out my morning routine

    Morning time is like a "little spring"; it's a time when the day's energy is just emerging and is not yet at full force (little summer). At that time, a healthy approach first nurtures the new energy rather than making sudden demands on it. A warm/hot nourishing drink, a small soup, or a small portion of hot, easily digestible grain adds heat energy, food energy, and nurturing yin (moisture) to the body.

    In contrast, working out hard first thing in the morning and then having a cold protein shake may be invigorating, but places a demand on the internal energy.

    You may like to try this order of things:

    • Hot tea with honey/grain drink/small soup/small bit of hot grain
    • Workout
    • Shower
    • Meditate
    • Mindful meal

    The hot drink first thing in the morning will help your body to wake up through gentle coaxing.

    The workout and shower will help you feel very settled and fresh as you enter meditation. If you need a few more bites of grain or sips of tea or soup before you sit, no problem; just don't fill the belly. If you're one who tries to take in protein within a half hour of working out, let it be light as possible, like branch chain amino supplement (tablets) with some tea.

    You will be able to feel whether food is interfering with meditation. In general, it's better to be a little hungry rather than bloated. (In an interesting side note, it's also considered better to have a little less sleep rather than too much: too little sleep is said to harm the body, but too much sleep is said to harm the spirit, which is the greater harm.)

    Eating a meal mindfully after sitting will not only help to gradually extend the meditative mind into the day's activities, it will help you to digest the meal better than if it were eaten immediately following a workout, both due to the energetic calmness and because the blood will have had a chance to return from the muscles and be applied to digestion.

    Having said all that, this is maybe a meticulous approach. You can do well enough if you just keep balance in mind. Try not to be too lax, and try not to put unreasonable demands on yourself. More demand also requires more nurturing (yin and yang have to grow together, not one without the other). If you stay mindful, you will get feedback from your body and from your life. You will know soon enough if you're eating too much, or not enough, or at the wrong time. And you will also get feedback when you are eating properly.

    In many temples, hours of early morning bowing, chanting, meditation, work practice, and even subsequent exercise come before the first meal of the day. But that's all within a context: one of the aims is to overcome attachment to both sleep and hunger, maintaining practice as the guiding principle; and a regimen where the mind is kept simple and focused throughout the day uses energy in a very different way than going out to work in the city, so the demand on food for energy is different.
