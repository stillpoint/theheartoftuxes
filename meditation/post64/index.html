    Sometimes we need a crutch for a while to help us live without it. When we're injured or sick, a crutch might help us get better.

    From one perspective, any kind of structure, schedule, routine, method, or tool could be conceived of as a 'crutch'. Just having a body is a crutch! Why did we take a body; why were we born at all? "Being born is the first mistake."

    But that's great that you don't want to lean on something unnecessarily; you want to find your own strength. Great!

    A lot of people here recommend Mindfulness In Plain English. The question is whether you can have faith in what it teaches. How do you gain faith? You need confidence in the teaching; and you need to verify it in practice.

    Confidence in the teaching generally comes through lineage and tradition (knowing that the training is passed down by enlightened beings and had been proven by countless practitioners over the centuries), through the certification of the teacher (validation of her/his attainment and quantification to teach by other masters), and through the living example of the masters and senior practitioners of the school (i.e., you see them embodying the good qualities you seek, you see them doing what it takes to practice completely, you see that their actions match their words, and so on).

    Verifying through your own practice means you learn the method well and try it out consistently, then you discover whether there is some result or not.

    To an extent, if you have faith in what the book teaches and if it teaches a good method, you can make progress learning from a book. Then at some point you will want to move on. But those who don't have faith, and try to invent, mix, hedge, or tweak the teaching, just get caught up in their own doubt and manipulation disguised as cleverness.

    Books can be nice supports for practice in that they organize your thoughts, highlight important points, and provide touchstones you can return to repeatedly. But books are also limited in their theme and scope. They don't evolve with you; they don't reflect your mind back to you, they don't respond to changing situations, they can't teach the broad path which addresses all areas of life, and they can't assure that you learn the right things in the right order.

    Also, some books are both written by an attained and capable teacher and are well written. But online forums are very different. Here, most people have little true experience and a lot of opinions. Many of them (us) talk in order to feel more like an expert. In the old days, a student wouldn't dream of spouting off while they still hadn't resolved their own practice; and their teacher would hit them if they did! These days, access to a forum or blog makes ignorance marketable. People participate in the fallacy that group opinion equals good teaching, and consensus equals truth. Basically, we mutually encourage each other to be deluded. Just look at how many posts here start with "Does anyone else...?" or "What do you think...?"

    So this is not the place to receive good teaching. Even if there were a group of highly evolved practitioners here (which we're not, for the most part), they'd still be anonymous and they'd still be talking about a bunch of different concepts from different schools and based on different training and experience; and furthermore, they don't know you, and your situation, and your journey. Too confusing! Not a source of proper teaching.
