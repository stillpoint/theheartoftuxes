    I've never heard of such an issue with meditation. I think you should at least rule out both ocular and neurological issues with medical specialists first.

    I do recommend open-eyed meditation whenever possible. A few methods may require closed eyes; but when that's not the case, open eyes is much, much better on several levels. (I've written about this in greater detail in previous posts.)

    Sometimes eye strain can happen, for a couple of reasons. First, there may be an attempt to focus on a single point in the field of vision. This amounts to the same as focusing on a computer screen or small design work; the eyes need to rest. So unless the method specifically calls for it, most open-eye meditation should be performed with a soft gaze, and not with sharp light striking the eyes.

    When instructed to gaze downward or forward, it means that while the eyes are cast down or forward, the vision is openly receptive rather than piercing or grasping at one point. Obsession about what's happening with the eyes is overcome by relaxing and offering more attention to the meditation method.

    Second, a somewhat related cause is the sort of dualistic seeking attitude that creates tension. The meditator conceives of practice as an attempt to attain a certain state or insight, and pursues the object of meditation as if it is external and out of reach. This is inherently disconnected and tense. Pushing, striving methods crop up here and there, and even boast established lineages behind them. It's not that they don't produce any result; in fact, they may produce quick results; but problem is the cost in terms of suppression, disconnection, and lack of proper integration: the quick result emerges without proper ground and direction, and can engender unwarranted pride. It's like the ancient and modern myths of heroes who attain power without having earned it; from Icarus to Doctor Octopus, we know how those stories turn out.

    But I digress.... In terms of turning the sight inward, this is not the physical sight, though it is common enough to find people making that mistake. It refers to the attention. Without proper teaching about where the mind resides, one may think of it as a physical organ, and thus one consciously or unconsciously tries to turn the eyes around to see it. This of course creates strain.

    Consider the Zen/Ch'an/Soen method of hua t'ou: when the essential question of a koan is distilled, the words are released and the questioning attitude or 'fathomless space' remains. I often refer to it as Unknowing. 'Hua t'ou' means 'word-head'. The tail of the word is the form and language; the head of the word is before thinking. This is the same place you come to in self-inquiry, the before-thinking place. It is okay to rest there. You make the most of your practice by resting there as long as possible.

    Turning the attention back toward the self is not dualistic. "It is like an eye trying to see itself, or a blade trying to cut itself." To seek it as if it's something outside of the seeker is a paradox, a mistake. (An eye can't turn around to see itself.) When you turn the attention around, soon you find nothing-to-find — an ungraspable, unknowing space. Because of the very deep habit of attaching to objects, this space is often regarded as wrong, insufficient, incomplete, 'not it'. But actually, that space is precisely where you are to rest. You must make friends with it. Make your comfort with it.

    > The shared human sickness is agitation about emptiness.

    (Zen Patriarch Seng T'san)

    The task is to rest in emptiness, despite one's habitual mistrust or agitation about it. It's when you try to find something there to grasp — when you revert to object-consciousness — that you go wrong and develop tension. There's no 'thing' to be found, and no thought-form insight to be gained. The spaciousness you find when you seek the self is the self. It is the space in which everything happens, the whole universe and all of life.

    If you are doing Dzogchen style, I hope you know that you absolutely must be doing it with the training, permission, and empowerment of a Dzogchen lineage guru. There is no exception. And if that's the case, then you should consult your guru about these kinds of things at every opportunity. This allows you to get correct and complete training so that you can proceed without doubt; it allows the teacher to know your situation and what you need in order to progress; and it deepens your connection to the teacher and lineage.
