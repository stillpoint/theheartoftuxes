    Meditation is like weather passing through space. Your original mind is like open space, and the situation is like changing weather. Experiences come and go â€” sometimes brighter, sometimes darker. The focus is on your spacious nature.

    So actually, you shouldn't expect joy from the outside. Rather, you apply you own capacity for joy, regardless of the weather. Sunny day doesn't make joy, okay? You make joy through your embrace of the sunny day. But also, rainy day can be joy; snowy day can be joy; even earthquake can be joy.

    It is because you no longer identify with the mind that wants this, not that. You identify with your spacious mind.

    So it depends on how well you have practiced it, how well you have trained your capacity to apply your all-embracing, non-attaching mind like space.

    Having said that, if you practice the method consistently and frequently and your life is not becoming happier over the months and years, then you have to take a look at your motivation, the source of your teaching, and the methods and course of training you engage in.

    Motivation means why you do it. If your motivation is selfish, unconditional joy is not possible. Your joy will depend on getting what you want; and if you know life at all, you will know that it is not possible to get and permanently hold what you want.

    >"When a gaining idea arises in our practice, it is a sign that our practice is in trouble." (Shunryu Suzuki Roshi)

    Source of teaching means that you should have one consistent source, with a method that aligns with your proper motivation, and who has themself attained the fruits of the method they teach. The source should be verified as sincere, with deep attainment, not merely someone posing as a teacher; because they are guiding you in a very important personal journey. The teacher should be at least capable of consistently returning to the view beyond ego, if not living there permanently.

    Furthermore, the path and methods should be shown to further your aim (this usually means a traditional path of training, which embraces the complete life â€” all the various causes that influence the mind â€” and a path which has been refined and proven by many practitioners over centuries). You should then learn well from the source, and apply the teaching meticulously; and if there are doubts or problems you should consult with your teacher(s) and get them corrected.

    These requirements form the usual basis for good, fruitful meditation beyond temporary feel-good efforts. There are very rare occasional exceptions, like people who innately have an extraordinarily great innate capacity for meditation, and who can make great progress with little teaching; but for 99.9999% of us, we need these conditions to form a proper basis for step-by-step progress.
