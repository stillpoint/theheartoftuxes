    Extreme wakefulness following mindfulness meditation

    >Has anyone experienced this?

    This question is asked about so many different experiences, and is asked frequently not only in this subreddit but also out in the world. Whatever the experience, the answer is invariably yes, someone out there has experienced the same thing, and likely many people have.

    Sometimes extreme wakefulness, sometimes extreme drowsiness... as well as countless other moods, energies, visions, sensations, etc..... Many things may arise that will be unusual to you. They all amount to different expressions of your particular configuration of mind.

    The first thing that pops out at me when I read your comment is that you meditate with your eyes closed. Although some systems teach this way, I would not recommend it. It greatly increases the likelihood and intensity of strange experiences, dreamlike states, and disconnection from reality. Keeping eyes at least slightly open will allow you to have a bit of a lifeline to the waking world. In my experience, even if a meditator thinks he is clear when meditating with eyes closed, it is almost invariably the case that a dull, dreamy state has descended (along with unconsciousness about the dullness and dreaminess). This is not clarity.

    There may be various reasons for your experience of wakefulness, from a clarifying of previously clouded mental functions or attention, to increased agitation from pressures put on the mind. To assess the cause it would be necessary to know more about you and your relationship to your mind, and especially to know more about what precisely you are doing with your mind during and after meditation.

    Next, the title of your post says you are doing mindfulness meditation. How do you conceive of that? Usually mindfulness and meditation are considered to be two distinct practices.

    ~

    It is quite possible for someone to actualize a brightness and aspiration that make long periods of meditative wakefulness possible. In these cases, one becomes very engaged with meditation and urgently wants to continue it. The meditation stays clear and fairly effortless even through long hours and beyond the usual sleep time.

    If you don't have a very strong drive to stay in constant meditation, or if the drive to meditate doesn't have this natural and effortless quality, then this is not likely what's going on for you. It is more likely that there is some increased agitation — some blocked energies got released, or you touched on some psychological hotspots, or there is some pressure being put on the psyche.

    You need sleep for your health. Some very experienced deep meditators need very little sleep, but in general if you do not sleep you start to die. So I recommend seeing your doctor or visiting a clinic, if only to rule out the possibility of a disorder that has nothing to do with meditation. (Just because it happened in proximity to meditation doesn't indicate a cause and effect relationship.)

    After checking with your health professionals about it (physical and mental health), there may not be much to do but relax. If you have access to a meditation teacher, he/she may be able to guide you. If you have spare waking time, you can spend it in meditation. But perhaps — and consider for yourself whether this seems like good advice — for the next while you should...

    1. Clarify your method of meditation. Check that the method is a good one that can lead to awakening (or whatever good results you are seeking), and check that the way you are applying the method is correct. It is vitally important not to force the mind into any particular state.

    2. Apply the theme of relaxation. Meditation is not something you drum up or force upon yourself; it is a letting-go and settling in your original nature. Relax the body while maintaining a noble and aware posture. Relax the mind while maintaining presence. Relax the attitude, letting all things come and go by themselves.

    In the vocabulary of Qigong and Traditional Chinese Medicine, you may be experiencing an imbalance between Yin and Yang. You may have an excess of Yang — too much bright, active, expansive energy flaring up; and/or you may have a deficiency of Yin — too little cool, calm, containing energy. Having too much Yang in your life can use up the Yin that normally embraces, contains, and balances Yang. A vicious cycle can appear where staying awake too long or being overactive (like exercising too much, overthinking, or using the mental power too much in meditation) depletes the Yin that makes sleep and calmness possible.

    The remedies for such a condition may include participating in more rest and calmness (more "yin" activities), eating cool watery vegetables and fruits and other yin foods (raw lotus root is particularly stabilizing), or receiving professional herbology or acupuncture or massage to balance your energies. Most lifestyle changes and treatments are best guided by a well-trained professional.
