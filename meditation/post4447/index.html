    Yes, you meditate with your mind; so any position is possible.

    But your mind and your body are not two different things. They co-create each other, they influence each other. So body posture and mind posture arise together, and certain postures help or hinder your efforts to keep your mind relaxed and alert. In long-term and advanced training there's also the issue of energy circulation; but maybe that's not critical for you at this stage. When you train with a teacher at high levels the posture may become far more important and in some ways indispensable.

    But if you have the motivation for it, any posture is possible fit practicing clear and present mind. The main thing is how you keep your mind.

    Have you noticed how lying down tends to promote sleepiness and dullness? This is one of the issues you have to contend with. Every posture has its pros and cons. Lying down on your back is a posture traditional used for Å›avasana and death meditations as well as sometimes body scanning and psychotherapeutic exercises, since the posture promotes a deep sense of surrender and acquiescence; but it's not used so much for concentration and alert mindfulness practices.

    Having said that, I know of people with back injuries who have completed entire week-long and month-long retreats lying down; and when I had a back injury I did some sessions lying down. It was difficult for me to stay awake, but at the time it seemed like the only alternative. Certainly in group practice you have many supports that can help you maintain alertness even in less than optimal postures: the setting and structure, the group leader, the other practitioners, the teaching, the physical and auditory signals -- all of these help to regulate your mind. In your own, the task requires that much more devotion and focus.

    What postures are comfortable or beneficial for you? What kind of practice do you do?

    The lion pose as taken by the Buddha at his time of passing is another traditional and recommended lying posture. Here, you lie on the right side with your right arm bent and your right hand under your ear or head, and your left arm extended along your body. This is another traditional posture for death meditation, and is sometimes recommended for mindfulness practice in cases like yours.

    Other options include mindful movement, which can actually help heal your injury in addition to training focus and open-minded acceptance. Yoga, Taiji, conscious therapeutic movement, and free dance are all possible meditative activities.

    There are also many physical sorts out there, including meditation belts and harnesses that go around the lower back and legs, as well as chairs and back supports. How does sitting against a wall or a chair back work for you? Or reclining on a wedge rather than lying flat?

    If your aim is wakefulness you should do what you can to help yourself maintain it. Drink a bit of clear water or weak tea, get fresh air in the room, keep the room temperature a little cool rather than too warm (or practice outside if possible). Keep your eyes open (unless your teacher specifically instructs closed eyes for a particular practice). You can also use sensory supports to help keep you from drifting off, like using a mala and burning high-quality incense.

    Mind is primary. That's the bottom line; and if you can follow through on that basis you don't need to concern yourself with any other issues. But a good meditator is very practical; and most of us are benefited by the teachings about form and setting that help us regulate the mind.
