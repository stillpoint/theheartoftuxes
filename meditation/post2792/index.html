    If the muscles and tendons linking the legs to the pelvis and spine are short, the knees will tend to rise. To counterbalance this, the lower back becomes concave, and higher up the shoulders and head can move forward. So in terms of geometry, the knees should ideally be firmly planted, and lower than the hips. The base should be firm. That may mean raising the seat (and hips) by using a thicker or more firm cushion, or by placing another cushion or folded blanket under the zafu.

    Some centers offer flat square or rectangular support cushions. Flexible meditators sit on those alone (sometimes stacked in two) rather than the thicker zafu; less flexible meditators place the support cushion beneath the zafu. The cushion or folded blanket can be placed completely beneath the zafu for height, or just used as a wedge to raise the back.

    Posture tends to be a complex matter of interrelated bodyparts as well as mental attitudes and energetic realities. (Body and mind influence each other.) Although flexibility of the psoas and iliotibial muscles is often an issue, many other factors may play a role. Often poor core strength and tone is implicated in painful slumped posture, and building core strength can improve posture, comfort, and endurance in sitting.

    If the shoulders and head are not sitting well over the spine and hips, they will require a counterbalance that employs muscular tension rather than skeletal support. This soon leads to fatigue, spasm, and pain. Many people habitually round their shoulders and just their head forward. This is one reason why instructions regarding posture often include the step of drawing the chin back. Unfortunately this has become a formulaic instruction, and people who would otherwise take a comfortable posture pull the head back too far and put tension on the neck. Either way, the weight of the head is moved off of its center of support over the spine, requiring muscular tension to keep everything still.

    The head should float on its orbit at the top of the spine, the shoulders and ribcage should hang with the weight transferred straight down through the spine to the hips, and the arrangement of the legs should allow the hips to find their free balance without tugging on the lower back.

    The sitbones and pelvis should be vertical; that is, weight should be transferred directly downward through them, without an excessive concave or convex curve in the lower back. When the sitbones are vertical, some people have a bit of an inward curve in their lower back; others have a fairly straight lumbar area or very shallow curve. The formulaic instruction to 'create a forward curve in the lower back' is really meant to let the pelvis be vertical so that it can carry the upper body weight without requiring constantly firing muscles to do it.

    As to what you can do:

    • Many yoga classes help tone the core as well as increase functional flexibility of the hips. Alexander Technique, Mitzvah Technique, and Feldenkreis have been found by many meditators to be helpful. Pilates can build an integral body, with strength, alignment, and flexibility all working together.

    • All-around core exercises will almost certainly help with sitting comfort. Don't just build the abs, but also the obliques, the muscles along the lower and upper spine, and the deeper core muscles and pelvic floor.

    • You can Google 'stretches for meditation'. Most offerings are similar. But take care that flexibility is trained in conjunction with strength and tone. If you gain mere flexibility while keeping the same bad posture you can destabilize the joints and do damage.

    • Restorative yoga postures can be good for improving the posture, relaxing and stretching key muscle groups like psoas and pectorals, re-aligning the skeleton, and training the breath. It also gets you used to relaxing and letting go, which is of great benefit in meditation training, as some of the tension that often builds up comes from anxiety around letting go and not having anything to hold onto.

    • One good activity to counteract rounded shoulders is to lie on the floor or on a very firm surface with a long bolster under the torso. You could also use a rolled cotton blanket or rolled bathsheet towel. The roll or bolster should reach from your sacrum to your head. The head can either hang off the end of a shallow roll or be supported by a thicker bolster. The main thing is to bring the shoulder blades together, push the spine and sternum forward, and let the shoulders hang back with gravity. Breathing deeply in this position helps the body re-learn this position of relaxed shoulders back, head not jutting forward, and free, open chest.

    • A good activity to help rest and relax the all-important psoas is to sit on the floor or on a mat with the legs in a z-position, shins resting on a sofa or bed. All right angles: shins on the sofa, vertical thighs, and torso flat on the floor. Consciously relax the muscles in the pelvis and lower back. Breathe deeply in a relaxed manner.     • Check my posting history. I've written several very detailed posts on posture, including alternative arrangements for sitting and using cushions in various ways.

    • Find out what support is possible during the retreat. Is movement/changing position possible? If necessary, use a chair. Some meditators move between mat and chair during retreats; some use a bench; some shift between various postures on the mat; and some just go with one posture.
