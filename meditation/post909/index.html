    I have no need.

    >I realized while I was attempting a quiet mind this morning the root of this all.

    >I call it an assumption of need.

    That's a good point to understand. It saves you from having to chase anything. It saves you from having to chase the countless false hopes that countless other people chase.

    And when you cease chasing, you have a chance to be yourself. It's the assumption of smallness that keeps you small; it's the assumption of lack that keeps you impoverished; its the assumption of separation that keeps you separate. How very fortunate to see this.

    But "I have no need" is not quite correct. Why make "I"? Assuming some particular form of self is also an assumption to let go of.

    Next, if there is no need, you don't have to say anything about it. So "have no need" is redundant.

    You write that you were trying mental harnessing, and attempting a quiet mind. This rings some alarm bells. It suggests a bad relationship with your own mind. Trying to force the mind into submission involves yet another false assumption â€” of two parts of you, one in proper control and another that needs to be controlled.

    It is true that attaching to thinking can keep you from seeing clearly; but doing away with thinking completely will make you stupid. Your true mind is already quiet and clear. Only don't attach to the objects that arise in it.

    You have a very good chance of progressing well in meditation, but you must now let go of this realization, as you were willing to let go of the other frenetic thought activity you've built up. As lofty as it may seem, this is just another thought activity. Don't hold it. Rather trust where it is pointing: let the mind be. In meditation and in life, let the mind be.

    I suggest finding a good, proven meditation method and sticking to it. Finding and following a good teacher is extremely beneficial. Those who try to guide their own practice build their own blind spots into the structure of the practice; as practice grows, the blind spots grow.

    Because of your thinking habit there will be a tendency to judge results in terms of thinking-understanding and realizations. This is not correct. There is no measure for the results of letting-be. It doesn't have a flavor, it doesn't leave a mark, so it easily seems insignificant. Never mind; only practice one good method and the mind that already has everything will emerge by itself.
