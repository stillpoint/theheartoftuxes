    Balancing daily rhythms is most helpful. It's largely a matter of supporting the hormonal balance, evening out the daily hormonal tides, and preventing hormonal exhaustion.

    In terms of meditation, vigorous physical practices in the morning (prostrations, sun salutations, yoga, zhan zhuang, etc.) and calming physical practices in the evening (restorative yoga, shavasana, gentle flowing movement, walking meditation) may be most effective in addressing SAD.

    Breathing practices (proper, guided practices) can help balance hormonal circulation and cycles. But if you emphasize tense or out-of-balance breathing you can put your body and mind out of balance; so get proper training.

    Morning movement practice followed by brief, enjoyable morning sitting meditation is a very positive combination.

    If you want to practice mindfulness or de-stressing during the day, you can set a timer or pleasant phone alarm to ring at regular periods, to serve as a mindfulness bell. Then you can take a few seconds to reset: sink the weight, relax muscles in sequence head to toe, take a few smooth and deep breaths of mindfulness, and re-enter your activity in a gentle manner.

    Here are guidelines that some find helpful (do your own research, and check with your health professional):

    • Wake up before dawn.
    • Expose yourself to bright (blue/sky) light in the morning.
    • Welcome the body to wakefulness and activity with supportive intake, not shock: a glass of hot water, perhaps with a bit of honey or honey and lemon.
    • Do vigorous, whole-body-and-breath movement upon waking.
    • Morning meditation can come right after movement and before eating.
    • Take Vitamin D in the morning or mid-day (it signals the daytime cycle). Recommended dosages vary from 2,000 IU to 6,000 IU. Check with your health professional; but some doctors are not up on the latest info about Vit. D.
    • Get sufficient essential fatty acids (EPA and DHA, as found in fish oil and flax seed oil, for instance.)
    • Eat meals at regular times.
    • Eat balanced meals.
    • Try to eat your last meal before 8pm (to allow internal organ rhythms to harmonize with sleep).
    • Start to turn lights down by 7pm.
    • Start to taper down activity, especially activity with 'thinking machines' and artificial light like computer and phone screens, by 9pm.
    • You can do gentle, calming movement, and/or bathe, and/or do other activities that help you get out of your head and into your body before preparing for sleep.
    • Start your sleep routines at the same time every evening.
    • Go to sleep by 10pm if possible, but 11pm at the latest.
    • If you use melatonin to help regulate sleep, take it only at the correct bedtime, at the same time each night, and don't stay up afterward. Melatonin is usually only recommended for short terms (check with your health professional).
    • Sleep in total darkness. Even the slightest hint of dim light is perceptible through the eyelids, and can disrupt the cycle of melatonin and other hormones. Use blackout curtains or a good sleep mask.

    Some winter morning or daytime practices may visualize the sun or use other ways to inform the body of its natural connection to light and heat.

    • Vigorous exercise is very important for health; but you should be careful not to exhaust yourself with either physical or mental or emotional activity.
    • All kinds of erratic ups and downs are stressful and draining to the hormonal balance.
    • Rest more in winter. Do intentional resting or restorative practices. (Restorative Yoga offers a lot of resources in this regard.)
    • Avoid coffee, nicotine, sugar, and other stimulants.
    • If you're looking for a hot drink substitute for coffee & tea, consider pure cacao powder. It has a much more gentle stimulating effect (no caffeine, but rather theobromine, which supports heart health). It is also extremely high in antioxidants and fiber.
    • Avoid alcohol, cannabis, and other depressants.
    • Some people supplement with B Vitamins and 5HTP (5HTP intake should be spread through the day, usually 3X/day is recommended. Check with your doctor.)
    • In general, look to a more internal, conservative life activity in winter.
    • Stagnation blocks energy; keep moving. Move every joint fully, every day. It is healthy for mood, immunity, and cardiovascular health to at least do enough movement to break a sweat every day.
    • In winter we often miss the opportunity to ground the electrical charge of the body. In summer we are so comforted by getting our feet on the ground and discharging/balancing, but we miss it in winter. Some people do well with grounding mats or patches that connect to grounded pipes in the home, or finding other ways to reconnect with earth.
    • Spend time with joyful, loving people. Spend time with pets. Spend time with natural settings.
