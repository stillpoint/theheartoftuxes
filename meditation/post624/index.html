    Attaching to objects doesn't mean you are present. If you are getting exhausted, it means that there is still separation -- still a 'you' trying to get something or force something to happen. It's a common stage among newcomers to practice.

    This highlights the difficulty of being your own teacher. Although intention is sincere, it's easy to latch on to the wrong thing. Learning from a living teacher would be of benefit.

    If you relax and don't attach, you are already present. That is an important point: presence is not something foreign that you get; it is the innate, natural state. It is revealed rather than made or obtained.

    The only way you can not be present is through thinking. Only thought gives the illusion that you are not present -- that you are one thing, over here, and presence is another thing, over there. So the busy thinking mind is definitely a factor in your practice experience.

    In fact, the language of "being present" or "developing presence" is already problematic. It can tend to influence your thinking and lead it toward that dualistic, separate view. But really, you are presence, and presence is you. You are the moment, and the moment is you. There is nothing to grasp for.

    So although effort is required to overcome the habits of distraction, attachment, and dualistic thinking, it's also necessary to include a relaxed dimension of practice. The relaxation doesn't only aid concentration (which itself is an important benefit); it acknowledges the very basis of the practice: that you and reality are originally the same. You don't need to get something. Everything is already complete. If the weather is hot, hot is complete. If the weather is cold, cold is complete. Rain or sun are complete. Talking or silence are complete. Getting what you want and not getting what you want -- both are complete.

    If a student's 'getting' mind is extremely active, sometimes it's used to advantage. Some meditation styles are active and forced. It's not the pure way, though, and it comes with tusks and problems (and I'm not sure it's what Tolle would recommend). But if you're going to try to 'get' something, rather than trying to get some particular thing within the moment, try to get the moment. That is, be momentary. If you attach to objects, you will always be jumping around from object to object, and it will be agitating.

    This is why many sitting meditations for beginners are really concentration and not meditation: they select one beneficial object (i.e., something that doesn't promote attachment or thinking) and use it to help let go of all other objects.

    And in mindfulness throughout the day, as you are talking about, recommended methods often have a single object as an anchor: breath, or a mantra, or feet-on-the-floor, etc. The anchor is maintained in the background, and everything else is allowed to come and go. It is expected that having an anchor encourages one to relax and be more openhanded about the coming and going of phenomena.

    In the absence of a guiding teacher, I'd suggest you emphasize relaxation. Make it a project. There is nothing to get. The process is just letting go of obstacles; already you have everything. So focus on that faith in your own completion and reality. And maybe learn some relaxation methods, like Śavasana in Yoga or Zhan Zhuang in Qigong. Doing physical practice or just being physically active can also contribute greatly to your relaxation. Restorative Yoga is a great companion practice to meditation.

    And it's great to spend time with advanced practitioners and masters, to witness the depth of their relaxation, which comes from ongoing practice and confirmed faith in their own completion.
