    First of all, I would look into how you are approaching the meditation: What is the motivation? What mental attitude is being applied? What is the method?

    I would also look at the basics of posture and breath. If the posture is tense or lax or misaligned, it can interrupt the flow of breath, blood, and energy, causing dazed feeling. If the breath is forced or suppressed, likewise it will lead to imbalance.

    Next, a great many people have a tendency toward headiness. It is more and more pronounced in our society as we move into more disconnected and 'virtual' life: people live up in their heads, not in their bodies and in the real world.

    And that pattern of habitually going up to the head is applied like a template on every pursuit. When problems arise, people in this pattern automatically go up to the head, to the thinking, for a solution. When shock or sadness or delight appears, they go up to the head to process it. And when it's time to take on a spiritual pursuit, they assume it exists up in the head, and try to digest it through mental activity.

    This is the common pattern of one's energy getting stuck up in the head. Much of traditional training (and it is even more necessary these days) is geared at bringing the energy down, settling it in the body, and helping the person become simple and grounded. So physical practice is extremely important for most meditators, and most humans; but physical practice is largely ignored. Why? Because the societal pattern is up in the head: mental engagement is respected; simplicity and physicality are not. And the ways meditation is being transmitted these days — books, apps, videos, (gasp!) online forums — couches the practice as a head-related one rather than a practice that involves the entire being.

    So that's another possible cause of disorientation and disconnection.

    And further, since we habitually attach to objects, the practice of non-attachment may sort of pull the rug out from under us. As we get closer to our true mind, which is boundless and ungraspable, we may feel that we are losing landmarks, losing things to hang onto. The greater our attaching habit, and the more we have acted out of desire, aggression, or confusion, the more we will feel lost and disoriented by approaching simple open clarity.

    Someone else's post-meditation routine (if they have one) is not necessarily the one for you. So what can you do?

    • You can do walking meditation to bridge the stillness of sitting meditation and the activity of daily life.

    • You can introduce physical practice into your schedule. Get the body moving, and your energy will be more settled. Prostrations, sun salutations, Taiji, Yoga, and movement/dance practice are very good. Even if you just get active with your whole body and breath every day by cycling, running, rowing, or swimming, that can go a long way toward settling you.

    • You can do specific grounding practices. See a Yoga or Taiji teacher.

    • You can make a point of feeling your feet on the ground as you stand up and move out of your meditation session; and then you can return to that mindful groundedness throughout the day.

    Feeling your feet helps to bring your energy and awareness down into the body, and at the same time it provides a sense of presence — the opposite of aimlessness.

    • Thich Nhat Hanh offers a group of walking and breathing practices that key on the phrase and sense of "I am here"; you might want to check them out.

    • After meditation it can be good to disperse the energy from the head:

    • Rub the palms together to generate warmth, and cup the eyes for a short while.

    • Repeat, and cup the ears.

    • Rub the face gently, and massage the head and neck.

    • Massage and with the tips of your fingers tap the hollow spaces at the base of your skull, on either side of your neck muscles. This is called 'Beating The Heavenly Drum' in Chinese energy practices; it helps prevent energy from getting stuck in the head.

    • You may also like to give the hands, legs, and feet a bit of a massage.

    • Then you may want to drink half a glass of cool or hot water, depending on your constitution. (Hot water will be beneficial for most people who aren't 'hot' types.)

    • If the feeling of aimlessness continues, you may help yourself by having prepared your 'next steps' previously — say, the night before. Have a clear idea of what you will do next after you get up from meditation: will you get ready for work; will you eat; will you plan your day; will you offer service to someone? Meditation has a momentum; and if you don't move meaningfully into your day, you can feel an inner depression or disappointment, as if something miscarried.

    • Although it is beneficial to do physical practice before meditation, you may want to do it after instead, or in addition. It can help get you back in the body and back in the flow.

    • Many people find altar practice to be very grounding and delightful. If you want to try, you can create an altar and 'open' it before meditation by offering clear water, candlelight, incense, and perhaps a flower or fruit, to Buddha, God, or your deity of choice. This is the same as offering to your highest nature, your original self; because you and the Source are originally not separate. Then, after meditation you can 'close' the altar by emptying the water and putting out the candlelights. You can also spend a few moments making prayers and recalling vows, which provide your day and your life with direction: you can use the boundlessness and peace that you contacted in meditation, and move it in a positive direction in your life. "I vow to wake up and save all beings from suffering." Or whatever great and positive prayer or vow you choose to participate in.
