    Yes, meditation can help; but in so many of these kinds of cases, other healing activities like therapy focused on the anxiety are more helpful in the short term, or even necessary. Sometimes you need new understanding and coping mechanisms in order to settle down enough to even meditate.

    If you can perform the right meditation the right way, and be consistent at it, it can contribute to healing. However, I'm sorry to say to you and the very many people coming to /r/Meditation looking for a cure for anxiety, PTSD, ADD, and other mental/emotional difficulties, but meditation is not a cure-all. If it is part of the healing picture, meditation should be accompanied by the other activities that help you to understand and turn around the causes for the ailment. That may include therapy (or coaching or counseling) as well as lifestyle changes.

    Concentration meditation helps you to disengage from the patterns of thought that burden you. Concentration itself is the disengagement from distraction such as trains of thought, and the return to mere presence. Usually it is practiced with an object of focus, so the meditator has a particular, obvious thing to return to each time the thought pattern takes him/her away.

    At deep levels of concentration, one can experience the pure and open nature of basic mind, which is the core self. Actually, to call it 'self' at that point is not correct, as 'self' is just another thought pattern (albeit a very ingrained one). But that still, pure, open essence is what you really are. So yes, concentration meditation can give you a taste of that.

    Mindfulness can carry the ability to disengage and return to neutral — which has been trained in concentration meditation — into daily activity. It can help to gradually diffuse the mental habits, since the practice is to apply neutral attention to all activities.

    The question is how the motivation and the mental patterns match up. Sometimes the mental patterns sabotage a person at the point where they begin to practice; so even trying to practice meditation can prove to be a frustrating project. And quite often mistakes are made in the approach or method, due to poor or inconsistent or conflicting teaching, lack of a living example, lack of feedback about issues that arise, and wrong assumptions about what is supposed to happen in meditation. A few people, who somehow naturally understand the aim and approach, are able to start on their own (and then hopefully find a guide at a later juncture); others need guidance from the start, and ongoing live feedback throughout the process.

    But as I wrote above, it's often the case that other things should be happening in addition to, or instead of, meditation. For example, if your strategy is to meditate in isolation until your mind is somehow perfected, and then later to re-enter social engagement, that strategy might be too heavily influenced by the social anxiety itself. It may only deepen the pattern of fearing and retreating. This is one of the many ways one can sabotage oneself, and why living guides for both mental healing and meditation are necessary.

    Doing what you love to do, or learning what you wish to learn, or doing what you're already good at, in the company of others may be most helpful. Sometimes social anxiety is easily overcome with a few gradual entries into social activity. And sometimes the issue is a deeper family wound (for instance) that requires therapy — an outside perspective, an ally, a person with special knowledge and skill, a person who can hold the healer/witness position so you can be free to go through those wounding experiences with compassion and new understanding. In these cases, one is seldom qualified to heal on one's own.
