    Letting go vs pain

    I suggest acquiring a mala. A mala can be used as a support, and can help you keep the thread of mindfulness even in great pain. I know — I've practiced through tremendous migraines that have lasted for days, and have also practiced through flu and fever.

    Practice with the mala is very simple. You can work it in time with the breath: one breath, one bead. The tactile feedback gives you more to go on, more to return to. At times when you are not working with the breath, you can just work the mala bead by bead, staying present or returning with each bead: just now... just now... just now... just now....

    Any time during the day, you can access your mala and have a few moments of simplicity. No need to strategize or try to get anything. Only a simple moment. That is very good practice.

    When you are racked in pain, even if you can't do anything else you can work a mala bead by bead. The bead becomes your object of concentration. You can turn your attention from other things (including the pain) and focus on each new bead. Let your life be completely there: just this bead, just now.

    Later, when you are going about your day's activities, you can work the mala as a way to keep you anchored. In this case you don't focus only on the mala, but you use it in the background (perhaps in your pocket) as you're walking, riding the bus, watching TV, or talking with someone. It rhythmically keeps you more engaged with the present moment.

    Instead of thinking that 'just this is truth' is some kind of destination that you will arrive at if you get good enough (or lucky enough), start from this point. This is the point of your life; and this is the point of your meditation. Just this is truth. Breath is truth. Pain is truth. Thinking is truth. The real situation, as it is, is truth.

    It is because we believe that there must be something else, something special, something that feels better, something that is more fancy, something that pleases us — it is only because we think this that the moment is insufficient. Actually, the moment is always enough. The moment is already truth. So this is where you start it is not some other place or state to get to.

    This attitude is open-handed. It is the correct attitude for entering meditation. As-is.

    Then, if you have pain, that is already your meditation situation. If your mind is agitated, that is already your meditation situation. Your task is to be present, unmoving. Neither to avoid what arises nor to try to hold it. Neither to add your own material to the way things are nor to subtract your awareness. You sit as the witness and let life flow around you, the whole universe swirling and unfolding in its own way.

    If you think "I can't, it's too painful", that is the thinking that you add to the situation. You make "I can't". The situation doesn't make it. Actually, the pain is helping you to be present. It is very, very obvious. It brings you to the present moment. And if it is overwhelming, the pain encourages you to go to the place where the mind doesn't move.

    If your mind moves and you start thinking about yourself, you encounter a lot of suffering. The more you think and struggle, the more you suffer. So pain encourages you to settle more deeply. Instead of saying that the pain makes it impossible for you to concentrate, you can legitimately say that concentration is a way toward ease. As you return to the object of concentration, you disengage with the pain. You give it less power; you give it less attention. The object of concentration becomes like a haven in a storm, an island in a tumultuous ocean, a cool oasis in a burning desert. This is one style of practice.

    When you are driven by pain to deeply unify with your object of concentration, that is called Diamond Pain Samadhi. If you want to try this style, you must not give in to indulgence. Self-defeating thoughts are just thoughts. Your task is to let go of thoughts that appear, and return to the object. No thought, no matter how reasonable or lofty it seems is worthy of disengaging from your method. Only return to the object, without fail. If you keep this clear form of practice you will soon unify with the object and experience moments of bliss.

    That is one style. It brings the mind to one point and lets go of all other points. It is not the only way.
