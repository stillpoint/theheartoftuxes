    Stopping meditation

    >Most people are not qualified to lead their own practice past a very basic level.

    Please read my post in reply to another meditator who has become too embroiled with his/her own experience.

    The problem is not the attention itself, nor the development of more sensitivity and awareness. The problem is that a habit of involving the "I" has developed. With every sensation, you add a little story about the fact that it's happening to you and what it means or might mean for you and what you think about that, and so on. The events themselves are neutral â€” they're just stuff happening, some familiar, some unfamiliar.

    To offer advice I'd have to know what method you are following, and what you're actually doing with your mind as you engage in the method. But much better is for you to have a relationship with a teacher or a clear and experienced student (not just a student who has been practicing for a long time, but one who has engaged with these issues themselves and who has used practice to move beyond them to some degree).

    What you describe sounds like it would be more commonly encountered in a sitting Vipassana meditation than in a mindfulness practice undertaken throughout daily activities. Here again, we would need to know what your practice is, exactly.

    Mindfulness practice should be very simple, and shouldn't involve discursive thinking about the experience or practice. If there is, as you say, paranoia developing, that means there is too much thinking. When you engage with your experience mindfully, thoughts then arise and you follow them rather than returning to the experience.

    Here are a few options (among many other possible aids):

    Bring your mind back

    When doing mindfulness practice (which can be literally all the time, if you choose), take care to stay with the experience rather than the thinking of the experience. When your thinking takes you away, then return: very easily and instantly and without a fuss, return to the experience of the moment. Returning a million times is the work to be done.

    Get grounded in simple reality

    Mindfulness practice leaves a lot of room for your awareness to shift around, so it is perhaps easier for your story-making mind habit to take over than it would be with some other practices. But if you want to continue, you can bring the focus more to everyday objects, simple grounding sensations, and engaged activity.

    Instead of being so internal with your attention (feeling the heartbeat and tiny pains and subtle sensations), keep your attention engaged with your feet on the floor. If you're sitting, feel your feet on the floor and your bum on the seat. If you're lying down, feel your contact with the bed or sofa. This is a very simple and grounding practice that tends to do away with much of the self-involved thinking that you get.

    I'm loath to offer another alternative mindfulness method, as we run the risk of confusing the issue and playing the "which practice is better?" game. As long as it helps you to be present and aware and sane, the best practice is the one you chose and do. Suffice to say that mindfulness can also take a more active direction: you act mindfully â€” sitting, walking, watching, washing... working, smiling... â€” you bring your awareness to your momentary activity, and release the deep-digging listening to sensations that you find disturbing.

    Choose a different method

    Choose a method that doesn't lead to speculation and self-analytical thinking. You will still have this thinking habit to deal with, and you will still need to keep returning to the method to get free of that habit; but some methods will not be so prone to body-identification. Your particular character may be too attached to the body and its processes.

    One cure for attachment to the body, offered by the Buddha, is to contemplate the decay of the body: consider all the parts of the body and how they are interrelated, and then see them all dissipating, disintegrating, dying, rotting, and returning to the world. My sense is that this may be a good occasional practice for you, but it won't do as a daily or moment-to-moment practice.

    You may, then, want to try something like mantra practice, which has a single very clear object of meditation. You keep the mantra and only return to it. Thinking arises: return to the mantra. You notice a sensation: return to the mantra. You get distracted: return to the mantra. Having the mantra as the object makes it very clear when you have gone off in your thinking. The object (the mantra) should be very clear in your awareness; when it starts to fade or when you lose it, let go of everything else and return to it. A clear method and object helps you to learn how to let go of your mind's habit.
