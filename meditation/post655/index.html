    I'm not saying 'should' or 'shouldn't'. I'm saying that there is an effect to ever choice, a result of every cause. Different method has different effect.

    So what do you want? Clarify that point, then find a teacher and study situation that can bring you there. If you want to pursue breath-based meditation, the right teacher will understand the cause-and-effect of breathing, will have gained certainty through her/his own practice and attainment, and will have the wisdom and compassion to lead to to your own attainment.

    In this day and age, we look for the quick answer. It's the age of Google, the age of hyperlinks, 140-character tweets, and 15-second sound bites. But the quick answer is often not the full or even helpful answer. So much depends on the student having the humility and patience to receive the teaching fully — to hear the various angles, to awaken to the various perspectives, to digest the teaching at levels deeper than cursory thought.

    So this is why traditional courses of meditation are recommended. Traditional yoga, for instance, has a lineage of teachers; so that each teacher has been fully trained to the level at which they're teaching, and has been tested and approved by previous masters. This is not the case in some modern iterations of yoga and other meditation modalities.

    Traditional training also tends to be complete, wholistic. For instance, yoga offers training in yama (ethical training), niyama (personal discipline), asana (psycho-physical training through postures), pranayama (comprehensive breath training), pratyahara (freedom from the senses), dharana (concentration), dhyana (meditation), and samadhi (effortless absorption).

    Very complete! Other traditional paths are also like this — wholistic, though perhaps arranged and conveyed differently. The physical, emotional, mental, relational, communal, and spiritual levels of being are all addressed. The lone beginning student can't hope to guide their own practice with this degree of breadth and integration.

    If your aim is to awaken to the nature of self, letting the breath be (refraining from manipulating it) is usually a part of that path. There are some forceful, controlling styles, which may indeed suit some very fiery individuals; but they come with some serious risks. Every cause has an effect. In general, letting the breath be is recommended.

    There are ways to both regulate the breath and let it be. A Hindu yogic path may, for example, teach breath control during pranayama training and natural released breathing during meditation training. Gradually, the two streams merge.

    Taoist yoga may similarly offer qigong breath exercises and then 'forgetting breathing' during other meditations.

    These and other paths have many methods. Chanting is another kind of breath training, blended with concentration (mind) and devotion (heart). Zen may bundle several methods into one overarching practice (though Zen also has many clever practices that aren't apparent at first, including physical practices, energy practices, and — if it's useful to you — methods of self-suggestion).

    So what you 'should' do depends on your aim. Take the time and effort to clarify that point. Don't just choose arbitrarily, but find out what is most important in life; then pursue it, offer yourself to it wholeheartedly. This will guide the kinds of practices you should choose; and you will know better which situations to embrace and which to abandon.
