    Without the mirror of form, your own habits, preferences, and blind spots run the show.

    Of course it feels 'right' to you; because you always get your way. And if you go along with the trend you'll even convince yourself that it constitutes good practice.

    But that's not freedom. You only increase attachment to your own likes and dislikes, and you deepen your inability to show up for unpleasant and neutral situations, and you ingrain your knee-jerk clinging to pleasant ones.

    So what if you don't always get to decide your favorite situation? How is your mindfulness then?

    The litmus test is whether you can commit to regular schedule and timed sittings and be completely content. If not, you have a problem with form and avoidance. And further, can you enter into commitments and difficulties and unpleasantness while maintaining contentment, awareness, stability, compassion? Or does "I like... I want... I need..." control your life?

    Regarding limiting distractions or otherwise optimizing the practice environment: it's done to help the practitioner meditate either at all (since many people need external support to even begin practicing) or to meditate deeply.

    Even experienced meditators cherish that opportunity to plunge deeper, deeper, deeper throughout the duration of a sitting, and reconnect with very subtle and sublime mind states that reflect the most essential being. They can then return to everyday life even more firmly rooted in them, more able to express them spontaneously and embody them beneficially in everyday encounters, more able to shine them to the world.

    A master who has, through long and exceptional work with the form, attained absolute stability can go through life without requiring any special circumstance. Even so, a great many masters remain extremely faithful to form, routine, and ritual -- not because they're not free, but because they are free, and they choose to compassionately offer an example to students, or they are using the form to express their enlightened nature.

    But students and especially many newcomers and many people who have not trained in authentic settings (though they may have dabbled for years) are often disturbed or distracted or fall away from practice altogether if they don't have controlled circumstances. Phone or TV takes their mind away. Conversation or reading takes their mind away. Sleepiness or eating or sex or the demands of work or family take their mind away. Not getting their favourite situation and not being with their favorite person takes their mind away.

    And those who don't think it's happening to them usually have little to no awareness of what constitutes clarity. They've been sitting on a mat, but they haven't been doing real meditation. So a significant portion of 'meditators' are mostly resting and drifting, basically enjoying mini-naps, rather than really practicing.

    For all these reasons, we wisely use form to help us wake up. Because left to our own devices, we inevitably fall back on our habits and indulgences, and may not even know we're doing it.

    If waking up from the dream of life is important (it is), one ought to gather the conditions that will help one do it, even when all habitual thoughts, feelings, and reactions are resisting or chasing distraction.
