    This is a problem of translation. When you say "meaningless" it can lead people to believe that the koans don't actually point to anything; i.e., that they are arbitrary, random, absurd, trivial tricks for the mind. This is very far from the actuality!

    Although a koan may not be soluble with the thinking mind, that is not at all the same as saying there's no point to it. In fact, the original interaction, the teacher's presentation, and the student's completion all are exclusive of Bodhi, of Buddha-mind. Empty, awakened mind is both the method of solution and the essence of the case situation itself.

    参话头 (Can Hua Tou) is literally translated as "participate (in) word-head". The 'tail' of words and thoughts is their manifest form in speech and printed word. The 'head' or origin of words and thoughts is emptiness (or no-mind, or prajña) -- the mind before thinking. This points to the method of practice.

    I am hesitant to set out the method lest people take only my explanation as basis for investigating koan and hua tou. Face-to-face training with a master is necessary; and though the hua tou method is shared by several schools of Ch'an and Zen, approaches to it may vary.

    But briefly, in practice one doesn't struggle with the meanings of the words; instead, one rests in Unknowing, in prajña, until that source of wisdom provides the response.

    I just want to remedy the rampant misconception that koans are meaningless, or that they 'have nothing to do with anything'. This notion is a tremendous mistake and degradation of Dharma. To the contrary, koans point to the center of life -- the most important point -- and how it functions from situation to situation. (For example, "cypress tree in the courtyard" has a very clear and significant, life-changing meaning.) Hardly anything could be more valuable; but those who misunderstand often spread the idea that koans are silly and trivial.

    Thank you for taking care with language.
