    Has anyone had to deal with over-thinking?

    More practice is necessary: mindfulness practice, to establish awareness of when your thinking runs off needlessly and handily; and meditation practice, to establish the good skill and habit of letting go and re-setting, and to connect with your deeper mind.

    Mindfulness provides you with the option of change, because if you're not aware you're doing it there's no possibility of escaping it except by accident. Meditation seats you progressively deeper in your true mind, which is already complete and satisfied, and doesn't need to fret about things.

    So you just need more practice. Cause and effect are clear.

    Beyond that, there are specific practice recommendations for overthinking. In broad terms, we find practitioners tend to be centered either in the head, in the heart, or in the body; and different types of practice will dovetail with those predilections. Analytical meditations, visualization, and self-enquiry make use of the mental activity, for example.

    To counteract unbalanced mental activity, getting physical is the standard and very effective remedy. “Get out of the head and into the body." Besides being a balancing influence, physical practice reveals a very important aspect of practice — of realization — that is lost on many who learn about meditation through reading. Integrity of the whole being — physical, emotional, mental, relational, energetic — is essential; it's not just about thinking-understanding.

    Prostrations and physical labor have been considered crucial parts of training in many spiritual paths and schools, for centuries; and for good reason! In this day and age where our interactions with thinking machines may cause us to live up in our heads almost exclusively, the need for physical practice is even more important. It's far from being a throwback to a former age; it's even more needed now.

    Bowing practice, rhythmic whole-body-and-breath exercises like running, rowing, or swimming, yogic sun salutations, and free dance are all great ways to help the energy settle out of a busy head and into a satisfied body.

    Mantra and chanting practice are also indicated for over-thinkers. Mantra can be used as both meditation and mindfulness practice. It uses a mental phrase to occupy the thought and provide a clear object to return to. When the thinking gets busy, the practitioner can speed up the mantra and even use the voice — loudly if necessary -- to maintain connection with the stabilizing practice. In the right settings, chanting can provide similar benefits (and also has a soothing or healing effect on the heart).

    Short, quick mantras are usually prescribed for overthinking, but you can also try a dharani — a very long mantra. It will require a certain amount of concentration to perform, but may benefit by more fully occupying the mind. The drawback is that a long mantra or dharani is more difficult to use throughout the day at work and in interactions with others.

    Finally, you may look into Traditional Chinese Medicine or Ayurvedic Medicine. They both recognize overthinking as an imbalance that can be addressed through diet, lifestyle change, and herbal treatments (and acupuncture, in the case of TCM). Overthinking tends to be associated with weakness of central energy and the Spleen system of digestive energy (this does not refer to the spleen organ in western anatomy). Indigestion and malabsorption of food can relate to indigestion and malabsorption of thought. Over-worry tends to be associated with the Lung system; and jittery instability may involve other systems like the Heart or Liver. An experienced practitioner should be able to identify quite quickly your type of constitution and cure.
