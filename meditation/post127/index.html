    One of the most common misconceptions about meditation is that it's about being distant or shutting down parts of oneself.

    Non-attachment doesn't mean detachment or standing aloof and unfeeling. It means you are not controlled by the usual things (wanting, avoiding, frustration, dullness).

    Laziness is a symptom of self-involvement. It's the stagnancy of the mind that keeps obsessing about itself. This is a very common pattern in depression, for instance. Those who try to counteract it by forcing themselves into a show of activity create a pattern of overexertion and exhaustion, and seldom find actual balance or sincere expression in life. But when action comes from your true center (rather than obsessive agenda) it can radiate outward, always appropriate to the situation. You find your true center with correct non-grasping, non-making meditation. It is like the way a scale finds its neutral balance when weight is removed from it.

    All of your worries and the worries that other people have over this issue come from thinking. You imagine some future state and either crave it or fear it. It's all unreality, all made up in your head. So the first step for you is to let go of this unreal speculation and return to the real moment: feel your feet on the ground, see what's actually in front of you, take a breath, listen openly.

    When you are back in this real moment, you can influence your life. This is the only place where you can have an effect on your life. If you want to live passionately, the present moment is the only time when you can do it. Trying to do it in the future is just fantasy. Trying to do it in the past is foolish, useless pining.

    The only point where you are actually alive and interfacing with reality is the present moment. That is why you meditate: so you can readily return to the place where you can make a difference. If you want to be free to choose how to live, you need to be in the present moment.

    And one more thing is required for this freedom: you have to be able to be unmoved by habitual thoughts. Like all of us, you have a set of patterned thoughts, created and conditioned by the way you were raised, the emotional wounds you incurred, the cultural conditioning and education you've received, and so on. Your computer has a specific program that causes you to — for example — fear loss of passion. This program may help you avoid dispassion at times, but it may also make dispassion unavailable to you when you need it, it may make you doubt your actual passion and drum up fake passion, it may make you awkward with natural emotion, it may make you less able to relate to stable and balanced people, and so on.

    In order to be free and alive you need to get out from under the control of your conditioning, which appears in the form of thought. A truly passionate and alive person trusts his movement in the moment. He doesn't live according to a script. You can only do this by (A) being present and (B) not being controlled by the thoughts that appear in your head. Then you can use all your faculties, including feeling and thinking, rather than be used by them.

    In case it's helpful to know, I've been around thousands and thousands of meditators and also many teachers and masters for most of my life; and they are far and away much more alive and unique and spontaneous than the common person. Others may put on fancy fake shows and quirks, but those who keep returning to reality through practice are better able to express the totality of who they really are. The most energetic, passionate people I've known have been masters. But their passion is not self-centered. It radiates outward, brightly and confidently, illuminating everyone.

    Your concern is not without merit. If you misunderstand practice you can indeed force your mind into unnatural formations, suppressing parts of yourself. That's why it is very important to understand your correct aim in meditation, learn the correct method that will address that aim, learn it from a single reliable source, learn it completely, and follow it properly. You would do well to learn from an experienced and certified meditation teacher, or even better an enlightened master; and you would do well to join a meditation community or group, follow a schedule, and learn in a complete way over the coming years. It could be the most significant thing you ever do, for yourself and for the world.
