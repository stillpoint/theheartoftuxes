    You use a controlled situation to practice non-controlling mind. Without the controlled situation you and most people wouldn't train well in pure non-grasping awareness. (There are some rare individuals like, say, Krishnamurti, with great capacity and great auspicious circumstances — some might say karma, based on their past-life activities — who naturally perform moment-to-moment non-grasping awareness without applying an external discipline. The rest of us use a good environment and course of training to guide our mind until the habits of wrong perception and distraction have been purified.)

    What's good for the soul is hated by the ego. Your illusory, control-seeking idea of self always feels hemmed in by good practice. But true freedom doesn't mean you get whatever you want; it means you can be with whatever is. This is what we practice; this kind of freedom: "this is okay, and that is okay, and I will show up for all of it."

    You need proper teaching. Piecing practice together from various comments online is not the way. Find one proper source of teaching — a living teacher who can receive you and respond to you, human to human.

    >How do you 'Gently' return...?

    When you take a step, you let go of the place where you were a moment before. Like that. You already know how to gently return; you just have other, judging, thoughts that say it shouldn't look or feel a certain way, or that once you let go of a train of though it shouldn't come up again. These too are thoughts to be released.

    When you drop a towel on the floor, it falls without any other effort required. You don't have to make it fall to the floor; you only open your hands in the moment. So your meditation practice is very, very momentary. Completely momentary. You are not to check to see how it's going over a span of time.

    Never mind what happened a moment ago, or ten minutes ago, or last session. Never mind whether similar or different thoughts or experiences will arise in the future. Never mind what happens to others. Never mind what you hope and expect. If your practice is mindfulness of breath, only return to the breath, just now.

    Being gentle about it is very helpful. If you try to fight, you build up inner tension and create more stories in your head. Let go of this momentary distraction the same way you let go of someone's hand naturally after you shake it. It's nothing. You just move on to the next thing, which in the case of mindfulness of breathing means returning to the sensation of breathing (there are many different possible methods; but the letting-go and returning are the key points, regardless of your chosen object of meditation).

    If another thought arises, great! No need to follow it.

    If you happen to get distracted and follow the thought, great! This is another chance to practice gentle, effortless, instant letting-go, and to return to the breath. Every time you practice returning, your capacity for returning to and staying with reality increases. Every time you let go gently, effortlessly, your mind becomes that much more free — because you establish the fact that this thought does not have the power to control your mind.

    Another thought arises.... Well, there isn't even such a thing as 'another' thought. If you have 'another' thought, that implies a comparison between a previous experience and the current experience. Meditation is completely momentary. So only deal with this momentary situation. Establish your correct relationship to the moment, with is aware and open-handed. When taken away by the natural habitual process of your mind, as soon as you are aware of being taken away, gently and instantly let go and return.

    The only difference between a beginner's letting go and returning and a master's is that the master keeps that mind consistently: continuously letting go. The letting-go and the returning have merged in a constant stream, inseparable from life-as-it-is. The master is life-as-it-is; the master's mind is the moment, as it is, with nothing added or held back.
