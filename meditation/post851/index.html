    You don't necessarily know while it's there, because activity has ceased. After, when you come out of it, you may perceive that it has happened.

    It's not important for you to know. Also, depending on your purpose and the nature of your method, it may not be important to achieve silent mind. Often the important things are awareness and equanimity (even-minded non-attachment to all states) rather than a particular state of silence. Some paths, though, do stress silence; and there is some benefit to it.

    But if you're grasping at a state, and if you're agitating your thinking because you're constantly checking what state you're in, none of that is meditation.

    Crying, energetic sensations, chills, bubbling, dream images, exapansive feeling, and many, many, many other manifestations arise for meditators. None of that is the point of meditation. On rare occasion an experience may serve as a sort of waypoint, marking what stage you're at; but once that is recognized there is no value in holding on to the experience or spending mental effort analyzing it. When doing concentration-style practice, which most silence-aimed paths adopt, strange experiences will tend to be more frequent. It is both because of the stripping of mental layers and release of inner psychic/psychological material, and because of the pressure one puts on the mind to keep returning to the object of concentration. The more tense and grasping your relationship with meditation, the more strange experiences will occur. So many people fool themselves in this way, thinking the experiences indicate something special! In fact, it's just a result of how they are using their mind.

    Coincidentally, many people with addictive personality are drawn to silence-based meditation. It can generate blissful, trippy feelings that the addicted mind seeks. Ultimately, all addiction seeks spirit — pure presence — and silence-based meditation can often be a positive substitute and indeed a cure for objects of addiction.

    But by the same token, the addicted mind can make an addiction out of meditation. One can get attached to silence or to 'special' states, not realizing that the true value lies beyond, in what is revealed by the silence. In pure silence, one rests in one's original nature which is boundless and pure. Coming out of the state and regaining the common dualistic perspective, one can have glimpses of that boundless and pure nature, and realize what it means to the supposed self. This is insight. It is unfortunate, but common, when insight into true nature is pushed aside and attachment to special states becomes the main agenda.

    For an example of meditative perspective that doesn't depend on silence, consider the teachings of Dzogchen master Chogyal Namkhai Norbu, who speaks of the awareness that is like taking a trip to the seaside: When you sit by the ocean, you can enjoy any scene. Whether the ocean is perfectly still and glass-like, or whether the ocean is wavy or even tossed by a storm, you can witness and enjoy every state. When waves are crashing, the depth of the ocean remains still. When the waves are still, there is yet life within the ocean. So one doesn't have to attach to any particular state; one can practice all-embracing awareness.

    There are different ways, different methods. To know which path and method is for you, you need to clarify your own life direction. What is your life for? Why wake up every day and put effort into something? These are issues that are to be clarified now and over the course of your life.
