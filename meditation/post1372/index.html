    Using meditation in college

    Regular and frequent moments of letting go will be helpful. If you set a (pleasant) timer to go off regularly, it can serve as a reminder to disengage from what you're doing and take a few moments to re-set. Relax the body, let go of the mind, let go deeply with the attitude. It's like returning to zero, re-setting a machine, re-booting a computer to clear its function, or letting go of a steering wheel so the car can find its own straight line. A few moments of this, performed repeatedly through the day, can help in itself, and it can also help form the very good habit of remaining in a neutral and balanced place.

    Your ability to focus and learn efficiently will be greatly improved through physical activity and open breathing. The improved oxygenation and blood flow will obviously help; but furthermore, engagement of the body and the neural pathways has been shown to improve learning. For example, students with ADD have been shown to improve greatly in their attention and acquisition when sitting on those big exercise balls instead of chairs — because the balls require constant subtle movements to readjust balance. Some prominent business executives swear by their standing desks (no chair) for similar reasons: they feel more engaged, more active.

    All kinds of physical activities may be helpful, but the best of all is free dance, because it (A) aligns all of the aspects of self in activity — the body, mind, the emotions, the imagination, and the attention all work together — and (B) it allows all of these aspects to express and regulate themselves rather than having an external structure applied (as in yoga, sports, or ballroom dancing, for example).

    Insight into the body-mind connection has reached spectacular levels in many South and East Asian traditions. Taoist practices, for example, use movements of the eyes in accessing various faculties, a connection that has turned up more recently in the West in the controversial findings of Neuro Linguistic Programming and in behavioral psychology training given to police and military interrogation specialists. The Facial Action Coding System and the concept of microexpressions hinge on the connection. The significant part for you, though, is that active physical articulation can improve mental articulation; so, in a nutshell, getting all of your joints and muscles moving will help you learn. (And it will help you feel better too.)

    I've worked with students at exam time who have had to digest and/or memorize large amounts of data in a short period of time. We did it with movement, acting out concepts physically, assigning movements to various parts of a formula and "performing" the choreography, and so on. The more pathways one can engage, the more anchored the learning will be, so we use voice, physical movement, visual cues, auditory feedback, and visualization all in the same session — with great results.

    If you want to add a dedicated meditation session, I might suggest something very calming, and something that doesn't require much mental output. If you learn to breathe from the belly, a few minutes of soothing, slightly extended breaths could help balance the heady work you'll be doing the rest of the day. Taoist, Buddhist, and Yogic (Hindu) traditions all offer instructions on belly breathing. You may find guidance for "longevity breathing", "qigong breathing", or "alternate nostril breathing". Find a simple, soothing practice and try it. If you try this, it is important that the breathing be continuous and easy, never held or forced.

    In summary:

    • regular and frequent moments of letting go
    • physical activity outside of study time and also during study
    • free dance (the best exercise for maintenance and improvement of mental function)
    • simple, soothing breathing practice

    Edit: It should go without saying, but for many of us it still needs to be said: correct diet and sleep are invaluable benefits. Until you experience the difference for yourself, it can be difficult to conceive of how much impact the right food and the right sleep schedule can have on every part of your life.
