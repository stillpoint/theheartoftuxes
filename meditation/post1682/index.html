    Raging and need serious calming power.

    You might be interested in this article by the Zen Master Thich Nhat Hanh, offering both method and explanation on dealing with anger through mindfulness.

    It is fortunate that you recognize you are triggered, and that the emotion is not helping you -- you need to take care of things on a practical level. That is fortunate to recognize, so you can do something about it and not just get lost in the feeling.

    For any meditative response, getting the big-picture context is very helpful. See the issue not only as your personal burden or wound, but as part of the shared suffering situation we all have. Even the people who co-created this situation with you have their own blind spots and shortcomings, their own suffering just as you have yours.

    One teacher suggests taking the view 'from the moon'. Imagine your standing on the moon, looking back at earth. You may have a general knowledge of the countless cycles of birth and death taking place, and the broad swath of history, but from the moon it all blends in and seems less significant. I'm not saying that you should stay in this view -- you need to take care of practical things -- but it is helpful to acknowledge that your small circle of personal hurt and rage is not the only perspective, not the only reality.

    It can also help to recognize the cause and effect that leads to difficult situations. People don't mess up and hurt each other naturally; we do it because of our ignorance. If people are blind to certain effects of their actions, or if they are motivated by greed, pettiness, spite, and other negative motivations is because they are ignorant -- they haven't learned what is truly worthwhile.

    Ignorance is ignorance of our true self. People who hurt you with bad action are mistaken about their true self, and driven by attachments based on this mistake. Your sense of wounding and anger are also completely based on your misunderstanding of self, and your self-based attachments. If you understood the empty nature of this thing you think of as 'yourself', anger could take no hold.

    So a certain degree of understanding and compassion can be offered to everyone involved in the situation: if they knew better, they would have done better. This all-inclusive compassion cuts through anger.

    Part of this calming knowledge and compassion is the recognition of your own karma; that is, the ways you've used your mind and action in the past, which have led to your current situation. Facing the results of your own past actions with even-minded compassion amounts to a purification: if you don't keep regenerating the negative causes you can finish with these particular results; they are on their way out of your life. Just don't cling to the situation, and your karma will start to become more clean.

    People often experience this: with a loss comes a gain; with a crisis comes an opportunity; when one door closes another opens. If you keep your even-minded relationship to the moment, you can make use of this time of purification, even if it feels not so nice.

    Sometimes anger is so visceral that you can hardly bring yourself to even start thinking rationally. Breath is traditionally (and therapeutically) a major vehicle for returning to an even-minded state from which you can start to deal with the issues (rather than merely react to them). In the linked article, Thich Nhat Hanh uses breath in combination with mindfulness.

    There are many other breathing methods that deal with the initial raw reactivity of anger, or of other disruptive emotions like panic. One method is square breathing, in which you inhale, pause, exhale, and pause in equal measures; say four counts for each. As you calm down you can take longer breaths and pauses; if you are very agitated you can take shorter ones, but still maintaining the 'square' format.
