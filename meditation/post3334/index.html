    Both situations are real.

    I don't know if its included in the other posts I linked to, but I can share some of my experience with sleepiness in practice. For quite a long time, maybe two years, I would have very drifty, sleepy sittings most of the time. I tried overcoming it by force of will, and tried various other tactics. I would ask each new teacher I encountered how to get over this 'problem'. I mean, it wasn't just once in a while, it was ongoing for a long time!

    I did consider that it was an avoidance response of my psyche, in reaction to the possibility of breakthrough. I had previously been aware that sleepiness would sometimes arise when I was faced with difficult or exposing situations like, say, 'relationship talks'. It led me to consider that the sleepiness could be a certain layer of my being putting up resistance: on one hand my intention was quite sincere, but on the other hand I had a psychological (or we can say karmic) pattern of avoidance. But when and how would it end?

    I also considered health and lifestyle issues, but looking back I'm not sure how well I took care of them. I can't remember that part of the story very well.

    In any case, I was abroad for a few months doing one-on-one apprenticeship with a master, but I would travel to and from my temporary residence at a Zen Center in a nearby city. At one of the group sittings at this center I had the opportunity for interview (what would be called dokusan in the Japanese tradition) with their guiding Zen Master. If you're not familiar, often in interviews the teacher will ask if you have any questions before testing you with a koan. I spoke up about the problem with sleepiness and sleep. Without hesitation, and in all warmth and interest, the Zen Master leaned forward and asked "Oh... What is that like...?"

    That question hit my mind. I looked for the 'problem' and couldn't find it. There was only looking. Open asking-looking.

    After that, sleepiness was not as much of an issue. Of course I encounter it, as anyone who does residential or retreat practice does; but it's not a pattern and not a problem any more.

    This points us to the place in practice where truth is just truth. Sleepiness is not a problem; it is just sleepiness.

    It is not even that! It is not the word, not our label or story for it. It is not a 'pattern'; it is not a 'thing'. There is no such thing as sleepiness. Sleepiness is just a word we give to a fluid situation. The actuality of that situation is what we attend to. We sit with openhanded awareness — interested without reaching out, detached without retreating — and let things be what they are.

    We sit as things being what they are. We sit as the scene. Not as a separate entity trying to do something. We sit as the scene. It is all-inclusive.

    But from what you say, it seems that energy management is an issue for you: sleep, balanced work, letting go of worry and wasteful thought and activity, exercise and rest, balance in eating, and diurnal harmony. Lifestyle changes may help you be wakeful later in the day.

    Especially avoid a big evening meal before sitting. When you eat, your circulation of blood and energy are directed toward digestion, and that can cause sleepiness.

    Both the mental activity of the morning and the dragginess of the evening are very good practice situations for you. It is a benefit to keep returning to presence within both situations!

    I hope that helps.
