    At this stage, I'm not sure there's a 'should'. What center should you be attending? Don't know. What would you like to try?

    Often we focus needlessly on trying to get a perfect situation. No teacher or center or relationship or possession is going to make you perfect or keep you from discomfort. There is no magic formula. There are only various opportunities to do the work you need to do.

    Do you like Chinese food or Indian food or Mexican food? Which restaurant should you go to? The only 'should' is that you should get food into your belly. The style is not that important; so you can try a style and see if it's okay.

    Perhaps more significant than the style is the teacher. Even here, you don't need to seek a perfect situation. You can learn a lot — and in certain phases of your career, maybe more — from an imperfect but sincere teacher, a teacher who is 'good enough'. Then you can also learn how to support your teacher and not just be lazy and let them be all wonderful and do all the work.

    But having said that, sometimes the difference between options comes down to how deep the teacher goes. How deep do you want to go? Sometimes we think we're ready to go deep, but when we contact an uncompromising teaching situation we freak out. That is also fine. If we freak out, we learn something about ourselves and where we're at just now; then we can make new choices based on that information.

    I suppose I'd like to invite you to invite yourself. What do you want to invite yourself into? What invitation do you want to extend? What invitation do you wish someone would extend to you?

    You can invite yourself to show up. You can try both centers; you can try one; you can try something else. If you keep your feet moving you'll soon find out "Oh, actually I'm ready to move on" or "I want to challenge myself to stay" or "Yes, my feet are landing right here, I feel it right here."

    Style doesn't matter so much. Your showing up matters. It is your life. Moment to moment, I hope you show up for this life.

    ~

    The Shambhala center, I can't speak to very much. I like Shambhala and their concept of non-sectarian practice. I like the development of Dharma warriorship. It suits some people. Although I've visited a Shambhala center and had some tangential relationship with students, it never clicked for me. On the website you link it's hard to find mention of a teacher, and it's not clear who is leading the community. If it's important to you to co-create a community, this may be a good place for you.

    As to Red Cedar Zen Community, I've only briefly met the guiding teacher, Zoketsu Norman Fischer. He's a friend of a couple of my masters, and for that reason and because I've read some of his writings I would tend to trust him; but you need to do your own research and see how your teachers carry themselves — whether they walk the talk. Then you can commit yourself accordingly, step by step.

    I always feel that our interest informs us of where life is unfolding in us. Instead of figuring it out on a spreadsheet (which, to tell the truth, is one option) you might look into your interest. What does your organism respond to? Maybe you'll find out that you want to study with Gil Fronsdal in California, or maybe some other place or activity is calling you for a yet unknown reason. Rather than looking for a place to stop, keep moving.
