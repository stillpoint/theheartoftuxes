    It requires good training.

    Concentration (the Wisdom aspect) means that the object of meditation remains clear and bright -- it is vivid in your attention. So if you're counting breaths, the number you're at is very clear in the moment.

    Awareness (the Compassion aspect) means that you don't lose the scene you're in. So in the same scenario, you don't lose count because you're too fixated on the present number.

    These are difficult for the beginner to perceive and understand. Practice is practice -- effort over time. Things get digested in the right order. Obscurations like tightly-held views need to soften and dissipate over time. Knowledge has to come to you bit by bit, and often has to be revisited time and again before it sinks in, and often becomes something completely different as you clarify it through your own effort.

    It's not something you just grab and go, like a candy bar at the subway vendor.

    It's not a bad thing to have the lingering question: "Am I awake?" Just let it illuminate your practice rather than become a checking, an analysis, or an interference. Let it pop into your head if it happens to. Let the question be there, and your experience within it. And let it go and move on.

    For instance, if just now you try to find "What is 'awake'?" without going into discursive thought, the question becomes very wide and open. The moment appears in it, as-is. You can't grasp anything, but what's there is clear. You see what's in front of you. You smell what contacts your nose. You hear sounds just as they appear.

    (This reflects the Buddha's teaching in the Bahiya Sutta, in case you're interested: "In the seen, there is only the seen. In the heard, only the heard.")
