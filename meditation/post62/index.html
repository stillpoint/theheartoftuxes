    Starting zen mind, beginners mind - differences from mindfulness?

    Zen means you and the real, momentary situation come together. The word "Zen" derives from the Chinese Ch'an and before that from the Sanskrit Dhyana, meaning deep meditation — one-pointed concentration that unites with the object of concentration. When the mind is concentrated and still, there is no separation between you and the real-life situation you're in. So things just are what they are.

    This is very, very simple; and it seems to go without saying; but it is also much easier said than done. Zen doesn't talk about life, Zen is life, as it unfolds.

    So Zen Master Joshu gave a now-famous response to the question "What is Zen?" "The cypress tree in the courtyard", he said. The response in your case could just as well be "the computer in front of you." Or "sitting here, reading this post on Reddit." It's real life, before you say a single word or think a single thought about it.

    Mindfulness is a practice that also brings us closer to simple, real life. The direction of the practice is the same, but it is — at first, at least — a more formal and object-related practice. In mindfulness practice, you pay attention to whatever you are engaging with. If there's something happening in your thoughts, you bring awareness to it; if you're doing something with your hands, you bring awareness to that. Action by action, you apply your attention. At first, this means that there is still a "you" and an object to which you apply your attention. As the practice deepens, your mind settles down and you can have experiences of simplicity or unity with what you're doing.

    It is a gross generalization to say this, but in broad terms: mindfulness practice can have more of a structure that moves from object to object, whereas Zen practice tends to be more penetrating. In Zen, often either intense concentration or a big, all-inclusive question will be used to bring you close to reality. The practices have the same direction, but different flavors.

    The aim and intention behind the practice is all-important. Is the practice "for me", in order to get some peace or insight or abilities for oneself? Is the practice "for all others", in order to manifest awakening wisdom and compassion in the world? Is the practice "only this", in utter equanimity and surrender? These are significant differences, and your aim and intention make all the difference not only in your practice but in every part of your life. Methods are just tools, and can be used however you choose; they can give life or take life.

    In any case, it is not necessary to keep track of these things. It is not necessary to figure out the similarities and differences between practices. The main thing is to find a practice of presence — a practice that brings you close to real life — and to do it. Not read about it or discuss it or think amazing thoughts about it — do it.

    The vitality, the real life, is in the doing.
