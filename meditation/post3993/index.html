    Most meditative traditions acknowledge the connection between mind, body, and environment; i.e., the fact that they condition each other and arise together. How that awareness is worked with differs between traditions.

    Some traditions focus on mental practice; others focus on energetic practice (and the mental aspect follows or is influenced by the energetic); others yet focus on physical practice (service in the world or exercises like kung fu) or devotional practice (making offerings, chanting or praying to a deity, visualizing a guru, etc.).

    The chakra system, in some variation, is part of Indian, Tibetan, and Chinese methods of energetic cultivation, circulation, and transformation.

    When you transform your mind, your energetic and physical postures also change. Someone who has fallen in love, for instance, has a different posture and quality to them than a person who is grumpy and miserable. Vice versa, some systems do it the other way around, working with energy (say, love) to bring about changes in thought, insight, world-view, and relationship. The energy practices have developed to a very sophisticated level, and are considered by some to be a kind of science.

    When mind is clear, energy flows clearly. This is eminently observable in life: when your mind is unattached to the things that usually toss it about, you can relate clearly to your work, to your momentary activities, and to the people in your life.

    Similarly, when energy flows well, the mind and heart and body are in harmony. There is not one side without the other. And even the meditative traditions that focus on one aspect do, almost universally, recognize the holistic nature: all of these issues — physical, mental, emotional, energetic, spiritual — arise together, and ultimately they are all the same thing.

    The chakras are just one way of communicating about and becoming aware of various aspects of oneself. The aspects we are aware of, we can harmonize with and transform. The aspects we are not aware of control our lives from the shadows. For instance, if you're not aware of a general mistrust of people (or if you don't want to admit it), you may constantly find yourself in unfulfilling relationships, always blaming others. Once you become aware of the pattern, though, you can begin to change it. That's one type of "chakra issue", an issue related to a particular energy center; but it's up to you whether you want to engage in the issue that way or some other way (say, psychotherapy, shamanism, some other meditation, art therapy, dream work, and so on).

    Chakras are just like a template, a way of organizing understanding around what's happening in one's body and one's life. I'd venture that most people who practice with chakras don't actually know what they are or whether they exist at all, there's some confirmation bias going on — but perhaps a useful one. We are all prone to concretizing subtle teachings into dumbed-down versions.

    If you see a whirlpool in a river or lake, the whirlpool is undeniably there, but it has no reality separate from the water that is moving. There's no separate whirlpool that you can remove from the water and put under a microscope. A chakra may be like this: it has no reality of its own, but we identify this pattern of movement (of energy, thought, emotion, relationship) so that we can begin to talk about the effect that this "whirlpool" has.

    Look at the night sky, and if you've learned to identify the Big Dipper it's very difficult not to see the Dipper up there. But there is no real Big Dipper, there are just stars. The Big Dipper is something we have added with our imagination. We add our own template so that we can relate to the configuration of things and make use of them. It may be similar with chakras. No big deal, just a potentially useful way of understanding life.
