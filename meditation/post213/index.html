    If you practice it, it gets better. Using the app develops ability while you are using the app. If you want to get better at applying it in everyday life, you have to practice applying the non-distraction in everyday life.

    That's where apps, for all their other benefits, fail miserably: they can't guide you in everyday life, and they can't respond to your ongoing questions, doubts, and needs as you navigate life's path. So if you want to progress, at some point a living teaching situation is necessary.

    Distraction and anxiety are now, I think, the dominant characteristics of our society. We are disconnected from place, history, tribe, family; we are disconnected from each other; we are disconnected within ourselves. Our bodies give us certain messages, our minds fly about here and there making all kinds of other ideas, and our emotions put something entirely different on top of it all. To a large extent, integrity — where body, speech, and mind all align with the true living situation — is absent and difficult to attain.

    There are many causes for this distraction and anxiety. First; most of us have grown up in distracted, anxious, non-integral communities and families; and we've received many psychological and emotional wounds from it.

    Second; our environments are practically designed to distract us: we spend increasing time living in virtual worlds, flitting here and there in cyberspace, jumping at this or that text or tweet. Our minds are so noisy that we clamor for something to take them away; and so we go to gaming or TV or movies or always-the-next-activity to have something that will temporarily take our minds away so we can rest. Furthermore, our environments are often over-full and unnatural, full of agitating or distracting elements rather than open space and calming influences.

    Third; we don't use our relationships well. We are shadows hanging around with shadows, trying to fill up our inner voids with each other. Rather than gathering consciously to nurture and develop integrity, we habitually engage in contracts of mutual indulgence and unawareness. We don't call each other to high standards of behavior; we gossip and talk about trivia, we rant and complain, we veg out together or go drinking or get high. We get wrapped up in dramas like romantic love, professional competition, sibling rivalry, and so on.

    And, very significantly, we belittle and ignore ethical issues. This is very influential. Our minds can not possibly be at rest if we are doing bad action, harming others. No amount of meditation can compensate for unethical behavior (lying, stealing, harming through sex, harming through violence, etc.).

    So these all constitute causes for our disconnection, anxiety, and distraction.

    Recognizing this, we need to regulate our environments, activities, and relationships to help us. We need to start limiting the causes for distraction and anxiety, and generating the causes for mindfulness and peace.

    But the habitual, indulgent mind (the ego) doesn't like this at all! It thrives on disconnection. The ego's agenda is disconnection!

    So we have to realize that there is a very strong motive in us to remain in our suffering, to cling to the distracting, anxiety-producing, disconnecting environments, relationships, and behaviors that (in our more aware moments) we know are harming us.

    If we are able to impose healthy changes — sane, supportive structures and commitments — by ourselves, then we should do it. We should start immediately.

    And if we see that we are not capable of maintaining those structures on our own, we need to commit to external situations that will do it for us. This means finding and committing to a good teacher, a teaching situation, a practice group, and a practice schedule. It also means making healthy personal commitments and being accountable for them — commitments to certain standards of behavior in relationship, commitments to certain healthy activities — and our decisions need to have some 'teeth'; there has to be some kind of oversight and accountability to prevent us from sliding back into habitual indulgence.

    If you want to overcome distraction in your life, you need to cut off the causes for it; and you need to develop the causes for mindfulness and well-being.
