    I'm want to do 2 hours meditation in the evenings. Better to split it up, or do it all together.

    That's great that you want to do more practice. Good luck!

    First I want to note that it is difficult to maintain an overview when designing your own practice and following your own situation. It is difficult to be both engaged in presence and interaction with what is going on within you and immediately around you, and mindful of the big picture. You are not always conscious of your own awakening unfolding, and you are not always conscious of the strengths or weaknesses of your own practice.

    Sorry for that preamble, but it lays the groundwork for what I would suggest. Without knowing the details of your character, intent, situation, and depth of practice, I/we can only generalize. Even if we had all of that information, we would all vary in our ability to interpret it and guide from a perspective of awakening rather than from ego-based views and opinions. But by way of generalizing:

    I believe that adding some walking or movement between sittings would be most beneficial to your overall development. When the aspiration builds to a certain level, and the interest in (or attachment to) stillness and introspection is strong, there can be a tendency for meditation to crowd out other balancing aspects of Dharma, including activity and relationship in the world, physical cultivation, ethical discipline, etc. The inclusion of walking meditation maintains a bridge between meditative and active/social aspects of practice. It also "turns the earth" within you.

    If you study reports of enlightenment experiences, you find that the experience often arises when there has been some relaxation or movement outside of meditation. Often enough, it happens in meditation, but perhaps just as often or more often it happens when the practitioner has gotten up from the cushion, while working, or swimming, or upon hearing an unexpected sound, and so on. Venerable Ananda got it in his movement between lying down and standing up.

    Meditation is very important, but movement is also very important. Stagnation can be very insidious. Meditative walking, as recommended and practiced for centuries in many traditions, would be an appropriate activity between long sittings. It includes movement and (somewhat) relationship, but is not so disturbing or distracting as to undo the effects of the previous sitting.

    Beyond the meditation period(s), both meditative and relaxed, joyful styles of walking have benefits. One can develop an internal pressure, unbeknownst to oneself, when doing longer sittings. One can even become attached to that internal pressure, as it fills the void that we have so much discomfort with. An easy, enjoyable walk around the neighbourhood or in the park can sometimes provide the mental space that further effort in meditation can't.
