    Physical and mental discomfort when dropping a thought and returning to concentration

    It could be an interesting experiment. I'd like to hear what comes up.

    But this is not Vipassana. Vipassana and other proper meditation does not attach to thinking. So thinking, and thinking about thinking, are both dropped in order to return to reality. That returning to reality is so vitally important, and so tenuous in most of us, that compromise is not made on that point. When thinking is dropped, it is dropped.

    Your feeling of discomfort at abrupt dropping is actually another kind of thinking. This, too, is to be dropped. When attachment grows and becomes an ongoing problem for us, causing difficult feelings and dysfunctional activities, it is because we ourselves have been giving it a foothold. We are the agents of our own demise in this way; it may seem like these feelings and patterns are imposed on us from the outside, but we ourselves have prepared the ground and sown the seeds.

    You associate kindness with listening to your thoughts; you don't associate kindness with letting go and being silent. That is your own story, your own ongoing attachment. Of course, we are comfortable with our thoughts and uncomfortable with emptiness. That is our shared human situation, brought about by the ego, the sense of self. The self-image wants to stay alive, so it needs to be constantly propping itself up with thoughts, concepts, opinions, stories. But Vipassana requires you to move beyond the false structure of self, and see what is real before the structure appears â€” the real world, before thought drapes its overlay upon it.

    It is the struggle to maintain a solid, secure, fixed, controllable structure that is at the root of most nausea experiences in meditation. Of course, nausea could be caused by some physiological or mental condition; so checking with a doctor should be your first step. But far more commonly in meditation, nausea arises as a rejection of the meditative process itself, a process where you let go of the structures you've been clinging to.

    At a very basic, gut level, nausea is a rejection. When the false structure of self begins to be dismantled, and one starts to perceive the gaps where empty space and boundlessness are revealed, it can be terrifying. The ego fears its imminent death.

    I suppose it's not too different from being on a roller coaster with nothing to grab on to: in meditation, you start to let go, and you approach the place where nothing can be grabbed.

    The most difficult part is the approach. Once you are in a concentrated state, there is no problem. When you are just approaching emptiness/boundlessness, you still grasp your old structure and you still think of yourself as a separate little being that is faced with the looming infinity. But you are already, and have always been, that infinity! When you let go of the false separate self and its structure, you allow your true clear and boundless nature to come forth.

    You are not a separate self beset by an overpowering universe. You are the universe, and more.

    You mention the case where nausea arose yesterday: you were ready to leave and others weren't. Here again, your mind was seeking control, but your circumstances required surrender. If you had been able to let go of your own preference and expectation, there would have been no problem â€” you could have waited comfortably, or perhaps communicated clearly, made a decision, and gone off on your own.

    Similarly, if you can surrender your thinking there will be no problem. Nothing will be harmed if you disengage from trains of thought. Nothing vital will be missed. The only thing that will be missed is the ego's chance for more attention.

    In your experiment about listening to your thoughts more, you may add a part and also consider what is so darned important about these particular thoughts. Who or what is going to die if you don't indulge this thought for another second? The inevitable answer is that there is nothing of paramount importance about these thoughts.

    It comes down to trusting yourself. Can you trust what you are before ideas and stories are added? Meditation is the practice of that trust: opening the hands, putting it all down, letting reality be.
