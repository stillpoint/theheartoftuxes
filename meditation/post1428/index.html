    Meditating on motivational phrases/quotes..?

    Sure, you can use phrases to catalyze aspects of your character. This is something distinct from mantra work and other meditation. Keep that distinction clear, and you can use each according to its intended purpose. If you mix up the two, the functions will get messy and you run the risk of losing clarity about your own correct direction.

    You may be interested in techniques like Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP) and affirmation work. These may be helpful techniques, but are not meditation.

    The tricky part of what you're considering is how it strengthens the illusion of yourself as a separate agent, and deepens the enticement of power to steer events according to a personal agenda. You may gain a certain kind of productivity, but will you also be ingraining the ego — the self-image — of someone who is or should be productive?

    This raises some questions about what lies behind the motivation:

    • Do you have value if you are not "productive"?

    • What is the true direction of your life, whether you are "productive" or not?

    • What is the correct aim of productivity — what is the doing for?

    Don't think that I'm arguing against productivity. Production (along with destruction and letting go/letting be) is an inescapable part of life's great process. These are just important questions to clarify. After all, why pour time, effort, and life into a project that goes contrary to, or distracts from, your true purpose?

    Many, many people are so caught up in their mundane struggle to make and get, that they miss the big picture of what the whole thing is for. Life is fleeting. We should clarify our time here.

    If your direction is clear, then all kinds of techniques can be employed to help you on your way. If your direction is not clear, then attachment to techniques becomes a waste of your life. What is the central point?

    Many meditation masters are known to distill a point of practice into a phrase that they repeat over and over, through the course of their teaching career. Studying with them, one may encounter the phrase or idea many hundreds or thousands of times. This helps the student to internalize the teaching. The phrase or concept integrates with the student's own effort and experience over time. The student begins to recognize the inner wisdom that has been speaking this teaching already. Then at some point, the teaching no longer belongs to the teacher, but is one's own active wisdom mind.

    This process is centered on the wisdom mind. The teacher points it out to the student; and the student grows her/his connection to it. But when a person chooses a phrase for oneself, this critical foundation may be missing. The phrase — even if it is similar in wording — may be coming from an idea up in the head, or an image the person is trying to live up to, or a deep attachment (to be or look like a "good person", for instance). The foundation is then not wisdom, but attachment; and the fruits of the activity are then all rooted in that attachment.

    This is an important point to clarify when working with affirmations or self-programming.
