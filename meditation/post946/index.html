    That's right, both aspects work hand-in-glove with each other.

    The concentration necessary for liberating meditations to be effective is simple freedom from distraction — upacara samadhi or access concentration, also called khanika samadhi or momentary concentration. Further development of concentration is not necessary but can certainly lead to a depth of experience beneficial for the meditator and those he serves.

    The danger is that attachment to the jhanas — states of profound concentrative absorption — can develop at almost any stage in the game, from newcomers chasing after pleasurable or peaceful feelings to long-time monks believing that feelings of expansion or boundlessness are anything less than empty. I'm not saying that this is the case for the monk you met; I'm saying that this is a clear risk, an old story repeated time and again — a meditator losing his way because the lure of experiences gained through concentration practices is not sufficiently tempered by insight into the essential emptiness of experiences in general.

    Concentration and insight should develop together, and the mere ability to maintain engagement with a method is enough for liberation. .

    Moreover, training the direction (Right View and Right Intent in the Noble Eightfold Path) should precede training the concentration, just as knowing why to use a knife or gun or any power-augmenting tool should precede its actual use.

    Ethical cultivation and open-handed development are not sexy, especially not in this society, in this day and age. The prospect of gaining power, special abilities, or overwhelming insights is a tempting draw even for sincere and long-time practitioners. But balance and the right order of things are necessary for us as individuals, and for our world.

    Because of the very prevalent risk of attachment in concentrative (samatha/tranquility) meditations, a popular tradition arose in Vipassana of training only the pure insight practices, without training the concentrative absorptions. Time will tell whether this proves to be a safer approach given our addictive society, or whether it will mean the loss of a valuable part of the meditative culture.

    Feeling one's being expand to fill the universe is not unusual; anyone who practices meditation regularly can have glimpses or even a full-on experience of oneness. It is what we start with, after all — the truth that is closest to us. Concentration is not special. But it becomes a dangerous diversion if we consider it to be special.
