    How you should use your mind derives from the aim: what you are trying to accomplish. Reducing anxiety can be accomplished in a relative sense through relaxation; i.e., you can feel somewhat better, more of the time, by practicing relaxation with or without music.

    On the other hand, overcoming anxiety, uprooting it, requires an altogether different method and different level of commitment. At deeper levels of patterned thoughts and primal habits, one has to recognize that anxiety is ultimately the defense of an unreal self. One constantly carries the sense of being a separate entity that somehow doesn't fit in with the rest of life.

    All problems arise because we don't understand what we really are. All anxiety has at its root anxiety about one's true boundless, ungraspable nature. So to do away with anxiety, one has to wake up to one's boundlessness (some call it emptiness) and become completely intimate with it.

    In this (second) situation, relaxation is still an imporant part of the practice; because without relaxation, the movement toward reality, toward one's original nature can't take place — tension always keeps you separate. But relaxation is not enough. Insight is key. The two — relaxation and insight — support each other.

    So there are two different aims: temporarily getting more comfortable and reducing some stress; or finishing the whole issue. Two different aims, two different courses of meditation practice.

    The next variations may arise from your own situation. What are your natural proclivities, what style works better for you? Also, if you can associate with a good teacher or clear and wise companions, that could be more significant than (some) differences between methods.

    For stress reduction, there's nothing wrong with listening to and enjoying the sounds.

    • When the sense of relaxation appears fairly obviously, you can start to connect with it and deepen it. Use your consciousness and will to go further in that direction. That way, relaxation doesn't have to depend on music, or on any particular situation. By connecting with it and deepening it yourself, you gradually learn to oversee your own inner situation — you become more aware of your tension and grasping, and develop the ability and habit of letting it go. This is a valuable life skill.

    If you want to focus on the breath, that can also be a helpful meditation object, which can be carried into deeper and more formal practices. What you want to guard against, though, is trying to control your mind or experience by controlling the breath. That is bad practice, and will make you sick and unbalanced.

    My suggestion would be to only practice with the breath in a very gentle, simple way, for just a short duration at a time. When you are ready for a more aware and disciplined engagement, that will be a somewhat safer situation for breath practice.

    Safest is to practice regularly with a teacher, who can respond to your particular situation as it changes, and who can guide you past misunderstandings and past your own blind spots.
