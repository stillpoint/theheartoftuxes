    Eye twitching

    Yes, that's one of the many common possible occurrences in meditation. You can't even imagine how many different things can come up. But even if no one else experiences it, that's okay; you're experiencing it, and it's not a sign that something's wrong.

    Eye twitching can have various causes. In Traditional Chinese Medicine is a common sign of what they call 'wind' (specifically, liver wind). Sometimes tension, stress, excess activity or lack of restful and calming influences can cause the liver's soothing activity to diminish, and a wind-like energy appears in the body, which has a sort of agitating, jittery quality. Sometimes it manifests as spasms or tics, sometimes as headaches, or local inflammation.

    If you're not getting good sleep this can happen. A hot and dry diet can also contribute to it. And also, if there is emotional or physical stress. So for instance, if your meditation is more pent-up than calming, spasms may appear.

    There can be other physical or psychosomatic causes, but usually other signs would appear. If it doesn't happen at other times is likely only connected to meditation, but if you're concerned you can mention it to your health professional.

    Another very common dynamic is that a sort of feedback loop develops between an otherwise innocuous occurrence and the meditator's attention: the more one thinks about it, the more energy hours there, and the more troublesome it seems... which makes one think about it all the more.

    Along with visual, auditory, thought-based, and other hallucinations, physical tics can be the ego's way of taking over the process. The ego doesn't want you to surrender, because then it loses control over you; so it sets up all kinds of distracting strategies. Even if the original tic is caused by lack of sleep, it becomes all-important and dramatic through the action of the ego that tries to get you to abandon the meditation. So just return, without drama. No big deal.

    Other possible causes might have to do with the vision and how you use your attention in relation to the eyes. It's also possible that in the openhandedness of meditation, previously suppressed material is being released; and sometimes that manifests as physical movements.

    The remedy for a lot of this is to settle. As you become more grounded, the 'wind' and other forces find their natural pathways. With practice, meditation shifts from something effortful to something deeply calming. The attention becomes rooted in the method rather than checking the outside situation, so thousands of issues drop away. When the concentration becomes completely one-pointed and effortless, the blissful aspect of every experience emerges. Pains and frustrations completely dissipate for a time, and pure silence permeates your being.

    The instruction in most cases -- as for the vast majority of issues in meditation -- is to return very clearly and completely to the method. Don't let distraction get the better of you, but keep applying the attention to the matter at hand -- the method. No fuss, no battle with oneself; only drop it and return, once, twice, ten thousand times.
