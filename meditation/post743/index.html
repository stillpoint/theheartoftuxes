    Using pain to help meditate

    Pain has been used as a vehicle to concentration, and even further to trance, since prehistory. Whether it is uncalled for our weird depends on the context -- the culture and tradition of practice. Many practices that necessarily involve pain happen within a very developed tradition, under strict guidance and very clear parameters of conduct and attitude; in short, there is a very clear and strong container for the practice. The Native American sun dance is one such practice. The tradition and ritual are very strong and well defined.

    There has been much pain-based practice, though, coming from essentially neurotic, perverted mindsets. The self-flagellation of medieval monks comes to mind as an example. When it comes to using pain as as method, the psychology of self-harm looms large.

    Whole systems and religions have been based on misguided self-denial or self-harm. Even so, the capacity of a sincere heart to turn absurdity into meaning can't be discounted, and many people make significant achievements despite the mistake.

    Regarding the use of pain in a solitary, self-guided context: As already mentioned in this thread, there is the problem of seeking help from the wrong place. If you rely on externals to control your mind, that is not really control but rather dependence.

    Next, how will you determine that this is not a neurotic or addictive behavior; i.e., that subconscious dynamics aren't actually driving the behavior, in a pattern similar to cutting or bulimia?

    Many practices involve pain that is not sought out but just appears because of the conditions of practice. If you sit still for a long time (or for some, a short time), you will almost surely get some pain in the shoulders, back, or legs. Practitioners may also have pain arising because it has been precipitated out of their particular constitution -- their karma; for instance, headaches or other sensations arising due to the pressure of practice.

    One thing is clear: although pain is inherent in life, it is not necessary to pursue pain in order to practice. The correct application of your own effort is the deciding factor in your progress.

    People sometimes say that they learn from mistakes; and that can be true, but it doesn't mean that we need mistakes in order to learn. Similarly, we don't need pain in order to concentrate.
