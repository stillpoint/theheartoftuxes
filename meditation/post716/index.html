Many things that may seem strange to newcomers are found, through actual experience, to be sensible, valid, helpful. There are other instructions or forms that may make sense within a particular practice and cultural setting, but may not be necessary for particular mediators. And there are yet other forms that become part of a tradition without being questioned much; they are rarely harmful, but may be included in things that can be discarded without diluting the practice.

    It is important to know the difference between these. You wouldn't want to reject something vitally important just because it is foreign to you, or cling to something nonessential because it seems special. Beginners are generally not equipped to know the difference -- they have not yet become familiar with how the mind functions in meditation, what constitutes correct practice, and what kind of effort is required to bring certain results. So connecting with a good teacher is important, invaluable. Online forums will bring a mish-mosh of opinions, sincere guidance that gets mixed up between various practices and traditions, unclear aims, and various levels of attainment that are sometimes obscured by bravado or a nice way with words.

    My tips for newcomers to meditation:

    • Get clear on your intent. Why meditate? What do you want to achieve? Ultimately this means getting clear on you life purpose. Meditation is not only for our own self-centered aims; it is to enable us help ourselves and all others. When your direction is clear, then you will better understand many of the forms and instructions you encounter.

    • Choose a form and stick to it. Constantly changing form to suit your whims and sense of comfort only strengthens your whims and comfort-seeking. Sticking with a form faithfully gives you a clear mirror in which you can see your own mind and its habits. Be meticulous about following the meditation method, and it will be much easier to be aware of the times when you want from it. Loosely-goosey practice does you no favors, as it becomes difficult to know when you've wandered off (and why).

    • Find a good teacher. How do you know a teacher is â€œgood"? If your direction is clear -- if you understand the true purpose of meditation and the true purpose of your life, then you will be much more clear on whose teaching and action aligns with that direction. There are other points you can consider in the meantime: Is the teacher responsive or dogmatic? Does the teacher have a sense of humor? Is the teacher happy? Can the teacher explain the practicality of various instructions?

    • Practice with others regularly. This will help keep you from living in a bubble and developing bad habits (which grow in isolation). When you practice with others, you have to put down your own selfish ways to some extent, so this is very good training in equanimity and free action -- much better than practicing alone, where you always make your own favorite situation and thus strengthen your self-centeredness.

    Mantra practice can be a very good practice. Chanting also. And bowing, sitting, walking, working. There are many possible forms of meditation, but what matters is your direction. How do you use your mind in any of these forms? Why? All of these forms can also grow your ego and make you a bitter and evil person if you use your mind in a controlling manner. So being clear on your direction is essential.

    Mantra, like many other practices, is just a way to help the thinking settle so you can be present. Mantra helps train the verbal aspect, while other practices may train the body's action or the intellect, for example. The main point is being present. If you don't like mantra, you don't have to do it. But choose a form and use it to help you return to the present moment, which is the only real place.

    Good luck!
