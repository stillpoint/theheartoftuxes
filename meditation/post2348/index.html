    title: Mantra Meditation: focus on the mantra or focus on the breathing? summary: 2.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2014-06-12 15:08:09 some_url:

    There's a reason for the method; so follow the method as instructed.

    If you have not received a clear instruction, that may be the first step for you: get clear on the purpose of the meditation and the method that will achieve that purpose, then follow the method closely.

    All three versions you mention are possibilities, depending on your situation — what you are trying to achieve, what form and tradition you are practicing with, and also your bent of nature. The particular mantra will, of course, make a difference as to how well it fits in with breathing.

    It sounds like you are reading different instructions for various methods. That's not good. Each method has its own place within a view and system of training; each method has its own roots, as it were — connected to many other aspects of training. So it will easily be confusing and counter-productive to go to varied sources for instruction. At best, you will get a lowest common denominator of teaching.

    I'd need to know your aim and the particular mantra you're using to provide further guidance.

    It may not be necessary to think of the breath and the mantra as separate at all. It's not that you focus on two things at once, but that you don't make the (wrong) assumption that there are two separate things. For instance, when you listen to the sound of a garden, are you listening to each particular clap of a single leaf or are you listening to it all — the wind-in-the-trees sound along with water, birds, car sounds, and so on? When you bring attention to your feet, does the mind divide between five toes, the ball, the arch, the heel... or further, does it divide to individual cells? The moment is one. Everything is here, and individuation only happens because we make it happen.

    If you are particularly interested in joining breath and mantra, and if you have good reason for it, I can share a method.

    In other cases, focus on the mantra eventually opens up on its own to include everything (the breath becomes the mantra, walking becomes the mantra, sad or joyful feeling becomes the mantra, the person facing you becomes the mantra, the earth and sky become the mantra). Mantra can also be used devotionally or in conjunction with other practices like prostrations or deity practice. And there are also methods of using mantra that turn the mind inward to its source, so one pointed focus on the phrase itself would just be the first phase.

    The bottom line is that there is no one blanket statement here that will serve all possible situations and methods.
