    Meditating on a break up

    Your original mind already feels better. Your original mind is complete and content and balanced and aware. It is bright like pure light and clear like space. It reflects like the most perfect mirror.

    The bad feeling part is attachment generated through thinking. The thinking is the problem. Originally, the situation is not good or bad. But you make ideas about relationship and you make wanting, so you get the result.

    Actually, this bad feeling is a very good result. It teaches you the problem with attachment, and it shows you exactly where to let go. Letting go doesn't necessarily mean the feeling or thinking disappear immediately; and it doesn't necessarily mean feeling or thinking don't arise again in the next second or in the next week. It means you let go. Don't be insincere about it: you know how to let go. You just don't want to. The drama and pain are more important to you, more comfortable for you, than being balanced and at peace. This is your choice. If you want peace, you can have it immediately; but you choose not to let go, so you have what you choose.

    I think your meditation method is not so clear. It is still vague in its form and in your understanding. So if you want to practice truly letting go in meditation, you should find one good form and learn it well, then practice it meticulously. I recommend starting with a neutral object of concentration rather than going straight to encountering feelings and thoughts. If you don't already have stability of mind, going back into your thinking-feeling is only going to take you away. So first perhaps practice breath counting meditation. It is a clear method: count your breaths to ten, then start again at one. It has a clear object: the counting. It has physical support: the sensation of breathing. It can have vocal and auditory support too, if you want to use your voice to count. The auditory support is there through the sound of the breath as well.

    Since it is a clear form, you have something very definite to return to. Once you notice that you've gone away in your thinking, you can know for sure that you've gone away. It is not vague. If you're not with the counting, you've gone away. For a beginner, this is different than a practice of noticing thoughts and feelings; because when you start attaching to a thought or feeling, you may not be clear on whether you're doing the meditation or attaching.

    So whether you do the breath counting or some other concentration-style practice, it will help you to have one definite object of concentration to return to. Then you can practice your letting-go. Just for a moment, let go of all clinging and hoping and wanting and figuring; and give your mind a chance to return to its original completion.

    As you return to your original completion, your experience of it will deepen, and you will recognize that nothing outside can fix you. No possession or relationship will make you complete or bring you happiness. You have to find the completion and happiness that are innately yours, from before you were born. That is done by letting go of everything and letting everything rest as it is.
