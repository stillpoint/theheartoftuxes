    I'm curious as to whether or not this is an actual posture... (xpost from r/Buddhism)

    The general posture is usually referred to in Buddhist iconography as the "one leg pendant" (one leg swinging down) posture. It is not a standing posture; I believe the animation intends to show the figure seated on a lotus throne, as are most iconic Buddhas and Bodhisattvas.

    Various Bodhisattvas and deities are depicted with one or even both legs extended; and there is also variation in how far the leg is extended. These postures have subtle meanings, telling us about the specific quality of mind or heart being expressed. From the pure, cool equanimity of the lotus pose to the leisurely, warm engagement of the leg fully extended, there is quite a range of slight differences.

    The one leg pendant pose is sometimes taken as a variation on either the royal ease pose, where the non-extended leg is stood up to the side, with an arm resting on its knee, or the sattva pose, which is a lotus or other cross-legged pose while seated on a throne.

    Iconographic postures are not necessarily meditation postures, as they are meant to convey a particular aspect of awakened consciousness. Some aspects are actively engaged, like Maitreya's two feet on the floor while seated, as he radiates love to all beings. Some are quite responsive or ready to act, like the leg pendant pose. Some rest in an organic physical ease. And others yet are adamantine in their purity and stillness.

    The one leg pendant posture is not usually shown with the hands in that prayer mudra (anjali or namaskara mudra, called gassho in Japanese and hapchang in Korean). A more active mudra is usually paired with the leg posture, usually "turning the wheel of dharma", the mudra indicating active teaching. Images of Manjusri may also have a sword being wielded beside or above the seated figure.

    Every posture has a meaning. Rather, I should say, every posture is a meaning. If you are perceptive you can find out the meaning yourself by taking the posture. For example, if you bring your shoulders up to your ears and clench your jaw and fists, the meaning of tense aggression is very clear. More subtle changes, like differences in extending a leg while seated, may take a more refined perception.

    While you can certainly take the posture to understand the quality of awakening being presented in a statue or image, it is not necessarily conducive to meditation. When practicing meditation you should follow the prescribed method. It has been conveyed in that form for good reason, within the context of a whole body of teaching and training and specific practices. You can't take things out of their context and expect them to have the same depth for you. It would be like picking a sprout out of the ground and then wondering why it's not growing any more.
