    Lack of sleep and excess sleep are both problems, both unhealthy.

    Lack of sleep depletes the body. Excess sleep is much worse, as it depletes the spirit.

    Your criterion for 'better' is health. Although health is a positive aim and a very important support for other aims in life, it's not the highest aim, nor even the central aim for many meditators.

    But supposing we do key on health. In that case, balance is most important. Only getting more, more, more of anything can't help you. Too much exercise, too much work, too much food, too many supplements, too much rest, too much sleep — all 'too much' is unhealthy.

    Advanced meditators achieve the rest of sleep without the unconsciousness of sleep. They remain awake and aware, not depending on outer circumstances. Rather than falling more deeply into the attachments that enslave humans (sleeping, eating, sex, money, and fame), they gain their liberation from these desires and become free in their activity.

    > When you sleep, you trip out, have visualisations and gain new understandings.

    To suggest that dream-filled sleep is equal to understanding is just wishful thinking. Equating tripping out with growing in awareness is ridiculous. Often people dependent on drugs have this kind of wishful thinking; they can't escape their dependency, so they justify it to themselves. All kinds of dependencies are enabled with this kind of spin-doctoring, this kind of justification.

    I lived with a Zen nun who would sleep only three hours a night, and she is one of the most lively, vibrant, bright, and intellectually sharp people I've ever met. It's certainly not because she indulges in sleep! It's because she is able to maintain her effortless center, whether awake or sleeping.

    I've practiced with a monk who does chanting practice throughout the night, and never lies down to sleep. This is not just 'special' practice; it's what he does every day, all his life. Again, he is the most healthy and vital and bright person in his community. It comes from the power of meditative practice, not indulgence in sleep.

    I have done all-night practice, experiencing first-hand both the depth of habit and attachment we have with sleep, and the prospect of freedom from them — the possibility of not always succumbing to dullness and laziness; the possibility of being a noble human, not controlled by habit.

    I have practiced sleep meditation, and know many practitioners who continue one-pointed awareness throughout so-called 'sleep'. Although they are lying down, they are in a sense still awake. Rather than being pulled here and there by desires and attachments they keep their center throughout sleeping and waking life.

    Balanced sleep is necessary. For the common person, with many attachments and stresses, 7 or 8 hours of sleep may be ideal, although the entire modern lifestyle is not actually in good balance. For those who use their bodies intensely, perhaps an hour or so more is necessary for recuperation; but there is accompanying impairment of spirit — of presence and clarity.

    For those whose minds don't go around and around so much, and who aren't controlled by outer circumstances, less sleep is necessary, and less sleep is healthy.

    In the Traditional Chinese Medical system, which is based on harmony with natural rhythms (seasonal cycles, cycles of aging, daily cycles, tides, and so on), it is recognized that going to sleep before 11pm and waking before dawn is healthiest.

    For spiritual practice, midnight and 3am are particularly powerful times. Advanced practitioners often do midnight practice, either staying up or sleeping earlier and waking at midnight. And a great many temples have wake-up at 3am. Most urban Zen centers have 5am wake-up at the latest. It is widely considered healthy to wake before the first light of day. Receiving the blue light of dawn informs and balances the hormonal cycles (as does avoiding artificial light in the evening and preparing for sleep with the falling darkness), and in more esoteric paths like Qigong and Taoist Yoga the light of dawn is considered a kind of nourishment.
