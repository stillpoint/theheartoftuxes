    About timing

    In addition to phone timers, hardware timers like kitchen timers and alarm watches, and computer timer apps, you can also use incense to time a session. The standard short, thin stick burns for about 30 minutes; longer sticks go for 40 minutes to an hour. It depends on the type and thickness of incense, but once you've timed one stick you know approximately how long you can expect from the others.

    If you sit with others you can use a plain watch or clock; just periodically rotate who's in charge of timing the sessions. It is good to just focus on the method of meditation; but there's also something to be said for being aware of the outer situation as well, so keeping mindful of the time while meditating can also be a beneficial practice once in a while.

    When I started meditating I used a watch, and would just check once or twice during the sitting when it would occur to me it was about time to stop. Sometimes I'd be pretty close with my feel of it, other times I'd be maybe 10 minutes off. But I eventually developed a very keen sense of time that had nothing to do with shifting my attention and making concerted guesses about elapsed time. It got so keen that I would just feel when the session was at an end, like an inner alarm clock going off, and would be correct within a few seconds without fail.

    Now, you say that in meditation you are supposed to have your eyes closed and not focus on anything. That sounds more like sleep. While some systems will teach closed eyes, in my experience it is not a good idea. Without the grounding influence of waking reality the insidious dreamlike activity of your mind can easily take over. Most people who like meditating with closed eyes aren't even aware of how dull and dreamy their mental state is; they just like the feel of it. Retreating from the world is not correct meditation. Correct meditation doesn't go toward or away from anything. Concentration, though, may be trained as focusing on one object and withdrawing from all other objects.

    Not focusing on anything in particular is a pure and advanced meditation, so difficult for most beginners that they are not likely to achieve a present and open state unless an unusual effort is applied over months or years. Beginners are thus much more likely to confuse dull half-sleep states for open objectless meditation. They think they have not grasped any object during the sitting, but really they are just unaware that they have been continuously engaging with vague and subtle objects.

    When you say that the instruction is to not focus on anything at all (rather than not focusing on anything in particular), that starts to sound like imposed dullness — becoming unaware of things. This is sometimes recommended in the context of retreating from a world of illusion and finding an ultimate reality. This is not necessarily bad in itself, but in practice it tends to build a mistaken view: it runs the risk of setting up a duality by making the idea of something that is illusion (which we usually experience) versus something that is real (which is not an object). The mistake is that this duality itself is an illusion. The right way is not to impose your own ideas and your own distinctions upon life, but to allow things to be what they are. Then reality and unreality are both clear.

    If you really want to try not focusing on anything, then that is wonderful. Just don't conceive of it as an escape from anything. It doesn't mean that things disappear; it just means that you are perfectly still and centered among things, and that nothing has the power to grab your attention and make it move to a particular place. You are simply extremely awake without going toward any object.

    As I said above, most beginners find this too difficult. So an object-based concentration style meditation is often recommended — usually counting the breath or following the breath, although many other possibilities exist.
