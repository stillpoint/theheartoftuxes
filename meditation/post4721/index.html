    How do you guys handle a tired meditation session?

    > If this doesn't work you are genuinely tired.

    Very good, helpful post, except for this part. We can't necessarily say that if a bit of effort doesn't change the pattern, that means it's about physical ('genuine') tiredness. We don't know the karmic causes involved. Patterns of resistance may be piled up deeply. If that's the case, more ongoing effort is required.

    One may need to harness external factors to one's benefit, changing lifestyle, adapting environment (clean space, fresh air, the right amount of stimulation — neither dead silent nor clamorous), connecting closely with a teacher, practicing with a group (where both the schedule and the group energy carry one through), and so on. Maybe cold showers twice a day....

    As in my post in this thread, other lifestyle factors may apply, beyond mere exhaustion.

    And substituting a nap for a sitting may or may not be appropriate, depending on one's intent in meditation. If the intent is to transcend deep habits, caving in to tiredness will not help. In contrast to one redditor's strategy of napping and then sitting, if one's motivation goes beyond temporary mood alteration I would suggest the other way around: don't skip practice ever, but take a nap or improve your sleep schedule after.

    There is the exception in cases of extreme exhaustion, where not only is the slightest meditative effort impossible but one's health may depend on getting sleep now. Those cases are rare, and point to problems elsewhere in one's life. But even this exception is relative. For example, monks and advanced practitioners may commit to periods of practice with little or no sleep. Because of their motivation and their meditative focus it is possible. For someone with very clear motivation, it is even possible to be brighter and more energized through meditation than through sleep. I have met and lived with monks and nuns for whom this is the case, and I've experienced it myself at certain periods in my life. I know one monk, for example, who practices through the night — every night without fail — and who never sleeps lying down. It is beyond the capacity of the common person to conceive of this; but if you've done no-sleep practice, you may have a taste of the possibility.

    And just a note on naps: Five to twenty minutes seems to be the ideal duration, according to studies. Short naps contribute to mental acuity and a feeling of brightness. After 20-30 minutes it becomes far more likely that you will experience stagnation and a feeling of extreme tiredeness. It would be better to take a short nap and then focus on getting a good night of sleep. For some people even a short nap during the day will make it more difficult to sleep well at night, in which case it would be better to forego the daytime nap altogether.
