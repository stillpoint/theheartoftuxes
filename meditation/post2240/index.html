    In any case, I can respond:

    Human beings don't understand who they really are. Except for people who have attained this point — the people we're talking about here — we all have a misunderstanding about our true self. All problems come from this one central problem.

    Looking at the world around us, we can see that while we might sometimes respect and value kindness, courage, love, integrity, and what we call positive character traits, much of the time we only talk about these; we don't actually embody them. A great deal of the time, our focus is quite a bit more vulgar: a great deal of the time, we value distraction, sensual pleasure, fame and other ego-boosting... or a good deal on a pair of jeans.

    As a species, we are collectively the worst influence on the world. We have come to the point where the vast oceans and vast atmosphere themselves are polluted to the point of dysfunction and to the brink of mass death. Locally, we have turned lakes, rivers, and landscapes into toxic wastelands. We have decimated entire species of animals, wiping out an estimated 100,000 species per year, at a rate 10,000 times greater than the natural (non-man-made) average rate of extinction. We have deforested 3,587,700 hectares of land this year alone. We have turned 8,278,500 hectares of land into desert this year alone. We have lost 4,830,00 hectares of land to erosion this year alone.

    Wars or very serious armed conflicts with hundreds to thousands of deaths are happening in Syria, Iraq, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Nigeria, Ukraine, South Sudan, Israel/Gaza, Somalia, Yemen, India, Uganda, Libya, Sudan, and Egypt. Many more countries are involved in ongoing violent conflict. Genocide is happening in several areas. Mass kidnapping, rape, torture, starvation, and displacement are happening in several areas. In the meantime, some 790,562,000 people are starving or malnourished around the globe.

    In our own communities there is violent crime, crime of corruption, addiction, racist violence, sexist violence, homophobic violence. Religion fights religion; political faction fights political faction. In our own families there is alienation, disrespect, mutual abuse, and sometimes horrific depravity.

    And even within ourselves, we wage wars and pollute the landscape — our belief says one thing, our habit says another; thought and impulse and emotion are seldom in harmony; we promise something in our minds, but our speech and our body's action are completely different. We lie to ourselves, and create various facades to present to others. We hate and disown integral parts of ourselves: our appearance, our mindset, our vulnerability, our insignificance. Most of us cannot be with ourselves quietly and peacefully.

    All of this comes from not understanding who we really are. We very deeply believe that we are fixed, separate beings within a separate (and overwhelming) world. We believe that what we need is outside of us, and that we need to manipulate our situation in order to get it. And we believe that we — this single self — is more important than anything else. Everything we do orbits around this idea of "I, my, me". Even our ideas of altruism are based on self-centered heroism and self-centered gain: we make sacrifice for my family, for my country, for my ideals.

    So we have a world of people acting in habitual service of illusion. It is as if we are a mass of unconscious parasites.

    But a few of us have awakened to what we really are, and have turned around or even completely ended the consumptive, toxic, violent influence of this mistake.

    That is what is to be attained.

    Along the way, there may be many positive benefits to spiritual practice, like temporary and conditional improvements in mood or efficiency or ability to relate with others; but ultimately the attainment is to overthrow the destructive effect of our delusion about selfhood.

    Who we really are has nothing to attain. It is already complete and pure. But because of our mistake about who we really are, we have something to attain. One might say that we have to attain that we have nothing to attain.

    ~
