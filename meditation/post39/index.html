    Non-spiritual meditation?

    >I'm not spiritual and find anything that relates to a "higher power" uncomfortable as I'm an atheist.

    That's a very interesting declaration. It's such a strong rejection and denial of even the possibility of a spiritual level of existence. It prompts me to wonder what you understand by the word "spirit". In some meditation traditions as well as in, say, traditional Chinese sciences, "spirit" simply refers to presence — your awareness here and now. At deep experience of your own awareness may quickly lead you to assert quite the opposite: "I and everything else — it is all spirit"; that is, the substance of all of life is mind. But maybe that's getting ahead of things....

    Correct meditation doesn't have to depend on any overriding belief, thank God. (Pardon the joke.) Well... it may require something like a belief in causality, the belief or understanding that cause and effect hold true, but not much more.

    Some meditations are investigative and others are quite purely and radically open-minded.

    Examples of investigative meditations include Vipassana, which looks into the nature of reality through your own direct experience, and self-inquiry, in which you seek the source of the experiencing mind itself.

    Examples of radically open meditations include Shikantaza and Dzogchen, where you simply sit in open presence, without letting the mind move toward or away from any particular object.

    All of those types of meditation rely on a certain level of concentration, so it is quite often (perhaps most often) the case that concentration practices are assigned before any of these meditations. These practices are often named "meditation" as well, but they are more correctly termed concentration. They involve working with an object toward which the attention is applied, and so the wandering or distracted or dull mind has a target to return to.

    There are many possible types of objects of concentration. (Visual objects like a candle flame or colored disks, sounds, physical sensations, visualizations, words or strings of syllables, tactile objects like meditation beads, perceived qualities or elemental principles, etc..) Some are almost universally workable, others work better for some types of people or situations and not as well for others. Also some objects of meditation will allow for certain attainments in insight while others remain almost solely applicable to the development of concentrative power.

    Concentration practice (sometimes called Shamatha or Samatha), when correctly applied, is calming to the mind. It is therefore quite healing to the mental, emotional, energetic, and physical layers of your being. Some people use concentration practices solely for health reasons, although the other benefits still take hold.

    Concentration practice also helps a person get closer to reality. Since it requires letting go of thoughts and returning to the object of attention, one can eventually experience spaces within the usual filter of ingrained thought patterns and constant inner dialog — spaces where things-as-they-are can be perceived (rather than what we think they are, which is our everyday perception). Gradually, the whole filter of thinking itself may diminish and clear, allowing a more constant engagement with reality.

    The first glimpse of the true nature of things can be life-changing. If that is of any interest to you, then you should enter practice with an open-minded attitude. Rather than saying "I am this" or "I am not this", just say "well, let's see what's here".

    People attach so much to the words and ideas of "spirit" and "higher power" and "God" and so forth that the words and ideas become very strong filters, or even completely opaque blocks to the clear perception of reality. The idea becomes more important than the actuality. But rest assured that you can get your own experience and see for yourself. Then all words and ideas will be re-arranged in your understanding.

    So there are many practices available to you. As to recommendations, given your situation I recommend a simple concentration practice. Counting the breath is very broadly applicable to almost all types of people. Counting the breath offers several great advantages over other practices:

    • Involving awareness of the breath, it brings you to the bridge between the "inner" and "outer" worlds — the connection between your mind and your physical body and environment. (This connection becomes clear when you see how your breath responds to your fear or anger or calmness; and how your inner state responds to differences in breathing.)

    • The breath provides a feedback mechanism: you can see very clearly when you are agitated vs. when you are calm.

    • The counting and breath provide a very clear object of attention.

    • The counting provides a very clear structure: you can see very clearly when your mind has wandered away from the practice (because you will either lose count or no longer be aware of the counting). The benefit is that this makes it more difficult to trick yourself; it is clear when you are doing the practice and when you are not.

    This can develop into a deep practice in itself, or it can serve as the foundation for other meditations, some of which are mentioned above. Good luck!
