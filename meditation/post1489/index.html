    Quick question for experienced meditators

    It's possible that both of those methods, in their respective situations, may work for you. Just listening to the sound of the fan is a valid practice, if you keep re-applying your attention and if the sound of the fan remains clear to you. You may find that listening to a "single" sound is not as definable as looking at a single object; the hearing does not form boundaries between sounds in the way that vision forms boundaries between objects (even if those boundaries are not real). So you may eventually come to your second method, which is simply to listen with the hearing, allowing all sounds to arise, remain, or pass away. The clear presence that neither retreats nor chases after objects (in this case, sounds) is what you are exercising. You become pure, unmoving listening.

    I can relate to the pleasure and comfort of listening to fans, ocean sounds, wind in the trees, and other "white noise" kinds of sounds. I have always enjoyed it very much, and have found comfortable havens within those sounds at times when I've been in tremendous physical pain. They've helped me to settle.

    The other side, of course, is that we need to exercise our ability to listen and settle without our favorite aids. If we are to be free to move through life, then we need to be able to apply our attention and non-reactivity to all environments, all sounds, and the absence of sounds as well. Otherwise, how will we be able to function clearly? When we need to face the angry, or dangerous, or sorrowful, or confused person in front of us, will we say "wait a while so I can go home and listen to my white noise machine"?

    Our peace and clarity has to be able to function in everyday situations, not just lovely natural environments; so we also should practice in challenging settings.

    A couple of notes that you may be interested in following up on:

    • The breathing creates a comforting white-noise sound, and if you are sensitive enough to it, it can offer similar peaceful and healing qualities.

    • If you eventually come to level of concentration where thoughts don't distract you from a constant stream of attention applied to the sound... if you then look into who or what is hearing the sound, that can be considered a Zen practice. A similar practice, resting in the "source", having traced the experience back to the place where the sound is heard, is sometimes referred to as the Kuan Yin method of meditation. It is in turn very similar to the hua t'o (Korean: hwadu) practice of resting at the place before words and thoughts. These all point the attention back to the source of self, or the mind-source, or whatever name it is given; there are just subtle differences in the approach and flavor of the practices. I'm not saying you should try these as opposed to what you're doing already; I'm just letting you know that these other approaches to listening practice exist.
