    The Zen view of enlightenment is not a view. Anything you say about it is incorrect, because words and concepts by their very nature are limited.

    Saying that "we are just..." is already a limited concept. So what is the truth; what constitutes sincerity in your situation (in our situation)? If we are sincere, we must admit that we don't yet know. This sincerity is the gateway to spacious mind. The spacious mind that doesn't attach to concepts is the source of Zen's view of enlightenment, and the source of enlightenment itself.

    This is very beautiful. It is not contrived; it is not special. It requires your sincerity.

    Zen teaching often seems to bring one back to the mundane and the concrete, because people's minds tend to fly off into big ideas and fancy experiences. So grounding in this present unfolding moment is necessary. But it is a mistake to think of it as only mundane and only concrete. Zen never says that. It never teaches "You must only be mundane." Not at all.

    Zen Master Dogen said:

    >They understand that in the mundane there is nothing sacred. But they don't understand that in the sacred there is nothing mundane.

    On the other hand, it often happens that a meditator will get a taste of emptiness and mistake it for full attainment in Zen. That's also not the case. It would be very helpful to realize that Zen is never limited. It never gets stuck in only Samsara or only Nirvana; a true Zen person can move freely between mundane and spiritual, conditional and absolute.

    The training develops that freedom.
