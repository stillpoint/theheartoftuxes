    The motivation is there, but perhaps asleep. Your original mind and your clear motivation are not separate; so in a sense you are never apart from true motivation, it only gets covered up by thought and perverted by incorrect view.

    The bland, vacant aspect of life is the truth of life's emptiness, blindness, and ungraspability -- but missing correct view. Since we are mistaken about who or what we really are, we remain separate from life's innate fullness, its vibrancy and meaning. The notion of 'myself' as a separate self causes me to assume everything revolves around my personal issues, and causes me to experience aloneness and discontent regardless of who I am with or what I try to get.

    Overturning this ignorance about self goes hand-in-hand with changing one's view and approach, from self-obsessed to compassionate.

    On a personal level, the appropriate motivation involves getting cured of the basic mind sickness of ignorance about self.

    > If you know, apply it. If you don't know, find out.

    But the cure necessitates moving beyond the personal -- beyond motivation based on the root cause of the whole problem. So correct motivation must involve movement beyond the small, separate self. Essentially, it must involve compassion for all others and embrace of the whole, infinite view.

    This is experienced very directly. When the attitude shifts toward compassion, happiness emerges, meaning is felt, energy rises. For most of us, so stuck in our self-centered view that we can't even imagine what compassion looks like, a living example is incredibly valuable, indispensable. Meeting and spending time with an awakened being, or even with practice people who are very dedicated to a wakeful, compassionate path, can be transformative. Some are moved deeply just by witnessing their teacher's day to day activity. Others are transformed when the compassion is applied to their own particular suffering.

    The absence of strong wants in your life may be a function of your even-mindedness. In some people, it is a function of depression. But the issue is not necessarily to feel a strong wanting for yourself (unless it goes along with a certain necessary mental or emotional healing). It's when you start to feel deep care for others that happiness starts to flower everywhere for you.

    Coincidentally, the change in motivation also makes you less susceptible to error and manipulation, making you more or less impervious to the kinds of issues you brought up regarding mantra. If you don't want anything for yourself, you don't follow the bait that others grasp at.

    Something came up spontaneously when I posted this lady comment. Maybe it will mean something for you:

    All people, but especially men, need challenge in their lives. Stagnation is a big problem, especially in societies like ours in which we enjoy great privilege and leisure, but without much meaning.

    A practice of challenging oneself regularly can be spectacularly transformative for one's energy, motivation, and mental health. One teacher recommends small weekly challenges (like abstaining from caffeine or whatever small addiction is an issue), larger monthly challenges (like facing some deeper discomfort, perhaps such as public speaking or traveling alone), and very big yearly challenges (something that you should never think of doing in your life (like climbing a mountain, or going to college if you've only ever done manual labor, etc.).

    This practice can, of course, be undertaken together with a practice of compassion (for example, doing beneficial work in 3rd world countries).
