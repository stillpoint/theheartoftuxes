    > Just so I understand what you say: When I dont cling to the habits/patterns of my natural mind and use my ‚ù§(reason) to guide me, im using self discipline.

    No, that's not what I'm saying; thank you for requesting clarification.

    Habits and patterns are not of the natural mind. Your mind unconditioned by habit is natural; habit is not. Habit only feels natural because that's what we're used to and that's what we believe about ourselves.

    We use a different terminology than "natural mind". We usually use the term "original mind" or "true mind" or "clear mind" or "ground of mind" to point at what we're getting at, because the term natural can evoke ideas about the wild outdoors or philosophies about being laissez-faire, and that's all something else. Your original mind is uncontrived. It is pure and clear, open like space and perfectly reflective like a mirror. It is this mind, which is neither conditioned by habits nor dependent on reason, from which wisdom and compassion arise. The pure, uncontrived reflecting quality of the mind sees things as they are, which is wisdom, and responds to things as they are, which is compassion.

    So there are no ideas to make. But neither can habits see and respond clearly. Unstained open presence is required. Open presence without adding some conclusion or speculation. Open presence without holding on to some opinion or plan from the past.

    Therefore discipline in this sense is completely momentary.

    So what is momentary? Can you demonstrate with an example, not from your idea or memory but from your momentary experience? Discipline is the discipline to return to the moment as it is, without adding or subtracting anything. No manipulation. It is what the Taoists call Wei Wu Wei: non-action, letting-be. It neither refrains from anything nor forces anything; it doesn't impose a point of view on reality, and it doesn't ignore reality. Discipline only responds to what is actually there before you make any idea, before your thinking sums it all up with a conclusion.

    The heart is your devotion to that, your devotion to the plain unmanipulated truth, as it unfolds in this living moment. It is the sincere aspiration and willingness to allow the moment to speak louder than your habit or opinion.

    This devotion in turn arises when you see your situation clearly and take it to heart. We all know that we're going to die; but how many of us know it? We all know that we can't predict the time of our death, but how many of us actually live with that knowledge, in deep awareness that every day could be our last? We have to look into these things, and not wait for them to come to us.

    Waking up is not only compassion and appreciation. It means seeing directly the nature of your mind ‚Äî who or what you really are. Among all of our habits, our deepest habit is regenerating an illusion of being a separate being. All other habits follow from that mistake, and contribute to it.

    It is like we are trapped in a dream that we completely believe. We see ourselves as a character interacting with the other characters and events in the dream, and we completely believe in that reality, taking everything very seriously ‚Äî our gain, our loss, our fame, our feeling, our money and possessions, our opinions. All of these dream things drive our life. Waking up means we see directly, beyond philosophies, "Oh, I have been dreaming. That dream character and all its dramas are not what I really am." When you wake up, you realize the nature of the dream. So when you wake up from your illusion of life, you realize the nature of the universe and all its inhabitants.

    You are the one viewing the dream; and that one is free. The discipline is to return to a clear moment, which is beyond the dream. The motivation is to be clear, to live beyond the dream, and to realize the nature of your own mind and everything that it witnesses, so that you are no longer trapped and you can help others who still are.
