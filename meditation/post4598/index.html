    Which meditation technique should I choose?

    You may get a dozen different answers here. You may get a hundred different answers here. You may get a thousand answers on the internet and in books and in magazines and from various people you meet. There is no single, perfect response to your question. It's not as if you can be handed a ticket and that finishes the issue for you: "Okay, all done; you go inside now."

    You have to do the research; you have to take the time to try out various systems and various teachers to find what is worthy of your trust and effort. It takes time.

    Many traditional paths of training will supply all the results you're looking for. There are many good methods; and there are many questionable and downright bad methods.

    The clear and definite answers I can give you are as follows:

    • Traditional methods have been passed down by many exceptional masters and tested in real-life practice by countless people. In general, traditional methods within a lineage are more complete and reliable than methods invented quickly or by a single person.

    Having said that, new discoveries are always emerging; so one shouldn't discount or stay blind to what can be learned from modern psychological and scientific approaches.

    • A living teacher is irreplaceable as a guide, a source of information, a role model, and a stabilizing influence.

    It will take some time to investigate and assess your relationship with a new teacher; and it may take a few tries to find an acceptable teacher. (You may hit it off on the first try, or you may need to search.) Don't expect perfection; distinguish between the two sides of your teacher, the human being and her/his flaws, and the guide who has your best interest at heart.

    • One minute of doing is better than a hundred hours of thinking and talking. When you try something out, even if it turns out not to be ideal for you, you will at least have that information and you will be able to move on clearly.

    You won't be able to get a proper feel for a method and teacher until you have tried practicing with them for an extended period. Just one or a few encounters is often not enough to go on. Try it out until you get into the rhythm of it. Along the way you will be able to see whether your teacher's speech and actions align, whether compassion is foremost, and how you respond to the teaching.

    • The student makes the first steps. This is correct relationship. Don't expect things to come easily; if they did, what value would they have for you, and what respect would you have for the practice? But when you come at least half-way, your teachers will appear and will respond.

    I can vouch for the fact that when a student makes a sincere effort, the teacher's heart naturally wants to rush forward to support them. But when a student strolls in with a sense of entitlement, and expects everything to be handed to them on a silver platter, they can't even receive the teaching when it's offered â€” the silver platter may even appear in front of them, and they are not open to it. So you have to be in charge of your own awakening, okay?

    If you want to say more about your interest and living situation, I might be able to suggest an avenue of investigation.
