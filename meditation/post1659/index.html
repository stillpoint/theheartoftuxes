    Is lotus supposed to hurt your ankles so much?

    No, that's not intended. It's a sign that the ankles are taking up the strain that should be diffused at the hips.

    In particular, in correct Lotus the feet should be flat at the top crook of the lap, soles upturned. But what often happens in people not ready for the posture is that the feet and ankles form a crescent curve that twists and strains the ankles. No doubt there is also twisting force on the knees that will show up over time as injury.

    The hips need to be free enough to allow the femur to rotate, so the feet can rest at the top of the thighs next to the torso, without a twisting pressure. And the hips need to be toned enough to keep the femur in its socket without gradual dislocation and weakening of ligaments and cartilage. The balance of flexibility and tone is very important.

    You should find a posture that doesn't put that kind of strain on your joints. It is not correct. Lotus is an ideal posture for someone who can maintain it properly; and it's a harmful posture for someone who can't.

    You can work on attaining the Lotus posture through separate practice. Yoga practice is one of the most accessible methods for eventually achieving Lotus. If you can find it, the much more rare (in the West) Sun Mu Do is also an excellent preparation for meditative postures. Thai Massage may also offer some benefit.

    In the meantime, try Quarter Lotus, with one foot on the opposite shin (not thigh); or Burmese posture, with one foot in front of the other; or Seiza/kneeling, with or without cushions. Quarter Lotus and Burmese are asymmetrical, so you should alternate which foot is in front, to maintain some semblance of balance in the posture's effect on the body.
