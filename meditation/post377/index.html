    I'd like to emphasize what I feel is the single most important aspect of meditation....

    That's a bit of a mish-mosh.

    To say that every other possible aspect, every other necessity just works itself out if you have consistency is starry-eyed dreaming. If you happen to have consistency, maybe it's sitting upon these other important factors. Is it possible you just want to hold on to your notion that you've found the "one key"?

    Among people capable of understanding the Dharma, difference in intellect doesn't limit possibility for liberation; but intelligence absolutely does matter. You don't think a worm has the same capacity as a human, do you? There are many people who due to defect or injury just don't have the capacity for correct view, and may not have it for many thousands or millions of lifetimes. So unless you're saying that a person lying in a coma is the same as an enlightened being, you need to rethink your position.

    Saying that every being has the same essential nature is quite a different thing from saying how a certain being is expressing that nature. If a person can't understand cause and effect, that's a problem. If a person can't use the power of reason to distinguish between valuable and worthless, or between good action and bad action, that's a problem.

    Don't get attached to words. That quote is true in a certain context; but it doesn't mean that intelligence is not an issue.

    Are you equating bad habits with makyo? Only in a broad and vague usage of the term makyo. Unless there's good reason to use terms in a vague manner, it's best to stay faithful to their defined meaning so that they can continue to mean something and we can continue to have worthwhile conversations.

    Makyo, strictly speaking, is the delusion of making something where it originally doesn't exist. It is most commonly used to speak of hallucinations and unusual states that appear in practice, to point them out as "demon's lairs" or "ghost caves" where one should not linger. As it refers to projections of mind that lead away from the path, makyo could also refer to making a ghost cave out of an idea — like "single most important aspect".

    Bad habits would more correctly be called vyasana (bad habit/danger) or vasana (habitual karmic tendency) — Sanskrit terms.

    You could just share that you've come to value consistency. That's enough. You don't have to make an idol of it. Unfortunately, once you put it out there in a public post you're more likely to attach to the idea and defend it. How quickly can you let go and return to the openness that clearly inspires you?
