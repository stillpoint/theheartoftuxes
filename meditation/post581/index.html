    TheHeartofTuxes

    We might distinguish between 'activities you normally do' and 'everyday activities'. Some (many) of our usual activities involve neurotic grasping, overload of disconnected images and data, generation of opinions and ungrounded concepts, agitation of emotions, attachment to sense objects, and trivial and meaningless activities that don't help anyone. Many common activities range between escapist waste of time and quite negative use of mind and body.

    So 'usual' may be very different from 'plain and regular'. Eating and washing and going for a walk have quite different karmic content and quite different effect on the mind than surfing the internet, escaping through music, and gossiping, for instance.

    Presumably you'll want to preserve whatever groundedness and spaciousness you've gained in meditation. So you might create a buffer period against excessive sensory input, and especially against the consumption of words and images, and especially against the use of 'thinking machines' (computer, phone) that replicate and agitate mental processes. You might give yourself a time after meditation to be very much in the body rather than returning up to the head, and to be very simple rather than gorging on mental/sensory input.

    Notice the habitual reaction to abandon clarity and openness, the habitual return to dullness and the egocentric agenda of control. We desperately crave freedom and openness, which are our true nature; but the ego is terrified of them and habitually covers them up. The habit of immediately turning to something consumable, something that will fill you up, shut you down, keep you doing with your thoughts, is in direct opposition to the aim of most meditation. So it's very easy to undo what you've gained in your meditation session by jumping into negative activity.

    Notice the habitual re-assertion of the controlling ego and it's favorite activities. It's not much different from saying "well, that was a lovely meditation session; now let's pig out on ice cream" or "...now let's shoot some heroin" or "...now let's masturbate." Part of us rejects the calmness and openness of meditation; because it believes that it has to do, do, do in order to exist. Simple life is not enough for this mind.

    Our practice is to keep calling on 'enough' mind -- the mind that is already content with things as they are, and doesn't need to fill in the space or cover up the intimacy.

    Our medicine against this 'not enough' mind sickness is our awareness and intention. We can realize that the grasping, controlling habit is entering immediately after meditation, and we can remember our intent to become clear, grounded, noble-minded, compassionate humans -- our intent to cultivate concentration and mindfulness and other positive qualities. Then we can use structures like schedule and commitment and routine to help us.

    We might consider having a simple, positive, grounding activity to do after each meditation, during which we can more deeply enjoy the fruits of the practice and more deeply root then in our being. We might do it the other way around and commit to a silent 'no-electronics/no words' time for, say, 30 minutes or an hour after meditation. At first one may feel agitation at being away from one's habitual activity, but one may also be greatly relieved and may enjoy and love being more in real life, with new possibilities opening.

    Open space without anything occupying the mind can be liberating; and it can also be agitating or terrifying. When we have nothing dulling our distracting our mind, we are faced with ourselves and our responsibility for our life! We are responsible for all our moments! This precious, precious life: when we near its end we will no doubt clutch and flail to have one more day, one more minute; and we will no doubt lament the ways we wasted our talents and time. There is something of the realization of both our boundless nature and our utter vulnerability when we exit meditation and have nothing in particular to do. It is a time when we can courageously face that precarious discomfort, and choose positive, compassionate, awakening options.

    It's hard to just be spacious, though. One may get lost. Therefore, as it is said in the I Ching, "it is favorable to have somewhere to go". It is good to already have a positive activity in mind, rather than just slipping into the default activity. So for example, the time after meditation might be involved in taking a quiet, enjoyable bike ride or walk around the block. Or working out. Or cleaning one's living space (or neighborhood). Or being in service to someone else (taking care of an elder or supporting a parent or partner or teacher, for instance). Or one might use the spaciousness and clarity to begin organizing one's day, keeping one's highest intent in mind: "What do I want to accomplish in my life? And what next to haven this month, this week, and today, to further that project?"

    Setting a framework of activity that reflects one's deep and conscious intent will help one avoid getting sucked down the drain by mundane details and negative habits; there can be inspiration and enjoyment about having a noble, compassionate, fulfilling project and making headway on it.
