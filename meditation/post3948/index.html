    Strain

    As with all of these kinds of issues, consulting with a healing professional is the first step if it may be something you can't resolve on your own (or even just to get some perspective or added support). This is true in both emotional and physical areas. You have to decide what course of action you are well enough to tackle, and how that course of action will contribute to balance.

    When pressures and blockages build up in the mind and body, external methods can offer some relief, but the relief is temporary and limited; and moreover, you are then dependent upon the external. You can use exercise to help you, it can be very effective. But it cannot be the central cure. It will only help to a point, after which you need more exercise (or whatever external) than you can bear.

    The central cure has to be a conversion of the mind — from self-obsession to compassion; from confusion to correct view based on acquired wisdom and inner faith.

    The thought and behavior patterns that are oppressive all have self-centeredness at their root. The motive behind all the strategies one attempts in order to help oneself is self-cherishing. We try to always get a better situation for ourselves, and our sensitivity and checking become greater and greater: "Am I okay yet? Is this situation better than the previous one? Will it make me comfortable? What do I need now? Maybe I should eat/sleep/watch a movie/have sex in order to feel better. Maybe I should meditate/exercise/read spiritual books/eat kelp in order to feel better…." And so on, and so on.

    Regardless of the kinds of activities one engages in, the background motive is finding a better situation for oneself. It is such a burdensome pattern! The checking becomes ridiculous, down to the most miniscule detail: "Ugh, this tea is not my favorite. Dang, there's nothing on TV."

    Of course, at some level one knows the pettiness of it all; and this sets up an inner conflict. One convinces oneself to try harder, to clean up one's act. But the self-cherishing motive is the same. It is all "for me"; and that's where the suffering lies.

    So it's the motive that has to be overturned. A higher purpose exists, one that makes you much happier: to help others out of their suffering. This shift in motive resolves that self-centered checking. Comfort and discomfort don't cause such urgent reactions, because it's not about you any more, it's about waking up, and helping the world with your waking up.

    So several times a day, and for sure when you sit down to meditation or do your physical practice, remember and take to heart that it's not for you. Rather, you do it as a generous offering. You are improving yourself so that you may be a better vehicle of caring and wisdom and helpful activity. This should help your mind to relax.

    A different way to look at this is in terms of simple movements. Feelings arise that you wish to avoid. The action you take (exercise) to help avoid these feelings is helpful to a point, after which it becomes problematic. But depression is avoidance. So if you turn around the avoidance, it is no longer necessary to force the exercise. So non-avoidance is the cure. This may take the form of simply not using an activity to get rid of the feelings. (You say "depressed feelings", but really depression is more about lack or fear of feeling altogether.) Whether it's a kind of sadness, or frustration, or fear, or whatever, the name of it can't grasp the breadth and lushness of the feeling itself. The feeling has a life of its own, and if you open your hands and neither cling to nor push away the feeling, it will transform into the beauty and wisdom of real life.

    Your not-avoiding may also take the form of meditation like Vipassana, which brings open awareness to all the felt processes of the being. Or it may take the form of expression that allows the feeling to speak: journaling or free writing, visual art, singing, free dance or Authentic Movement, etc. Or perhaps just talking it out with someone. The main point here is to not seek some haven away from feeling, but to allow it to flow — let it appear, and then (yourself) move on to the next thing.

    Two approaches — develop a compassionate motive; and stop trying to escape — are two sides of the same point. Trying to protect and preserve the "I" is what leads to suffering.
