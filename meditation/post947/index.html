    Both Tibetan Buddhism and Korean Zen embrace the practice of full prostrations repeated many times a day. In Korean Zen temples and practice centers the day starts with 108 prostrations. Very healthy, and very helpful in settling the mind. For big problems, times of crisis, and times when the mind is moving so much that it is difficult or impossible to sit still in meditation, doing prostrations is extremely helpful. It is said that bowing "brings the mind down", helping energy settle at a lower, more centered place; besides a regular daily practice it is also considered an emergency method, as well as a method of special practice (when you want to express or get big Dharma energy).

    Students who want to do special practice commit (usually after building up to it) to 500 or 1,000 bows or more every day. This is usually broken into sessions before and after morning and evening sittings, plus a session or two in the middle of the day. You can benefit from a regular practice of 9 bows, or 27 or 30 or 54 or 108. In a sort of folksy belief, 108 bows is considered a complete "personal repentance", addressing the 108 categories of mind defilements; or in simple terms, doing 108 bows starts you off with a clean slate for the day â€” you still have everything you're carrying from the past, but for this day you've cleared the way.

    Special practice days are sometimes held, where participants bow 10,000 times over the course of 7 or 8 hours. Try one of these, and your mind will for sure become clear!

    Bowing 100,000 times is also a part of the preliminary purification training (Ngondro) common to all schools of Tibetan Buddhism.

    If you try bowing, choose one method and follow it. Even a few bows will be helpful.

    If you decide you want to increase daily until you're doing 108 or larger amounts up to 500 and more, there are a few things that can help:

    • Breathe fully and in harmony with the body movement. With Tibetan full prostrations you can breathe in while standing and sinking, breathe out while extending, breathe in while gathering back to kneeling, and breathe out while standing up. With the Korean style, you can breathe in when standing up and breathe out when sinking and bowing. If your breath is deep enough, you can also do this pattern with the Tibetan style. It is also okay to find your own pattern as long as it is full and in natural rhythm with the movements.

    • You can protect your knees by using double mats (one on top of the other) with the Korean style, or wear padding on your knees (and hands) in the Tibetan style.

    • In the Korean style, although it is not considered proper form for reasons of both ritual formality and energy circulation, in personal practice it is sometimes necessary to save the knees from stress; and so when returning from the bottom of the bow you can push off with the hands and then half-stand (butt up) and unbend from the waist without first rocking back onto your heels.

    Full prostrations condition many muscle groups in the body; coordinate body, breath, and mind; build cardiovascular endurance; circulate blood and qi wonderfully; protect against aging by tonifying the legs and pelvic floor; and help relieve constipation. The central meditative benefit is that one-mindedness is more easily achieved; the thinking settles and becomes more simple.
