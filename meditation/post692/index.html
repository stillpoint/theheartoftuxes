    Meditating while Lifeguarding?

    Your duty to protect others comes first, and that duty includes being aware of many possible events. This is the determining factor in what kind of meditation is available to you in lifeguarding.

    You should not do concentration-style practice when you're on duty. Any meditation that focuses on one object will tend to limit your awareness, and at worst will make you unaware of much or even all of what's going on around you, depending on how deeply you go into the concentration. It's not something you can necessarily control, nor would you want to get into the habit of checking where you're at in meditation; that's a bad habit, to be rooted out.

    So open awareness meditation is best for this situation. Simply put, it is a readiness that is not tense. Then apply that, moment to moment.

    Ready for what? You don't yet know. There's no one direction the attention goes toward. That's why we say 'open' awareness. You are able to receive sights, sounds, feelings, intuitions from all directions. It's not necessary to imagine things happening and try to be ready for them; only be ready. If there's any difficulty finding out what "be ready" means, it's probably that you need to be more plain and simple about it.

    Another way to speak of this same practice is that you put everything down in a moment. You just don't hold anything; you let things happen. Awareness is already there, as long as you don't block it or get distracted. So you put down holding and distraction when they arise.

    This is a momentary practice. Don't check how your mind's doing, don't check "do I have it yet?," because that's already a departure from open awareness. Just be aware in the moment. If you get distracted or drift off or start to check yourself, simply return to awareness. That means you see and hear (and smell, taste, etc.) what's actually going on in the moment.

    This is a very high-level practice. It includes everything.

    If the mind-habit is too attached to objects and you find it impossible to stay aware, you can use your physical foundation as an anchor for open awareness. Feel your feet on the ground or on your platform, feel your butt and arms resting on the chair. Keep this contact always in the background of your awareness; say, 10% of awareness can rest there, and the rest is open and available. When standing or walking, feel your feet contacting the ground, and let that be the background anchor. The function of this is not to go deeply into the feeling, since that is a distraction from your duty, but rather to keep bringing you openly to the present moment.

    A third way to describe this same open awareness practice is to just keep moment-mind. Keep your mind in the moment. Or some may say keep your mind as the moment. The moment is your mind. Whatever is happening is your mind. So just don't go away from that.

    Good luck, and thanks for taking care of us.
