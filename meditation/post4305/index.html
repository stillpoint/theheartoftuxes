    I suppose one criterion is that the actual result should match the aim. Some methods have a nice facade and fancy steps but don't actually achieve the point you aim at.

    Other practices are very vague because one's aim or direction has not been clarified. It's difficult to say they succeed or fail at their aim, because the aim is never clearly defined; so one wanders about in pseudo-spiritual wonderland without really going anywhere.

    Other practices may in fact achieve their aim if performed correctly, but may have harmful or wasteful aspects, or may be difficult to actually perform correctly, or may have requirements that the common student doesn't have the capacity or willingness to fulfill. Some practices very easily lead to attachment, and require a very solid external structure that includes method, schedule, preparation (in some cases initiation), and teaching and oversight from a master; and without that external structure the common person goes wrong. It's not necessarily that the practice itself is faulty, but that it is very prone to abuse. Many energy practices fall in this category: they are beneficial if practiced well, but people practicing them tend to get fixated on the wrong things if they don't have strong guiding support.

    There's also a very large category of shallow and experimental methods these days, created by weekend meditators or anyone with a blog or business card. These may include 'modern' twists on traditional practices, methods made up by people who have theory but no attainment, methods trademarked for money or fame but without valid and confirmed basis, and methods misunderstood or taught in shallow ways by dropouts from bona fide courses of training.

    Just doing a practice doesn't constitute attaining the fruit of it. Just understanding a lot of theory doesn't constitute gaining the wisdom mind. Just having a spiritual experience doesn't constitute digesting it, nor knowing how to transmit it well to others. There are many, many ways practice can go off.

    And let's add that personal opinion about it really doesn't mean much except for befogging the issue. Some people are tentative and mistrusting by character, and tend to apply inappropriate skepticism. Others are lazy and sloppy by character, and bristle at the notion of rigor and structure. These are just views conditioned by previous life circumstances; they don't actually perceive the cause-and-effect relationship of a particular method.

    You want to say that practice should not have mindful attention? Um... that goes against the teaching of countless masters and dozens of wisdom traditions. At the far end of practice, at the upper echelon, there is effortlessness; but if it's premature â€” if the ultimate is put in the place of the foundation, that just leads to incapacity. As we see in a whole generation of practitioners, many people talk a good game and learn all the catchphrases and own all the paraphernalia, but when it comes to transformation, year after year they make little progress. Can you demonstrate your understanding of "reality is mindless", or is that just the current concept-of-the-week?
