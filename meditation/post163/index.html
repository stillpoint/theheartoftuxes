    Meditation and studying

    It depends on what you mean by meditation, and what you will be doing with your mind.

    If you apply your attention to what you are doing, that is already mindfulness. If your mind remains spacious and equanimous while applying attention — not getting so caught up in an object that you lose your sense of place — that is a kind of meditation. But when you study, maybe sometimes you'll need to harness all your attention, and that's okay. After study, return well.

    I don't think you need to add a very structured method like breath counting to studying; I would think it would divide your attention. Just doing what you're doing keeps your mind in reality. For example, having the intention to study attentively will help you notice times when your mind would otherwise turn to speculation and fantasy (like imagining how you're going to do on the test, comparing yourself to others or to yourself in the past, or general drifting thoughts).

    When studying, just study. When washing your face, just wash your face. When sitting on a mat and counting the breath, just sit on the mat and count your breath. Each thing in its own time; each action according to the situation. Mindful event flows into mindful event in a continuous stream.

    What can be very helpful, since you are considering these things, is to be grounded in the body. In general our modern, westernized society is very much "up in the head"; and this habit carries over to our approach regarding spiritual practice: we try to do it too much through our thinking, and we leave the body and heart behind too much. Meditation and mindfulness both are whole body-and-mind events, and in them we rest back into our big body (the world-moment) and our big mind (all reality).

    The added benefit of inhabiting your body more fully is that studying takes hold much better. Studies show that incorporating physical movement into study aids both conception and retention. Physical movement and mental digestion go hand in hand (viz. Michael Jordan's tongue sticking out, rock guitarists twisting their faces and bodies as they play, and people moving eyes and face when they try to access different parts of their brain, or moving arms and hands when explaining something). Children tend to maintain that connection better than adults, whose movement has generally atrophied.

    So there are several things you can try:

    • Do some physical movement before studying. If it deepens the breath, so much the better.

    • Intersperse periods of physical movement during long study periods.

    • Take a rooted, erect, and relaxed posture. Maintaining this posture in a natural way provides a connection to the physical world and functions as a meditation while you are occupied with other tasks that require your attention: attention goes to the task, but some percentage of background awareness is rooted in the body.

    • If you want to increase the rooted contact with the body you can maintain awareness of your physical foundation — the place where your feet contact the ground and your butt contacts the chair. However your body's footprint rests on its support, you maintain awareness of that. This can be maintained throughout the day, in movement and stillness, and even when speaking, thinking, and relating are required.

    • There was a study that showed ADD/ADHD students achieved better focus and their symptoms diminished when they sat on exercise balls rather than chairs. The opportunity for movement, little adjustments of balance, and background awareness of the body's posture helped them apply their minds to the work. I would think this or similar techniques could also be used in your case to help maintain that background awareness of the body-and-mind posture.

    Although it has more to do with studying than meditation, this next tip is like an extension of the former ones, and confirms the effectiveness of strengthening the mind-body connection:

    • When figuring out or memorizing complex topics, using full-body movements (along with writing, speaking, and hearing) can help retention. The more types of cognitive connections you can make with the subject, the better it is digested; and movement is extremely helpful for this digestion. If we take a mathematical formula as an example, you would stand up and physically act out the formula as you repeat it, making different movements for the various elements and operations. I know someone who used this to great effect on exams that required memorization of many procedures and formulas.
