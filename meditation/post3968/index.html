    I want to meditate, but I don't want to be less selfish or gain empathy

    Some people do too much to help others, and lose themselves in the process. Their own light is dimmed by trying to be good; their own energy is drained by being too wrapped up in others' concerns.

    At the other extreme, some people can't connect with others, and lose themselves in their own grasping at power. Their own light is dimmed because it is always turned back on themselves rather than shining out; their energy is drained because they can't connect with the world that could nourish them.

    Both of these are examples of incorrect relationship — poor boundaries. They arise because certain thought patterns and habits of behavior have been established by an original wound: something has caused the person (usually as a child) to have a dysfunctional relationship with the world.

    To say it in another way, the idea that "others are more important than me" is out of balance; and the idea that "I am more important than others" is also out of balance. To know that we are all equally valid and deserving is the only mature view. To be able to function with that view is the only mature life.

    You may experience this very directly and strongly in your life when you encounter people who have the same mindset as you do. You want to win; but if you come up against other people who think they are better than you and deserve more than you and expect you to get out of their way, how does it make you feel? When someone cuts you off in traffic to get ahead, do you think "ah, there goes a mature person?"

    Someone has implanted in you the idea that this is the way to live in the world — to always need to win. Someone else's fear has been implanted into you. Who was that, I wonder.

    When a child is abandoned or threatened by a parent, they lose their protection against the sense of being small and vulnerable within a vast and powerful universe. Their character then turns toward building up power to take a stand in that universe, and to convince themselves of their place in it. The original free, equal, non-attached virtue of the child is replaced with rigid motives, need for superiority, and desperate grasping at power.

    These wounds can be healed, and the character turned toward a happy maturity. "Me first" can become "it's okay to consider my own needs."
