    A bed can be too squishy and unstable for sitting. Rather than having a firm platform from which to rise, your skeletal structure sinks in. Rather than being able to arrange your balance from a flat base, you are dependent on the resilient properties of the bed. It usually causes the pelvis to sink lower than the knees (because the bodyweight presses into the bed), requiring you to counterbalance by slouching forward with shoulders and head. This places strain on the back, which quickly becomes painful.

    Some people can manage sitting on the bed, either because their body type and bed type coincidentally work together, or because they have the core and postural strength to stay upright even though the balance is pushing them out of alignment.

    A firmer platform is usually much better. It is better not only for the relaxation of the body (because the skeleton can find its balance and self-supporting arrangement), but it tends to be better for the mind and energy as well because one is closer to the ground. Connection with the earth is significant from psychological and energetic perspectives, and the sense of humility and naturalness that comes with sitting on the ground is also not to be scoffed at.

    You don't say why you don't like sitting on the floor. What's the issue? Is it physical; is it something that comes up in your thinking? Is it something that can be dealt with (for example, by tidying the space, giving the floor a good cleaning, and using a proper sitting mat or folded blanket)?

    Traditionally, sitting on the ground is regarded as the standard form. But that tradition started in countries where most people sit on the floor from birth and through adulthood. In the west, we develop a new tradition based on new needs.

    If sitting on the floor is truly problematic without recourse, using a chair is possible. There are many people who sit in chairs to meditate; and there are also many people who "don't like to" sit in chairs, either for physical reasons or because they feel like they teeter more or are less grounded.

    In any case, you can find a good way if you determine not to let anything keep you from meditating.

    Whether sitting on a bed, on the floor, or on a chair, some basics come into play: You should have a stable base, created with a triangle of butt on a cushion and knees on the mat (or on a chair, butt on the seat and feet on the floor). Relatively few people can sit comfortably without raising the pelvis at least a couple of inches. Most require the platform for the pelvis to be 3" - 6" higher than the platform for the knees. Approaching and exceeding the high end of this range can cause you to teeter too much, can pull on the tendons of the upper thighs, and can slide too much weight onto the knees. If you need a lot of height before your knees can be solid on the mat, it indicates that you need to develop more flexibility; and in the meantime you should either support your knees with cushions or folded towels underneath them, or try kneeling posture or sitting on a chair (or even not sitting at all, but standing or walking).

    There are many points of posture for sitting on a chair. Use the subreddit search function to find previous posts on posture, or find guidance in many of my previous posts.
