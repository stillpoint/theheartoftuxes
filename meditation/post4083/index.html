    Guiding a meditation for a large group for the first time today. Any advice?

    Most important are being clear yourself on the purpose and method, and being sincere about your qualifications to lead.

    Being clear on the purpose means understanding, yourself, why meditation (this particular meditation you're leading) is beneficial, and why you want to lead others in it. It is helpful to be aware of the many ego-based reasons that can tend to creep in.

    Being clear on the method means that you know in detail how the body and mind should be arranged in meditation, and why. You should also have experience practicing the kind of meditation you'll be leading.

    Being sincere will help you as well as the people you're leading. If you don't have much experience yourself, then noting that will take the pressure off, and you won't need to struggle to keep up appearances. Then people can believe in you while using their own powers of discrimination to decide what is useful to them and what is not. If you start out by posing as something you're not, it can quickly escalate into a situation that is difficult to resolve.

    If you're just passing along something you're heard or read, I recommend being transparent about that.

    Breathing based meditation is pretty universally suitable. The breath is a valid and neutral object of meditation. It can be helpful to note that there are many different ways of meditating, and that the one you propose is not necessarily the best — it's just the way you're choosing to practice presence.

    Sometimes newcomers are fearful that meditation is a kind of brainwashing, or afraid of losing a sense of self. You can assure them that the point is simple presence — developing the ability to stay with reality rather than get distracted. ("The mind that jumps from object to object like a monkey can't be with reality enough to perceive it. The calm mind is both clear and peaceful.")

    It is also important for new meditators not to start out with the attitude that they are going to control or force their minds to become anything in particular. It is not a wrestling match. Repeatedly letting go of distraction and returning to the present moment is the practice; letting go and returning are the powers that are developed, no special psychic states or feelings are necessary.

    New meditators can realize that they already have resources at hand; they don't have to rely on some outer authority to tell them how to keep their minds: the breath, the posture, and the floor in front of them are already good mirrors, good teachers. If one loses track (or count) of the breath, or if the breath becomes unnatural, that is a good signal to let go of distraction or forcing and return to the present breath. If the posture either slumps or becomes too rigid (or with the meditation mudra, if the thumbs collapse or press together too tightly), that is like a biofeedback machine signalling that either more rigor or more relaxation is necessary. And finally, the floor should be clear in the vision; if the floor is not there, plainly, then one has gone off in one's thinking and should let it go and return.

    With first-timers and newcomers, it's good to choose one or two essential messages, and keep repeating them. If the message is something you yourself have experienced and have had success with, then your own confidence and interest can be conveyed.

    People believe in your energy, not your words; so find the thing that you connect with, the thing that interests or inspires you. It is enough to say that this is an exploration for you; only speak with certainty about things you are certain of. You already have some experience, and that is more lively than trying to convey someone else's experience or words. If you base your leading on simplicity and sincerity, people can trust you.
