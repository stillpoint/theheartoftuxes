    Many people with back problems still meditate sitting up. It is the ideal posture for most meditation. There are ways to deal with it, including limiting your duration, finding proper support with cushions (etc.), learning proper sitting posture, learning proper body function throughout the day, and directionless, strengthening, stretching, and aligning the body with remedial exercises.

    See my posting history for many instructions on posture and issues of posture.

    Lying down promotes drowsiness, and this may take effect in ways you notice and ways you don't notice. It's not always possible to eliminate that effect, but the main ways to address it are with aspiration, clarity about form, open eyes, fairly bright light, and supporting influences like wake-up bells, sensory input (like use of voice and hearing in mantra or use of touch in working a mala), group practice, or guided meditations.

    • Aspiration

    For those with exceptionally bright and sincere aspiration, drowsiness and even extreme need for sleep can be warded off. Very uncommon, high-class practitioners may be able to stay brightly aware and awake in all positions, long after others have gone to sleep. This tends to be something that comes naturally to those who connect to practice on a deep, heartfelt level. Although this aspiration (beginner's mind) grows with sincere practice, such a deep level is usually not just drummed up -- it is very apparent when someone is this bright based on purity of aspiration alone.

    A way that aspiration connects with determination in practice is through awareness of death. "Practice like your hair is on fire." When you are deeply aware of your impermanence, great energy is available for practice.

    • Clarity about form

    When form is unclear or messy, the mind drifts. But when no quarter is given to the wandering mind, one can counteract drowsiness and dreaming to some extent. The method has to be very clear; one has to know it very well and have a lot of faith in it. One has to agree within oneself that nothing is more important than carrying out the method in the moment -- no thought, experience, pleasant feeling, spiritual vision, or realization is more important than the momentary stream of attention. This rigorous discipline can keep one awake.

    • Open eyes

    Keeping eyes open is very important in meditation, unless the method specifically requires closed eyes. When lying down it becomes even more vital.

    • Bright light

    Relatively dim light is usually recommended, to help the mind calm down and to prevent stressful glare; but when you have trouble with dozing and dreaming, brighter light is better. Indirect sunlight is best. Some people meditate in front of light therapy boxes. Just avoid glare that tires the eyes.

    • Supporting influences

    There's a long and well tested tradition of using simple sensory supports to help maintain presence. You can set a nice timer bell to go off every five minutes. You can use your voice to help yourself when you start to drift, either doing mantra or chanting practice, or voicing your internal practice aloud (such as counting breaths aloud). The more dull you become, the louder and faster you can use your voice. (Some Zen and Ati Yoga methods even employ shouting.) You can use other senses to bring you back to the awake moment, for instance using the tactile quality of a mala along with your usual practice. A recorded or live guidance may also help one stay awake (though some voices tend to be sleep-inducing, and relying too much on external support can make one lazy).

    Practicing with others in a formal situation almost inevitably 'raises the vibration', as some like to say -- makes you more accountable and carries you along in the stream of group attention. Practicing with a teacher or master is extremely helpful in this regard; and sometimes the radiant influence of a nearby master or strong practitioner is itself able to focus the mind. Some find this kind of blessing is accessible even from a great distance. Practicing in monasteries and other venues where very strong practice has happened can also carry you along, sometimes with incredible effect, as if the place were practicing you (some practice places are built according to geomantic / feng shui principles to entrance this presencing effect).

    Some practices key on merging in sleepy trance-like states. If you undertake this kind of practice, you can damn well better be able to trust the practice and guide, because they leave you open to suggestion, manipulation, possession, loss of sanity, and other troubles. Waking-up practice is best for most people.
