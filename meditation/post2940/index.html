    I used to feel euphoric after meditation, now I get a headache.

    There are many, many styles of meditation, including many good methods and paths of training. Most Zen styles will tend to involve open eyes, so you can get guidance in books and websites on Zen.

    For methods that do well with closed eyes, like the very popular Vipassana, it is very important to have a clear and strong motivation (to counteract the tendency to drift into subtle or not so subtle dreaming) and to understand and follow the method meticulously (to avoid making bad habits with your mind like indulging in sleep or letting the mind wander). Often these methods are taught in the context of week-long or 10-day retreats, which address these issues of motivation and correct method, and strongly instill a good practice habit within a context of constant support. If a meditator goes off on his own and tries to find his/her own way, bad habits of drifting can develop even outside of his/her awareness.

    Here is a page that offers a Tibetan Buddhist approach, including the open-eyes recommendation. In advanced Tibetan Buddhist practice, most meditations involve visualization; so one will either work initially gazing at an image, or eventually do the visualization with the eyes either open or closed.

    Here is an instruction on basic Taoist meditation. The page initially recommends fixing the attention (not the eyesight) in the forehead (the 'upper dantien'), which is okay — not good, not bad. Many people can do well with it, but it is also problematic for many, as it can be too agitating or can promote a controlling attitude in relation to one's energy. People younger than 30 would do better to keep the attention in the lower dantien, a couple of inches below the navel; and in general it is a safer and more fundamental practice (and is still used by many masters, so is not in any way 'inferior'). The article goes on to recommend this lower dantien as an alternative.
