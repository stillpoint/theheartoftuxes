    Staying at a retreat and doing over 2 hours of structured meditation everyday but feeling scrambled and confused

    Mungojelly said it in their comment: falling apart is normal, and part of the necessary process. We fall apart so we can come together in a new way.

    It's difficult in self-guided practice to know when it's too much and when it's not enough, when to ease up on the gas because too much pressure is being applied to the mind, and when to keep going in order to break through. One's own feeling of comfort or discomfort is not necessarily the best guiding principle, because the ego will always feel discomfort at letting go and being reformed.

    The kind of experience you're going through points to the value of the container of practice â€” having a guide, practicing within a tradition, practicing within a community, using the grounding quality of ritual, understanding and surrendering to a very clear method and schedule, and so on.

    It's possible that you bit off more than you can chew; but you wouldn't be the first, and it's not necessarily a bad thing. Now that you are in it, use your time to your best advantage; this is a precious opportunity to clarify your life.

    First thing is don't check your situation or condition, only return to the method. If you have a clear, beneficial method and know how to follow it, then just follow it. Stepping out of the method in order to check your progress or label your feeling "good" or "bad" is not practice, and doesn't help to balance your mind. Once one self-concerned thought appears, a thousand appear; so don't go down that road.

    You can actively embrace the difficulty of practice. You can change your mind about it, from thinking of it as a burden or ordeal you have to endure to seeing it as a valuable challenge or adventure. Aware of the benefits that you are generating not only for yourself but for everyone you contact and even far beyond, you can have enthusiasm for the challenge. Each difficulty can be part of the joy, as each difficulty is another landmark, another step along the path.

    Physically/energetically grounding influences:

    • Solid food
    • Emphasis on mindfulness of the body, and especially contact with the ground (when walking: feet; when sitting: butt and legs; when lying down: the body in contact with its support)
    • Weight (letting the weight of the body sink relaxedly; or adding weight to the body with a blanket or cushion)
    • Sense contact (returning to the present moment through the senses)
    • Cold water on the face, or comfortable bath, shower, or sauna
    • Stretching & massage
    • Physical meditation (bowing, walking, yoga)

    Mind-grounding influences:

    • Letting go of checking
    • Letting go of everything in a moment

    If you really feel pushed to the edge, I highly recommend prostration practice. This can be done in between sittings, or may even replace some sittings. The legs may get sore if you haven't done it before, but it is an extremely grounding practice. The energy quickly comes down into the body and out of the head, but with a smooth and vigorous flow that supports sitting meditation.

    Barring prostrations, some running or other whole-body-and-breath rhythmic exercise can help to ground you and balance the heady nature of a lot of sitting.

    You can use the prostrations form in this link. Even a few, a few times a day, may help. When lowering and folding the body, breathe out; when standing and straightening, breathe in.
