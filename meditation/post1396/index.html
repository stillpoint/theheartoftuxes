    How should I meditate?

    More structure is better for most beginners, so your method one is better. The structure gives you feedback that helps you to know when your mind has wandered away from the meditation. Without that structure it is very easy to fool yourself and make unfortunate compromises; then you will be strengthening your ability to compromise and fool yourself rather than your ability to concentrate on one object.

    This is even more the case if your mind is very active (as in ADHD): a very structured meditation that is followed meticulously will be most helpful. By "structure" I mean that there is a very clear object (such as counting the breath rather than just feeling it) and a very clear method (so you know exactly what to do when the mind wanders). You should be clear on how to keep your mind at every moment and in every situation during the meditation. This will help you see when and how you drift away.

    A clear, structured meditation method is not inherently boring. It's just that your mind habit doesn't like it. Your mind habit is to jump quickly from one thing to another, and to attach to objects. Everyone has this habit to some extent, but some have it very strongly; they depend on outside objects — games, TV, radio, ideas, conversations, online sites — to occupy the mind. When asked to disengage from those outside objects for a while, the jumping-around habit resists. This resistance is interpreted as boredom. But if you learn to concentrate, you will discover the ability to be engaged and interested without always needing new outside objects to take your mind away — you will be able to be present with a person, or with a task, or with yourself!

    Euphoria is not the aim of meditation. If it becomes the aim, you will be controlled by euphoria; that is, you will only be able to be okay when you have euphoria, and you will never develop the ability to be okay with the rest of life, when things are neutral or uncomfortable. You will be stuck chasing something you can't hold on to. Meditation means you train your ability to be okay in every situation, whether it's your favorite or not. You train that ability by using it: you sit still and apply yourself to the method for a certain period of time, no matter what else is happening in your thoughts or feelings.

    Having said all that, sometimes with ADHD and other patterns of hyperactive thinking the mind is so active that one can't even begin to meditate. Since you are already trying meditation it sounds like this is not the case for you. But if it turns out that way, there are other ways to settle the mind and develop concentration.

    A more physical practice is usually very helpful in these cases. Bowing practice is the traditional remedy, used very effectively by millions of people over the ages. The whole-body-and-breath moves in a vigorous, rhythmic pattern; so even if the mind drifts away very soon, the rhythm in the body helps it to return. Also, this activity very quickly settles the mind, often much better than sitting meditation. The energy comes down out of your head and settles in your center. It is very much like the way you feel calmer after a long bike ride or a day of physical activity: first you are active, then you are ready to sit down and be still.

    Bowing is in itself a meditation practice, but even just doing more rhythmic physical activity like cycling, running, swimming, or fast walking can help bring the energy down enough for you to sit more calmly. Other types of moving meditation you might explore include walking meditations, Taiji, Qigong, Yoga, and free dance.
