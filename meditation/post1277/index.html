    Many of the same instructions as for cross-legged sitting apply. The main thing is to be aware of how the body stacks up on its own structure, and be sensitive to where tension appears. Rather than forcing yourself to fit into an imposed shape, feel within the body to understand its relationship to gravity and the balance of support and ease.

    Many find that having the pelvis slightly higher than the knees helps relieve tension in the psoas and lower back. In any case, you should sit forward so you're not slumping against the back and so the edge of the chair isn't digging into the back of the legs. The pelvis should be free to tilt so you can find its balance point where the sit bones are upright and the spine is transferring weight straight through them. If parts of the spine jut out, it will require more muscular effort to hold the body upright.

    If slumping happens even when pelvis and spine find their neutral relationships, it probably means that more tone is necessary. People who think of meditation as solely a mental process may be surprised to learn that fitness helps. A certain level of strength and tone in the core and in the upper postural muscles (mid-to-upper back, shoulders, and neck) helps prevent collapse that leads to pain.

    We may tend to think that slumping is the posture of relief from having to hold ourselves up; but slumping produces unnatural and extreme curves and asymmetrical shapes that generate tension and pain, and over time make it difficult to even find a balance point. Not to mention the dulling, defeating effect slumping has on the mind.

    I encourage the use of necessary supports like the rolled towels and folded blankets, bolsters and support cushions, etc. At first it is good to use them; and then as you get stronger in sitting it is good to gradually wean off of them, if only for the sake of independence. But if you need them, absolutely you should use them because the main point is to provide stillness and ease in which to practice alert and present meditation.

    The feet should be planted firmly, to create a stable base triangle with the pelvis (the three contact points). Everything else in the posture depends on the contact points, so they should be right.

    If the seat is too high, you might want some kind of mat or folded blanket for the feet. In long sittings or in retreat you may find that the feet get cold or sore from having the weight of the legs pressing on them hour after hour, so some relief may be in order. If it is a serious concern, you may even acquire a standing mat/wellness mat that some people use for work at standing desks.

    One way of setting up the upper body posture is to emphasize the weight and solidity of the base (lower body), feeling it sink into the ground like the bottom of a mountain; and then let the upper body waft upward like the upper part of a mountain and the sky above it. Sometimes the image of a string lightly pulling the crown of the head upward is recommended; but in all these kinds of images, if they are taken too seriously and you push yourself to achieve an imagined ideal you will likely create more tension.

    I'd suggest some core exercises, as found in many styles of yoga and in Pilates, to help buttress the waist and keep the spine aligned well. Stance practice (Zhan Zhuang in Qigong) is very, very good not only for developing lower body strength, but for learning the relationship between a strong base and the deep relaxation it allows; and it is also very health-supportive in general.

    One alarm bell for me is your use of a folding chair rather than a solid one, and the fact that the seat is cushioned. A different, more solid chair may remove the cause of slumping, or at least help you feel when you are out of balance. Any cushion you sit on should be firm rather than bouncy or shifty, both for balance and for the comfort of your butt (which has implications in the responses that occur elsewhere in the body).
