    TheHeartofTuxes

    I use the word 'form' in both broad and specific ways here.

    The broad meaning is all form that appears in life: your body, your environment, sounds and colors, feelings and mental formations... and how all small and grand human creations unfold from mind. Our media and devices and cities and cultures all take the shape of our minds. Individual mind makes individual life; communal mind makes communal life.

    The more specific meaning points to the form in spiritual practice: meditation method, posture, schedule, style of teaching, style of practice, rules, intentions, commitments, rituals, roles within the community, and so on.

    In life and in practice, we need to work with some kind of form. And yet we often feel hemmed-in, controlled or oppressed by the form in our life. We may resent having to go to work every day; we may resent the demands of family, even though we also love them. We may hate what we look like or what we feel like. This all falls under the category of "difficulty with form".

    Therefore, we practice with form in order to understand freedom.

    Though we have a yearning to move beyond form and be free, the solution is not to try abandoning form. Anarchism, for instance, is insincere: it is only selective about which form it tries to abandon or destroy. The anarchist still has a name and address, still has a body and set of opinions, still needs to brush her/his teeth and eat food. All of this is still form.

    In spiritual practice, we approach, contact, become friends with, and merge with our original mind -- our original nature, which is complete and free and not dependent on form. When you find your true mind, you find that which is free. It doesn't need to get rid of form; and it doesn't need to attach to form.

    When you deepen your contact with your true mind, someone else's opinions and ways of doing things don't limit your freedom. Even your opinions and preferences can be let go of. You then adopt form not because of your attachment to a particular one, but because it is an opportunity to express your original freedom and completion. You don't resent your job or family; you show up for them, as the embodiment of your completion.

    If we want to attain our unconditional mind, we have to do our unconditional practice. Form helps us with that. We choose one clear method, and a schedule and commitment that matches our capacity as beginner, intermediate, or advanced practitioner, and we just do it. When the form is clear and consistent, we get a chance to see all the many ways we try to escape and avoid -- resistance, distraction, flights of fancy, pettiness, grandiosity, dullness, discursive thinking, sensual indulgence, self-tricking....

    Since the form is consistent, the mind comes up against it. If the form is messy, we have 'outs' and compromises; we easily miss seeing the ways we avoid showing up.

    So Zen Master Dogen said that authentic practice always feels like something is being imposed upon you. That's very interesting! Because often we imagine that practice needs to feel easy and wonderful; but really it's correct if it feels like a bit of a burden!

    Why? Because that's the feeling of our ego being whittled down. That's the feeling of our attachments, opinions, views, preferences, and habits being brought to light and diminished.

    The ego hates it; the soul rejoices.

    So when I was going through a difficult time in practice, one monk provided me with some direction: "Yah, usually in practice, when it's uncomfortable on the outside, the inside is very comfortable. And when the outside is very comfortable, the inside isn't comfortable."

    When we're making an effort in an activity that challenges our habit and attachment, inside we develop a great deal of joy and freedom. It feels very good at a deep human level. It feels true.

    But when we happen to get a cushy, spa-like situation and indulge in it too long, although it's comfortable on the outside, inside we may feel that time is wasting and we don't feel a sense of meaningful life. The more we try to get satisfied by manipulating outside things, the more distant we get from our innate completion and freedom.

    These things move from mere ideas to real-life experience when we do it, when we study with a teacher and group, and bring our unconditional mind to a practice with clear form.

    That is not to say that you have to do what everyone else does. There are many ways. There's such a thing as following your 'Dharma' without just seeking your own favourite, habitual, attached situation. If you stay present and open-handed, your path will unfold naturally, according to your karma. But wherever you go, there will be the issue of form: how do you work with it, how do you bring forth your original mind that doesn't depend on things being a certain way?
