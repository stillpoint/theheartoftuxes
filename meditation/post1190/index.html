    >That's the thing. I can't focus on one thing. Be it breath, counting or mantra.

    You said that you learned TM. You didn't mention studying other methods with a teacher.

    "I can't focus...": This can be self-defeating. What happens when you decide that you can and will focus? What happens when you watch extremely carefully for the entry of a thought, and refuse to follow it, staying with your object of concentration? Stay, stay, deeper, deeper.

    >My mind just wonders into the next thing everything. When I realize it, I bring it back to the mantra, but within a second, it wonders of again.

    Bringing it back is correct. If you keep bringing the attention back, why would this not be considered successful?

    Also, the wandering mind itself is correct. The thinking mind is meant to wander, to seek, to connect, to imagine, to leap to new places and digest old material. These are the brilliant functions of the thinking mind. Our practice in meditation is not to get caught in those functions; our purpose isn't to destroy those functions. So if you keep returning, and keep making your returning faster and more effortless, that is success.

    It's an issue of either method, motivation/application, external disturbance, or capacity.

    External disturbances such as chaotic lifestyle, addiction, unethical behavior, violent environment, etc. are sometimes easy enough to identify. If you use your mind in corrupt ways for most of the day, sitting down to meditate for a few minutes won't counteract that. If your life situation is disturbing, changes may have to be made there. But even if the outer situation is dire, if the practice is strong it you can have clarity nevertheless.

    In terms of capacity, it's possible that a mental illness like depression or anxiety disorder, or even a physiological issue like brain injury could make concentration difficult or impossible. Have you investigated these avenues?

    In terms of method, I have no idea if the TM method is valid, or how it's taught, so I'm afraid I can't comment on that point. But if you think the method is the crux of the matter you might seek out another method. Do you suspect the method might not be valid?

    In many cases, the motivation and approach are somehow tainted or askew. People may enter practice with an expectation that meditation will feel a certain way or bring certain benefits; so rather than surrendering to the method and devoting themselves to truth-as-it-is, they chase effects or try to force them to appear, or they spend a lot of effort avoiding certain feelings and states.

    When the mind attaches to a thought rather than letting it go and returning to the object of focus, at some level the meditator is asserting to themselves that the thought is more valuable than the letting-go. As much as we believe in meditation theoretically, when it comes down to it, we often believe that it's more important for us to figure something out through thinking. When we pursue trains of thought it means that we are trading open space for thought. We make that trade willingly at some level.

    So if the other aspects of method and capacity are not in question, more clarity and rigor are necessary in the application. Are you doing this just to get a good feeling, or does your motivation give you good reason to give yourself over, to surrender yourself? What is the underlying aim: only for yourself, or for all beings?

    Live teaching, group sittings, practice schedule, and group retreats provide the strict form that can soon reveal your mind habit to you. When the form is lax (as when you practice on your own) it's difficult to see your own mind and the reasons why you go off; but when the form is clear and rigorous and held firmly regardless of your personal preferences, you come up against your mind habit very clearly and frequently, so you'll soon be able to see when you make the trade (of spacious mind for thinking) and why you make it.

    In addition, being in a teaching setting with a live teacher will bring you into more organic contact with the teaching. Lessons may come to you at opportune times, or a clear teacher may even point directly to your stumbling block.
