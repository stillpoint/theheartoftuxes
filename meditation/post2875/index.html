    Just started seeing a psychologist...

    When I was in my late teens and twenties I had terrible bouts of meaninglessness and despondency. I had already been practicing meditation for some years, but had not integrated it well with my life. In part this was because I didn't have a teacher and a context of life training at the time; my practice all came from my own idea, with other ideas piling up through reading.

    So this is the first problem in these kinds of situations: it's all about 'I, my, me', and there's little objective perspective. Self-concern is inherently suffering; it already separates you from your broader and complete mind.

    This sort of self-embroiled isolationism is built upon the second, more basic problem, which is overthinking. Confusion, loss of meaning, existential crisis, stagnancy, and other sorts of spiritual sickness almost all come from overthinking. One gets so caught up in thinking about things and figuring things out that one misses the fact that attachment to thought itself is highly frustrating and depressing. There is no essential liveliness to thought; it is an empty shell, an illusion. Thought is an implement, not the artist who uses the tool or the art he produces. Thought is the packaging, not the food.

    I agree with the psychologist's recommendations, and I've recommended them many times on these forums. Yes, getting active is a great way of getting out of your head and into real life. It doesn't have to just mean 'exercise' as in fitness regimens; it can mean being physically active in ways that you enjoy: walking, running, cycling, rowing, swimming, playing frisbee or hackysack or tennis, playing with a dog, dancing, doing martial arts, and so on.

    There are many good mental, emotional, energetic, hormonal, and physical benefits to physical activity. Especially, rhythmic whole-body-and-breath movement is very good for grounding the racing mind, and is used as a necessary balance to sitting in traditional courses of spiritual training. Prostrations are commonly regarded as the best way to quickly settle your mind to a clear, centered, content, and bright state.

    Meditation might seem contrary to that physical grounding, but really it's not. Meditation — correct meditation — also helps free you of attachment to thinking, and gets you into your body and into the real moment. Because you use your will to face whatever arises, you get a chance to have a clear relationship with your heart and mind, on the most intimate levels. It is very important to our happiness that we actually face and experience what's going on for us. Bringing that neutral, openhanded awareness to whatever emotion (or lack of emotion) may appear is very healing.

    Movement and meditation have been very excellent and vital tools in my life. They inform and support each other. In particular, I highly recommend free dance/conscious dance as a movement practice, as it can embrace and integrate literally any mental or emotional situation you may go through. You put it in your dance and it gets digested; you learn how to trust the flow rather than get stuck in your head.

    Physical practice has also helped me to be more human. There is a great risk of withdrawing from relationship through meditation; and I have known this risk personally. One may use meditation to escape feeling, to escape responsibility, to escape intimacy. It's not correct meditation, but it happens to many meditators. Movement can help shift you from being up in the clouds to just being clear, within real life.

    Since the whole suffering comes from this self-obsessed overthinking, I have a third recommendation besides physical activity and meditation; and that is active compassion for others. If you start doing caring things for others, there is no doubt that you will experience more meaning in life — not based on theory, but based on a spirit alive in you. Caring for others will not only help you out of your small-minded self-focused prison, it will ignite a joyful warmth in your heart. Even pain and grief, when shared between people, can be cause for warmhearted joy. That is my experience.
