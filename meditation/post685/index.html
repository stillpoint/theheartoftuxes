    How to focus on the breath?

    The first point is what you are trying to achieve with meditation. Why do it?

    If you are trying to get a particular feeling or state of mind, you will introduce stress and make problems for yourself. The proper attitude is altogether different: letting it all be; being whatever you are in the moment, and letting the world be whatever it is in the moment. This is a return to integrity, as opposed to forcing your agenda upon life.

    So if you expect or try to generate certain things like visions, you will suffer.

    It sounds as though a very clear and formal method will help you. You have a habit of stressing and you have lots of "loud" thoughts, so a structured method will provide you with quickly repeating aids to letting go. Try counting the breath to ten. Count both in-breath and out-breath: In (onnnne) Out (twoooo) In (threeee) Out (foooourrr)... and so on. When you get to ten, start again at one.

    When your thinking takes you away, no problem. It's very important not to make more stress at that moment! Just, whenever you realize it, return to one. Whether you get lost in thinking for a second or for twenty minutes, just return immediately and without any drama — without any further thinking — to one.

    Don't think that getting to ten in your meditation is easy. It may take a few weeks or months, if you are doing the practice correctly. Also don't think that getting to ten is the goal. It's not a big deal. The important thing is training your ability to return.

    Currently, your mind moves here and there very easily. That is the natural process (karma) that has developed in your life. Never mind that. What is wonderful and precious is the moment of returning. Going away in thought for an instant or for weeks and months is just part of your cause-and-effect; but returning is very, very wonderful. Train that returning, and let it all be.

    When you sit down to meditate, take a few moments to align your body. Take a posture that is both relaxed and awake. It has a nobility to it; you express and support your clear, noble nature through your body's posture. During this setup time, take an inventory of your attitude. Are you trying to make something particular happen; are you stressed; are you looking forward to the practice of letting-be?

    Let the weight of the body settle downward into the mat or chair. Your skeleton and your concentration support you. As much as possible, release the muscles in your torso, your shoulders, your neck, your face and brow. The jaw should be relaxed, the mouth should be closed, and the tongue should rest in a relaxed state touching the upper palate behind your teeth. The eyes should be gently open. You gaze softly downward at an angle or you can gaze softly forward. There is no need to focus on something like a laser.

    The un-gripping of your body and mind are very important. This is already meditation. In this un-gripping you are coming closer to reality. You let go of your own agenda, your own opinions, your own habits and conditioning, and you say with your whole being "I accept the truth of this moment; I am the truth of this moment. So be it."

    Settling down in this way is your bodymind's expression of "so be it", which is Amen in Christian prayer, Svaha! in Buddhist prayer, A-ho! in Lakota prayer. You establish that this moment, this reality is okay as it is. You don't need to add or subtract anything. The counting meditation then helps you return to that letting-be again and again and again. Nothing extra, just the number, just the count, just this breath.

    Good?
