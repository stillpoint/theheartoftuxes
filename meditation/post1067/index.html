    TheHeartofTuxes

    It varies. It depends on the motivation (the reason why the person wants to try), on the capacity of the person (how capable of facing silence and boundlessness, and in fact of facing their own mental habits; also how physically resilient they are to long sitting and new schedule), and on the style of retreat (as some are more demanding than others).

    A rule of thumb is that if a person can sit and apply themselves to a practice reasonably well for about 20 minutes, they can probably take on 40 minute sittings in a supportive group environment.

    One would be inclined to say that any effort is a benefit, and that it's almost never a bad idea to try a retreat. One wants to encourage the will to practice Dharma whenever possible -- to welcome and encourage that aspiration.

    The risk, however, is that one bites off more than one can chew and is then soured on the entire prospect of practice. A bit of effort or discomfort for a good purpose is no problem. But people whose plan completely outstrips their capacity sometimes find themselves dejected, self-blaming, and even shell-shocked (traumatized by taking on too much), all of which form obstacles to future progress. Some people fall off the path completely, which is tragic.

    Gradual progress tends to 'stick' better than periodic heroic efforts.

    The things that buffer against these negative results include correct Dharmic motivation and gentle self-compassion. 'Correct motivation' means doing it for the right reasons: not from a dream of gaining special power or situation for oneself, but in order to wake up, go beyond self-centered motives, and help all beings. It is when the motive is self-centered that the practitioner is most vulnerable to excesses, disappointments, and failure to listen to the messages of their own body and mind.

    Further, when the approach is openhanded and free of expectation, both difficulties and rewards can be accepted with equanimity as merely part of the landscape of practice.

    Self-compassion means loving and uplifting the best in oneself, regardless of outcome. Don't pin your sense of self-worth to successes and failures, but rather to sincerity and kindness, no matter how things seem to turn out.

    When considering "should I try?" it is no doubt helpful to talk to the retreat leaders and see what's expected. One might also consider whether one's physical/mental constitution leans toward challenge and adventure or whether one fares better in more gentle and gradual processes.

    Both sides are important in practice: our attempts should match our capacity; but by the same token, we often (usually) don't know our capacity until it is tested, and may find that it is far greater than we thought; so frequent challenge is very important to stimulate growth in practice.

    All other considerations swept away, if the mind is sincere, anything can be undertaken. The sincere beginner's mind is boundless.

    >In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind there are few.

    >-Shunryu Suzuki
