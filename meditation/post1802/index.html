    Once again I'll say this: don't put all your eggs in the meditation basket. It's a common mistake to try to use meditation to gain more control; but then the feedback loop of control — the dynamic that causes anxiety in the first place — increases. People coming to meditation from a therapeutic setting are even more prone to this pattern. Meditation should be considered a possible tool to help you come down, rather than the one magical thing that will fix your life.

    In response to your comments:

    Don't meditate lying down.
    It promotes a sense of surrender and weakness rather than self-possessed awareness. Lying down is appropriate for relaxing and dissolving practices, and for special practice situations (like back pain) where the person can maintain a very bright and stable attention even despite the lulling, dulling influence.

    Don't meditate with your eyes closed.
    It greatly increases the activity of the imagining, dreaming mind, and it greatly encourages dozing and dullness — so much so that people aren't even aware of the effect. For specific practices that require it, eyes closed is appropriate; but from what you write it seems that you shouldn't be doing those practices. Keep your eyes open if you are meditating. It will help you stay real and not have your mind kidnapped by hallucination.

    Choose one proper method; don't mix, don't invent, don't switch around.
    Switching and mixing add to the unclarity you are already experiencing. Meditation is for the purpose of making you more clear and stable, not to encourage you to flit about in your mind. So — if meditation is right for you at all (we haven't established that it is) — use one very clear structure to help you stay focused and grounded.

    Do some research to find a method that doesn't exacerbate anxiety.
    You are already discovering that not all practices are equal. Just diving into something without knowing its aim or provenance (where it came from and why it is offered) is inviting trouble. These days, anyone with a video cam or blog account can give instructions as if they're an expert. That doesn't make them an expert!

    Furthermore, not every practice is good for everyone. Some are not even good for most people. And some that have been invented by self-styled teachers are actually harmful or at best a big waste of time.

    At this point it sounds like relaxation practice, rather than meditation, is more your pace. You might continue with guided relaxation practice or find a Restorative Yoga class.

    I find that a good number of therapists and doctors don't actually know much about meditation other than what they read in their journals (i.e., that 'studies find' that meditation can be beneficial). Make sure your support team knows what's going on with you and how you relate to the different practices, and take care that they themselves are qualified to give recommendations.

    In many cases of anxiety disorder meditation only makes it worse; and occasionally we've seen breakdowns in people who push too hard at something that's essentially not benefiting them.
