    Is there a style of meditation that cultivates a sense of urgency in life ?

    Vimutti's suggestion of death meditation and other comments are right on point. Death meditation is far from morbid or morose; it brings us to the vibrant point that every day and every moment of life is a precious gift and opportunity — and it brings that point home in a way that is not merely theoretical. The heart and attitude are transformed.

    Besides the 9-point analysis linked to by Vimutti, there is also the visualization of going through the process of dying and dissolution, which I very highly recommend (and which I practice myself). Few things can bring home that compelling point so poignantly.

    I'm not sure that 'urgency' is the best word for it; but it's something like that. It's urgency without the agitation. You get serious about your life without losing the capacity for play and joy and levity. But awareness of death — sincere, heartfelt knowledge of the end that faces us — is like a knife that cuts off large portions of waste. You find yourself caring more; you find yourself motivated to practice and to help others and to do things that matter.

    I believe that both the 9-point meditation and the visualization on death are explained in The Tibetan Book Of Living And Dying by Sogyal Rinpoche, along with many discussions on how awareness of death can greatly enhance life.

    Next, you say you have good weeks of practice and then experience setbacks. Part of this comes from motivation; part of it comes from the structures in your life and from training.

    Checking your practice is not right practice, and anyway you are rarely the best judge of how well you're doing; but also it doesn't help to be complacent about backsliding. A great woman master, Jetsun Khandro Rinpoche, said that many people and especially many western students have this problem of backsliding and not being able to progress beyond a certain level. She said that we have many good qualities of sincerity and intelligence and investigative nature, but there is one reason why after a point we fail to go any further or progress is exceedingly slow. That reason, says Jetsun Khandro, is self-cherishing. It is very simple cause and effect. We don't progress because we still hold on to what we want and don't want; we still seek our own comfort over that of others; we still seek to impose our influence on events in order to have them turn out our way. Self-cherishing is the block, and self-cherishing is what has to be excised in order to progress.

    Left to our own devices, we have not the will or ability to overcome our own self-cherishing. We habitually seek our own best situation, so "I, my, me" inevitably gets put ahead of great compassion and boundless mind. We could have great compassion and boundless mind if we chose, but we don't choose it; we choose "I want my way".

    Khandro Rinpoche pokes fun at this, but points it out unflinchingly: in theory we know and talk about the fact that we are one with everything, that everything interpenetrates; but when it comes to our action, we put ourselves ahead of everything. Likewise, we talk about the truth of impermanence, but day to day we act as if we will live forever.

    Since we are not able to get beyond this mentality ourselves we need to arrange our life situation to help us. That means studying with a teacher and submitting to their guidance. It also means associating with other Dharma practitioners, both for the example of those ahead of us and for the shared aspiration of those behind us and beside us on the path. And it means undertaking a samaya, a commitment to use our body, speech, and mind in ways that lead beyond self-cherishing, to awakening.

    It doesn't have to be a Buddhist path, but the unavoidable point is that self-cherishing has to be overcome, and that we have to make real efforts in our real everyday lives in that regard. It won't only happen on the meditation cushion. We have to start putting others ahead of us in a million different ways.

    Maybe one person in a billion is able to do it themselves without a teacher, but that is because of many previous lifetimes of practice and cultivation, guided and supported by teachers. If you want to avoid wasting your time in life, you should submit yourself to a good course of training under a wise teacher or enlightened master.
