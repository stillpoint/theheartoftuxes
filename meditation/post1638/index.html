    There are many methods that can be applied to that goal. But also, you should consider whether meditation is going to be the thing that helps you at the stage where you're at.

    A lot of people ascribe some sort of magical, all-healing power to meditation, but it's not always the case. If someone is so anxious that even sitting down and applying the mind to an object of focus is problematic, then some other kind of help is necessary before meditation can become effective.

    In many cases, counseling or therapy before or in conjunction with meditation practice will be most effective.

    But if you are able to sit still and apply your attention to an object of focus, a concentration-style practice would be in order. This is sometimes called 'shamatha' (Å›amatha) meditation, which means 'calmness' meditation. It doesn't necessarily bring insight that will change one's relationship to the world, but it can calm down the thinking mind that creates so much trouble. If it is learned well and practiced meticulously it can provide both short-term relief and gradual long-term increase in groundedness and calmness.

    There are many forms of concentration-style practice. The main point in all of them is that there is a single object of focus. You keep bringing yourself back to that single object, which means you keep releasing all other objects. All the thoughts that seem so urgent or attractive, so agitating -- you let them go, time and time again, and return to the one object of concentration.

    Once you understand this basic form of concentration-style, you may be less confused about many of the methods you encounter. Most of the popular meditations recommended online and in books are either concentration-style or have some part that is concentration-style.

    The recommended object of focus can be within any of the six spheres: seeing, smelling, tasting, touching, hearing, or thinking and other mental activity. Some spheres are more appropriate because we tend to recognize and attend to them more easily. Some are more or less appropriate depending on the meditator's character. And some are more or less appropriate depending on the issue at hand (for example, if thought-based anxiety is an issue, a meditation that brings you closer to your thoughts may not be the best choice at first).

    You don't need to find a 'perfect fit', you just need to find a method that had been proven to be effective, and apply yourself to it. It is the consistent repetition of the practice that makes it work, not any magical power in one method over another. Any of a great number of methods could do well for you, as long as you are able and willing to repeat it often and regularly.

    Breath-based meditation has a lot of benefit for cases of anxiety, but it's perhaps more crucial to receive good training and understand the method very well, because the desire to control the breath can arise and create more problems with self-manipulation and frustration. So although it is a very good method, I would recommend that you learn it from a capable teacher and receive ongoing guidance with it.

    Mantra is very helpful for overthinking, and is very easy to apply.

    Focus on a single visual object (like a colored disk) or single ongoing auditory object (like a bell or shaker) is possible, but requires a bit more apparatus and can't be practiced in as many settings.

    Physical practice is especially effective for anxiety, and I do highly recommend it, either as your sole practice or as companion practice for sitting meditation. Prostrations are very, very effective at bringing the mind and nervous energy down to a more settled state. It is a practice you can do regardless of how much the mind is racing, unlike many sitting meditations.

    Other physical practices include walking meditation and movement meditations like Yoga, Taiji, and free dance (5Rhythms, Open Floor, Soul Motion, Authentic Movement, and others).
