    So your ego knows how to catch you. After a bit of practice in sitting you start to approach actual meditation; and when that happens, the ego knows just how to fool you into stopping.

    At first, it was general distraction that ego used as its tool, to ensure its own reign. Then as concentration began to develop, maybe aches and pains, or thoughts about your difficulties kept you from following through in meditation.

    With yet better concentration, even these could be overcome; so ego reached into your mind to find what you attach to more deeply — to find the trick, the leverage that keeps you from being simple and clear. It found that 'awesome' ideas catch you.

    For some it's thoughts about sex. For others it's an ongoing story of how 'spiritual' they are or how much they understand. Some minds are moved and dissuaded by fear; others by blissful feelings or thrilling visions.

    Unbeknownst to you, all of these have the same substance as any other mental manifestation, any other thought. They are equal in essence to any petty musing or fantasy: it is all just mental movement. But because you attach, you believe that some mental movement is worth clinging to. Wrapped up in self-fascination about wonderful thoughts and how wonderful you are for having them, you lose equanimity. You lose insight into the common essence of it all: mental movement, mental projection.

    That's where ego catches you. It gets you to fixate on its own agenda of division, manipulation, busy-ness, self-aggrandizement. It keeps you from completing the task of meditation. Because if your mind becomes simple and unattached, that's ego's demise. In true meditation, small self dies, boundless self lives.

    So one great Zen Master said:
    >Our school has no good idea.

    This is a very wonderful teaching. There is nothing to be held or made or wanted. We don't buy into the fantasy that the self can become powerful enough to rule life. We turn around, against the stream of ego, and go the other way — toward surrender and trust and acquiescence, resting in original empty nature.

    A helping teaching from the Tao Te Ching:
    >Do you want to improve the world?
    I don't think it can be done.

    >The world is sacred.
    It can't be improved.
    If you tamper with it, you'll ruin it.
    If you treat it like an object, you'll lose it.

    >There is a time for being ahead,
    a time for being behind;
    a time for being in motion,
    a time for being at rest;
    a time for being vigorous,
    a time for being exhausted;
    a time for being safe,
    a time for being in danger.

    >The Master sees things as they are,
    without trying to control them.
    She lets them go their own way,
    and resides at the center of the circle.
