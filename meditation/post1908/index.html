    When the head is full, more ideas don't tend to promote balance.

    Space is necessary. But the doing, grasping, controlling habit of ego is very mistrustful of space, very uncomfortable with it. We need to realize that this ego discomfort does not mean that spaciousness, ungraspability, and unknowing are wrong!

    This is where we need to apply our reason, and welcome ourselves into openness... ease... non-doing. The answer to the issue of balance is not in getting more, more, more. At least, after you get the first core of teaching it's not. Most of us already have enough tools; we know what we have to do, we just have to do it. (Show up for the moment. Do not-doing. Very simple.)

    So I might give your question back to you and ask how you can offer yourself more space or set up a supportive schedule, environment, relationships, or attitude that will invite you to stop looking elsewhere and just sit and relax where you are.

    ~

    When a car's wheels are properly aligned, after you make a turn you let go of the steering wheel and it finds its own straight line.

    When you have finished weighing something, you take it off the scale and let the counter return to zero.

    When you finish your own activity, let go so you can return to 'zero', return to neutral readiness. You find your balance by taking your hands off the steering wheel or scale.

    Lightning finds its own course through earth and sky. Water wends its own way downhill, finding the path of least resistance. When the wind settles, trees and grasses return to their innate integrity. Everything in nature knows how to let go and return; only humans attach to doing and getting, and mistrust letting go to open space.

    ~

    You can help yourself by frequently stepping outside to gaze at the sky. Learn from the sky, let it teach you about returning to your own spacious nature. The relationship is very direct: you can download spaciousness directly; you can quickly remember your own sky-like mind, within which all experiences appear, but which is never marked or moved by any of it. This is your original, balanced nature.

    Having frequent moments of balance will bring more balance into your life.

    ~

    In terms of organizing your life, from time to time it can be beneficial to get an overview. This is practical, but it shouldn't spill out everwhere and take over your mind — you shouldn't always be analyzing and arranging.

    But maybe at the end or start of your week you might consider your priorities for the coming week and do a bit of planning. Don't be shy of recalling your life's highest aspiration at the beginning of your days and the beginning of your weeks. Everything you offer time and effort to can follow and support your life's central aim.

    Then, maybe once a month or so, you might take a higher-level view, considering the big picture of your whole life, remembering that you will certainly die and that you don't know when you will die, and then using that knowledge to bring clarity to what is worthwhile pursuing. Be bold in your intrepid adventure, and be bold in cutting off what is not useful. If you are willing once every month or so to look at the broad swath of your life with clarity, you can avoid falling into a pit of habit and being trapped by ego patterns simply because they happen to be comfortable or because "I've always done it that way."

    If you devote very clear time to these check-ins, and if you look very honestly and practically, you can make balancing decisions and then let it go. That is, the clarity of your decisions will allow you to move on clearly, without lingering with issues, mulling them over uselessly, dithering and hesitating and procrastinating and basically wasting precious life.

    ~

    So as a matter of course, embrace openhandedness, space, and unknowing. Become friends with these qualities that otherwise agitate the common, unconscious person.

    And in practical matters, take the time to look and plan clearly; then let it go. Once you've decided, just act; don't hedge.

    Don't be afraid of mistakes. If you make a mistake, correct it. If your plan isn't working out, adjust it. That's why you schedule these check-ins, so you can adjust. It's the fearful attempt to make every last point perfect that paralyzes us into not doing anything. We end up being busy, busy, busy about nothing meaningful... and then we die.
