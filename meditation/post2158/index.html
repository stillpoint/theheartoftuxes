    TheHeartofTuxes

    It doesn't matter, but there's cause and effect.

    Being hot and sweaty during meditation is not bad. If you ever practice with monks in East Asian styles (Mahayana and Zen) you will find that it is a very common experience, part of the fun. There's usually vigorous bowing before the first sitting of the day, and in many cases even a work period (gardening, chopping wood, cleaning, preparing food, construction, etc.); so even in cool and cold weather it is possible to be sweaty during some sittings. No problem!

    The trade-off is that the breath and blood and energy are strongly flowing, and the mind is much more settled, grounded in the body. Although it's not necessary to do it this way if it works for you otherwise, it is very beneficial to do vigorous physical movement before sitting meditation. Even just the fact that the joints and ligaments are warmed up can reduce strain and injury from twisting limbs into place first thing in the morning.

    The only detriment may be the sort of buzzing quality of energy that could take a while to settle down. Depending on your fitness, usually after four or five minutes the heartbeat and breath have settled. If your sitting period is quite short you might find that you don't settle fast enough to get into the wheelhouse of meditation. But for the most part practitioners will experience quite the opposite: that physical practice before sitting helps them settle down more quickly and deeply, once the initial commotion has abated.

    When you become familiar with it, the warm glow and even trickles of sweat are taken as very enjoyable part of practice â€” you are in it with your whole body-and-mind; it's not an alienated function, just up in the head. There's a special, ardent feeling to sit in a room of huffing, sweating practitioners, all in their robes, all having exerted themselves with zeal... and as the group's breathing settles and stillness descends the sacred but physically grounded feeling is palpable. It occurs to me that the sight of beads of sweat on a brother or sister meditator's brow is one of those unacknowledged messages of devotion that sink into the consciousness of people who practice in community.

    In most centers, temples, and monasteries where I've practiced there's a 15-minute break between bowing (usually 108 prostrations) and sitting sessions. This gives people a chance to have a sip of water, change out of their bowing robes, and/or splash water on their face, and anyway to have a breather and settle down. In solitary practice I usually take just 5 minutes to stretch my legs and sip tea before sitting back down. I enjoy getting right back to the mat without thinking or doing much else, and I also enjoy the feeling of my heartbeat finding its calm pace, though by that time I'm quite focused on the meditation method.

    If you want to do it the other way around, no problem. There's no harm to it if it works for you. I just want you to be aware of the possible benefit, and the fact that you don't need to judge sweating as somehow undesirable. Sweating seems to be a sort of gateway or at least signpost to surrender. Many people tend to keep themselves 'arranged', so to speak, staying prim and proper and under a certain control, all the while avoiding sweat; but once sweating becomes inevitable it's as if they give themselves over: they settle, relax, stop resisting and manipulating their experience. Furthermore, it's as if the whole being melts and merges. The effect of this shouldn't be underestimated in an undertaking that aims at merging inner and outer (self and reality). This is one reason, besides the physical purification, why sweating has been part of sacred rituals around the world. (Viz. the Native American sweat lodge, Scandinavian, Turkish, and Siberian saunas and shamanic sweats, and the sweaty venues of modern ecstatic dance.)

    If you are concerned about staining your robes you can wear an undershirt and change it before sitting. It is also a lovely and grounding practice to wash the feet before sittings.
