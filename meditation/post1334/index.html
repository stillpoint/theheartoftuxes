    If you only get to twenty before noticing, you're pretty well off. Sometimes we lose track for minutes at a time. Sometimes a person loses the way for hours, days, or even years.

    If you can return soon, that's wonderful. If you go away for a long, long time and then immediately return, the returning is still very wonderful. Is that clear? It's the returning that is wonderful; it's the returning that we train.

    The rest of it, the losing awareness and going away, happens by natural process. We already have deep patterns of distraction. That pattern is already at play in us. It's cause and effect: because of how we've used our minds in the past, we now have this pattern, as surely as a cliff wall has been shaped by the constant movement of the ocean upon it. Cause and effect.

    So we are changing the cause. We are now practicing presence, bringing our mind back. New causes will mean new effects; and gradually the old pattern will diminish. But effects from old causes will still come up for some time. We can expect this. Whether it's general ungroundedness, or attachment, or persistent thoughts of a particular kind, effects of past causes will come up. So it's up to us not to make the new causes of checking, comparing, judging, reacting with frustration, making more thoughts, and so on. Our task is just to return as soon as we notice. Very simple.

    The method helps you. Realizing what's at stake helps you. Good teaching, clear schedule, supportive environment, and companionship in practice help you. But then you just do it. Don't make a big deal, don't make anything; only, the moment you notice you've gone away, return instantly.

    So when you find yourself having lost count at 'eleven', or 'twenty', or 'fifty-three', you can know that's just natural process of cause and effect, and you can just return very simply and quickly to 'one'.

    Also, if you sort of have the count in the background but you notice that you've been following a train of thought, same response: just return to 'one'.

    One Zen Master said she knows when a beginner is not doing the practice correctly because they tell her they have no problem counting breaths to 'ten' without distraction. It shows her they are not being meticulous about their attention, because it would be very unusual indeed for someone to have even a few seconds of unbroken attention if they weren't experienced in practice.

    Try to keep count; but also try to have each number clear in your mind. The clarity of each number reflects wisdom, the momentary aspect. Keeping the count reflects mindfulness, the aspect of awareness in the world of time and space and other beings.
