    > We always talk about focusing on our breath, but what does this exactly mean?

    It depends on the school and method. Even within Theravada Buddhism, for instance, there are different interpretations of the scriptural instructions, and different skillful adaptations.

    You ought not mix and match various paths and instructions, because then you lose the integrity, the context, and the proper order of each path, and you confuse the various ways and make yourself into an arbiter rather than a student.

    Don't close your eyes.

    You have doubts, but you don't realize the source of your doubts or the way to dispel them. You believe that gaining more ideas will resolve the doubts; but actually your doubts come from a proliferation of thinking and a lack of one clear stream of teaching.

    This (Reddit and other online forums) is not the place to come for dispelling doubts. Practice isn't a matter of consensus, where votes or agreement win. There is a right way, according to cause and effect, regardless of what someone says about it. Trying to learn from random strangers online, you open yourself to too many different instructions, some of which may be fairly authentic but shakily based on numerous disconnected courses of teaching, and some of which will be largely based on shallow understanding, incomplete experience, misconception, opinion, and questionable motive.

    Suppose someone says to focus on your nose, and someone else says to focus on your belly, and yet someone else gives a response you don't quite understand. How will you choose from the three? Based on your own guess, your own preference? How will you apply it to ongoing practice, without knowing how the advice fits in and supports the other points?

    Teacher is not only for 'someday, to get far'; teacher is for right now, to begin correctly.

    Even if you don't have a teacher right now, you still need some clear, consistent, dependable source of teaching. Not an app with shadowy origin. Not a conglomeration of quotes and other snippets. Not some amateur's or scientist's invention. But a clear, complete (at least enough to start) exposition, based in a proven tradition.

    Knowing that the method is passed down and maintained by awakened beings, and that it has been proven by countless other meditators will allow you to proceed on reasonable trust.

    Receiving one clear instruction will allow you to cut through speculation about other ways.

    Learning the instruction well will support you in immediately letting go of trains of thought when they arise.

    Knowing that countless others have tread the same path and attained fruition will help you continue even when you don't yet see your own progress.

    It is your job to excise doubt from your practice. Don't frantically grasp at more and more knowledge. Aim at the right knowledge, and then act on it. Only when you act on it will you be able to verify its result (which sometimes takes time). But if you are greedy and lazy about it, and try to get everyone else to hand you your practice on a silver platter, you will get precisely the result of that attitude, which is ongoing doubt — not only doubt of the pratice, but doubt of yourself and your capacity for progressing.
