    This usually appears in its famous form, called 'the monks dance', which means nodding off. The sense of great comfort and peace, along with a feeling of refreshment, and the disappointment when it's over, all tend to go along with attachment to stillness and sleep.

    Most people who fall into these states aren't aware that they are drifting and dreaming. From their perspective, they have been doing their practice continuously, and it has been going swimmingly well. It can be difficult to dislodge this habit outside of the context of formal training with a teacher and in a group practice situation. If one equates good practice with good feeling, why would one want to change?

    Without knowing your practice directly it is hard to say if this is the case for you. The litmus test may be to do formal practice and adhere to the rules of posture, then see what kind of attachments are revealed. Practice with a teacher is a good idea in any case.

    But if you are very intuitive and you are following a shamanic path (which isn't clear from your post -- engaging in a few ceremonies doesn't mean you've committed to a path), you may have a different methods and approach than the usual meditation. If so, consult your teacher/shamanic guide on how to proceed. Sometimes symbolic sorts of messages appear from the depths; and if your practice involves working directly on this level there may be a purpose to following spontaneous movement.

    It is essential to be clear about your practice. Know what method you are doing, and why. Some people will read the above paragraph and take it to mean they can dabble and tinker and invent their own practice, which only invites ego and its shadows to take the lead.

    If you are clear about your purpose in meditating, which necessitates being clear about your purpose in life, then you can more easily know why to adhere to a form our why it may be okay to abandon a form. "Because I like it" isn't sufficient, unless you want to become even more enslaved to preference.
