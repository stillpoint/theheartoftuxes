    I have a noob question about mindfulness meditation

    That shows the importance of a deep, vibrant practice at times when the situation is not as dire or agitated. We tend to slack off when things are going well, since it seems we don't need practice; but that's when we should be building our strong center so it's available when the wheel turns the other way.

    Physical practice is highly regarded among advanced meditators for its ability to settle the mind quickly. Many courses of training are predicated on the body's ability to ground the mind and emotions; first the body is trained with physical exercise or work, until some degree of will and focus are developed, then deeper meditation is possible.

    Among the monks, teachers, and resident practitioners I've practiced with, many go straight to the Dharma Room for bowing practice (prostrations) when crisis or mental turmoil arise. It is extremely inspiring to see this vigorous practice undertaken, and the way it transmutes the energy of distraction into the energy of sharp clarity and dedication.

    I highly recommend prostration practice; but many kinds of physical practice can be helpful to settle the energy until such a time as sitting is possible for you.

    Some styles would have you just 'sit through it' or 'sit with it'. That is, sit with the agitation. That is also okay, but we should understand that it's a teaching within a particular context. When you are supported by the practice space of a Zen Temple, under the guidance of a master, with the mutual encouragement and example of other practitioners, and with a clear schedule, and having absorbed a method until it's second nature... then it may make sense to say 'just sit until it resolves'. If you can do it on your own, also that is a good way. But if you are so agitated that you only fight with yourself when you sit, it could be a waste of time, whereas other methods may help center your mind quickly.

    Prostration practice is actually performed in many Zen schools, including schools where the 'just sit' instruction is given. I've experienced it in the Korean, Chinese, and Japanese Zen traditions as well as in all forms of Tibetan Buddhism I've encountered. It is that widely respected.

    For more suggestions, here's an excerpt from one of my recent posts:

    >Bringing yourself back to the moment in times of difficulty is possible if you have a lot of experience with practicing through difficulty. Mostly this is not the case with people who practice alone; their preference guides their practice rather than pure motivation, so the grounding is not there when the shit hits the fan.

    >If this is the case, what can you do? In the short term, the techniques you try may not be working. In that case, you need to arrange your outer structures to help you. This may include:

    >* changing your environment to make it more clear (more sane) * associating with people who can help you in practical terms or with people who have a stabilizing influence because of their deep practice * establishing regulated schedules of sleep and activity * ingesting healthy, supportive, life-giving food (belly food as well as eye food, ear food, and mind food: the things you look at, listen to, and think) * doing physical practice like prostrations, or to a lesser extent simple work or walking * seeking the advice and contact of a teacher or mental health professional * over the long term, submitting to clear, consistent, and rigorous spiritual training led by a master

    >If you can't help yourself directly, you have to change the channel and use your external circumstances (including your body) to help you get centered.

    >You can't magically reverse cause and effect. You make the causes, you reap the effects. Your salvation lies in recognizing your responsibility for your own condition, and doing the things that lead to clarity and freedom, and doing away with the things that lead to confusion and habit-based suffering.
