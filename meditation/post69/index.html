    How can I deal with the physical sensation of pain while meditating?

    Welcome to the club! Almost everyone who tries sitting meditation will experience pain. Sometimes leg and back pain will continue to be an issue; sometimes it will be intermittent or only present when you again lengthen your sittings or intensify your practice; and sometimes it will go away with consistent effort. One thing is clear: without consistent effort it will be difficult to re-pattern the causes of the pain.

    The next point is that it is not necessary to be pain-free. It is also not necessary to hurt yourself. Find a middle way that respects the reality of the situation as well as keeping the body free of injury.

    There are many points of posture that can be conveyed in person but are difficult to correct just through reading. Some of these postural corrections may help with pain. Unless you are born into a culture where sitting on the floor is very common, it will take some time to gain the hip flexibility and build the postural muscles of the waist, back, and neck to a point where long sessions of cross-legged sitting is comfortable.

    There are several reasons for the recommended sitting postures:

    • To embody self-reliance â€” not leaning on any external support, but getting along by the strength of your own resources
    • To bring the body into a very stable form so that the mind can relax and the senses can settle. (If the form is stable but very painful, this can work at cross-purposes; so you have to seek your own balanced approach.)
    • To allow the breath to enter the body fully and smoothly. This means that the belly should be free to expand and settle without stress.
    • To allow energy to move through the body without blockage, circulating through channels up the back and down the front of the body, and allowing the energy channels of the two sides of the body to interconnect.

    These are aspects of the ideal physical conditions for meditation. The body, mind, and energy are all interconnected, and influence each other; when one is brought to calmness and stability, the others more easily follow. Posture of body and posture of mind are not separate. So there are good reasons to persevere with posture if you are seeking anything more than simply a 'nice time' in meditation. But on the other hand, the mind is primary; and although many outside conditions can help, if the intent is strong and sincere then meditation doesn't have to depend on achieving one specific posture.

    Some suggestions for posture:

    • See if certain supports can help. You want the hips to be high enough so that the knees can be stable on the mat. The hips should also be at a position where the lower back / waist is not collapsed back or forward. This means the lower back will be either slightly curved inward or, in fewer people, fairly flat. This position has the sitbones positioned vertically so they can take weight straight downward through their structure without requiring extra tension to maintain the posture. There is however some tone or firmness to the curve of the back. It also means that the belly can hang without tensing to support the body or being crushed inward, allowing the breath to come deeply and smoothly into the body.

    • If extra cushions under the hips or under one or both knees relieves the pain, try using the supports and gradually weaning yourself from them. Do part of the sitting with high cushions and part with lower cushions. Gradually work toward less and less support until eventually you can just sit on the mat itself for some time before discomfort arises.

    • A bit of pain is okay. We need to develop stability of mind in all kinds of situations so that we can be free. Happiness doesn't depend on getting our favorite situation. But if there is no way to sit without a disturbing pain or or pain that warns of injury, then try a chair. Some very experienced meditators use chairs at least part of the time during long sittings or retreats.

    • If no kind of sitting posture can be free of pain, then either you need help to build your postural ability (yoga, good chiropractic care, etc.) or you may want to devote yourself to a different method of meditation, like walking, bowing, or work meditation. Active meditations like these can also be deep practices, even if they are inherently more agitating at first.

    • Last, but not least, consider the mind-body connection. The body has its specific pattern of tension and laxity for a reason. The way you have used your mind to relate to the world may have set up sites in the body where there is armoring or aggressive energy or slumping or numbness. Where one bodypart is not in natural alignment, others are required to compensate. So pain and what you think about pain are closely related. If your relationship to the pain is tense, then you may create more pain. If your relationship to the pain is open and accepting, then you may soon dissolve the pain.

    Here's an equation for you:

    pain x resistance = suffering
