    Your situation highlights one of the major downsides to practicing without a living guide or teacher. You muddle through, and glean advice from various people practicing various methods, many of whom are also muddling through.

    Strange experiences will appear if you meditate for any significant period and intensity. Get used to it! Overcome it!

    But having your sleep ruined is certainly an important consideration. People who practice at a high level, with great devotion, may give up sleep in exchange for practice. Some overcome their deep human attachment to sleep to a great extent; and a few do very fine indeed with little to no sleep, since they are nourished and refreshed by the meditation itself.

    But I don't believe that health should suffer because of meditation practice. Certainly not as a matter of course (sometimes you'll do a special retreat and it will take a toll, but then you recharge your batteries).

    It's difficult to know the origin of your disturbed sleep. It could be energetic: many, many meditators experience greater wakefulness. I can hardly count the number of retreats I've done in which lack of sleep became an issue because a sort of bright, wakeful quality took over.

    It's also possible you are noticing more of your thoughts and obsessing about your condition, and subsequently being more disturbed by them. You do 45 minutes of sharp awareness along with letting-go, and when you finish meditation the awareness continues but the letting-go does not. This often happens.

    In both cases, among others, some physical movement and/or relaxation practice could help.

    Most energy-based meditations conclude with exercises to dissipate built-up energy. Do you do something like this at the end of your sittings? Perhaps rubbing the hands together and cupping them over the eyes, and then again over the ears... feeling the soothing warmth, letting tension melt....

    In addition, you can tap the skull with the fingertips, and especially 'Beat The Heavenly Drums' by tapping the depressions at the base of the skull on either side of the neck muscles. Bunch your first three fingers together and tap or drum them on those spots (the same spots you massage for certain kinds of headache).

    Whole-body dissipation can also be a good idea: standing up and gently but loosely swaying, jiggling, bouncing all the body parts. Like rag-doll style, but very gentle. A variation on this is 'Willow Branches In The Breeze', in which you stand loosely and allow bursts of energy to move you spontaneously — it ends up looking like flowing or shaking appearing here and there in the body, but 'you' don't do it, you just allow it to happen for a couple of minutes.

    Other very good physical remedies include all kinds of flowing whole-body-and-breath movements like swimming, brisk walking or jogging, cycling, flow yoga, prostrations, or flowing dance. You could do this in the hour before bedtime to dissipate accumulated or blocked energy.

    As an overall practice, grounding could be very helpful. As much as possible, settle your weight downward and connect with the earth throughout the day. You can let your bright buzzing mind settle at your feet when walking or standing; you can settle down in your seat when sitting; and you can even lie down and intentionally release tension to gravity (similar to the entry to śavasana practice). If you are a sensitive, nervous, or fiery type, grounding will be even more important for you.

    Some meditative traditions offer guidance regarding sleep, and even offer sleep and dream meditation. This requires a teacher, and also the motivation to practice to that extent.

    Good luck.
