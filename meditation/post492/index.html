    Meditation for negative thoughts?

    This is an ego issue. Correct me if I'm wrong, but it sounds like you've developed a self-image dependent on always succeeding/winning. This arose according to cause and effect: someone (likely a parent) gave you the raw material by telling you what you should be like and/or providing the example through what they value or obsess about, and you gradually built it into your own self-image.

    The ultimate cure is to understand what you really are, to understand your true self, which already has everything. This is the higher aim of meditation. In the meantime, you can gain understanding of the false nature of the self-image by studying Dharma and hearing good teachings, and/or by looking into your own patterns of thinking and reacting, with a responsible guide -- a counselor, life coach, or psychotherapist.

    There are many practices that can help you. Almost all kinds of meditation will require you to let go of trains of thought and return to a method, and that in itself will help you see your patterns and learn to disengage. Mindfulness practice --maintaining awareness throughout the day -- will help you see when those negative thoughts arise, and let them go before they take control of you.

    There are many good practices for transforming negativity itself, and identifying with positive, fulfilling intentions. Metta and Tonglen meditations develop compassion, which can grow to fill the emotional emptiness upon which many negative patterns are based. Many instructions and guided versions of these practices can be found online or in libraries or bookstores.

    One simple but profound practice you can do is thankfulness practice. At the beginning and end of the day you can dedicate three minutes or so to considering things you're thankful for, and joyfully feeling the support and the associated gratitude.

    This can also become a sort of mindfulness practice where you intentionally bring forth gratitude for everything you encounter. Easy and pleasant things make your life delightful; difficult and unpleasant things train your character and teach you how to let go and move on. Neutral things invite you to be more aware of your life. People you like are joyful gifts. People you don't like are the joyful basis of your practice of compassion. "Encounter every situation with joy/gratitude" is one of the slogans worked with in the lojong Seven Points Of Mind Training in Vajrayana Buddhism. Very good practice.
