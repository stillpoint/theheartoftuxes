    title: [Question] Posture summary: 2.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2014-03-27 14:01:52 some_url:

    Let's distinguish between the things you need to do to change your physical posture, on one hand, and your time in meditation, on the other. If you expect your time in meditation to change your posture through the rest of the day, then of course it will be very uncomfortable for a while. You can do it that way if you choose, but you will need to push your body into unfamiliar form and you will need to hold that form for longer and longer durations to build the muscles and skeletal relationships that will maintain the new posture.

    So, much easier will be to separate the two issues for a while.

    Check with a good chiropractor or other related health professional. But if you feel you are in proper condition to try these, you might want to work on your posture in a few ways (outside of formal meditation time). Some people find these methods effective:

    • Keep your motivation to be awake and uncontrived, and to lead a noble-minded, integral life. Mind is primary; the body follows mind; so make your mind correct and eventually the body patterns will change. Five minutes of effort at clarity in meditation and 16- to 18-some-odd hours of self-centered, distracted, slouching mind will produce a similar proportion of effects in the body.

    • Whether you undertake a yoga practice or Pilates or some other postural work, or whether you just do some simple exercises (nearly) daily, building the postural muscles is important. First, it helps to train the body to set itself in certain efficient ways of sitting and moving; and second, it allows greater ease (the weaker the muscle, the more it has to strain to have the same effect).

    • Also, in many cases developing flexibility along with strength is necessary. Sometimes just releasing one tight, frozen set of muscles will help everything settle into place without much effort.

    • To train the postural muscles, the movement you're looking for is the palms of the hands rotating outward while the shoulder blades draw together and the upper chest rises. Simultaneously, the top of the head is drawn up and the chin is lowered and drawn in. This lengthens the neck and corrects the curve that comes from the head jutting forward.

    Performing the above movement while pulling the chin back and drawing the shoulder blades together (for a count of five to ten), you'll feel a nice little ache of effort appearing mid-back between the shoulders and in the long muscles that run down the front of the neck.

    • Next, lying on the floor, you can put a rolled towel or small pillow behind the curve of your neck, and press it (the curve of your neck) into the floor. The same front neck muscles are activated. You can get a similar effect lying flat in bed by pressing the head back into the pillow for a count of five to ten; but make sure the chin is pulled in with it (rather than arching the neck).

    • Depending on how stable and healthy your back is, you can perform rowing and/or bent fly repetitions with light weights to build the muscles that draw the shoulders back. Use lighter weights, but hold for a couple of seconds in the position where the shoulder blades are closest together. Therabands or rubber tubes can be used in arrow-shooting movements to work the same muscles. This is made more effective if you are mindful to keep the chin tucked in.

    • Then there are a couple of semi-static resting exercises you can try. One is to sit with your tailbone close to a wall and just try to keep most of your mid- and upper back and back of the head in contact with the wall. Breathe fully and easily.

    • Finally, roll a towel to the length of your hips to skull, lying over it on the floor so your spine is cushioned and slightly raised for its entire length. Just rest in this position, which will help your chest to open and your shoulders to fall back restfully.

    At meditation time, until your posture is rectified find a compromise: be comfortable, but feel and embody your nobility and full-heartedness and wakefulness. That attitude will help you adopt your best posture given your physical situation. Avoid forcing posture unless you are up for the pain it will cause, and unless you can maintain concentration nonetheless.

    Learn about the kinds of support you can derive from mats, cushions, benches, chairs, etc. A certain amount of relief can be derived, within an upright posture, if you know your options.

    Edit: Some time after posting I realized I gave no attention to the supportive core muscles in my recommendation. Training the muscles around the waist — the abdominals, obliques, muscles of the lower back, and underlying muscles — not only helps longevity in sitting but allows everything above to rest better. When the core muscles are toned and supportive, less muscular effort is needed to stay stable. Doing away with the forward, backward, or sideways collapse of the lower spine, the imbalance and tension is no longer transfered to the upper torso and neck.
