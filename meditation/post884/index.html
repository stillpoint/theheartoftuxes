    You don't necessarily know. It's not for the meditator to judge meditation; it's for the meditator just to do it.

    What is your aim; what are you hoping to achieve? There are so many different effects, aims, and methods! It's important to be clear on how meditation is going to further your life.

    There are methods that have been re-worked from traditional paths, into science-based methods. These tend to have a much narrower (and we might say shallower) focus, and since they are based on studies there should be some paramaters offered on input versus output: "If you do such-and-such with this regularity for this long, you can expect this result."

    But traditionally meditation is not cut and dried like this. Methods differ. The ways a method is learned, and the depth of that learning, differ. The sincerity, consistency, intensity, and openness with which the method is applied differs. The constitution and capacity of the meditator differs. The presence of supporting environment differs. So it's not possible to say that you will get a particular result in a particular time.

    Is the meditation worth doing anyway? Is it a worthy, noble use of your mind, of your time and effort?

    Even when results start to emerge, the student doesn't necessarily recognize them. For example, it's usually the case that one goes through a period of mental agitation, where there seems to be more thinking, more depression, more pettiness, more crap coming up. This phase can easily be judged as a step backward, and the meditator can slack off or give up. But actually it's usually just the process of becoming more aware, and of suppressed material percolating and being liberated. Without proper guidance, one can go very wrong in one's reactions, either giving up or (perhaps worse yet) trying to suppress the experience.

    A clear teacher is the one better able to see your progress or lack of it. A clear teacher is not hampered by doubt and speculation, much less mired in thought and reactivity. That is just one of the many very significant ways a teacher is helpful. Beyond the most beginner levels, a teacher becomes quite vital to progress, until one has learned all of the fine points of the method and becomes grounded in practice beyond the reactions of ego (and thus able to continue on one's own).

    Even Zen Master Dogen said
    >Don't think you'll necessarily be aware of your own enlightenment.

    So let's start at the beginning: among the very many possible aims of meditation, what is the best aim — what is the best result to work toward? That will begin to define what is 'working' and what is not.
