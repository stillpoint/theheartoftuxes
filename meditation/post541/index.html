    Meditation "on" things.

    There is object meditation and there is objectless meditation. Correct meditation is objectless; it doesn't presume the existence of anything outside of oneself.

    Many object-related practices exist, and they are more appropriately called concentration practice or contemplation, but they are also often grouped under the umbrella term "meditation". The crucial point is to know why you are meditating. What is it you are after; what is the aim? When that question is clear, then knowing what kind of practice to choose will be clear.

    Objectless meditation expresses faith in one's original completion. You are already complete. You are already everything. Nothing is missing. So there is no need to go "outside" and get something.

    But due to our deep attachment to the illusion of subject and object — the illusion that there is a self and a world of separate selves outside of it — it can be very difficult for many people to just start from a pure faith in their own completion. For many, first some degree of cleansing is necessary, to clear away the disturbance and sense of separation that come with attaching to objects. So it is sometimes a more skillful choice to practice with an object, until an access level is reached where meditator and object come close enough for the meditator to let go of the illusion of separation. This is the process of developing concentration. It is also called samatha meditation (calmness meditation). In some systems, the meditator will then move on to practices that focus on insight into the nature of reality; in other systems the meditator may continue deepening the concentration until a breakthrough experience is attained; and in yet other systems the meditator may take up objectless meditation as a direct expression of true nature.

    If object-style practice is in order, the object can often be chosen according to the disposition of the meditator. Some objects, like breath, are very broadly applicable and helpful for all types of people.

    Other objects, like mantras, discs of a particular color, natural elements like water or fire, visualizations of particular deities or energetic aspects, and many others, tend to address or access particular qualities within the meditator or particular aspects of truth. They can be applied to encourage particular strengths (physicality, devotion, mental clarity) or to balance certain patterns that block the meditator's clear perception (overthinking, lust, anger, etc.).

    There is also a subset of meditation that cultivates and radiates beneficial energies. While these can also train concentration, they are sometimes assigned side-by-side with concentration practices (or other meditations). Although they may be thought of as object-related, as they focus on a particular energy or motive and also initially focus on sending that energy to others (other object beings outside of oneself), when these meditations are fulfilled they do not have a subject-object quality. They radiate from within and embrace everything. The Brahma Viharas or "divine abidings" practices are like this. Loving-kindness, compassion, shared joy, or equanimity are called up within oneself and then offered to others in ever-widening circles. In some practices, one visualizes sending the motive energy to people and beings in close relationship to oneself, then moves on to neutral people, and finally to people with whom one has difficulty or negative emotion. In other styles, the energy is radiated in all directions, gradually spreading outward and including all environments and dimensions.

    If you know why you are meditating, what your aim is, then you can be guided properly. What are you hoping to get or offer? I wouldn't recommend Reddit as a good place to get advice on this. There will indeed be some good advice, but there will also be large doses of inexpert advice, advice based on shallow experience or personal opinion, and advice that sounds lovely but has nothing to do with attaining reality. It can be very difficult to know the difference, especially without a good deal of experience under your belt. Seek out reputable teachers and communities. If you like the Brahmavihara meditations, you can try them based on instruction from a good book or video. Their nature is inherently generous, so it is a bit less likely you will get caught up in trying to grasp a certain state for your own glory (although that is a common pitfall that can certainly happen with any practice).

    Good luck!
