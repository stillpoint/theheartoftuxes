    Why is mindfulness better than escapism?

    > …that seems much more effective and beneficial than what I've found….

    The fact that you have not yet plumbed the depths of such peace doesn't mean that it is not a universal and imminent reality. Your practice is simply not deep enough yet, likely in large part due to your belief in the superiority of escapism and the impossibility of peace in certain circumstances.

    To put it bluntly, you are the one keeping yourself from that peace.

    Your practice is still growing up; and your mind is easily swayed, and your heart is easily bruised. Many of us are like this. So it may be that you are not yet ready for the very tough, rigorous practice that reveals deep peace. You may need to take a gentler, more gradual path in order to maintain balance as you find your way out of depression and learn to identify your wrong thinking.

    You may need to appreciate the small steps of peace that you can take, or even the small steps toward peace when you are not finding it.

    Please remember that the appropriate attitude of a beginner is humble enthusiasm, not judgmental arrogance. Know that there is a wonderful direction to this path, and that you are moving in that direction; but don't assume that you have the same level of insight as more experienced path-travelers, much less great masters like Thich Nhat Hanh.

    Although you should investigate teachings for yourself, you should also give credit to your wise teachers and receive what they offer, and let it sink in deeply. Keep a curious sense of possibility; don't be quick to fix opinions in your mind. In fact, if you are truly on the spiritual path opinions should be considered poison. Put down all judgment; then you will create a space in your mind and heart through which authentic wisdom can emerge. Not opinion, not belief shored up by bravado, but real, grounded wisdom.

    Seeking to rule over a great teacher's advice as if you are a judge or referee is the activity of ego. It is an attempt to sidestep both the effort of finding out for yourself and the empty nature of reality itself. One seeks a firm definition and opinion so one can feel safe in the certainty. But that kind of certainty is an illusion. Seeking it is not mature, and it is not transformative.

    If you want to be transformed, you need to let the Dharma in. You need to let the profound teachings work on you. If instead you dismiss the teachings too early, simply because you can't yet conceive of them yourself, then you reject precisely the thing that can transport you to a greater view than the one you already have.

    It would be valuable to ask: How is there peace in every moment? Even if it takes your whole life to find it, it would be a most worthy effort.

    Best wishes in finding and expressing great peace moment to moment.
