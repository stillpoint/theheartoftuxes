    Who are the people telling you these things? Is the source reliable? Is the source consistent?

    I think in many cases we don't have an inkling of what we really want until we see it displayed in front of us. I used to read a lot. I had a huge library of books on all kinds of spirituality and religion. I got a lot of ideas from reading, about what I was supposed to be aiming at, and so on. Many good ideas, but ideas nonetheless.

    But when I first met an awakened being, suddenly all the teachings were there in this living, vibrant embodiment. In a single moment, the master expresses what a thousand books can't reach. Maybe its the light behind the eyes that reaches into you; maybe it's the spontaneous compassion — the action that could not possibly come from figuring and arranging — that gets beyond your words.

    To meet and witness a being of integrity is a great gift. I recommend it very enthusiatically and urgently.

    Next, it is important to have a clear and dependable source of teaching. Going around and around to various sources is confusing enough, before even factoring in the questionable basis of their authority. Different traditions, different methods, different languaging, different contexts — this is all confusing to the new practitioner.

    And worse yet, when the practitioner is confused and doesn't know that he is confused, thinking that 'more is better' with respect to gaining methods and concepts.

    So, another reason to find one reliable, living source of teaching. A traditional method has been passed down by awakened beings, and tested in practice by many thousands or millions of practitioners. A lineage master has done the practice, followed through to the end, attained the full results, trained in the fine points of passing the method to others, and has been confirmed and certified by other masters as someone qualified to teach. It is not an infallible process, but there's a lot to speak for there, a lot you can hang your hat on if you stay aware, do your research, and maintain self-responsibility.

    If you are dreading your sessions, something is wrong. It is not necessarily the wrong practice or wrong teacher (though in some cases it may be). More often it is a mistake in approach, which more direct in-person contact with teaching could remedy. But in any case, it is an indication that there is an opposition in you, something that's not going along with the agenda, for better or worse.

    Really, true meditation should be your favorite thing in the whole world; because it means you have a chance to just be completely yourself, with no struggle, no hiding, no making, no needing. So whether there's something that has to be contributed to your understanding or something that has to be adjusted in your approach, a change is required.

    > I don't have access to a teacher, nor money to pay one, so this is as close as I can get.

    That is your decision. It is your decision to think that way. If you make "I can't", then you have "I can't." If you make "I can", then you have "I can."

    What this really means is that you think of it as out of your reach, or too difficult. You are not willing to try, to see what options you have, or to travel a bit, or see a teacher less frequently, or work for it.

    Sometimes money is required, in which case you can work and save. Sometimes money is not required, in which case you can trade some work in the community or even just join certain parts of the training for free. But if it's important, it ought to be a priority; and if it's important but out of reach, you ought to set about taking steps toward it.
