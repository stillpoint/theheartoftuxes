    Opening eyes is not only not a problem in many methods, it is ideal for maintaining your groundedness. When the eyes close, the world of dream and fantasy gains a greater foothold (which is nevertheless accepted in some closed-eye meditations), and the tendency toward dullness is also greater. This leaves a beginner more at the mercy of his thinking and dreaming mind than if the eyes were kept open.

    There are many supports for your meditation. The first and foremost ones are to find a good, reliable, proven source of teaching; and to learn one proven method very well; and to apply it without wavering or tinkering. A living teacher is incalculably valuable; and a practice group can provide support in all kinds of ways, both obvious and unimagined.

    For beginners, unless there is some extremely clear aspiration or unusual capacity, I think it is very important to proceed gradually. It is better to practice well for a short period of time than to repeat and entrench vague, sloppy, or dull practice performed for long periods.

    It is also better to experience pleasant and positive effects through most of your early attempts, so that your interest and connection can deepen naturally. The path is littered with people who went out of the gate too fast, soon got discouraged or made big mistakes that harmed or confused them, and then lost their positive connection to practice.

    And finally, trying to make or get a particular experience is not an appropriate attitude for meditation. You should not be tight-minded like that, chasing after something that exists only in your imagination. You should be with the present moment as-it-is, practicing even-mindedness, openhandedness, stability, presence, concentration â€” applying these to the actual current moment rather than trying to get an imagined future situation.

    Where are you learning meditation from? Is it a good, clear source? Is it a proven, approved source tested by others who have attained the fruits of the practice (or is it only someone's best guess, or opinion, or contemporary experiment)?

    A proper source of teaching will include instructions about what to do with your eyes, how to arrange your body, how your breathing should go, what kind of environment is best, what you should do with your mind... and very importantly, *what is the aim (or aims) of the practice.
