    Mindfulness, not concentration

    It's not that you have developed too much concentration, but rather it may be that you have developed attachment to the state, attachment to stillness. Concentration itself is no problem if you are not attached to it. But this issue does come up for many people involved in concentration style practice. The mind can start to habitually focus on separate objects rather than being open to the broader situation. And that is quite contrary to the purpose, which is to go beyond object-consciousness, beyond separation.

    Since you have already demonstrated a devotion to practice, it would be best if you took the next step and found a good teacher who can instruct you in a balanced approach. Balance is not something that can be taken care of in a one-off comment on the internet; it is a matter of motivation, attitude, approach, and lifestyle.

    You seem to be involved in Theravada Vipassana teachings, so I'll offer a suggestion on that basis. One of the traditional ways of balancing concentration style practice is walking practice. You can start out with concentration-style walking, where you attend to the sensations of the feet swinging, touching the ground, and lifting again. But after a minute or two, as you walk you can let the awareness of those sensations become a background anchor rather than the foremost object in your mind.

    The feet on the ground becomes a background awareness that keeps you present, but you remain open to the scene as it is. You see just what is around you as it appears and changes; you hear sounds as they come and go; you witness smells and feelings and thought events as they appear and pass.

    This is all very relaxed. No need to do anything at all with the scene as it is. No need to check or try to 'do it better'. You just enjoy walking in a relaxed way, in the present moment, as all things come and go. This is a practice in letting things be, without withdrawing your attention to a single point.

    This then becomes a bridge between concentration practice and your everyday life. You can engage in whatever you do, whatever you feel, whatever you think, with the same kind of relaxed and broad awareness. The feet on the ground (or if you're sitting, your feet on the ground and your butt on the chair) can provide a background grounding in the present moment, but basically you just go about your day without making a big deal of anything. No need to grasp or avoid or try to make something happen.

    You can still do concentration practice if you like. Just don't attach to any results in it, and be willing to move out of stillness and emptiness. If it continues to be an issue, you may do much better to move onto a broad-minded meditation, like hwadu practice in some Zen lineages. I personally am not a big fan of concentration style. If one's motivation is not completely clear, this style can indeed be unbalanced; and in any case it is not true (or shall we say 'pure') meditation, as it focuses only on a single chosen object and thus there is still an intent and a dualism to it.

    Best of all is to find a good teacher who doesn't only teach concentration style.
