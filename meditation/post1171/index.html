    In the short term, you experience the effects of meditation as you perform it. It's up to you how you keep your mind; so if you want to be non-reactive, keep the mind that doesn't chase or avoid any object, event, or experience.

    In the long term, as a lingering effect, your experience of the results of meditation depends on whether the method is effective (is it a proven traditional method), on how well you've learned the method, on how perfectly and consistently you practice it, on whether your involvement is even-minded and non-grasping, and on the depth and intensity of your obscurations (your attachments, mental habits, and ingrained tendencies).

    For many people, life changes may appear within a month of consistent and proper practice. Just consistently applying the wrong practice, or consistently applying the right practice wrongly, will not yield benefit. Correct practice, correct application, and consistency all have to be there.

    But if you are frequently checking your practice, that in itself is self-defeating. Checking practice is not practice; it's checking. And it kicks off trains of thoughts and reactions that obscure rather than support clarity. So although it's difficult in the beginning, the meditator should as soon as possible settle into just doing it. Get into the routine of doing moment-to-moment practice without checking, without analyzing, without judging. It's a very farmer-like approach: just do the work day by day, and let the crops do their own thing.

    It can be difficult to trust the process when you're on your own. That is yet another reason why a teacher and practice group are so supportive, so necessary: they provide guidance, stability, encouragement, and living examples of attainment.

    In some respects, it gets worse before it gets better, like many healing processes. Early on in your meditation career, you get a rude awakening to just how distracted, habitual, and petty your mind can be. There can be a huge gap between your sincere aspiration and the reality of what you have to work with. In order to progress, you need to work with those things nonetheless; so it's helpful to have a very workmanlike, even approach. Just take care of the moment, and the future results will take of themselves.

    If you focus on results you depart from the practice of the moment, and you get involved in an unreal story and leave yourself prone to discouragement. Even if you enjoy positive results, fixating on them impairs the practice of the moment.

    So to sum up: you help yourself by keeping very focused on your momentary practice. Don't check or judge progress, just follow good teaching and good method.

    Occasionally, perhaps once a week or so, you might set aside a dedicated time to consider how things are going and whether you need to change the conditions of your practice. Do you need to talk with your teacher; do you need to alter your schedule or mindset; do you need to resolve or otherwise let go of a particular doubt? Having considered the issues and applied necessary changes, you are then free to practice with confidence.
