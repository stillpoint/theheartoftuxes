    For the sake of decorum and to avoid the poisons of pride and comparison, I'm not going to talk about the states I've attained other than to say that both ASMR and trance are familiar to me. ASMR is not deep trance. One might call it a psychosomatic response. Not in the sense of being somehow false, but in the sense that it involves a communication between sense, mind, and physical response.

    Trance that has pleasurable sensation (or any sensation) as its foremost feature is not 'deep'; it's still in strong association with objects. At more advanced levels, one associates less with objects and more with the awareness itself; and beyond that, with neither-perception-nor-not-perception. In early (shallow) stages there may be uncommon pleasure, the joy and bliss of stable, non-attached mind. But then this pleasure is also released. When it is released 'by half', one is said to have moved on to the next stage; and each stage is progressively released and cleansed of attachment. Profound, radical non-making and non-grasping characterize 'deep' trance. It is very plain, very silent, and flavorless, with no sense of a self who is experiencing it and no mental overlay of place and time.

    [Since OP uses the word 'deep', I talk about deep and shallow levels; but in authentic, living practice, these distinctions are done away with. One only keeps practice in the moment, without checking levels or results.]

    One of the most common errors of beginners to meditation is attachment to experience â€” attachment to particular feelings, special mental states, visions or illusions, special powers, and so on. The range of these experiences varies from common fleeting events to full-blown delusion; but the common thread is that the meditator fixates on the experience and thinks of it as important.

    But correct meditation is freedom from attachment to experience. It is finding and resting in the mind that is already complete, and doesn't need anything special. Those who attach to pleasurable feeling or go in search of unusual powers are being led about by the illusion of ego. (We distinguish here between 'spiritual' ego as the dysfunctional and illusory idea of self and the psychological meaning of the word, which points to an important managerial function of the psyche.) The ego (the idea of self) always wants more, more, more: more control, more self-aggrandizement, more drama and distraction. But this kind of egocentric attitude is the source of all suffering for the meditator and the for world at large. It is the origin of both internal and external terrorism.

    Meditation turns around that delusion and suffering, asserting the meditator's freedom from egocentric clinging. With correct meditation real life is possible, because pleasurable, unpleasurable, and neutral experiences are all fine. None has the power to sway the mind; one meets them with equanimity and awareness. And no special state or external power is necessary, because the basic mind is already complete and at peace, not depending on anything.

    Unfortunately, the internet and our new-found ability to access almost any kind of spiritual technology with a few keystrokes contribute greatly to the mistake of looking for salvation in special states and experiences. Rather than learning the proper direction of life and the proper use of method, people fumble about with the method itself, thinking it holds some key to happiness. It is like people who buy loads of fancy, exotic, expensive tools, whether they know how to use them or not, and then find that their lives are no happier and they have no greater ability to help others.

    I know a golfer who is like this, buying every contraption under the sun in hopes that it will be the magic key to glory; but he doesn't even practice golf with discipline, much less transform his life with it. This is pretty much the same dynamic: seeking some kind of magic, and failing to recognize where happiness really lies.

    When speaking of 'deep' practice, you should think more in terms of not-special. Special is not deep; it is contrived and clinging â€” though what we regard as special states and attainments (siddhis) will appear on their own down the road, and will manifest depending on the meditator's approach and karma. By that time, hopefully the meditator has clarified the proper non-attached, selfless, compassionate direction of life and practice; so her/his relationship with special attainments is utterly different from that of the clinging, agitated beginner. The special attainments then only further the meditator's compassionate activity: they are not 'for me' but 'for others'; they are not objects of attachment but tools to benefit all.
