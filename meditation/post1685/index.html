    Beginner mindfulness meditation problem.

    This is dreaming. If you engage with the dream, regardless of how the scenario plays out it will still be dreaming.

    Meditation is for waking up to reality.

    You have four helpers in this task:

    • Method
    • Posture
    • Breath
    • Ground

    If you want to practice meditation, you should learn a proven traditional method, learn it well, and apply it scrupulously without 'tweaking'. Especially if you don't have a teacher to constantly watch over your practice, you rely on the method to guide you; so you should be very clear on the method and you should apply it clearly. Then the method can help you.

    It is like medicine: not just any medicine will do for your illness; you need the right kind. And even if you find the right kind, if you experiment with dosages and ways of taking the medicine, the best you can hope for is a lucky accident. Likewise, the method can be medicine if applied as instructed within an established tradition.

    When the mind wanders from the method, if you are clear on the method you can return. If you are not clear on the method you may compromise or may not even realize when the mind wanders. But if you have a clear method, when the mind goes away you can eventually realize the fact, drop the line of distraction, and return to the method without much fuss. This is the main thing that meditation does to help you in your life. It liberates you from your own delusory thought.

    Correct posture supports your mind in relaxation and focus. The posture is also taught traditionally, but some traditions are better than others in conveying correct posture to people who haven't sat in those positions before. In many Asian cultures, people take these postures naturally, starting in childhood. Their bodies are prepared in a different way. Sometimes you need to find more nuanced instruction regarding posture.

    But when you do find your proper posture: both relaxed and upright — both at rest and alert — this will help your mind to be relaxed and upright, at rest and alert. Furthermore, when the posture either slumps or becomes too rigid you will have an indicator that your mind is becoming too lax or too rigid. Then you have a chance to return to even, balanced practice, neither grasping nor avoiding anything.

    The hand posture in particular can be a good biofeedback indicator. If you take the classic 'cosmic mudra' with palms upward on on the other and thumbs touching, forming a graceful circle, then when the thumbs collapse it is an indicator that the mind is dull or lazy, and when the thumbs press in tightly it is an indicator that the mind is tense.

    Breath is perhaps the most sensitive biofeedback machine. It follows the thinking and feeling closely. There will be many changes of breath as you go through stages of concentration. These have been categorized by yogis from ancient times. No need to try to control the breath. But if you become disconnected from the present moment, you can use the breath as a way of returning. Some methods even focus on the breath, so this connection will come by default.

    The ground is your fourth teacher, your fourth helper. When you can see the ground in front of you, you are present. When you feel the ground under you, you are present. When the ground in front of you disappears because you've been lost in thought, then you know that you've gone away. When the ground beneath you disappears because you've fallen asleep or have started to dream, then you know you've gone away. The simple, plain presence of the floor is a great helper in this way. Meditation is 'just like this'; everything as-is. You don't go to any other place. You don't try to control how things unfold. You control your mind by returning to the method, by which you stay stable as the whole universe unfolds and circulates. But the floor is just the floor. This is a very important point.
