    This all hinges on the purpose of your meditation. Realize that many activities that fall under the umbrella term "meditation" are not true meditation.

    Vagueness about one's aim in meditation is one of the leading causes of failure. Countless events follow from that one flaw. Imagine that you set off on a trip without knowing what destination you're aiming at: it would then be up to chance whether you arrive in Denver or not. You could try a million different things, and you could even skim past the suburbs of Denver at times, and you would still not know what direction to turn. But if you set out knowing that you're aiming to arrive in Denver, and you get the proper maps and the proper advice about how to get there from people who have been there before â€” then the chances of getting to Denver increase dramatically.

    It often happens, though, that even people who have been trying to meditate for a long time are not completely clear on their purpose in doing it. They may have a list of benefits they seek, and may even have a list of benefits that actually arise from their meditation, but they do not know and take to heart the ultimate destination.

    If your main aim is to rest or fall asleep, closed eyes are a good option. If the function of your meditation involves introspection into bodily processes, closed eyes may be involved. If you are trying to wake up or are training your ability to exercise wisdom and compassion in the world, open eyes become more important.

    A practice that is like watching a movie or attending a light show at a planetarium will reap results like the results you get after a movie or light show; that is to say, not much of anything other than temporary distraction. Looking at sparks of light doesn't get you much more than temporary distraction. Meditation goes in the other direction, in the direction of training your attention so it can be applied to perceiving truth. You train your mind not to waver, not to go looking for shiny objects and fancy thoughts, but to penetrate the true nature of the moment.

    Don't try to invent your own practice. If you do, it will have your own ignorance and blind spots built into it. Find a well-recommended, well-tested practice that will take you to a good destination. And as soon as possible, consult someone who has experience ferrying people to that destination.
