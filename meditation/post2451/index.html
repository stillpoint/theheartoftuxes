    Fairly new to meditation, and i have a few questions

    Visual and tactile manifestations in meditation have many possible sources. Posture, breath, flow of energy, underlying conditions, and how you use your mind all may (or may not) factor in to the situation.

    It would be best to have a teacher or other experienced and qualified guide check your posture and practice to see if things are lined up correctly.

    You say "left side of my brain". Please describe what actually happens. Does this mean something happens on the left side of the skull, or in the left eye, or something else? The left side of the brain actually communicates with the right eye.

    Next, what is the actual experience of what you call "blindness"? Does it mean you no longer see anything; or do you see something that covers up what should be in front of your eyes; or is it that your attention goes elsewhere, or...?

    It is always prudent to speak to a medical professional about this kind of thing to make sure it isn't a sign of some underlying condition (having to do with the eyes, the brain, the circulation, the blood pressure, or other issues, for example). Once a physiological condition is ruled out, then you can start to look at how you are using your body and your mind in meditation.

    >i have been using breathing to focus during meditation

    This phrase raised a flag for me. I wonder if you are manipulating your breathing and disrupting the oxygen/CO2 balance in the blood, altering the natural flow of energy, or otherwise creating an unnatural situation for yourself. Meditation should be open-handed; you mustn't chase after concentration or focus. Even if it doesn't happen through breathing control, just using your mind to try to make, grasp, hold, or control something is a perversion of the natural meditative state. But we are often not very aware of the ways in which our minds express their manipulating habit. That is why a very clear practice form is necessary, along with a very clear practice setting and a very clear practice guide or teacher: they provide the mirror that we often can't provide for ourselves; they help us see our blind spots. (In your case, this may even be literally true.)

    Next, you ask about mantras. I've written about mantra practice to another meditator; but before responding to your question I need to ask you why you want to practice mantra, and why you want to practice meditation at all.

    Your intent creates your experience. If you have grasping intent, then you get grasping result, which is suffering. If you have narrow intent, then you have narrow result, which is also suffering. Clarifying your intent in meditation and in life is all-important; this one point is the pivot of great progress or great difficulty, and it all comes from your own mind and heart. So if you want to practice mantra because you think it is more special than other practices or will get you some kind of special mind state or ability, that is wrong relationship to mantra practice.

    Mantra practice is often helpful for people who have a lot of mental activity, a lot of jabbering in the thinking mind, since that jabbering energy can be used in a focused way. You can do mantra out loud or silently. When the mind's jabbering is loud, doing mantra out loud can help you let go of the inner dialog; you help yourself with your own voice. But the inner voice is the primary one, and doing mantra silently is a practice that can be done in almost any situation.

    You could make up a mantra to use. If you use it devotedly and wholeheartedly, that's enough. But the traditional mantras each have a certain flavor, a particular quality that can be applied in an almost medicinal way. Some are very broad, directed at helping your mind be big and open, and some have more specific influence like helping you clean your speech or get rid of a particular habit. If you use a mantra with a broad and open mind, any mantra will have a broad and open effect.

    It is maybe like hiking through a forest: a pine forest has a different quality than a birch forest. Both are fresh and beautiful, they are just fresh and beautiful in different ways, and different aspects are emphasized.
