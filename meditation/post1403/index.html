    How to deal with "sinking mind"?

    Whatever the label, it is what it is. What I've written above stands: both motivation and method should be very clear.

    This is the kind of thing where seeing you face to face would give the necessary indication of your energy and quality of mind; so it would be beneficial to study with a teacher face to face, to get that immediate and precise response rather than bearing around the bush.

    It sounds like your thinking is very active, and that it's your default mode of being. When the self-image and self-experience are heavily tied up in thinking, the prospect of letting go of thinking may seem threatening. It may even seem life-threatening, in a way. Giving up the thinking for a few moments is a relief, but many of us can't conceive of that, and instead experience a dread or mistrust of the non-thinking space.

    At some level, you still believe that your thoughts are more important and valuable than your meditation practice. When it comes down to a moment of just being with the breath (or other object of focus) versus a moment of thinking, you choose thinking. Sometimes these patterns have physiological, hormonal, or psychological reasons for being there; but just as often they're there simply because we've built a strong habit of glorifying our thinking and avoiding space and silence.

    So motivation has to be clarified, so that when we come to the point where the decision has to be made -- attend to the object, or keep wandering -- we can know and feel in our heart the reason for attending to the object: why do meditation. (Why do meditation correctly?)

    And method has to be clarified so we know exactly what were supposed to be doing with our mind, if it isn't wandering around in thought. The method should be very precisely followed. Then, when the mind starts to wander off we will know very clearly, â€œOh, I'm going off. Time to return."

    If the method is sloppy, we indulge little transgressions. The mind wanders a little or tries to invent a new method depending on how we feel, and so there's no accountability. We give ourselves an 'out', and that diminishes awareness of how and when we wander off.

    The resistance to letting go of thinking, and just being simple and quiet and spacious, can easily manifest as sleepiness. You bring the mind back to the object of concentration, but the thinking mind rejects that space. The thinking mind says â€œwell, if you're going to keep moving toward the space and stillness, I'm just going to shut it all down. It's a bit like a pouting child, this kind of mind.

    So when that happens you can't give in; you have to keep waking up and keep applying the attention to the object of concentration. You have to be the one who frees you from the grip of the thinking habit. ...So you can be clear, free, living in reality rather than your thinking illusion, and so you can show up for others compassionately rather than stay stuck in your own thought patterns.
