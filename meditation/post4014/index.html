    Do the different types of meditation provide different results?

    > So no matter what technique I decide to use, it all comes down to what i'm trying to get out of it which will shape the meditation experience?

    No, I didn't say that. These things have an influence, and the technique itself has an influence. Different techniques may in some cases yield quite different results.

    But if you use mindfulness of breathing because you want to reduce stress, and you don't really believe or care that it is good for anything else, you will likely get a different result than if you firmly believe in your original enlightened nature and use the method to help reveal it. Some effects will be the same -- for instance, you may relieve stress in both cases -- and some effects will be dependent on the context.

    Now, having no plan can be a very big influence. Choosing methods according to what 'feels good' has several possible effects:

    First, rather than presenting a traditional, proven method in its purity it alters the method; important steps may be neglected. Furthermore, that alteration has a basis in ignorance -- because we all are starting from a position of relative ignorance about our own nature and the higher purposes of meditation, that ignorance gets hard-wired into one's decisions. All that follows is tainted by the ignorance. That's one of the reasons we may instead follow the guidance of a wiser person and tradition.

    Second, the decisions are made according to one's personal preference, and so like and dislike are given priority over the process. Attachment to like and dislike are binding, not liberating. Ego grows, sensitivity to spiritual issues diminishes.

    Third, good execution tends to be sabotaged. When you have a very clear method and follow it closely, you can see the times when you wander from it. When the method is vague and you make compromises about it, it is much more difficult to see when you wander off. You may feel somewhat comfortable and think it indicates success, but you may in fact just be drifting or daydreaming, or lingering in a thoughtless but dull state. There are many side avenues that the mind can travel.

    Fourth, consistency may suffer. Both because you don't identify the times when the mind wanders off and because your like and dislike get a stronger grip on you, you may end up practicing less often and less regularly.

    If a person is serious about attaining something significant through meditation, it is highly recommended that they take their own preference and inexperience (or educated but dogmatic opinion) out of the equation: they should choose a proven traditional method, learn it well, and follow it closely. They should also be guided and checked by a capable guide -- an accomplished practitioner, teacher, trained monk, or meditation master.
