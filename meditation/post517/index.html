    Can anyone else not meditate when it isint almost completely silent?

    At first it may be seen as a difficulty, but there is no difficulty. All phenomena are grist for the mill; sights, sounds, sensations can all be embraced by the meditation. The main obstacle is the thought "this is a problem".

    If you are practicing concentration-style, indeed it is ideal to have a quiet place or a place with only natural sounds. But as the practice deepens you will be able to concentrate deeply under almost any conditions. In fact, if you didn't have the challenge of dealing with extraneous sound you wouldn't be able to train the ability to deal with it. So your situation is helping you; it just doesn't feel that way because more effort is required, and you have an underlying expectation that certain things will happen in certain ways under a certain timeline.

    If you are practicing concentration-style, there are things you can do to deepen your practice so that it is not so disturbed by outer conditions. But your reason for using a particular practice should first be clear, and your understanding of the method and purpose of the practice should be clear. That will provide the confidence to continue. If you don't have clarity in these areas, you will naturally want to move on to something else when things get difficult, because you will have doubt â€” you will question the practice at the critical junctures. So first things first: build the proper foundation.

    If you are practicing "true" meditation, then everything is already no problem. Since true meditation neither chases nor rejects any object, it doesn't matter what else is going on in the meditator's surroundings. The meditator keeps to the awareness that embraces it all. Just don't move the mind. No special effort has to be made, other than to stay aware, because awareness already embraces everything that is happening. If it didn't, you wouldn't be aware of the thing (in this case, the sound) and it thus also wouldn't be an issue.

    This style of meditation has a few different methods, but the main point is keeping a relaxed, openhanded awareness. So it is different from concentration style that tries to hold on to a single object to the exclusion of all others. Clear mind/shikantaza/unchoosing awareness are examples of this second style which appear in various traditions. Hwadu, self-inquiry, and the Kuan Yin method of turning the mind back to its source are other methods that are very similar in their approach. Everything is grist for the mill; everything brings you back to the all-embracing awareness. When you hear a sound, it is the sound of your mind, the sound of your awareness. Every experience appears in your awareness, so you can trace and return to your awareness through every experience. There is no good or bad situation, only flickering phenomena, like light on a lake.

    Sound is sound. What makes it "noise" is your own idea.
