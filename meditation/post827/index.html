    That varies according to the tradition and teacher. Follow the guidance of your tradition. If you don't have one clear method and one clear source of teaching, that is the first place for you to get more 'strictness' (or more caring support).

    Breath counting is not as common in Theravada and other 'mindfulness traditions', where it could be used as merely a background anchor for general mindfulness.

    Breath counting in Zen is a concentration-style practice; so keep that in mind: with this practice you are working on developing deep and continuous concentration. In general there are two crucial aspects to breath-counting. (This instruction comes from various Zen traditions): first, each number should be 'clear and bright' in the awareness, vivid rather than vague or loosely held; and second, you should not lose count.

    The clarity of the present number relates to Wisdom; awareness of the count relates to Compassion.

    I practiced breath counting when I first started practicing Zen from a book (The Three Pillars Of Zen), and I don't remember how it dealt with the issue; but I remember that my attention was very, very focused -- like a tiger. I wouldn't allow myself to wander, even slightly. I allowed the present count to be my whole universe, and aimed at being absorbed in the count, as each number/breath rose prominently in my consciousness.

    You might try that. You are already aware that you go off in thinking. So don't go off in thinking. Don't follow trains of thought.

    After my beginnings in Zen, I met teachers and practiced the method they taught. Each method has its own integrity and makes sense within its own tradition, for its own reasons. You shouldn't think you can necessarily 'mix and match'. There is a purpose to each method; and sometimes methods are taught in a particular order -- you start with one and graduate to another.

    In conversation with one Zen Master who teaches breath counting, I found that her application is what you would call 'strict'. She says that when beginners claim they can reach 'ten' without losing count, she knows they are not applying the method correctly, because usually one's mind would wander at 'one' or 'two'... or even before 'one'. In her application, you return to 'one' when you are even momentarily distracted by thought.

    So check whether you are actually with the count, or whether you go back and forth. In society in the early 00s, the concept of multitasking became popular. It became a sought-after trait on the employment boards: "must be able to multitask." But psychologists discovered that there really is no such thing as multitasking. They say you don't do two or more things at once, rather you switch back and forth rapidly. So check that you're actually with the count seamlessly, not switching back and forth. This is what will hone profound concentration.

    And I suppose the first step is to determine the aim of the method in your tradition -- whether it's concentration-based or mindfulness-based.

    Edit: Just for the record, I disagree with those in this thread proposing (with helpful intent) variations and alterations to practice. This attitude invites doubt and encourages dithering and tinkering. The form and method are offered for good reason; if you trust the form and method, only do it, and don't invite doubt. It's also not the case that students are equipped to assess their own progress and the effect of the method. Many people nevertheless hedge and compromise on the instructions, and spend altogether too much time trying to make practice into their own image of what should be happening, rather than surrendering to practice and allowing it to reveal what is new and yet unknown.

    When you are a Zen Master, then you can alter the practice. Before then, commit yourself to whatever practice you have been given. It's not that the other proposals are inferior or incorrect in their own context. All schools of Buddhism can lead — and have led — to enlightenment; but each requires the student to complete its own path. If for good reason you feel a practice or school is not right for you, make the change with integrity; make a clean break, honor your teachers, and take up the new practice wholeheartedly and single-mindedly.
