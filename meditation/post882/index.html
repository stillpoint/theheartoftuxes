    Everything is multi-tasking. There is no single-tasking anywhere.

    Everything we think of as unified is compound; everything we think of as solitary is interrelated with all other things; everything we think of as stable is impermanent. The instructions are just useful deceptions, employing concepts as if they really existed.

    How narrow the focus or how broad the openness depends on the particular method and aim of the method. Is the purpose to develop concentration and states of absorption? Is the purpose to bring mindfulness to the nature of things and develop insight? The amount of apparent multitasking depends on the course of training.

    But from another perspective, there is no multitasking, and no person performing the task. There is only ever one thing. Your original mind is never separate from this one thing (or more accurately, this not-even-one thing).

    What you are experiencing is called prapañća in Buddhism (pra-pahn-cha, Sanskrit; Pali: papañca). It means 'proliferation'. The moment a single division appears between subject and object, a cascade of division follows, and eventually an entire universe of separate objects arises, with all the confusion and conflict it entails. (The 'ten thousand things' in Chinese philosophy.) So all the multitasking blossoms from the arising of one initial concept. If you refrain from making concepts, prapañca and multitasking don't appear.

    This is another reason why we are to follow the method quite simply and meticulously, leaving no room for doubt or speculation or messing about. We leave no gap for concept to enter. There should be no thought about oneself as the performer of the meditation. Let go of your situation, let go of your condition, let go of your relationship.

    In practical application, Bodhisattva activity is said to appear "like the way you adjust your pillow in your sleep". Only do it. When you are sleeping and you adjust your pillow, that could also be considered multitasking, as countless mental and physical processes have to come about for the pillow to be moved by your hand. But in application it is seamless and simple. No concept is made; you only do it.

    In meditation practice, usually you just have to do more meditating. Learn the method better, apply it better, throw yourself into it more completely, do it more frequently, and continue through days, weeks, and months. If it is a good method and you apply it well, you eventually unify with it.

    At first, you may be separate; and the activity of your conceptual mind may create all kinds of categories for multitasking. But really, these categories don't exist. The momentary experience of breathing is only one thing. The momentary experience of this world is only one thing.

    It is like skiing: First you think of all the things you have to do, and it's quite awkward. Then you experience moments of unification, and you begin to let go and just flow. At this intermediate stage, a new issue appears, which is that as you begin to let go of the thinking you may also let go of the awareness — things become too automatic. But eventually at a higher level, it all comes together, and you just ski. Not only is there no longer a sense of multitasking in the method, you may also experience unity between you, the skiing, the mountain, and the day.

    Meditation may initially be taught as a sort of multitasking, because there may be several, or many, important points to be respected. It doesn't necessarily reflect the nature of meditation itself.
