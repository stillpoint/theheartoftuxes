    There are many styles of meditation. Every meditation has its own form. The form is what you return to when you let go of distraction.

    Ajahn Brahm's recommended meditation (at this stage) seems to be a steady, gentle welcoming of present-moment events. You return to being with the event foremost in your attention, without clinging to it. So the effort is to use your senses to bring yourself here and now.

    Feel what the body is feeling, but don't linger on any one experience to the exclusion of others. Hear the sounds of the moment, without making any of them into a concept. And if a thought comes to the front of your attention, see it for what it is -- a present-moment experience of thinking -- and then let it go the same way you've been practicing letting go of other experiences, and move on.

    The key theme, says Ajahn, is letting go. Something appears in your awareness, you offer momentary attention to it, but you don't get caught up in it. Getting caught up makes you stuck; it keeps you with a 'thing' rather than with the flow of experience. We let go of making and holding 'things', and practice moving on to the next real, plain and simple experience.

    This non-clinging, non-stopping practice disengages the meditator from the illusory world of things. Rather than getting grabbed by a thought or 'thing', as if we were Velcro getting stuck on something, we make ourselves smooth. Nothing sticks. We always move on. Then the constancy of attention itself emerges.

    Previously, we live in a life of attachment, where the things we make with our mind grab and control us. Now we move toward a stable, smooth, non-clinging mind. We only perceive, without resisting or holding.

    So what appears in the attention is not so significant. Blue fabric, refrigerator sound, and a thought of what someone said yesterday are all equal, all just momentary phenomena. You let them appear and you let them pass.

    Your leaf-floating metaphor is apt; but this doesn't seem to be the method. That is, you shouldn't start to focus on thoughts in particular, trying to watch them float by. If you focus on thoughts in particular it shows that you are ascribing a special value to them; but really thoughts are just another part of the whole scene. So the emphasis is on always letting go and staying present.

    You bring forth your soft, open attention; and allow the events of the moment to come and go within it.

    So thought is part of the coming-and-going universe. No more or less.

    > To sum up - if I feel my mind wandering during this stage of meditation, do I interrupt them and bring myself back to the present or simply observe them?

    You simply observe, without following or generating more thought. If you start getting taken away by thought, you let it go and come back to the next present-moment experience (maybe the feeling of clothing touching your arm, or the feeling of your body pressing into the floor, or a traffic sound).

    You will work it out by doing it. Many of us want to know everything first, before we try; but traditional-style training is 'only do it'. Doing it teaches you and makes the lesson your own rather than something from the outside. So as you try, you will find out for yourself the difference between being lost in thought, on one hand, and perceiving the momentary event, on the other.

    In the beginning, it is appropriate not to know everything. If you have confusion, perceive it -- know it as thought in the moment -- then move on to the next thing. Then it is not confusion, it is clear practice.

    As you keep letting go and returning, you may find that little by little the thinking starts to diminish, and sensory events become more prominent. Even within one session you may have some strings of moments where everything is simple. You're just seeing what you see, hearing what you hear, feeling what you feel, in a very calm and simple space. This shows that the mental activity is starting to settle. No need to try to make this happen, no need to force any agenda. Stay soft, relaxed, and open in the moment. This attitude is what will help you tremendously as you advance through the other stages.

    I believe this is what Ajahn Brahm is recommending. There are other equally valid methods; but it's important not to mix them up.
