    Focusing on your breath vs. focusing on externals

    There are many possible objects of meditation. Someone skilled can use almost anything that appears — any sensory or mental object — as a focal point for concentration or contemplation. But one can't say that "anything goes"; there are a few important points to consider:

    • First, the purpose of meditation in general should be well understood. This understanding can't be taken for granted; we can't just have a vague sense or a hazy image about it. We have to be clear on this point, because when push comes to shove in our relationship with our own minds and hearts, if any doubt exists we will inevitably default to following the impulses of the ego, pursuing what we like and avoiding what we don't like rather than staying to the correct Path.

    • Second, the purpose of the specific meditation should be well understood. Each object and method has its particular effect and expected outcome, and each has specific signs of attainment. Let's also consider objectless meditation, which is more aptly called meditation, whereas object-related meditation will usually be more about concentration. What is the aim of the practice? Some objects are appropriate for practice all the way to the attainment of Nirvana; some objects can be used for advancement through several states of concentrative absorption; and some objects are fine to work with in early stages, but must be abandoned if further progress is desired.

    • Third, there are various beneficial or detrimental aspects to various objects. For concentration practice, a simple object is better, because more complex objects will encourage discursive thinking. So for example, a blue disk of uniform color may be used as a focus, whereas a disk with a fancy paisley pattern can also be used but will encourage the eye and mind to wander. There are certain objects that have positive or negative connotations or effects on one's aspiration; so meditating on the Buddha is very positive, but meditating on violent interaction (say, visualizing martial arts moves) may not be. Some objects that encourage discursive thought may be used if the aim of the meditation is to think, to analyze, to consider deeply for the sake of developing certain motivations or insights. There are many variables. A teacher who understands the variables is the best resource when choosing an object and method of meditation.

    I mentioned the difference between simple and complex objects. This is where practice with sound can sometimes be problematic; the attention may jump from sound to sound, remaining unsettled. Thus the correct method should be clear in the understanding — you should know the proper method for working with sound, and you should know why that method is used. One can think one is meditating, but the real agenda, the secret agenda, may be to avoid meditation by letting the mind wander (but in a way that seems excusable). So take care that listening to sounds (rather than listening to sound) isn't merely a new kind of self-distraction, similar to changing the channel when watching TV.

    There are several kinds of sound meditation. Before undertaking a method the purpose should be clear; so what is it that you intend? What do you intend to achieve through meditation? You can use that as the jumping-off point for your own investigation, or you can respond here.

    ~

    Edit: I should mention that in the Theravada Buddhist tradition there are forty recommended objects of meditation: ten kasinas or disks (including four elements, four colors, light, and limited space), ten types of bodily decay, ten recollections (such as recollections of Buddha, Dharma, Sangha, virtue, death, peace, and others), the perception of repulsive aspects of food, the analysis of the four elements within one's body, and the four Brahmaviharas or Divine Abidings (also called the Four Immeasurables). Besides these traditionally recommended objects from the old school, many more objects, topics, and methods exist not only in Buddhism but in many other religions, cultures, and traditions.
