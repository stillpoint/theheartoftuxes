    That surprise is not a bad thing. If it's too upsetting, change the clock, change the alarm sound. But the alarm has a profound meaning: wake up!

    In the moment you are shocked by the sound, your mind is nowhere. This is true meditation. If you can grasp that mind, you can get a glimpse of it, a glimpse of your true nature.

    In the moments after the shock, you may be disoriented. This is shock and confusion: the mind that originally is nowhere now seeks a place, seeks something to hold onto.

    So in the couple of instants during and after the ringing of the alarm, you can — if you are watchful — see the whole situation, the whole difference and distance between your true clear and open nature, and your mind's grasping for objects and its lostness because of it.

    Some people don't move in the slightest when a sudden alarm rings, or a car backfires, or a door slams. Why is this? They are grounded; their mind is stable, not attaching to sights and sounds and thoughts. So very few things (if any) have the power to unbalance them. This is something you can consider and aim for in practice: to be so settled and present that nothing has the power to move your mind. The mind has become smooth and non-attached, so nothing can grip it. (Edit: It should also be noted that some people don't react because they're very dull.)

    The shock at the end of your practice may also be a valuable signal that the practice itself is not correct. Have you learned a proper practice; do you know what you should be doing with your mind? If the practice is dull and sleepy — if it retreats from real life — then of course, waking up will be a shock. And if the practice is agitated and forced, again the alarm will be an intrusion rather than simply part of the landscape of coming-and-going perceptions.

    Many people only follow their own habit and preference when inventing their own 'meditation': they like to veg out, they like nicey-nice feeling, they like to avoid seeing their own faults; and so they decide to withdraw and have a little nap... but sitting up in a posture that looks spiritual. Correct practice brings habit and preference into the light rather than indulging in them. So it's very important to get proper teaching in a proper, proven, traditional method, and to follow a set form by which you can identify the moments when you depart and fall into habit and preference.

    But having said all this, if you are repeatedly agitated, that's not healthy for mind or body. You can get a dedicated meditation timer. You can use a different alarm tone on your phone. You can use a recording that ends meditation at a set time (there are some that have only silence for a set duration, and then a bell at the end). You can use a short stick of incense set up in a bowl of ash — when the stick burns down to the ash, a different, somewhat acrid smell appears for a few seconds (if you're aware enough to notice it). u/macjoven has already helpfully listed most of these alternatives.

    And if you're doing prostrations or mantras, you can just choose the number of repetitions that can be done within the time frame. For example, 108 prostrations done rapidly take about 10-12 minutes. And how long a mala-string worth of mantras take depends on the length of the mantra. You can also use a mala to count breaths in the same way, to a set duration.
