    I find it extremely difficult to remember to be mindful during the day

    >After so many years of allowing those discursive thoughts to run wild, it's extremely hard to break the habit.

    That's the point right there. It's quite appropriate that you have difficulty, because your habit has built up over many years. So you need to establish a new habit. It will take some time and effort before the new practice habit can compete on the same level as the old one.

    But it's very important that you not check and judge your practice. Don't make the new habit of comparing, analyzing, stepping out of your experience and labeling it good or bad. You don't know how much progress you are making. Your efforts are seeds -- they have not yet come to fruition. So you can't judge whether you're doing well or poorly; you can only decide to keep planting good seeds or to give up.

    Checking your practice and focusing on results will lead to disappointment and giving up. Only keep your mind well in the moment.

    The correct relationship with mindfulness is to do your best. It is the nature of the mind to wander -- to be aware of new vistas, to consider new possibilities, and to dream freely. That is appropriate. It's not the thinking that is a problem to us, but rather our attachment to thinking. When we attach, our thinking kidnaps us, and we wander around with it. When we are not attached, our thinking can wander but our awareness has a strong center that doesn't move with the thinking. This center is what you need to train and strengthen; so thousands of letting-go moments are necessary.

    You will lose yourself throughout the day. Never mind that; only try sincerely, and when you go away, quickly return. Return without checking or judging, just drop it and come back to the moment. This will strengthen your ability to return.

    If you try to force concentration, two things will happen: (A) you will develop a manipulative habit, and (B) you will not be able to attend to the movements of the situation because you will be trying to keep your mind in a fixed state. So focus instead on momentary returning. Sometimes your mind may wander for a few minutes, and sometimes you may go away for hours or even days. Never mind. Just, the moment you realize it, return without making a big deal of it. Don't cry over spilt milk, just instantly return to the moment. This will train your ability to return, and your center will become stronger.

    In order to assist your mind I highly recommend using a body-based mindfulness practice. The body has a tremendous grounding influence on the mind. Just consider the difficulty of staying mindful in your (disembodied) sleep, and you'll get an idea of the grounding influence offered by the body.

    Some people use breathing as an anchor throughout the day, and that may work for many but it also tends to lead to a manipulative habit where you try to control your state through your breathing. I instead recommend awareness of your foundation, your interface with gravity: When standing or walking, keep some awareness in the feet on the floor; when sitting, keep awareness in the butt and feet; when lying down, keep awareness in your body's areas of contact with the bed or sofa.

    Keeping your mind in your feet (or other foundation) will provide you with a lifeline to the moment, helping you find your way back more easily as well as stay in the moment more persistently. It also has a calming, grounding effect on you overall, as your energy comes down from buzzing around in your head and settles lower in your body. In a sense, the effort of practice can be seen as an effort to settle the energy down, and not be so busy and reactive with the head and heart.

    There are many possible styles of mindfulness practice, just as there are many styles of meditation. The main thing is to return to the present moment. I believe mindfulness of the feet will help you settle into the moment without struggling as much. Keep 20 or 30 percent of your awareness in your feet, and you will be able to attend to most of your activities. If you sometimes find that you need to harness all of your attention for a task, then just do the task completely and wholeheartedly -- that is already correct practice! -- and then return to mindfulness of the feet when you can. Either way, don't check, only do it.

    Good luck, and thank you for your effort.
