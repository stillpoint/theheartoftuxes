    You have to learn a proper, proven traditional method; you have to learn it well; and you have to apply it meticulously. Guessing at the method opens the door for this and many other issues.

    What is your aim in practicing meditation? And then, what is your deeper, more complete, more luminous aim? Clarifying motivation helps to wake you up; it brings forth energy and other resources. It can take meditation from something that you just think might be a good idea to something that is important to you in a vital, heartfelt sense. This can cut off huge masses of dream and illusion.

    Then, if you are using introspection to gradually clarify your motivation, and if you are learning an effective method, the application becomes the main issue. Moment to moment you must return to the method. The moment you see that thoughts or images have taken you away, you return. You may repeat this letting go and returning many thousands of times; but each time is just one simple, clean returning. Just now, return to clarity.

    Relinquishing distraction and returning to your task is called concentration. If you practice a correct method, and practice it correctly, concentration will develop. Then your meditation will become more continuous, leaving fewer spaces for distraction to enter. The aim is to have an unbroken stream of attention.

    Along the way there are many lessons to be learned. It is much better if you have one experienced, wise, and compassionate guide within one tradition to guide you. Flitting about here and there, drawing speculations based on things you hear from various sources is a process of confusion. You don't really know whether the things you hear are correct; and even if they are correct, you don't know how they apply in the context of your practice (i.e., they may be correct within a different context of training; and the difference may not be apparent).

    A large number of people trying 'meditation' don't actually know what meditation is, or what they are supposed to be doing with their bodies and their minds. They are improvising as they go along. While some things have to be learned by doing them yourself, there's an insidious arrogance to our modern culture that assumes the individual can make his/her own way and heroically attain whatever is there to be attained.

    This arrogance is really ignorance of our interdependence. We are divorced from a sense of gratitude and a recognition that our achievement depends on all kinds of support from others — from the support of objects like houses and chairs and computers, to the support of life through food and water and air, to the support of our emotional and intellectual growth through relationships with others. Maturing in spiritual practice means recognizing interdependence, which is another facet of cause and effect: if you want a good effect in your meditation, you have to have good causes. This includes proper, clear teaching from a reliable source — teaching that is not blocked or disrupted by an onslaught of other inauthentic or opinionated messages.

    Cause and effect govern meditation practice. If you have confusing, drifting effect, then there are confusing, drifting causes producing that effect. The question is what those causes are: do they have to do with method, or understanding, or motivation... do they have to do with lifestyle influences, what you do with your mind throughout the day, or environment...?

    In the most simplistic view, at some level you are indulging your daydreaming. That is, instead of performing the method, you allow yourself to daydream. You buy into it, deciding that it is more important than the meditation. This happens from moment to moment. Sometimes the daydream can be seen as an illusion, and easily given up. Then it's relatively easy to return to the meditation method. But sometimes the content (or relaxed drifting feeling) of the daydream grabs you; it feels more significant, either 'important' or pleasurable or dramatic — at some point you decide that the daydream is more real and important than being clear and present.

    It is our human habit: we believe in our thoughts more than we believe in trusting the space that's there when we let go of them. Meditation practice is there to turn around that habit of delusion.

    Maybe realizing this simple point is enough to help you take one more step. But if not, you (or ideally, you and your teacher) have to look into the causes for the unclarity. Why do you trust the daydream more than you trust your basic self?

    As I said above, the causes can be any combination of poor training, poor understanding of method, unclear motivation, disturbing lifestyle influences (unbalanced diet, consumption of trivial or violent entertainment, poor sleep, lack of physical movement, shallow relationships, etc.), or other physical or mental issues.
