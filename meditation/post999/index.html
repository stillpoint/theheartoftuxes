    This is why traditional training includes much more than meditation. Meditation is only part of a complete training that includes socialization, character development, ethical training, physical and mental development, working for and with others, maintaining mindfulness in all kinds of activities, and so on.

    It's not that you're doing something wrong. It's that the growing sensitivity also requires groundedness and depth to go with it — the depth that allows you to digest all those energies rather than be swayed by them or avoid them.

    We inevitably need an external source of wisdom and an external structure to develop this depth. Left to our own resources, we do either what we find reasonable or fall back on habit, both of which miss what we don't yet know and what we have not yet attained. That is, our own ignorance, blind spots, and faults get built into the practice that we envision.

    As Frank Zappa said,

    >Without deviation from the norm, progress is not possible.

    We almost inevitably proceed on the basis of what we already know and do. The point of growing in depth is to proceed on the basis of what you don't yet know and do — to practice being what you are not... yet. And this must be done with unfailing wisdom. That is why it is important to connect with a teacher who can consistently hold the view beyond ego. Even if the teacher hasn't finished the work, she/he should be consistently able to go beyond self-concern and convey the true teaching.

    What you write is a very good description of what so many meditators and seekers go through. In our hope of attaining the light, we distance ourselves from the more coarse parts of our being and create a shadow. Instead of becoming broader, we become narrower. It is difficult when others fail to understand the process, as we get called out on our sensitivity: "I thought you were a meditator! What good is it doing you?" For a time, the more we progress, the more we move away from common (petty) ways of relating and mundane concerns. Not only does it move us to retreat from what we experience as increasing pressure of life, it can also threaten and distance others.

    So compassion and the ability to digest all kinds of experiences have to grow in balance with sensitivity. The bottom line is often that we have to go into territory we'd prefer not to. Occasional retreat is a benefit; but the general thrust of practice is engagement and embrace... and the courage to take on more and more. It is difficult (or unless you are an already exceptional being, for all intents and purposes impossible) to do this on your own without guidance and external support.
