    Samadhi is very good. You just need good teaching and good practice. Choose any of the many paths that teach concentration. Even other styles besides object-focused concentration require concentration and eventually develop samadhi. You can consider it something that will happen when you train well; it's not exclusive to one particular path.

    Different paths may name samadhi differently. Hindu yoga, for instance, may switch the meanings of 'samadhi' and 'dhyana' from the way they're used in Buddhism. The words point to the same attainments, they're just used in particular ways depending on the path you're involved in.

    Samadhi is subject-object unity that is no longer effortful. It is a kind of timeless grace that befalls you when you are both very concentrated and relaxed/non-grasping. Since it is beyond effort you can't push to make it happen, you just have to learn a path very well and apply yourself to it wholeheartedly. If you get good teaching and commit yourself to it, samadhi will appear.

    I don't recommend reading much about samadhi until it starts to appear in your practice. Then your teacher may give teachings or assign study. Making it into an object of striving only complicates and hinders the process of its arrival. The most important thing for you is to get good teaching and create the foundation of practice. Then samadhi will appear. If you get a lot of ideas in your head unconnected to any real experience it will only make problems.

    Everything in its right time. Beginning time means take care of beginning things. Everyone wants to jump to 'expert', but that's a misunderstanding of the path. The path is not 'this versus that'. Expert is not better than beginner. You are just where you are. Each phase has its correct relationship; and if you take care of your phase completely that's already essentially the same as samadhi. But if you don't take care of beginning things at beginning time you will have no basis for progress, including samadhi.
