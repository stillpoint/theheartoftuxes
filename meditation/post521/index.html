    Can I Substitute Focusing on Breathing

    It depends on your intended result. There are many possible objects that can be used to train the concentration. There is variation, though, in which objects can also train insight and lead to awakening. There is further variation as to the suitability of the objects in promoting a non-grasping mind. Some objects, for instance, will encourage attachment (because they are pleasant), others will be overly stimulating or agitating, and others yet will encourage aversion, withdrawal, or negative mind states.

    Some objects (like colored discs) depend on a special set-up; others (like particular sounds) depend on a particular convergence of conditions. Some, though, are available at all times and in all situations. (It should also be noted that some objectless meditations don't even depend on having a body.)

    A further variation exists in the objects' appropriateness for your particular bent of nature (your personal psychological type).

    Of all of these possibilities, the breath is considered a neutral (not attachment-inducing), beneficial object that can lead to awakening, that is very accessible, and that is suitable for almost all psychological types.

    What is it you hope to attain through meditation? This is an important factor in choosing your method. You may not be aware of the things that are possible to attain and worthy of attaining, and that's where a teacher is very helpful. A good teacher can guide your practice until you have your own clear direction.

    10 days is not nearly enough to go on in deciding to change! You won't know whether the method is for you until you make much more consistent effort and go much deeper in the meditation. “It's hard" and “I don't like it" aren't satisfactory reasons for changing. In fact, getting pulled around by your likes and dislikes is precisely what meditation is meant to free you from.

    It may not be that the breath is an inherently difficult object, but rather that your restless monkey-mind habit is deep (as it is with all of us). The cure for that isn't to then jump from method to method when discomfort or difficulty arise; the cure is to devote yourself to the method no matter what arises. This is the expression of your freedom. It feels like being trapped, but it is only the ego, struggling for dominance, that doesn't like it. In actuality, staying with the method is already an expression of your enlightened mind. It is Bodhisattva activity.

    It is possible to change your object of meditation, but you should be clear on why. Without knowing your aim and your situation, we can't advise you on the best method for you. Random, hit-and-miss advice is to be avoided if you are not just playing at meditation.
