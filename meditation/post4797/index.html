    Addicted to Meditation?

    Thank you very much for your reply. I feel the warmth in it.

    "How dangerous" is a completely different question, one that I wasn't addressing in my post. But it's a good question.

    The risk varies with strength of intent, repetition, and associated conditions.

    We also take into account the ultimate and best aim of meditation (and indeed, of our life's activity). You have some awareness of that issue, since you are sensitive to how one might waste time with movies, games, or more harmful activities.

    Any intent to escape reality harms the wholistic nature of the organism, requiring it to try not to be where it is. The mechanism (drugs… Netflix… meditation…) is a separate issue. If that intent to escape is very strong, then very strong separating forces are placed on oneself. If the intent is not so strong, the separation from reality is not so drastic.

    Where the mechanism makes a big difference is in the associated conditions. In meditation, presumably you are in a quiet, contained environment where not many negative influences are likely to enter (the negative influences instead come from how you use your mind). By contrast, with certain drugs, there are the possibilities of physical or mental harm, interactions with criminals, or doing further bad action due to impaired judgment; these are the associated conditions that are more harmful.

    The next variable is repetition. A mild habit repeated many times becomes ingrained into the character and into the person's mindset and view. A strong intent to escape reality, repeated many times, ventures into the territory of mental illness.

    So in meditation, the benefit or harm mostly depends on how one uses one's mind. It is possible for meditation to be sincere but weak in effect. It is possible for meditation to be somewhat grasping but weak (poor concentration, say). And it is possible for meditation to be very strongly grasping and strongly concentrated. The latter will be more harmful to the mind. You are right, that the more serious patterns tend to manifest among somewhat advanced meditators, because those are the ones who (A) have shown more persistent determination and (B) who can actually reliably achieve the more blissful or detached states of concentration that can prompt attachment.

    That is not to say there will be no benefit. It's rarely all good or all bad. In fact, it is possible that the meditator will eventually have an experience of effortless, unified concentration that shows him the error of his attachment. But the habit of attachment will then still need to be purified.

    The dangers to the psyche are variable, and debatable. As je3donut said, what seems desirable to the meditator may seem dysfunctional to others, and vice versa. Also within the meditator's milieu — in the yoga or zen center, say — you'll commonly find "dysfunctional" meditators who can attain rarefied states on their own, but who can't get along with others or who act with arrogance rather than compassion and humility. This is sometimes traced to the attachment or addiction in question — what they see as their right to their meditative experience, and a habitual shutting-out of other stimuli, situations, people, and concerns.

    Bliss is indeed an aim of some meditative traditions. This is only a worthy, noble aim if one understands and embodies the all-embracing nature of that bliss. When it becomes my bliss, "because I like it and screw anyone who gets in my way", well, that falls short of a worthy endeavor. Any repetition of self-centered, grasping activity strengthens the illusion of self, and strengthens its grasping.

    Full awakening is not an easy thing. If it were, there would be many awakened beings; but the opposite is true, awakened beings are rare and precious. If the instruction is off the mark, if the motivation is faulty, if the effort is less than complete, or if the execution is flawed, awakening will remain far away. This most, most valuable of all human achievements is lost to one who has this incorrect relationship with meditation. If the meditator doesn't receive warning from those who could offer it, then he goes farther off the path with each session, ingraining the bad habit that is his own undoing in terms of awakening.

    From one perspective, it could seem like a harmless thing. There seems to be little current damage, and much benefit in terms of calmness, pleasure, focus, avoidance of negative activities, and so on. From the perspective of final awakening, though, it could be a calamitous, life-altering error. But one who has not considered awakening as the real fulfillment of meditation would not think so.
