    I responded recently to a question about 'meditating on an issue' here.

    It's okay if you perceive a 'you' doing a contemplation. The function is different: to refine your conception, as opposed to gaining direct insight or other meditative aims. Of course, the aims overlap and blend, and one may find oneself experiencing direct insight or emptiness while contemplating; but there no need to check your state of mind. Only look into the subject matter sincerely, and make it personal. That is, bring it from the realm of scholastic discourse to a very personal experience that sinks into you deeply.

    When contemplating impermanence and death, for instance, you can consider the ways you've already come close to death. You can consider the illnesses and vulnerabilities that are already present in your life, which could very well be the seeds of your ultimate death. In most cases it's okay to improvise in order to create a deeper, more real connection with the subject matter.

    When it's time for meditation, let the thinking go and just follow the method.

    The Jhanas are not the be-all and end-all. Clarity and conception must grow together. Wisdom and compassion must grow together. If you are serious enough to be thinking of progressing through the Jhanas, it's high time you find a living teacher and receive guidance about these kinds of questions, because it is extremely important to know how to use the Jhanas. (I.e., to establish great compassionate motivation as the basis of your spiritual activity, rather than allowing grasping and ignorance to insinuate themselves, as they will do automatically if you don't have the proper training.)

    Just getting a state of mind doesn't mean you understand the path. The best teaching trains your life direction first, so you know the proper function of everything that arises for you. With correct direction you can use both positive and negative experiences as the path. Without correct direction, even attaining Jhanas can become a problem.
