    TheHeartofTuxes

    A great many practitioners get trapped trying to be perfect and denying their imperfection. It's not the trying for perfection that is wrong, it's the denial. This is called Spiritual Bypassing: using spirituality to deny or avoid realities and responsibilities.

    So from this perspective, return to your humanity can signal the beginning of a more mature phase of practice, a more inclusive compassion that doesn't depend on an image of perfection, but engages with this beautiful mess right here, as it is.

    But on the other hand, being human and being pure love are not necessarily the same thing; and quite often in the history of the world they have been diametrically opposed.

    You may feel you're onto something, and it may be true; but as soon as it coagulates into a philosophy you go off the mark. It becomes concept and attachment rather than flowing reality.

    It's also possible that the fear of loneliness may be behind this entire movement, and it's only justified by a personal philosophy. Fear and avoidance can set into motion all kinds of ecstatic dramas. The more urgent the avoidance, the more wonderfully blissful the new 'realization'. Fear of loneliness. Fear of commitment. Fear of dissolution. Fear of loss of self. Fear of change. Fear of not knowing. Epic personal stories and grand philosophies arise, unconsciously, to cover those fears.

    Just when you think you've finished, when you think you've finally 'got' something to go on, that could be the most dangerous time, the time when you fool yourself the most. Commitment to one traditional path of training offers safeguards against these kinds of self-deception.

    Gaining insight and dignity often sets us apart from others, and turns away those who want to indulge in more petty views and activities. We may find ourselves more alone as we progress. Until we find our community of people working toward more lofty aims, that is. Further down the path, the blossoming of love mitigates loneliness.
