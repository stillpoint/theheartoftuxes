    people who left vipassana retreat

    Well, the first point is that it's not necessary to check other people's stories. Letting the attention glom on to someone else's drama or failure essentially amounts to giving up on a retreat yourself. You give up your practice and leave your practice space, following external distraction.

    Our practice doesn't end just because the 10th day of retreat has passed. The purpose of retreat is to help us carry our clear mind through daily life. So we still need to let go of speculation, analysis, the gossiping tendency of our thinking — the same distractions that appear during meditation — and return to our proper job, which is to deal with our own situation clearly.

    I am not yet enlightened. I have not yet finished my life's retreat. I am drawn to mess around with someone else's situation rather than being with this empty, ungraspable situation right here.

    Other people's karma is not mine. I need to clarify this life, here.

    Having said all that, I can share a bit about the urge to leave. I've done a lot of retreats, and have experienced many, many, countless thoughts and urges to leave. Sometimes the justifications have seemed so reasonable; sometimes I've known right away they were trivial, and beside the point. But they all amount to agitation of mind, so if the meditation method is clear the meditator can easily identify all of these thoughts as a diversion from practice. Without indulging in thought, none of this is a problem.

    I suppose I've been very fortunate to receive good teaching that sank in: "Bring all blames to one place." Every problem in life has only one problem at its root: we don't understand our true self. So we should lay the blame where it belongs, with this root problem. Whatever else we may blame ("they didn't serve eggs"), the root issue is our discomfort with reality, our discomfort with emptiness. This discomfort comes from (and is) ignorance — ignorance about the true nature of self — and the only cure is waking up. So, you stay at retreat because you recognize the real culprit.

    The time I came closest to leaving retreat was early in my meditation career, after I experienced genuine samadhi on maybe the 7th day of what happened to be a 10-day Vipassana retreat. The experience was so liberating, so comfortable! Hours of... not even being aware of... space. Coming out of it, the leg and body pain I had been enduring had melted away magically; my mind was so very still; my body was so very light. I thought that I had gotten everything I needed. No need to continue retreat, I thought, I can just keep this effortless post-samadhi state everywhere in my life. Everything is equal; everything is light.

    And then I remembered once being chastised for leaving dishes unwashed in the kitchen. I thought I was free, unattached to clean or dirty; but my roommate yelled at me "you're not free; you just don't want to do it!" And even though it had happened years previously, that experience arose in my memory and I heard that yelling in relationship to my thoughts about leaving retreat: "You're not free! You just don't want to do it!" So I stayed. And of course, soon the after-effects of samadhi dissipated, and the pain and difficulty re-appeared, and I had a new perspective of the path that lay ahead and the dedication required to maintain clarity throughout both blissful times and difficult ones.

    ~

    We should also perhaps note that there may sometimes be very good and valid reasons for leaving a retreat. One should stay at the helm; although one may surrender to a method and even to a guru, one's self-responsibility doesn't disappear. If the situation is manipulative or abusive or needlessly dangerous, there is no need to stick it out just to prove one's dedication. The real surrender is surrender to the situation; we need to stay responsive, and take appropriate action, moment to moment, case by case.
