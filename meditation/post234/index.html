    Weed effects and Meditation

    Good for you for finding out. Yes, it's a bit beside the point. Meditation is not about going to the extreme and making everything 'amazing', but rather having equanimity -- having a balanced, open mind with things-as-they-are.

    It is not helpful for us or others if we pursue and grasp and cling to and drum up indulgence in what we like. That only strengthens our attachment to what we like, making us less available for reality. The point of meditation is to get clear with the real moment as-it-is, so we can show up in situations whether we like them or dislike them, and so that we can show up for each other -- for all other people, animals, spirits, even the grass and sky and rivers and land.

    We learn to show up in all situations, without adding our own preference or idea. Sure, we can have preference; but we learn not to let it control us. Then we can offer ourselves clearly and appropriately to each moment. This is the interplay of wisdom and compassion: helping the world by showing up clearly in the moment, neither adding our own wants and opinions nor subtracting our participation and attention.

    There are indeed streams of spiritual training (not only meditation, but complete life training) that focus on the senses in a very engaged, lush way. Tantrayana in Tibetan Buddhism is like this, very involved in seeing, hearing, tasting, etc. But these paths don't grasp at experience or try to make it somehow different, make it 'more'.

    Also, many methods of meditation use one or many sense objects as a center of focus. Some work with visual objects like a candle flame or colored disk; others may work with sound or physical sensation. Furthermore, mindfulness practice, which is a more open awareness of what's going on from moment to moment throughout the day, will in most cases involve awareness of senses as either a basis or as part of the whole.

    In all of these practices, open-handed attitude is important. Drilling down strongly and trying to get certain spectacular experiences is not right practice; it expects a particular result and thus discounts reality as it is in the moment. Our task is to have the courage and balance to face things without knowing, without expecting, without picking and choosing. This aware letting-be is the great gift in your life and practice.

    Just consider how much discord, chaos, and violence throughout history is driven by people holding on to their opinions and desires. Everyone thinks they are right. We habitually think our way is right; and likewise, fanatical terrorists from ISIS think that they are right. May we stop imposing our wants and opinions on the world!

    But when you can completely let it all be, when you can completely put down your opinion, then you can have correct opinion, which is the world moving through you. Then your activity can be compassionate and wise, according with things-as-they-are and helping all beings.

    So you may practice engagement with the senses, but don't assume that any of it is more real than anything else; you must remain open-handed. You must develop equanimity.

    The famous Zen text Hsin Hsin Ming (Faith In Mind) opens with these lines:

    >The Great Way is not difficult for those who don't pick and choose.

    Good luck!
