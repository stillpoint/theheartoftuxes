    Flexibility training should go hand-in-hand with strength training. If only flexibility is trained, the joints can lose the tone that protects them. Without that integrity, yes you may get loose enough to do the splits, but you could also get easily injured (even by just sitting).

    The flexibility and strength trainings themselves should also be balanced (between front and back sets of muscles, for instance).

    One classic solution to tight hips in a cross-legged sitting posture is to sit on as firm a cushion as possible, and pile cushions under the knees until they're supported. Then remove enough cushion height to allow the knees to be just a little less supported. This is supposed to gently stretch the groin and thigh muscles. Over time, more and more cushions are removed from under the knees, and then the cushion you sit on is also gradually lowered until eventually you are able to sit comfortably with no support at all.

    This method takes a lot of fortitude and consistency, and it can be difficult for those who need to see constant progress in order to avoid discouragement. It may be more viable in a situation where you are fully committed to daily or twice daily sittings (like either living with a meditation group or very committed to practice on your own).

    There are many hip-opening exercises you can try, but it might go better for you if you got direct instruction from a teacher rather than slap-dash tips from random people online.

    Some poses that might help:

    • Sitting on the floor or mate with knees in a butterfly shape, soles of the feet pressed together. Don't bounce the legs (which creates a tension response) but let the weight of the legs do the stretching.

    • Dove/Pigeon pose

    • Cobra pose

    • Lunge poses like the Warrior pose. These can be performed while you hold a wall or pole for support. The foot can be held straight and also turned out at right angles to stretch a different set of muscles and tendons, but if turned out, the thigh muscles should be held with tone to support the knee.

    • Sitting on the floor with feet flat on the floor in front of you so the knees form upward-pointing angles, let the legs sink to one side (all the way to the floor if possible) then raise them back and let them fall to the other side. Breathe smoothly and continuously, without holds or hitches in the breath.

    There are whole sets of hip-opening poses in all the body-yoga schools. A yoga teacher should be able to help you if you tell him/her that you want to be able to sit in meditation posture.

    Discovering what constitutes proper posture can be key, since the hips didn't develop that extreme tightness for no reason: they have been tensing in compensation for poor use of the body. Building the core muscles will also help to relieve the hips from having to be tense to support you. When the upper body slumps, the hips have to pull toward the legs to keep you sitting upright; but proper posture puts the weight on the skeletal structure, allowing the muscles to relax into a natural tone.

    You may also be interested in some bodywork modalities like Structural Integration/Rolfing, Deep Bodywork, and Thai Massage.
