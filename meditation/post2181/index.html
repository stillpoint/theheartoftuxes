    Edit: I thought it might be helpful to add some more guidance to what I wrote above. What you describe is a matter of focus: is the focus narrow, on one task or object, or is it broad and inclusive of the environment?

    Just to set a context: in meditation, one-pointed clarity represents the wisdom side of training; and broad awareness represents the compassion side. The former moves toward pure one-mind, with no subject-object division. The latter moves toward functional relationship, where you can be with the conventional world of illusory objects without losing yourself, and you can offer your presence and kindness to it.

    Some people are more apt to go deeply into engagement with a single object. Either because of a talent for concentration or because of a strong clinging to objects (as with addictive personality), they tend to go 'into the zone' more readily. This is why a teacher has to be careful with their instruction and prescribed practice: some people when given a mantra practice, for instance, will go into it and lose awareness of their surroundings, perhaps endangering themselves and others if they do it while driving or walking in the city. (The other option with mantra is to use the practice merely as an anchor to the broader moment.)

    If you want to keep some broader awareness while engaging in concentrated work, there are practices available to you. Perhaps the purest is to keep Unknowing at all times and in all situations: don't make anything, even the notion of a self that is going about its life activities.

    But a more accessible practice for many will have a clear supportive object. I recommend awareness of your physical base. It provides a clear object to return to, and it can function in the background of other activities without intruding. The physicality keeps you in contact with the broader scene, and has the great benefit of grounding you in the body, which balances our modern tendency to live up in the head. (This will be especially valuable for those who work sedentary jobs and use electronic 'thinking machines' much of the time.)

    In this practice, you maintain a background awareness of your physical contact with support: when standing or walking you feel your feet on the ground; when sitting you feel your feet on the floor and butt and arms contacting the chair; when lying or reclining you feel your body's connection with the bed, sofa, or floor. (When swimming, you feel the support of the water all around you.)

    This is easy to return to. Gravity helps you. When you are tense you can use the practice to release the tension down into the earth. The practice provides a settled, rooted quality to your mind and heart. It doesn't interfere with most (if any) activities. And it can go on in the background. (Just to give a feel for it we could say it uses, say, 5% - 30% of your attention.)

    And at times when you want to practice a more focused concentration-style practice, it can easily shift to one-pointed attention at the feet (80%), as in some traditional walking meditations.

    It may take a little effort to keep returning to awareness of the physical base at first, but it will soon become fairly effortless. If you find even this practice too intrusive, you can just use a repeating cue like a 10-minute bell or vibrating alarm on your phone or watch to bring you back to broader awareness throughout the day. This is also a good practice.
