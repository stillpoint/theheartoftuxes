    Not getting back to where you were before is correct. Movement is the nature of life.

    Before, when you were meditating happily, you weren't "trying to get back to where you were before"; you were just meditating. Also, it was new to you, so your mind was occupied in a different way. And furthermore, it sounds like there may also be some event or circumstance in your life that has influenced your emotions, thinking, concentration, etc.

    Meditation is not about getting somewhere else, much less going backwards in experience or going back in time. It's about being where you are. You are approaching meditation with a grasping, manipulative mindset, already determined that the experience must be a certain way rather than opening to what is.

    My suspicion is that what is may include some things you would rather not be aware of -- perhaps certain feelings that have not been able to move through you, because you've been avoiding or suppressing their expression.

    So you need proper openhanded approach, not to pick and choose what your experience should be. It's not for you to try to control how the universe unfolds. It's for you to witness, and practice non-attachment, which is stability. In other words, keep the mind that doesn't move, rather than expecting things to go as you prefer. Don't touch your mind; don't touch the movement of things.

    In order to apply the correct openhanded approach, you need correct faith in the process. You need to understand why correct meditation is openhanded. If you don't understand and take to heart that point, you will not apply it; your habit and preference will take over.

    Therefore:

    • Choose one clear, proven traditional method
    • Learn it from a capable, verified, living source
    • Learn it completely, both the why and the how
    • Apply it meticulously, without tweaking the method or manipulating your experience

    If you are 'stuck' mentally or emotionally, sitting practice may not be the first and best remedy. For those somewhat stable in practice, the instruction to "just sit with it" can be the right one; but for those unable to even begin to practice because of agitation, frustration, etc., other courses of action may be required.

    Physical practice is very helpful in these cases, as well as being a very, very good and important companion to sitting practice, and a complete practice in its own right. Physical practice is a good remedy for the pattern of energy getting stuck up in the head (overthinking or stuck thoughts) or stuck in the heart (overwhelmed by feeling or stuck feeling). These patterns comprise much of life in modern society, so physical practice is a very good, very helpful part of life practice.

    Prostrations, sun salutations in Yoga, stance practice or basic movements in Taiji, free dance, or even rhythmic whole-body-and-breath activities like running, cycling, rowing, or swimming, can help the energy flow and settle. Prostrations in particular are exceptional for quickly bringing the energy down out of the head and into its proper circulation and center of balance.

    Many high-level practitioners and masters do lots and lots of physical practice.

    If an event in life has prompted you to shut off emotions, some other processing might be necessary -- something to encourage flow, like artistic expression or journaling or talking with a friend; or something to initiate healing, like psychotherapy or shamanic ritual; or in many cases taking steps to remedy the actual life situation. If you've had a falling-out, resolve it directly with compassionate communication; if you've lost work, find new work; if you've been injured, work on rehabilitation and take care of your whole physical-mental-emotional health. Sitting meditation is not a panacea; and sometimes it is used to avoid the things we really should be doing and feeling.
