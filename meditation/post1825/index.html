    If you're following MTCTOTB, only follow it. When you find a living teacher, take up their method and their advice (don't mix or tinker).

    Unless the method specifically requires closed eyes, keep your eyes open — half-open, like images of the Buddha. Vague drifting in meditation is not exactly sleep, but it is pretty much sleep: without even being aware of it for moments at a time, you lose presence and become dull. It can oscillate between meditation and sleep; and usually the meditator feels it has been a 'good' meditation because they feel relaxed and refreshed; but it is because they have been napping!

    Clarity of method is important. When you adhere to the method very closely, you can better identify the times when you drift off. This ability can become more precise, so you begin to notice even brief or momentary sleep or distraction. But because the beginner doesn't yet know the standard to which he/she should hold him/herself, and because we easily fool ourselves, having a clear-eyed guide is extremely beneficial.

    >I'm going to meditate as long as I want and that's okay right?

    Yes, that's okay. Any style is okay. Just be aware of the cause and effect.

    'As long as you want' is completely fine; and it can have benefit in that it promotes a natural, easy relationship with meditation. But if you only ever follow what you want, then your wanting becomes the leader of practice, and you become more of a slave to your wanting. Therefore, in the interest of equanimity and unconditional zeal, meditators make commitments and stick to them. It is common to choose a duration and then sit for the duration, regardless of preference. This evokes a stability and contentment that doesn't depend on preference or whim — it doesn't depend on having things exactly your favorite way. You only practice showing up, no matter what.

    Another cause-effect point to be aware of is the dulling effect of long sittings. Rare meditators have unusual aspiration and unusual capacity for continuous, clear, bright concentration. Let me stress that this is rare, and usually limited to advanced practitioners (who have increased their abilities and have not become jaded). Most beginners are in no position to judge the quality of their own meditation; and it often turns out the reason they can sit for long periods is because they are in fact sleeping or drifting rather than maintaining continuous meditation.

    I'm not saying that's your situation, because I don't know; I'm saying it's a point to be aware of. If you develop a habitual attachment to dullness it will be much harder to do proper meditation in the future. People stuck in this pattern tend to stubbornly defend their attitudes, and many even resist any new teaching or guidance that requires more brightness and engagement from them.

    So on this second point, it is also common to split up sittings into shorter segments, to avoid deepening stagnation. Walking meditation sessions of 5 to 30 minutes is traditionally undertaken in between sittings of 30 to 60 minutes. Other schools may participate in sets of prostrations between sittings to more vigorously circulate blood, breath, and energy, and more deeply settle the bodymind.

    There are several other reasons for interspersing sitting and walking/movement meditations, not the least of which is the compassionate intent to bring the powers of meditation into active life and relationship, and not merely keep them to oneself in isolation.

    All of these kinds of issues and teachings are addressed in traditional courses of training, each in its own proper context and order of learning. It is difficult to gain complete learning in a slapdash manner, by accidentally encountering teachings from various sources, from various traditions, with varying levels of understanding and experience, and with varying motivations.
