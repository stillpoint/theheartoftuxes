    Take a breath.

    Your writing is run-on, without proper punctuation and capitalization. It indicates an attachment to trains of thought, and that attachment is facilitated by a lack of meticulousness about form.

    Applied to meditation, if you are sloppy about the form, you miss the chance to catch yourself attaching to trains of thought and being carried away by them. But if you follow the method very closely, it provides a mirror for you, showing you when you go off. You then have the chance to apply your letting-go and returning.

    It is the application of letting-go (of the thought-object) and returning that is the core of meditative practice. This is the power you train. Gradually, this amounts to non-distraction, as you return more and more frequently and reliably, and get taken away less and less. It all hinges on being very meticulous in form and very momentary in focus.

    The common misconception is that thought will no longer appear. Occasionally that may be the case, but in general we don't want to kill the thinking mind outright, or we'd become stupid, dysfunctional vegetables. It's not that you get a situation in which outside objects no longer appear and distract you; it's that you train your non-attachment to them as they arise. And then gradually 'outside' and 'inside' return to their original unity.

    But first you need to practice stopping, resting, and taking care of the moment. In your communication, be meticulous about punctuation. Have compassion for the reader, and take care to write clearly. Don't get so caught up in your own drama that you fail to pause, breathe, and rest in natural rhythm.

    In your meditation, learn a proven method, learn it very well so there's no doubt about what you're supposed to be doing with your mind and body, and follow the method carefully from moment to moment. The method will help you train your attention span. Don't get so caught up in your own drama that you fail to let go, breathe, and rest in natural rhythm.
