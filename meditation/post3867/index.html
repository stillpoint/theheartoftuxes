    After one year of meditation, I think I'm starting to lose the grip.

    (Continued from the first part of my reply because I reached the word limit....)

    You ask about how to practice with open eyes. You already have your good direction: to wake up to what is. This guides you. You should keep your eyes open in a non-grasping manner. Seek neither the formless escape of closing the eyes nor the form-obsessed grasping at objects through open eyes. In a phrase: "no big deal".

    You don't have to keep a laser gaze on anything, you don't have to focus on anything in particular. Just let the eyes be soft. Let them receive whatever is there rather than going out to grasp something. Your attention goes to your method of meditation. No need to refrain from blinking or have any agenda about blinking. No big deal. Just return to your method of meditation.

    Beginnings are often difficult. That is appropriate. So if difficulty appears, don't think that it means anything important. After a while you'll get used to open eyes and it will truly be no big deal.

    The usual instruction is to have eyes half-open (i.e., relaxed) in a soft gaze angled toward the floor two or three feet in front of you. That is a general instruction. The aim is to find the middle way between sleepiness and tension; so you're neither forcing the eyes wide open nor letting them drift closed. If you are prone to sleepiness, the downward-angled gaze may contribute to your difficulty; so in that case you can gaze softly straight ahead with relaxed eyes. No big deal.

    At times, what appears in the vision may draw you away from your meditation method. This is to be expected, because of our deeply ingrained habit of attaching to objects; and we attach to objects especially frequently and strongly through our vision. But this is a very good chance to train the letting-go faculty and the ability to return to your chosen activity. Without the distraction, you wouldn't have the chance to train letting go, equanimity, stability. But because eyes are open and sometimes the things of daily life snag you like velcro, you have a chance to train letting go, equanimity, and stability. Then your meditation experience becomes very useful in your daily life. In fact, it becomes a precious gift to the world.

    Moving through the day with openhanded presence is already a tremendous gift to others, setting them all on the inevitable path of awakening, whether they know it or not.

    ~

    Edit: One more point. You say that you want to feel present in your everyday interactions. Careful with your words: don't attach to feeling. Your practice moves from the inside out, so don't look back and try to check what's happening to you. Only do it. Only sincerely practice presence. This has (and requires) far more faithfulness than constantly looking to see "do I feel present now". The looking itself means you are no longer present. So don't seek to grasp any indicator; only do it.
