    Forcing meditation when the mind wanders

    Use of the word "force" is a bit questionable, and I wonder if you're trying to give yourself an out by overstating one side of the issue.

    It is beneficial to meditate regularly. It is beneficial to meditate in all kinds of conditions. Easy conditions may help us go deep, but may also erode our perseverance. Difficult conditions may train our adaptability and devotion, but may also discourage us.

    Either way, it will not be perfect. That is the First Noble Truth that Buddha taught: everything is Dukkha â€” everything is insufficient, unsatisfactory, incomplete. If you attach to conditions, then you will experience the insufficient, unsatisfactory, and incomplete qualities of your meditation (and your life).

    So your practice must become unconditional. Like unconditional love: "I love you no matter what." Only do the practice, no matter what, and don't try to arrange your perfect situation. That is how you become free. If you practice in this way, then no situation can hinder you. Even at your not-so-favorite times, you can show up fully present and ready to act with clarity and compassion.

    But the manner in which you bring yourself back to practice doesn't have to be violent or disturbing. If you are honest with yourself I know you can come up with an alternative besides "forcing" yourself back like a hostage.

    There's no need for drama; there's no need for even a second thought. When it's time to do meditation, just do it. And during meditation, when the mind wanders or you get distracted, just return. Let the returning be gentle, and let it be plain. Any drama or inner conflict you create about it is completely your own doing, and it just builds your ego (for example, by creating a story in which you are the hero, or the victim... or both).

    Problems arise in practice when you check. That means that instead of doing the practice you think about it. You step outside of yourself to see how you're doing. This is a very strong habit in humans; but it is no good. Don't try to figure out how to make a better situation. Don't try to judge whether your meditation is going well or not. Whether it's calm weather or rough seas, only try sincerely. That is already the expression of your unmoving mind, your enlightened nature.

    In the temple, all kinds of activities go on. Sometimes sitting, sometimes chanting, sometimes bowing. There are ceremonies and there are celebrations, weddings and funerals. Old monks walk through and young children romp around. But at the altar, the Buddha image (or Crucifix or other holy symbol) is always there, still and radiant. This is like your true mind. All of life goes on; around, around, up and down, in and out, appearing and disappearing; and your true mind is always just there, witnessing it all. In your approach to practice, be like your true mind. Only do it, without being moved by circumstances.

    There are special cases, though. If you go through a period of meditation where your mind wanders incessantly and the method is abandoned, then you have to bring more motivation and determination to bear. Likewise, if you start fooling yourself about why it would be a good idea to miss meditation periods, then you need to give yourself a shake and snap out of it. While those might be spoken of as forcing yourself, setting up an inner battle is never fruitful in the long run. It is not that you force and fight with yourself; it is that your wisdom mind cuts through the ignorance and helps you to surrender to the moment at hand.
