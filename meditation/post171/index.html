    Had an very very interesting experience. First attempt where I actually almost lost myself

    I'm happy to help, if I can.

    The first point is what are you trying to achieve through meditation? This is usually a vague issue for beginning meditators because they have not yet had a taste of the profound possibilities, they have not yet had the benefit of wise guidance over time, and they (like all of us) attach to phenomena.

    The great transformative power of meditation is in waking up, becoming clear, and perceiving the true nature of self and the world -- seeing what's really going on. This vital aim tends to get overshadowed by the novelty of strange experiences and mental projections. These experiences preoccupy a mind that is not yet still and clear. The new meditator takes them for significant, when in fact they are just distraction from the central point. The strange experiences are actually not at all special, and they will be encountered by practically every serious meditator at some point.

    Without a great deal of clarity on the true purpose of meditation, and allies (like a teacher and experienced companions) who can bring the new student back to the right orientation repeatedly, we often see new meditators spending a lot of time in a sort of self-fascination, as if they have stumbled upon a box of new toys. The real practice may go through a lot of new and curious territory, but the important thing, in terms of transformation, is to keep moving on without getting caught up in any phenomena.

    When the thinking steps outside of pure experience in order to assess, analyze, label, judge, and (usually) exalt or dramatize it, we call that “checking". Checking is the function of the ego, the sense of selfhood that wants to keep itself alive.

    When you start dissolving in the experience so that sights and sounds and sensations appear clearly (and the sense of self may start to fade) the ego convinces you to stop the process of surrender and keep being a “someone" -- an analyst, looking at some interesting event, thinking about it, making a story and drama about it. It's as if ego keeps finding shiny things to flash at you, the way someone distracts a baby with jingling keys. This all prevents you from taking that step into unity, that step of trust and surrender. Don't take the bait! Checking keeps you separate.

    For example, you may have begun to let go and unify in meditation, but then you started thinking about what was happening rather than simply keeping the mind clear.

    So meditation will actually entail letting go of enticement, leading to a more balanced stable relationship life. It's not as cool a “whoa, dude" way, but the power develop is much more meaningful.

    A very definite, formal method of meditation will help you recognize when you veer off like this, and will help you return. You should have a good method, and you should know it well -- know what to do with your mind and body moment to moment -- and you should follow it strictly. If you don't have other allies bringing you back to the path, a clear and meticulous method becomes that much more important.
