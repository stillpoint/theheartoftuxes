    Opening the 7 chakras?

    Thank you for your great heartfelt reply. I'm sorry that you are struggling with schizophrenia; but it is wonderful that you are finding out through your own experience the kinds of practices that can help you.

    I recommend working closely with a master or very experienced teacher as soon as possible, because they may be able to help you maintain that balance you seek. They can help you not only with their knowledge (of training methods, relationships between the chakras, signs along the way, responses to specific difficulties) but also with their perspective â€” a good teacher can offer an objective, stabilizing influence when our inner voices are loud.

    As to your original question, there are various methods for opening the chakras. In fact, there are various systems of working with energy centers which may overlap somewhat but not necessarily in all areas. Some call the energy centers "chakras", others use different terms. Tibetan Yoga (both Buddhist and Bon/native) works with energy centers. Various schools of Qigong and Taoist Neigong (internal energy) practices also work with energy centers.

    One school of note uses a method of opening that is inherently more balanced and less prone to agitation than many others, and that is the Water Method of dissolving blockages from the top down, in contrast with methods that try to force energy up through the centers.

    In general, I would recommend approaches that focus on releasing, relaxing, dissolving, and letting-be. When you practice letting-be you develop a strong center that can be present amidst all kinds of agitations, both external and internal. (This can be practiced along with object-related concentration methods, including focusing the attention at one place in the body.)

    On the other hand, practices that try to direct energy to specific places, hold it unmoving, or force it to move in certain ways are prone to manipulation by habitual patterns and unconscious forces. When a person attaches to one particular pattern or movement of energy, that person is more easily swayed; his attitude is unbalanced, focused on gaining something rather than stable, open, and ready to move with anything that may appear.

    So practices that are openhanded and free of a grasping, gaining attitude will help you find your stability. The sounds, voices, emotional uprisings, and pains that appear may or may not diminish, but your mind will not be moved by them as much.

    In simple terms, if you proceed in practice by clutching at results, then when results are chaotic you will be moved around as if you are holding the reins of a wild horse. But if you keep a non-grasping attitude your mind will be smooth and open, and chaotic movements will not have such a hold on you â€” as if you are standing back and watching the movements of a wild horse.

    The attitude and intent you bring into the practice is critical.

    Should you try to keep your mind at certain spots? That is not my favorite style, and so I don't have the kind of experience with it that a long-term practitioner might have.
