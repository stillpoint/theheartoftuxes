    TheHeartofTuxes

    A lot of unusual things might happen in meditation. There are many possible reasons; but one big reason is that you are suddenly using your bodymind in a new way. Although it's easy to start wondering and guessing about new things that appear, for the most part manifestations are not a big deal and will tend to settle down when you continue practice and become more balanced in your approach.

    But all that depends on having proper teaching. You need to be applying the right method in the right way, not inventing your own imagined version. If you go by your own 'best guesses', that is all you have to rely on. If you go by the guidance of an experienced, attained, awakened teaching who has mastered a valid, proven method, then you have the teacher, teaching, the tradition, and the community to rely on.

    As to your spacey feeling:

    First, unless you've been instructed otherwise your eyes should be open. Closed eyes encourages drifting and dreaming; and disorientation can be a very common symptom of improperly closing the eyes. Some practices call for closed eyes, but in these practices the structure of the method provides grounding that counteracts (somewhat) the drifting-dreaming effect. Why are you closing your eyes? What method are you practicing?

    Another point about closed eyes is that the drifting of the mind as well as the movement of the eyes themselves can cause the energy to fluctuate, which also can easily produce a spacey or dizzy feeling.

    If the method is not absolutely clear — if you don't know exactly what you should be doing with your body and mind, or don't have confidence in the practice — there will also be a tendency to drift. So very many people drift and waver during their so-called 'meditation time', imagining that they're practicing meditation when in fact they're just taking a different kind of nap or daydream. They feel somewhat refreshed or comfortable, but they don't gain clarity. If repeated, this can become a habit associated with meditation: the practitioner misuses the mind, the energy follows by also drifting, and dullness becomes more and more ingrained. People with this habit can become very insular, resenting teachings and practice situations that require them to actually wake up. Better not to fall into this habit from the beginning!

    Another possible cause for spaced-out feeling is imbalance of breathing. This is also quite common. Again, with proper instruction one can address the issue and even it out over time. But without the input of an expert one is left pitting one's good but uninformed sense against one's unconsciousness, misunderstanding, blind spots, preferences, and habits.

    If the breath is forced, the blood-brain chemistry will change. A good number of supposed 'mystical' experiences have their basis in imbalanced breath and, for example, CO2 intoxication or respiratory alkalosis. (That's not to say that true mystical experiences don't exist.)

    Using your mind in certain ways can also be destabilizing. If your relationship with the object of concentration (if you are using that style of practice) is anxious and grasping the energy can become tight and wiry. Overthinking or approaching practice from merely a mental perspective can cause energy to get stuck up in the head. In general, practice aims to let energy settle low in the body, near the center of gravity in the lower belly. Upright and relaxed posture help with this, and correct non-grasping approach also helps. Vigorous physical practice is a very good companion to sitting, and helps energy to quickly settle down in its proper seat.

    Without face-to-face experience with your quality of energy, and without knowing your method and how you apply it, it's difficult to say whether your experience of spacing out is an issue that requires remedy, or whether it's a common thing that will settle down with more practice.

    This much is clear: it's necessary to know why you're practicing and how to practice; so proper teaching is very important.
