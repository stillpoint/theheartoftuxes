    Eyes open or closed?

    Results of a search for the term "eyes open".

    Unless there is a specific reason for keeping the eyes closed in a particular meditation, such as the need for visualisation or to avoid stimuli other than bodily ones in body-scanning or energy-based meditations, keeping the eyes open is much, much better. Indeed, it is important to keep the eyes open for a few reasons.

    First, there is a very great risk of mistaking the comfort of vague dreaminess for the peace and bliss that come as signs of true clarity. I have known many meditators who have made this mistake, and as a result got stuck in their drowsy-dreamy approach, sometimes for years on end without progress. So this is a real danger: one may waste one's time going down a blind alley.

    Those who fall into the vague dreamy state usually are not aware of it. That is what makes it so insidious. They feel they are making a sincere effort, and they enjoy the comfort they experience. Of course, everyone wants to think they are doing a good job and making progress, so it is easy to make a story about the good feeling, taking it to be a sign of attainment, when actually it is quite the opposite.

    Next, correct meditation depends on correct direction. That is to say, your motivation must be right. Meditation, or any spiritual practice, that seeks only benefit for oneself is narrow-minded and ultimately keeps one in the cycle of suffering based on self-cherishing. Right motivation has compassion for others. The true purpose of meditation is to wake up from life's dream, from the illusion of self upon which all pettiness, evil, and neurosis is built â€” and to use that awakening to help all others. So rather than retreating into our own little world, seeking our own little experience, we open our eyes and rest as our great wide nature.

    The divisions between "me" and "not me", between "inner" and "outer" are only conceptual. These divisions do not really exist. In order to awaken to this reality, again open eyes are very important, very helpful. The meditator has a chance to experience directly the unity of self and world, inner and outer.

    Finally, awakening can be precipitated by any event. Keeping the eyes open allows the possibility of realization through seeing while in meditation.

    As I said above, there are certain meditations that require closed eyes; and these meditations may still include correct, broadly compassionate motivation. As soon as the eyes close, there is a greater propensity to both sleepiness, which is loss of consciousness, and to dreaminess, which is the wandering of conceptual and imaginative faculties. So if the eyes do have to be closed for some reason, one would do well to have several aids and checkpoints in place:

    • A sincere and exceptional aspiration to remain present and ultimately to awaken from life's dream

    • A very clear method, a very clear understanding of it, and a very firm grasp of its performance and signs of attainment

    • A good teacher who knows and has attained the fruits of the practice he/she has assigned, and who tracks your progress

    • A supportive environment for practice, and specifically for wakefulness: a tidy and clean practice space, clean air, temperature on the cool side, a good sleep schedule, a moderate diet, a strict practice schedule and/or practice together with others. (Diet has a tremendous effect on wakefulness/sleepiness: heavy and dead foods, overstimulating foods, poor nutrition, or simply too much food can all contribute to drowsiness. Practicing side by side with others can help somewhat in pushing past one's comfort zone, though I've known meditators to indulge in sleepy/dreamy practice even in groups or at retreats.)
