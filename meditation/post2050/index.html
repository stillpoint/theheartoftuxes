    Posture is of mind and body together. Ideally, one participates in a naturally upright, noble, non-grasping (and therefore without held tension) posture.

    But our common everyday way of using the mind and body is quite different, with many built-up patterns of tension and disconnection: some parts are overemphasized, with blocked up energy; other parts are disowned and deficient. So we may have to contrive helpful postures until they become natural.

    If you keep a mind of equanimity, the body will start to reflect it. If you keep a body of ease and balance, the mind will be supported in its return to that attitude.

    Just from a practical standpoint, certain recommended points of posture help you stay alert and stable, especially as your meditation periods become longer.

    In summary, you should follow the instructions for the tradition you are studying. Some traditions are very stringent about upright posture and not moving. You are expected to take a posture and keep it without moving. If you slump you can return to tall posture, but it shouldn't be repeated (and could draw a correction from the sitting monitor). There's somewhat of a trade made, where you may endure quite a bit of discomfort so that in the long run your practice is very alert and upright. That's not my favorite way, but it's a valid way within the context of its own tradition. I do find this way to be susceptible to a controlling mindset.

    Other traditions of zen sitting also call for stillness, but you are allowed to make one clear adjustment at a time for as many times as necessary (not fidgeting, but for instance standing and then sitting back down to change your way of sitting; or straightening and resetting the shoulders, etc.). It is left up to you to find the balance between openhandedness to all experience (pleasant and unpleasant) on the one hand, and taking care of the bodymind on the other hand. In this style, your own standards may tend to change according to your capacity; as you get more comfortable physically and more stable mentally you may call on yourself to stay still for longer periods. I find this to be a good balance of conditions.

    Finally, there's the other end of the spectrum, where you are allowed to shift here and there at will. Some traditions may even allow you to slump, though most teach that it becomes an obstacle to meditation. Most meditators will find that if they don't call themselves to at least some standard of stillness, the mind will also fidget, always trying to find the ideal comfort but never succeeding. Even so, a few people just never seem to settle down enough to do well with the other styles, and may require more permission to move before they can progress to stability.
