    Do you ever have "break-throughs?"

    >about 2 weeks I'm back to where I started in terms of mindfulness.

    Part of maturing in practice is learning not to check your situation and condition. The common habit is to step outside of your immediate experience, outside of moment-to-moment practice, and look at yourself with a judgmental eye. The common habit is to label your situation, to judge it good or bad, progress or regression, etc. This habit should be resisted and let go of at every opportunity.

    Checking is deemed human beings' number one mind sickness, and it certainly is the number one obstacle in meditation and mindfulness practice. Whenever you notice yourself stepping outside of the immediate moment, you should quickly and without drama return to simple momentary mind. Quickly/instantly is helpful, and without drama/struggle is also very helpful.

    So be vigilant about how you use words, even to yourself. You, in fact, don't know whether you're back to where you started. You only know a checking judgment based on a feeling. Neither the judgment nor the feeling are necessarily good indicators of progress/regression. And in any case, the sooner you drop checking the sooner you return to your real-life, engaged moment mind.

    Yes, certain things can sort of undo the effects of our practice. Being around words and concepts, especially debased or petty or trivial ones, can easily disturb and cloud the tenuous clarity achieved in beginning practice. Your environment can also affect your state: diet, sleep schedule, intent and activity of work, cleanliness and orderliness of living space, and clarity and intent of companions all have quite significant effects on the mind. For those who don't have command of their own center (i.e., most of us), regulating these life structures is a very important support for meditative practice and spiritual progress in general.

    One life structure is most influential of all, and that is your own intention and action with regard to other beings. If you are kind and generous to others your mind will be naturally more centered, free of fear, and open to spiritual attainment. If you harm others or harbor ill will, envy, or pride your mind will naturally be more agitated, unstable, and closed to spiritual attainment. This is simple cause and effect. As you grow in mindfulness you will notice these cause and effect relationships more and more.

    >Does anyone know why this happens or why it would go away?

    It is all based on cause and effect. Natural process guides the events of our lives, including the unfolding of our minds. Some effects are very immediate; like, if you watch a horror film or get into an argument it might be difficult to keep an even, open mind. Other effects may take days, years, or even lifetimes to ripen. Our good and bad actions are like seeds that stay dormant until they find the particular conditions for their sprouting and fruition.

    Just as seeds require particular kind of soil, balance of water and nutrients, amount of sunshine, and range of temperature to flourish, the effects of our actions unfold when they find their precipitating conditions. For example, someone may have made sexually attached thoughts and actions that have some immediate effect, but the long-term effects may only arise in the presence of a person they find attractive or in a setting that is sensually stimulating.

    That is why many spiritual traditions involve regulated conditions for beginners, like a temple or practice group, or a set of guidelines for living -- so the student's mind can be protected until they have command of their own center (until those outside conditions no longer have the power to push the mind here and there). Being with an accomplished teacher also tends to protect the student's mind, due to the teacher's strong, immovable center.

    If you are interested in following up on this issue you may check out the Eight Worldly Winds in Buddhism: the Eight categories of things that strongly move our minds around and prompt us to create causes that block our spiritual progress.

    Edit: In brief, although it may be considered ideal to stay mindful throughout the day (and for high-level practitioners, throughout the night as well), this consideration should not really be entertained. Your job is to be mindful for this moment, and that is all. Just this no-duration, ever-unfolding moment. Anything else is a departure from presence. Checking your condition and situation takes you out of the simple, alive moment.
