    I'm afraid I'm not familiar with Headspace. If it teaches a good method and teaches it well, that may be a good thing to continue.

    One benefit of guided meditation is that with repetition you internalize the instructions and learn how to coach yourself within that narrow field of activity (i.e., within that particular method).

    But what guided meditations can't do is provide new and ongoing teachings, evolving perspectives that unfold as you progress, wise responses to your particular issues and questions, a context of human compassion, and a living example of the effort and the result. For all of these, a living teacher and community of fellow meditators are necessary and irreplaceable.

    For your first tastes of meditation, learning from a book or from guided meditations may be very helpful. There's little risk or commitment required; it's easy enough to give it a shot and begin to get a sense of what it's about. But if you want to progress significantly and make meditation a part of your life, an experienced and qualified teacher is important, for all the trains I stated above, and more.

    If I give general instructions, they would be very general. I wouldn't want to contradict the source that you are learning from. Different methods have different instructions. If the teaching comed from a tradition, it has its own context, its own reason for the instructions being the way they are. That is why it is irresponsible for people to just give advice online without understanding the tradition and method the meditator is involved with. It leads to confusion and doubt for the meditator.

    Yes, open eyes is best unless there's a specific reason in your particular method (for example, if you're doing a visualization or inner scan). Closed eyes contribute both to fullness and to dreaming. The tendency for the mind to wander and make up images and experiences increases greatly with closed eyes; and many people who meditate with closed eyes don't even know that they are drifting and dozing. I've seen it many, many times: they wake up at the end of meditation and feel very relaxed and refreshed, thinking they had a great session; but it's because they were half-asleep!

    Open eyes assists with the main agenda, the main thrust of meditation, which is to be awake and aware in a non-grasping way -- to develop awake equanimity. It is said that the eyes are open to two realms: the inner and the outer. It is important not to exclude either one.

    If you have a good method, if you know the purpose of your method, and if you carry it out meticulously, then all you need to do is keep trying. Keep applying it freshly each time. It's hard to do this over long periods in isolation. The habit mind will eventually take over. Repetition will eventually become dull. Guidance and inspiration and external structure are all very helpful, very important of the meditation is to become part of your life path.

    So it depends on what you want from meditation, but you shouldn't underestimate the value of a living teacher.

    The other thing is that you shouldn't expect meditation to do everything. In many parts of life, you have to go out and do something to help yourself. You have to change your situation, begin gathering the conditions necessary for your advancement. In terms of emotional issues, appropriate cures for self-doubt and fear may come from more social engagement (doing things with good people), from your beneficial activity (doing work you're proud of, and being generous to others), from establishing better habits (avoiding disruptive influences in your environment or activity, mind-polluting substances, anxiety-producing entertainments, upsetting or unclean living space, confused or agitating people, etc.), and from counseling or therapy.
