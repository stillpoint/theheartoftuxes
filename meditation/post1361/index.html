    title: To a Medditor: On how to deal with stubbornness summary: 3.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2014-09-19 18:16:17 some_url:

    Awareness of the human shadow is so helpful in many different fields: spirituality, politics, culture clash, personal relationships....

    I recently recommended a book to another Redditor, and it would be a great resource for many people interested in meditation, spirituality, self-help, and similar pursuits. The Dark Side Of The Light Chasers, by Debbie Ford, gives an introduction to the issue of the human shadow, and especially how it appears among people (we) who sincerely look to improve ourselves and become better human beings.

    In our pursuit for the light, we often either neglect or outright deny the dark; and thus the dark becomes not merely negative material, it becomes shadow: negative material that controls you from unconscious parts of your mind. When what you don't want and what you try not to be gets shoved into unconsciousness (denied, ignored, skipped over, downplayed, avoided), it makes you into its servant. To your horror, you find yourself acting in ways that you had hoped and expected you would never act. And it happens again and again. This is the issue of shadow.

    We can point to many instances of very highly aware and advanced meditators — teachers, leaders — who have carried a large shadow and thus have acted out of unconscious, harmful impulse. There are many stories of community leaders who have in a big way transgressed the very values they publicly teach. It is not because they are bad people, or even that they have not achieved much through their practice; it is because they have not investigated and cleared out their own shadow (which involves, to a large extent, admitting that they are flawed, attached, fallible people just like the rest of us).

    So conscious, starkly honest introspection is required on the path. We have to take every opportunity to admit to our faults, even the small and seemingly insignificant ones; because it's the seemingly insignificant ones that are often the character's iceberg, with most of their bulk beneath the surface. If we start skipping over small character flaws and failing to admit to little transgressions, that is exactly the mechanism of the shadow. We figuratively brush our character under the carpet and keep it hidden.

    So when you meet an advanced student or teacher and they seem abnormally humble or self-effacing, it is not necessarily a sort of fake role-playing (though sometimes that happens too). It may be that they are trying to own up to every last fault, so as to avoid the growth of the shadow.

    For example, if you encounter a George Costanza type who knocks people aside in order to get to the birthday cake, the first impulse might be to think "Wow, look at that poor guy. That's so greedy. I'm glad I'm not like that." But a better practice would be to think "Wow, there is greed in operation. That is me. This is my own greed." Even if your greed is not that bad, still you have greed; so you can use the opportunity to admit it. Then when it comes to light you may discover "Holy crap! I actually do have that much greed! That really is me."
