    Does anyone have any advice for controlling breathing?

    The aim is to be present with reality as-it-is, and so we practice being present with the mind as-it-is rather than using the mind to cover up or avoid or manipulate our situation. Therefore, we should not have a manipulative relationship with the breath (nor with posture nor any other aspect of meditation). The breath should come naturally, and we should practice being present and open with it. At all times, non-grasping is the approach.

    It is very important to know and to actualize this. We don't make our practice. That means that the breath will sometimes be deep and sometimes shallow, sometimes flowing and sometimes heaving, and our job is to let it be.

    In fact, the breath changes with the mind; the two are intimately connected. As you travel through different levels and qualities of concentration, the breath may change dramatically. If you have a prejudice that says certain patterns of breath are better or worse than others, then you may have more difficult than necessary as you progress through various stages of meditation.

    But there are two points â€œon the other hand":

    First; in our modern society, many of us have developed unnatural breathing. Our default, the pattern that we always return to, may be shallow and constricted. So even when we let go and let be, what we let go to is not natural.

    There tend to be two different methods of training in this respect. One method says just keep letting go and eventually what is natural will emerge. (Some Vipassana training tends to take this approach.) The other method says first establish or approach what is natural, and proceed from there. In this method you may be instructed to regulate your breathing to make it more even, to engage the belly and diaphragm, to lift and open the posture, and possibly to make the out-breath longer than the in-breath. For a while you may pay more attention to regulating the breath, to bring it back to a more natural state, but eventually you let that go as well. (Some Taoist and Zen training may follow this approach.)

    There will also be many energy practices that require quite a bit of manipulation of breath. Although they are usually called â€œmeditation", that is not strictly correct. Any practice that manipulates the mind to bring it to a special state is not (yet) meditation. With energy practices in particular, it is extremely important to be very clear about one's compassionate and open-handed intent before engaging in the practice. This is true of all practices, but energy practice raises the stakes since it is so easy to abuse it and thus lose one's true way.

    The second â€œon the other hand" about breathing is that different breathing patterns can actually provide you with information about your state of mind. In the early stages, this can be a distracting hindrance. You should develop the habit of letting your mind be, rather than the habit of checking and judging your mind. That is one of many reasons why a good teacher is so invaluable if you're serious about correct practice -- the teacher does the checking, and you can just practice you right relationship with yourself (open-handedness).

    In advanced practice, however, getting that feedback from your breathing can alert you to times when you are entering a new stage (which may help you to trust the changes that happen), and may also help you identify times when you are stuck where you shouldn't be -- when some subtle attachment is at work.

    In general, and at all times, wield a non-acquisitive, non-grasping, open-handed mind, and everything will come out right. How you set up and relate to your body and mind are intimately tied with the breath. If you would like to discuss posture and how it affects the breathing, please feel to contact me.

    The first and most important point, though is to be clear on why you are meditating. When that relationship is clear, then you are able to choose a good teacher and a good practice, and your practice will be clear. If your intent (your â€œdirection") is not clear, that unclarity well be an issue throughout your life, appearing in physical, emotional, and mental manifestations, including the breath.
