    >So if we're not looking for side effects what are we looking for?

    This is a wonderful question. This question actually comes first, before all pursuits and strategies and attempts: "What is it all for? What are we really doing here?"

    We ought to ask that repeatedly, and sincerely. We ought to aim it at our life. Life is very precious. It passes quickly, ends certainly, and we don't know when our end will come.

    There is no guarantee of how things will go for us in an afterlife; and there is no guarantee that we will even be alive tomorrow. But what is certain is that some day will be our last day, some breath will be our last breath, some action will be our last action.

    So taking that to heart, what is our best use of the incredible powers we have available â€” the powers of the senses, of intelligence, of creative imagination, of physical action, of hearing and relating to other people and other beings, of caring and responding and loving...? What is our best use of today?

    In that context, what is the correct purpose of meditation? It isn't to get some special feeling or unusual energy or arcane vision.

    The question "what are we looking for?" already has a bit of a mistake to it, assuming that we should be looking for something. That is an aquisitive approach, an attitude of grasping and hoarding. It assumes an incomplete, needy self that is insufficient unless it finds some outside object. But our true nature is already complete. So correct meditation connects us to our original complete nature and helps us trust it and express it in life.

    Therefore the 'looking for', seeking, grasping, wanting, making, manipulating habit is set aside. "That is all beside the point." When all that agitation is set aside, we can experience our own contentment that doesn't depend on any particular experience. It doesn't even depend on life and death.

    The function of this central point is also very important. It's very important not only that we get that 'for ourselves', but that it allows us to go beyond selfish, divisive, ignorant views and behaviors. Rather than burdening and harming other people, other races, other nations, other species, and the world itself, we can be a force of harmony and benefit. But this is not possible if we don't understand and attain our true self. All problems in the world come from not understanding what we really are.

    So we set aside the grasping habit. We set aside the immature, petty need for everything to be for and about "I, my, me". Then truth appears, and we can interact clearly with truth.

    But if we attach to experience, we miss the central point. If we clutch at experience, we ignore and sabotage our original completion. If we fixate on experience, we lose our freedom of action and get pulled around by life, always chasing what we like and always running away from or trying to kill what we don't like.

    This central point is all-important. That is why it is central. It is the basic point of life.

    >if I am having unusual meditations I am doing something wrong?

    If you fixate on experience, you lose your way. If you try to have unusual meditations, you lose your way; and if you try not to have unusual meditations, you lose your way.

    The correct approach is like a person watching a seascape: regardless of the weather and the activity of the ocean, they just watch. When storm clouds are raging and the ocean is churning, the watcher watches. When the scene settles and becomes perfectly quiet, the water surface like smooth glass, the watcher watches. The watcher is not dependent on the scene; the scene doesn't control the watcher.

    So this is like meditation. Regardless of stormy thoughts, visions, and feelings, or placid thoughts, visions, and feelings, you just watch. You assert your freedom from experiences; you break the chains of bondage, saying "nothing has the power to control my mind". You show up in presence for any scene, any experience, any energy.

    Sometimes the mind or heart will be agitated. You return to the method and stay attentive. Sometimes the mind will encounter something new or internal blocks will open or close and you'll feel internal re-arrangements. You return to the method and stay attentive. And sometimes things will be very silent and peaceful. You return to the method and stay attentive.

    If you get caught up in the experience, judging and comparing, blaming or congratulating yourself, then already you have lost your independent presence, you have lost your freedom. So as soon as you recognize you've wandered, return. Return, return, return. Be the independent watcher.

    That watcher is already complete, not requiring any particular scene. What a different life! No longer controlled by praise and blame, no longer put off balance when you win or lose. You can maintain integrity at all times. You can show up for others, whether they are in a stormy frenzy or uncomfortably silent, or whatever their state, or whatever their need. You will be a force of compassionate presence in the world.

    But though life will be utterly different, things will be the same. Lemons will continue to be sour; the sky will continue to be blue; there will still be work and rest, gain and loss. It's just that you will no longer be attached to experience. You will no longer self-identify as a separate, needy, incomplete being; and thus you will not be driven by selfish ignorance. You will identify as the watcher, who is already complete and free.

    Don't expect practice to always be predictable or known.
    Don't expect to have no strange experiences come your way.
    Don't be either dissuaded by or arrogantly proud of unusual events.

    Only keep to the center.
