    title: "Meditate on it" what does this even mean!? summary: 3.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2014-04-08 14:54:29 some_url:

    A koan isn't an unsolvable riddle. It's a teaching situation. The master presents you with a situation that has something missing, and your job is to make it complete.

    Koans are unsolvable by the thinking mind; but the deep, innate mind of wisdom before thinking can resolve them. Some traditional koans have only one precise resolution, and others have many possible resolutions. Still other koans have resolutions that depend on the current situation in which the teacher and student find themselves.

    The aim of practicing koans is to learn correct function. So in meditation you may (hopefully) find your true mind, which is clear and perfectly aware. In this mind there is only silence. But if you only stay there in the silence you can't help yourself or others, you can't help the world.

    Therefore we train in how to use that clear, aware, silent mind correctly in all life situations. In koan training you use that mind, and especially its spontaneous reflecting quality, to fill in the missing pieces of teaching situations. Traditional collections of koans deal with issues from the nature of mind and "what is Buddha?" to the teacher-student relationship, free movement between absolute and conditioned understanding, various attachments, and "what is Great Love?"

    So it's not only possible but important to "solve" koans. Whether presented by a master in traditional form or presented by life's circumstances, we come face to face with koans all the time. Perhaps an intelligent person can understand the answer to some koans through thinking, but that understanding doesn't help, because it can't complete the situation itself â€” action and complete belief in oneself are necessary.

    Some of these koans may be familiar:

    • "What does it mean to love this person in this situation, without losing my own way?"

    • "If I intervene in someone's personal drama, will I be helping or hindering?"

    • "Do I try to keep going through this hard time, or should I try to get away?"

    • "My roommate wants to do things one way and I want to do things another way. Which is the right way?"

    • "What should I do when two people are fighting?"

    And of course:

    • "Who am I, and what am I supposed to do in this life?"
