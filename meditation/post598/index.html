    You're not horrible; you're starting.

    So you should take care of the start properly. Get a good start, rather than trying to be an expert.

    When you see a toddler trying to drive a car, you run to stop it. "No! That's for later." And maybe you give it a toddler car so it can enjoy and learn about steering, and so on.

    When a sapling you've just planted is exposed to a big storm or flood, it gets uprooted. It doesn't yet have deep roots to take on that situation.

    5 hours is not the aim. Jhana is not the aim. These may or may not be things that happen along the way (neither is necessary). But they are not the end purpose. You are still not clear on the purpose of meditation. So don't be defensive. Becoming rigid in your unclarity makes more and more problem. Rather, be open and inquisitive. Find out.

    It's like, when you ask someone their goal in life and they say "to make a million dollars". (Actually, a million dollars is no longer that much money; so let's say "a hundred million".) This is not a real goal or wish; it's short-sighted, only a stepping stone to a vague fantasy the person imagines is going to fulfill them. Their aim surely isn't merely to possess all that money -- to sit in a room with a bunch of paper and do nothing; or to loll on the sofa looking at the amount in their bank account. Their aim is to do something with the money. The money is meaningless otherwise.

    And the money-wishers all have more or less vague notions of what is going to fulfill them: "Once I get this power, I'll be able to do what I want. Then I'll be happy."

    What? What is the thing that is going to make you happy? Doing what; having what? It is very unclear, only speculation.

    It is the same attitude. You are imagining some special power that will get you what you want, but you are not clear on what you want, nor on what is worthy of wanting, if anything.

    What is 5-hour meditation for? What is Jhana for? This question comes first. Otherwise, you're chasing phantoms.

    I don't know if you're in your adolescent years, but it is a hallmark of that phase to become obsessive about things -- to take on an overt style and persona and decide on being 'something'. Styles of dress become very particular. Opinions become very finalistic. This approach has a function in the development of the psyche; but in large part it is also an attempt to avoid uncertainty. There is so much chaos and change in this time of life that one seeks certainties to cling to.

    I get the sense that this dynamic is at work in your relationship to meditation just now. I'd like to invite you to be aware of that tendency of trying to be at the end of practice without having to take care of the beginning and middle.

    Without a proper teacher, your own habits and limited views will control the outcome.

    Just now, take care of the first step: What is the right purpose of practice? What is the right use of your life?
