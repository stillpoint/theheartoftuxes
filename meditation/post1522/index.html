    I Astral Projected inside of a dream.

    All experience is projection of mind. What we call reality and what we call imagining or dreaming all have the same essence: they are made of mind. So astral projection, if that is what you experienced, is not special; it is only unusual, only unfamiliar.

    The three significant points here are (in ascending order of importance):

    1. How do you return clearly if you travel in a dream or in astral travel?

    2. How do you use any experience correctly?

    3. How do you wake up from the entire dream, the dream of life?

    There are many unusual experiences you can have. The mind is quite miraculous in its everyday operation, but just because it is everyday operation we have become used to it and think of it as less significant than unusual experiences. But if you look at the eyes of a baby you may remember how miraculous it is just to see, hear, smell, taste, touch, and think. Our everyday experience is of the same substance as astral travel.

    So whether through meditative methods, or the use of substances, or with the help of guides, or just by accident, it is possible to have many experiences. What is more difficult is being able to return to clarity and reality after you have the experience.

    Can you return with your feet on the ground? Can you relate well to other people, and can you function sharply and appropriately in regular situations? Can you help yourself and others be happy? Many people get attached to so-called special experiences without being able to find their own basis of sanity.

    This often happens among artists as well. Artists are often unwitting shamans: they have access to depths of consciousness, and bring forth images and energies that can catalyze cathartic or awakening experiences many people; but many artists also don't know how to return to reality, to balance. From Van Gogh to Kurt Cobain, so many artists through the ages have been able to 'go there' but not get back.

    If you're going to go there, you'd better have a strong ability to return. That is why great masters and great shamans teach the basics. The arduous, meticulous preliminary work is, in part, training the student how to return to the reality of the moment. Moreover, it is training the student not to get swept away in the first place, but to understand the equality of all experience.

    All experience is like a movie on a movie screen: there are pleasant things and unpleasant things, plain things and spectacular things; but this whole drama is just projection of light. When you understand the screen you can understand the equality of all the images that appear on it. Without being aware of the screen, you get attached to the drama of the movie as if it were real, and the movie -- whether the drama is sedate or very unusual -- controls you.

    Therefore understanding the 'screen' is most important. Understanding your true mind, upon which all these experiences are projected, is necessary if you are to be free. This is how you avoid getting trapped in the unreal drama of experience. What is the basis of mind? Who is the one for whom it all happens?

    Before venturing into strange new territory of mind, do you you know how to use your regular life correctly? Do you know the right purpose of your getting out of bed, eating, meeting, working, resting? This has to be clarified. Let's not waste this miracle!

    And even more so, if you are going to venture into unusual experiences, you should know their correct purpose. Why do it -- just to think of yourself as special? That's ego. Just for entertainment? That's wasteful and indulgent.

    How can your effort free you and others from the suffering drama, the suffering dream? That is the point.
