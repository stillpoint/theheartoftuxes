    That's all the 'checking' habit. It's the foremost obstacle for meditators: we constantly, habitually step outside of our pure activity to check and see how we're doing.

    "What's different now? How does it compare to last time? How did it compare to what I read? Is it good or bad? Should I try something different?"

    We check our eye experience, we check our ear experience, we check our body experience, we check our mind experience. Seldom do we ever let it be.

    So that's why we have a practice: to train in not checking. Then we can grow our ability to be with reality on it's own terms, which becomes our wisdom and freedom: when we let go of checking, we can access reality (wisdom) and not be reactive to it (freedom). Our confidence in our true self emerges -- the self that doesn't pre-know, judge, compare, or imagine scenarios and dramas and strategies; the original mind that is able to reflect any situation as it unfolds.

    And that is also why we have one clear and well-learned method: to have something very clear to return to instead of going on excursions of checking. The ego always wants to be the controlling expert, having its hand in everything. The meditation method gives us an alternative. We put our faith in our original mind, which doesn't need to react and fiddle and interfere. What promise this holds for our life and for the world! To free the world from that violence of the ego!

    But it means that we need to put our complete trust in the method while we're performing it. Don't think that you're going to be more clever than the masters who passed down the method. Don't think that fixating on any of the things that come and go will give you any kind of special insight. You are not experiencing anything more special or significant than millions of other meditators through the ages have experienced.

    Your job is to stay with the unchanging mind, not to attach to the objects that come and go.

    Don't check. Return to the method.

    If you don't have a clear method, learn a proper, proven, traditional method.

    If you are not clear on the method, learn it better; learn it completely.

    If having learned a method you still don't have faith in it, understand the reason for it (the 'why' as well as the 'how') and witness its fulfillment in those who have practiced it faithfully.

    Therefore, learning a proven traditional method from an accomplished and verified teacher, in the context of a practice group or community, is best for removing doubt and helping you to do the practice without checking.

    If you try to invent or tweak/'hack' your own practice, you will always be victim to your checking habit, and your meditation will never mature. This is a very common situation these days among dabblers. These people then soothe themselves by making all kinds of ideas and talking, talking, talking as if they were experts without ever attaining even a basic stability in practice. Sad.

    Yes, if you practice well and consistently that commenting will diminish and go away for longer periods. Your ability to stay with the method will deepen. You will catch yourself drifting off more readily, and you will return faster. You will trust Unknowing more and more, and the agitation that leads you to check will diminish (though it will keep coming back and you will have to keep practicing with it until all your obscuring tendencies are completely removed).

    But don't base your effort on whether your pattern will go away. This just becomes another form of checking. "Has it gone away? How about now? Is it getting better? How long will it take? Am I better or worse than others? Is it worth the effort? How much effort should I put into it?" Gak!

    Don't check at all. Be momentary. Whether it goes away or not, whether it's better or worse, whether it's easy or difficult -- all that is none of your business! Only take care of this moment. Only do your non-checking practice just now. When you go off, return. Your whole life is just now. Anything else is imagination, illusion.
