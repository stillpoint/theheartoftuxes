    Is there any way I can include my cat in my meditation?

    First recognize what is more important. Hopefully there's a win-win solution; but if there's not, you have to know that your meditation practice is ultimately for the benefit of your cat and everyone in your life.

    You might try creating a mat or box (cats love boxes, don't they?) or other sitting place for your cat, next to yours. Invite the cat in before you start your sitting, and place it on its spot with care. It may happen that the attention and comfort prompt the cat to stay there rather have to climb on you.

    If you feel that you can devote a couple of mock sittings to the process, you might just keep placing your cat back on her own spot every time she climbs on you or nudges you. You can do it just automatically, in a neutral way, without anger or a sense of rejection (which would incite your cat to seek more affection). For a couple of "sessions", as many times as your cat crosses your personal boundary, pick her up and place her back on her spot.

    If you engage and try to calm her, she will learn that whenever she nudges you, that's what you'll do. So there will be no incentive to stop. But if she begins to associate nudging you with being moved back to her mat, she may soon enough find it not worth her while.

    I wouldn't try to stretch this process out over more than a couple of sittings. If the behavior is ingrained, you shouldn't sacrifice your practice time to accommodate it. You'll have to keep the cat out of your room or otherwise out of contact while you practice.

    It's not the end of the world. Both you and your cat have to learn letting go, especially since she is old. It is much better for her if she can approach death without a clinging mind; and you can help her move through old age and death by keeping clear relationship yourself. There is a time for closeness and a time for letting go. Include both in your days together. This brings up the question of how much your clinging is reflected in your cat's actions.

    Cats and dogs and other pets do not have a different inner job from humans. Their outer form and situation are different, but their inner job is also to wake up. We can help them and ourselves by maintaining boundaries when appropriate.

    This reminds me of a story I heard about a Zen Master who approached someone's pet dog, crouched down, and said "Hey! This dog body is no good. Next time get a human body, [so you can wake up] okay?"

    When it's time for cuddles, you can give your cat all the affection you can muster; but know that there is kindness that you do for your cat beyond cuddles, and that includes your meditation practice, as well as demonstrating that although you are in different rooms you are still together and still loving. Even after bodies go away, you can still be together and still be loving.
