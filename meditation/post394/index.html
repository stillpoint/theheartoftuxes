    meditating on a thought vs. mindfulness meditation. can someone explain?

    The different methods have different functions.

    Although the word meditaton is used widely to describe countless different practices, if we are strict about the meaning, meditation means a mind that doesn't move. It is pure open awareness that neither moves toward nor away from any object. For that mind, there is no separate object, no 'inside' and 'outside'.

    What many people call meditation is perhaps more accurately called concentration practice. This includes any object-based practice where one limits one's attention to the object. One can practice this way with mantra, as well as with breath, bodily sensation, or sound. It is not the only way to work with these objects, but they are often used in concentration style. This is the form you describe: distractions may appear, but you keep returning to the object of concentration.

    Mindfulness is the practice of presence without limiting attention to a single object. There still may be a theme, such as being mindful of one's emotional states, or being mindful of one's relationship with the breath; but it doesn't exclude or seek to withdraw from other events that arise. Mindfulness is a good companion to concentration practice, since concentration promotes deep clarity and stillness that can be used to penetrate delusion, while mindfulness trains you to be aware in daily life. If you only learn to concentrate, maybe you get some kind of special perception; but if you have no mindfulness you won't be able to use that perception in your daily life â€” events, emotions, habitual patterns will still control you.

    Mindfulness will often work with an anchoring object. This can be especially effective if you also work with that object in concentration practice. For example, you might continue being aware of the breath, but you no longer withdraw your awareness from other things; the breath just anchors you in reality as you go about your other regular activities. So the objects of sitting practice and workaday practice could be the same, or could be different. If your ability to stay aware is fairly strong you could also work without an anchoring object.

    'Meditating on a thought' usually means contemplation. (These terms may be used differently in various traditions. For instance, Christian traditions may completely transpose the meanings of contemplation and meditation.) It can be of benefit to consider certain themes and work with them analytically, psychologically, devotionally, or in other ways. For example, you can contemplate your impermanence by considering the truth that you must as some point die and that you don't know when that time will come. You can think about all of the ways that death could come to you, both probable and improbable ways. You can think about the processes of the body, always changing, never remaining stable, always depending on the right conditions. And so on.... This is a contemplation of impermanence. 'Meditation' on impermanence would be something like Vipassana practice, where you actually use your concentrative ability to apply attention to the sensations of the body, to actually experience directly their insubstantiality and ever-changing nature.

    Another way yet of working with thought is hua-tou (hwadu in Korean), which means "word-head". If the word and its meaning are the 'tail' of the word, the 'head' is that which comes before these appear. In this practice, one usually works with an assigned question, which could be somewhat specific (like what is correct relationship between a student and teacher) or could be vast (like what is your true nature, or what is the true nature of everything). These questions tend to pivot on a short phrase or single word that is used to repeatedly bring up the questioning mind. When that questioning mind arises, one remains in it without moving, without trying to fill it in with the 'tails' of words and concepts. This practice is popular in some Zen schools, and is one way of working with koans (teaching cases engaged through questioning rather than analysis or speculation).

    These are not by any means all the styles or even categories of what people call meditation. Some practices combine aspects of some or all of the above styles.

    The right method for you depends in part on your aim. What are you hoping to achieve through meditation? For that matter, what are you hoping to achieve with all of your effort in life? The two aims could very well be the same, in which case your meditation will have a very wonderful and powerful purpose.
