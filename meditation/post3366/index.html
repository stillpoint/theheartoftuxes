    TheHeartofTuxes

    The nature of things is to come and go. If we attach to any particular state, it is a mistake, a failure to recognize the true nature of things. Our practice is meant to serve us within the true nature of things, not to chase the unreal.

    In meditation, you practice your own freedom from stressful interaction. This is the part you control: your own attitude. The outer state or situation is not your jurisdiction.

    You train your ability to show up openhandedly, time and again, in all circumstances. In part it is for you, to heal your disconnection from reality; but it is also for all others -- to train you in showing up for others, in all kinds of situations, not only your favorite, not only what you find easy.

    Therefore training should continue in both easy and difficult times. If we can only be openhanded in easy times, difficulty will always control our life; we will always have to avoid or try to destroy the things that are not our favorite. All problems in relationship, from drama in personal romance to world war, come from this inability to show up openhandedly, and our sense of absolute need to have our favorite.

    Regardless of the feelings that come, stay, or go, you can practice non-attachment, non-grasping, non-manipulation, non-disturbance. When this ability becomes strong, there is an inner comfort despite the coming and going of outer experience.

    Some concentration-style practices promote blissful feelings that, while beside the true point of practice, can be uplifting and nourishing to the meditator. If you do concentration style but are still put off and discouraged, there are instructions about going deeper. These are passed on in a live study relationship.

    In general, a few remedies:

    • Connect with your correct and deep motivation. This goes beyond just picking a goal (much less a favorite), and requires connecting with both understanding of the big picture of life and death, and the emotional involvement that constitutes the energy of practice. One has to feel the urgent and sincere desire to clarify life, wake up, and live compassionately.

    • Learn one method, learn it well, and apply it meticulously. Many people fall off the path because they don't really understand what they are supposed to be doing moment to moment, or are confused or ambivalent between various teachings and paths.

    • Acquire support: teacher (the most influential support!), practice group or community, course of training, schedule, practice space, living environment, healthy and regulated and practice-supportive lifestyle, practice-aligned relationships, and taking on commitments and challenges in balance with your level of practice.

    • Do more. If you are doing proper practice and not seeing results, you most likely need to do more. All of the former still applies: you need to know, beyond doubt what you are supposed to be doing with your body and mind, and you require the proper conditions (teacher, teaching, relationship, environment, health, etc.); but if the conditions are sufficient, you just need to do more practice.

    Most of the day, for most of our lives, we create division and dissension within ourselves. We act from habit and reactivity; we act with lack of awareness. These constitute numberless causes of instability and stress, which may not be counteracted by a single 20-minute or hour-long sitting every day. Not to mention that most of us are not even that consistent. To get more sanity in our life, we need to do more sanity.

    Also, practice is itself the thing that inspires interest in practice. We can't just wait for the divine inspiration to hit us; we need to do the things that generate inspiration; and that means more sitting, more walking meditation, more prostrations, more devotional practice, more visualization, more chanting, more mantra, more moment to moment mindfulness.
