    How can I use meditation to deal with intrusive thoughts?

    Meditation is dealing with intrusive thoughts.

    You let go of the thought that has appeared, and return to the object of meditation. Practicing meditation means learning, training, and strengthening the letting-go and the returning.

    As the meditator advances in practice, the object of meditation itself can be let go of, and stability in awareness remains. Thoughts may arise, since the seeds of those thoughts — their past causes — are still ripening and coming to fruition; but the meditator's mind is not moved by them. One doesn't follow the thoughts or make new ones; they only arise and pass away in the awareness, like clouds arise and pass in the sky.

    So the short answer is that practice is necessary. If you want to get better at playing hockey or playing the piano, you practice those disciplines. If you want to get better at letting go of thoughts, you practice letting go of thoughts. Not just once in a while, but regularly, frequently... day to day and moment to moment.

    But although meditation is a cure for attached, habitual thinking, it is not the only way; and it may not even be the best way for you if your thinking is so overactive that you can hardly bear to sit still with it. If professional help didn't help, then you may need to get different or possibly more skillful professional help. You may need to help yourself by trying again and again.

    In terms of personal practice, using the body is often most effective for dealing with rampant overthinking. A vigorous, whole-body-and-mind practice like prostrations helps the mind and energy to settle quickly. Other rhythmic physical disciplines like running, swimming, and cycling, as well as movement meditations like Yoga, Taiji, and free dance can be very helpful.

    The tension and upset in your gut are helping you to be aware of what you are holding. When you hold, you make your bodymind uncomfortable. Letting go of the discomfort will help you let go of the holding and worrying as well. You might try imagining releasing the things that you latch on to as if they are leeches or tentacles that you are disengaging from. Help yourself disengage and be clean and comfortable just this moment. And then repeat, and repeat.

    Some examples of grounding physical practices are noted in my previous post, including information on bowing/prostration practice.

    Ultimately, the cure for worry is understanding your true self. Because you don't know who or what you really are, you mistakenly attach to the form of your body or forms in your mind and try to hold on to them. You fear loss; you fear change; but all forms are always changing and changing. No holding is possible. You are not your body, and you are not your thinking. They are merely temporary shapes that appear and disappear, like foam on a wave. You have to understand your true self, then all worries and other difficulties will come clear.
