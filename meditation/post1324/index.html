    Do you know the story of the 'second arrow'?

    Someone might think that great masters or beings like Bodhisattvas and Buddhas are magically exempt from pain. It surely can seem that way, because they carry themselves with such dignity and even-mindedness that it seems they are not the slightest bit beset by discomfort.

    For example, one of my masters had diabetes and a heart condition, but never ever complained or curtailed his own practice. In fact, in addition to leading a worldwide community and undertaking all kinds of tasks, the master's personal practice was much more vigorous than that of even most young, strong students. So if one were apt to jump to conclusions, one might think that the master had some special protection from difficulty.

    Thus there's a story about someone with that kind of thought approaching the Buddha and asking if he ever actually experienced pain. "Yes," said Buddha, "I experience pain just like you do; but I don't get the second arrow."

    This story is likely connected with the Sallatha Sutta (the Arrow Sutra) in which the Buddha explains the difference between common people and practice people. Faced with similar life circumstances of pain, sickness, old age, and death, we all encounter similar difficulty. But people who practice well and are in command of their minds don't add to the difficulty with their minds.

    So it is as if common people not only get the first arrow of pain and difficulty, they also get shot by a second arrow, which is their story about the situation: "Oh no! Now this! Shit! Now I'm sick for how long? Everything's going to be hard now. Oh, man, that's just great â€” sore throat and headache and runny nose. Oh, I'm so woozy it's hard to even make breakfast....." And so on, and so on. These kinds of thoughts are familiar to all of us. The 'poor me' thinking.

    And even before the sort of gross-level 'poor me' thoughts are the subtle background stories we add to the situation. Yes, there may be pain; but then we make "I have pain". There may be runny nose; but then we make "I have a runny nose". We make these ideas as if there is a self that exists, and as if this self is inextricably married to and controlled by a set of circumstances. On top of that we create a scenario that extends through time â€” a story, a drama with I, my, me as the main protagonist. So we also add suffering over extended time that isn't even happening! We take on days, weeks, and even years of illusory suffering.

    Truth is momentary. Moment World and Truth World are the same. Just this. Right now, what? Reading these words on your computer or phone, feet on the floor, maybe some ambient sound.... The plain unfolding of the moment, without anything added, is already truth. But we add all kinds of ideas to it, and these ideas create suffering.

    > Pain + "I have pain" = Suffering

    Your life does not extend over a period of time. Your life is completely momentary as it unfolds. So do you prefer momentary truth or painful story over an extended period of time?

    Notice that you ask "...why does it help you..." rather than saying "Oh, truth. Yes, let there be only truth." The thoughts, the habits, the tendencies all bend to serve the idea of "I, my, me". It is a very deeply ingrained story, but a story nonetheless. All problems come from this one story. All violence, waste, ignorance, and abuse comes from our mistake about what we are.

    So this is not merely an issue of optimizing your nice situation when you are ill. It is a matter of liberating yourself and the world from the innumerable troubles and tragedies that arise because we are attached to the story of self rather than living in Truth World.
