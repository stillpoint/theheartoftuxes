    > ...the moments of not being focused on anything are what cause dullness which in turn distract me.

    It is not the absence of an object of focus that causes dullness or distraction, but the expectation of having an object coupled with its absence.

    The habitual mind seeks (and ultimately makes) objects. Part of the function of meditation is to wean ourselves from object-relationship. In concentration practice, a single object is used to help detach from all other objects. In the subtlety of meditative perception we can see that it is not really a single object, but really more of a 'theme', a category to which we return.

    As you have experienced, sometimes the theme doesn't continue. It has to do with what you assume to be the object — how you define it and what you expect from it. If you consider breath to be perceived sensation, sometimes it will stop. If you consider breath to include a spacious, empty phase, the theme can continue.

    The space at the turning of the breath is actually a fantastic opportunity to practice non-attachment to objects, non-attachment to name and form. The attention can continue; it just is not tied to a sensation during that phase. This is where concentration practice heads in most Theravada traditions: once reasonable stability has been gained the practitioner begins to let go of the object of attention and rest in attention itself. The still turning of breath is an opportunity for that rest.

    Why do you say that there is dullness and distraction — what exactly happens? And does it have to happen?

    It is possible that in expecting a sensory object you interpret its absence as a problem. (Or you think lack of sensation means the breath-object is not there, which is not necessarily the case.) You may think that emptiness is wrong, and that there needs to be some effortful, dualistic activity going on, a meditator on one side and some perceived object on the other. This subtle attitude can cause you to seek recourse, as the mind grasps at other object-experience (and then rejects it as non-meditative).

    This kind of wandering can be relieved by becoming friends with the emptiness. Realize the subtle judgment and let it go. In many cases, just learning to relax solves a lot of these kinds of issues.

    Then the breath is actually holographic, a macroscopic representation of the life cycle of all phenomena: there is coming, remaining, going, and emptiness. All life is included in this study.

    I think u/black_coughy_drinker's tip is a good one. The circle of attention is clear, and anything can appear or not appear within it. Colorlessness, tastelessness is uncomfortable for the habitual object-related mind; but it is nonetheless completely valid and real. So we urge ourselves beyond the discomfort of emptiness.

    Ultimately, the emptiness is the source of wisdom.

    So this is one guidance for Anapanasati: attend to both the presence and absence of sensation.

    If it continues to be a big problem for you, you can change to a more stable object of concentration like a kasina (a disc or field of color or element). The process is the same: first use the object to gain stability, then move from object to resting in the bare attention itself.
