    Derealization/Depersonalisation after meditating

    People who experience traumatic depersonalization when meditating often share some common causes:

    • drugs
    • unclear method
    • unclear direction and a grasping or controlling attitude
    • sleeping/dreaming

    Which of these are part of your picture?

    Lying down invites the sleeping, dreaming mind to enter and overtake the meditation. Many people become confused between meditation and the content of the sleep/dream experience, and a deep attachment to the escape and (often) comfort of drowsy floating can become entrenched.

    If the purpose and method of meditation are not clear, that can not only lead to lax physical and mental postures, it can prevent you from getting the feedback that shows you when your mind is drifting down the wrong path.

    In some cases, people have some mental instability already in effect, and meditation can bring it too much to the forefront. In other cases, a wrong attitude or approach creates pressure that splits the mind. Terror and grasping are intertwined. How to get through it depends on what's happening in your mind. Both of these situations are simply the function of unhealthy attachments, but the latter can often be turned around with some proper instruction. The former usually requires some treatment or balancing influence before meditation can be continued.

    The expert opinions of doctors shouldn't be taken lightly; but on the other hand doctors are known for sometimes acting like experts when they in fact don't know what they're talking about. A doctor who doesn't understand the purpose and correct mechanism of meditation may only be able to address parts of your situation rather than the entirety of it.

    Without knowing more about your mind and situation, I can recommend a two-pronged approach that should help you and should also satisfy your doctor's advice:

    • get simple
    • check your lifestyle

    1) Undertake activities that help you arrive in plain, simple, everyday reality. Use your mind in ways that engage with plain, simple, concrete objects and events.

    Be physical. Don't spend too much time thinking or imagining. That includes being on the computer, watching TV or movies, and playing video games, which all open the throttle of the thinking mind. Instead, take walks, go bike riding, play sports, do some cooking, play with pets, do repair work, and so on. Use your body and hands.

    If you want a specific practice of presence, you can maintain a background awareness of your contact with the ground: whatever you are doing or feeling, feel your feet on the ground when standing or walking, or feel your butt in the chair when sitting, or feel your body on the bed when lying down. This doesn't have to be a laser-like focus; it can just be like 25% background awareness. This will help you "be here" instead of getting lost in vague states of mind.

    2) Assess your lifestyle and bring things into a natural balance. If recreational drugs, smoking, or alcohol are in the mix, start to let go of them. Check your diet and make sure it is healthy and regular (if you feel you're floating away, sometimes a good substantial meal can bring you back). Let go of very sugary foods and drinks and high-glycemic foods like potatoes. Regulate your sleep. Get enough exercise. Find the right balance between alone time and time enjoyed with others. In general, balance the most basic aspects of your life. This may take some time — forcing it will throw you off balance — but it will help you arrive in reality and feel more like you belong in it.
