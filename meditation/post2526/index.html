    Jhana advice needed. Experienced advice much preferable.

    Relaxation is important in meditation, and even more so in jhana practice. Although you should follow the instruction of your teacher first and foremost, I would tend to suggest that you breathe naturally, and that you take a deeper breath without a second thought when you need to.

    Many of the instructions that come from the East, and from antiquity, are aimed at a certain audience that has been enculturated with a very different relationship to inner experience, breathing, and meditation. Our modern society is very stressed, and our families don't tend to pass down a general sense of relaxation, of "it's okay". So when these kinds of issues arise for us in meditation they may blow out of proportion quickly, as we worry, and then worry about worrying, and then suppress, and then deal with the results of suppression.

    Learning and practicing relaxation would, I think, be of great benefit to you. It is a good basis for future practice. Zhan Zhuang practice in Qigong is very good for this. Another good, but different, approach is Savasana practice in Yoga. Taiji may also be helpful. There are many ways, but the aim is to learn and practice relaxation and trust of yourself, and trust of the processes that move through your life.

    The following is only my opinion, but I think that breath-based practices are sometimes not good for some westerners, because of the very feedback loop that you describe. Some get through it with good teaching (yes, find a teacher in person!); but others struggle for a long time. Just know that breath-based meditation is not the only game in town; and if you feel that no progress can be made it would be far better to use a different method until your relationship with breathing becomes more natural.

    Next, like guise_of_existence, I also encourage you not to place jhanas on a pedestal. The aim is awakening, which can be achieved with the most basic level of access-concentration. It is far more important to clarify your life direction than to develop special states of mind. Why do meditation? Why get up in the morning?

    When your motivation and purpose are clear, then even imperfect practice will benefit you and others; but when your motivation and purpose are not clear, even expert practice can go off and be harmful. Cultivate a care and love for all others. This will provide the right context for your practice — the bigger view — which will settle your mind and protect you from some of the inner dramas that would otherwise appear based on fear and worry.

    You can try other non-breath-based methods, including movement meditations that may be more natural for you. Avoid taking bits of teaching from here and there, because that can be inherently confusing, and can add to the doubt you experience within the practice itself.

    My recommendations:

    • Find a good teacher so you can have the living example and get in-person guidance and correction

    • Practice relaxation

    • Clarify your direction in spiritual practice and in life (which may not be separate)

    • Barring the presence of a teacher, learn one proven method, learn it well, and follow it closely

    • Seek out good and consistent teaching, so that one true and influential message can penetrate you deeply; and avoid flitting here and there, getting conflicting accounts
