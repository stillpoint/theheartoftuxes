    Posture is important for meditation

    This is common, and there are many factors involved. Although I can't speak to your situation without knowing it (and you) much better, I can speak in terms of generalities; and in general, the meditator should make the extra effort to maintain good form — in physical posture, mental posture, and relational posture. The meditator then develops the proper foundation, and over time becomes more comfortable within the form.

    New form is uncomfortable. Of course it is; it's unusual, and it doesn't go along with the habits one has developed. So in this area, discomfort is the feeling of progress, the feeling of breaking down old forms and gaining strength in new ones.

    Mind and body condition each other. A mind patterned in a certain way will feel at odds with a body that is arranged in a new way. The mind's lax, resistant, tense pattern will tend to battle the uprightness required in meditation posture. So it will take some time and some repetition of good intent to help the mind come around. But if one doesn't adopt an upright, wakeful body posture it will be more difficult to bring the mind around. The attachment to laxity and physical comfort will have the upper hand, and will determine how meditation goes.

    One point that many people — especially westerners — don't realize or don't take into account is that posture is supported by the flow of energy. Apart from the ingrained tensions that prevent certain alignments (and which themselves arise from improper flow over the long term), the upward-moving support that makes an erect posture easy to hold comes from flowing, circulating energy. It is not just a matter of placing the body into a certain position and then holding it with muscular tension. Physical, emotional, breath-based, and energetic ease are necessary.

    When you are well-rested, having had several good weeks worth of deep sleep at night, and when your blood and tissues are well mobilized and oxygenated with regular exercise, and when you are happy and enthusiastic, I would bet that sitting upright is not only much easier but downright natural. The vibrantly alive bodymind naturally likes that posture, finds it more comfortable.

    Conversely, when energy is low through deficiency or stagnancy because sleeping, eating, exercise, emotional relationship, and/or breathing are out of whack, the body tends to collapse, and physical effort is required to hold it up.

    The good circulation of energy is effected by balancing these lifestyle issues, but it is also effected by concentration. When the mind is brought to stillness in meditation, the energy flows strongly. So you can help yourself with a two-pronged approach: balance your life in these areas, and continue returning to the meditation method and disengage from any obsessive thoughts about the posture.

    In the meantime, keeping proper posture — or as proper as you can — will help you get stronger in proper posture. The supportive muscles will strengthen, the antagonistic muscles and tendons will loosen, and the psyche will become more familiar with the new form.

    The effectiveness of strength training can't be overlooked as well. It takes a lot of sitting in correct posture (and thus possibly a lot of discomfort) to train those muscles. The process can be sped up greatly by using weight training, yoga, pilates, or the like to build the postural muscles. In particular:

    • strengthen the core — abdominals, obliques, transverse abdominis, the muscles of the lower back and waist

    • strengthen the muscles that pull the shoulder blades in and down (moving them "toward the back pockets"), raising the sternum and collarbone up and out

    • strengthen the muscles that draw the chin in, raising the crown of the head and flattening the back of the neck

    • stretch the upper thigh and hip muscles, maintaining a balance between stretching and toning practices (as stretching without toning causes instability of the joint)

    Study the right setup of posture. The right firmness and height of support under the hips are critical. The hands should (usually) also be held at or just below navel-height rather than in the lap, so that the forearms are more horizontal than vertical. This keeps the weight of the arms from drawing the shoulders down and forward, causing a slump, adding tension to the upper back, and requiring the lower back and hips to push back agains the slump. The knees should be firmly rooted on the mat, or on supportive cushions if they can't reach the mat. This keeps the ileopsoas and adductors of the thighs from having to use tension to keep the pelvis upright and in balance. Sitting on the ground or similar firm platform, cushioned by a firm mat, is much better than a puffy or bouncy bed or foam cushion.
