    We have processes happening on many different levels. There are physical processes like digestion, for which there are organs like stomach, liver, intestine, etc. There are mental processes like thinking, for which there is the mental organ of mind, and the physical organ of brain. There are processes of relationship that appear among our actions.

    Energetically, ancient yogis from several different cultures also identified processes and organs. In Indian yoga these were called chakras. So chakras are not special; they are just organs and their processes, like lungs that breathe or eyes that see.

    What is important is how we use our organs, whether they are physical or energetic. Whether we are trying to maintain our body's life and health, or whether we are trying to purify our chakras, the important point is why, what for?

    People with wonderfully functioning bodies and energy systems can still use them improperly, selfishly, harmfully. People with sick bodies and problematic energy systems can still use them with compassion. This is important to remember.

    A balance between the various parts of self allows peace and clarity to emerge. If your thoughts are fighting your emotions, or if your body and energy aren't working together, it will be difficult to settle into clarity or have the delicate perception of how the world works. So, many courses of training exist for the purpose of balancing these different levels of self.

    When the parts of self work in harmony, it is like a tree that moves effortlessly with the wind, or like water that settles into mirror-like stillness.

    If you like chakras, you can study with a yoga master. But it's not at all necessary to even talk about chakras, as long as you keep your mind in correct relationship to the moment. When your mind is in correct relationship to the moment, everything comes into balance.

    This means, moment to moment only keep open, non-grasping awareness. Don't try to make anything happen, and don't try to avoid anything. Don't want or hold or judge or manipulate. Stay open-handed: anything can come; anything can stay; anything can pass away. This allows everything to find its correct balance, and soon profound awareness of your true nature will appear, and you can wake up from life's dream.

    Here is an exchange between the great Hindu yoga master Sri Ramana Maharshi and a visitor to his ashram, who asked about meditating on one point in the head, which some consider to be one of the chakras:

    >A visitor asked: "We are advised to concentrate on the spot in the forehead between the eyebrows. Is it right?"

    >Sri Ramana Maharshi replied: Everyone is aware, 'I am'. Leaving aside that awareness one goes about in search of 'God'. What is the use of fixing one's attention between the eyebrows? It is mere folly to say that God is between the eyebrows. The aim of such advice is to help the mind to concentrate. It is one of the forcible methods to check the mind and prevent its dissipation. It is forcibly directed into one channel. It is [only] a help to concentration.

    >But the best means of realisation is the enquiry "Who am I?" The present trouble is to the mind and it must be removed by the mind only.

    -from Talks With Sri Ramana Maharshi

    In East Asia, the hara or dantien is/are the focus of energetic practice, as opposed to the chakras in Indian and Tibetan yoga. There are many systems, with some overlap and some differences among them. But again, these are all just organs and processes; the important thing is knowing how to use whatever you have, and for what purpose.

    This is a synopsis of the chakras, by the monk and scholar of Tibetan Buddhism, Lama Govinda:

    >While, according to Western conceptions, the brain is the exclusive seat of consciousness, yogic experience shows that our brain-consciousness is only one among a number of possible forms of consciousness, and that these, according to their function and nature, can be localized or centered in various organs of the body. These ‘organs,’ which collect, transform, and distribute the forces flowing through them, are called ćakras, or centers of force. From them radiate secondary streams of psychic force, comparable to the spokes of a wheel, the ribs of an umbrella, or the petals of a lotus. In other words, these ćakras are the points in which psychic forces and bodily functions merge into each other or penetrate each other. They are the focal points in which cosmic and psychic energies crystallize into bodily qualities, and in which bodily qualities are dissolved or transmuted again into psychic forces.

    -from Foundations Of Tibetan Mysticism
