    >Sometimes this is exactly how I feel about my meditation sessions, and is the sort of thing that makes me wonder whether the time might be better spent on some other activity, such as yoga.

    Yoga is a valid path. (And there are many types of yoga: physical, devotional, meditative, action-oriented....)

    The important thing is to clarify your direction, your aim and motivation. Why choose any particular path? Why do meditation at all?

    The underlying issue is your life direction. What is today for? Why stay alive; why do anything?

    Your spiritual pursuit should align with your life purpose. If it doesn't, then obviously it's just killing time. So we ask ourselves what we're on about in life. Not that we need to know 'what we're going to be when we grow up'; but that we consider how to go about our days, what constitutes a good and harmonious relationship with each other, with our world and life.

    Is life just 'for you', to chase after desires and impose your preferences on others? One master says this kind of approach is "like a dog". This mind is always controlled by its like and dislike, a slave to habit and preference. It is not being a true human.

    What is your true human direction? This is something to be clarified over time, and revisited repeatedly throughout life. When you connect to the greater aspiration that is for all beings, not just 'for me', a great joyful energy can enter your life, and all kinds of quandaries (like what meditation to choose) can become clear. Your direction prioritizes these things.

    >Or to put it another way, how would I know if I am, in fact, doing 'real' meditation?

    You don't necessarily know. That's what the teacher is for: to point out your true mind and how to access it, and to watch you and support you and sometimes test your progress, and when you veer off, to point you back in the right direction.

    Then, later, you will know exactly how to keep your mind at any moment.

    Your job is to learn the method completely -- the why and the how -- and practice sincerely. If you keep your sincere mind, everything unfolds from that center. Practice moves out from your center. It's not a matter of checking yourself and analyzing what's happening; it's a matter of making the center correct and trusting what unfolds.

    You can try on your own to start if you like, as many people do. But there will soon come a time when you need to bring your practice into the light and learn all its aspects fully and meticulously -- many of which you don't even realize are issues until you've advanced considerably.

    If you find an authenticated teacher within a proven tradition, a great many doubts can be eliminated. Then you can go forward with confidence instead of always wondering "Is this the right way... or should I maybe try something else?"
