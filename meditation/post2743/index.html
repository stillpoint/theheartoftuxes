    Meditation to release trauma and overcome narcissism

    >...how would I go about achieving that balance...?

    Let go of the grip, and things will find their own balance. Old compasses used to have a switch that would keep the compass needle from moving around while stored. To use the compass you'd have to press the switch and free the needle. Likewise, if you let it all be and rest in Unknowing, your own inner needle can find its own way.

    The important thing in your meditation is the non-grasping attitude. Any of a number of methods are possible, but the essential point is to not make, want, check, or hold anything. Don't move toward or away from anything; just keep returning to the method. This will gradually bring you back to balance.

    Now, that's just speaking about the narrow field of meditation. Your best help may not actually be meditation, but rather working together with others. Put yourself in situations where you have to work or practice closely with others.

    Ideally, these situations would be overseen by someone who can guide your character wisely, but sometimes the clear intention of the group itself is enough to keep you on the right path. So, practicing with sitting groups, doing retreats, and working with others who have a clear direction would, I think, be very beneficial for you. It will get you out of your head about yourself.

    When you act with others you come up against each other: you can't hold your own habit, you can't only get your way; there will be many situations where you just have to let go of "I, my, me" and just follow the group situation. This is very good training for your character and for the flexibility of your mind. I believe it will help you get unstuck from the clutches of self-concern.

    For a while you may do well to submit to (healthy and beneficial) external structure -- practicing in a group, following a schedule of practice, being responsible for certain tasks. The external structure is gradually internalized, becoming a healthy ego through which further progress is possible.

    Psychological methods are not my area of expertise, but you may find some help there too. Tibetan Buddhism is particularly adept at working with the psychology, and is sometimes undertaken in combination with psychotherapy. We shouldn't expect meditation to cure all ills.

    P.S.: Don't trip on mushrooms and other substances. It's not that there's something inherently wrong about it, but digesting ego-dissolving experience is precisely the issue you're having difficulty with. No need to add more problem. To some degree, you can also help yourself by normalizing your body's chemistry.

    Edit: Many traditional spiritual paths prescribe foundational practices as a prerequisite to further training. For example, in the Tibetan schools you are often required to perform 100,000 prostrations, 100,000 mantra recitations, 100,000 offerings, and 100,000 self-dedications to the master or lineage. Among other functions, these practices serve to construct a healthy and Dharmically associated ego that can carry the practitioner far into practice. In other schools these practices may take the form of menial work (cleaning toilets is a popular task) that serves both as initial foundational practice and as the expression of awakening throughout one's meditative career. Robert Bly has pointed to the term "dust work" as the period in which we undertake grounded but seemingly mundane tasks, consciously or unconsciously building a foundation for future transcendent efforts.
