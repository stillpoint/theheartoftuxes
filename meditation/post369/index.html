    After one year of meditation, I think I'm starting to lose the grip.

    Ah, okay, that makes sense.

    >With my eyes closed I find that often it takes hearing a door shut or a bird call out to make me realise I was swept up in thought.

    Yes, so your hearing is helping you maintain that connection with reality. The vision is very powerfully related to awakeness vs. dreaminess, so you can get more help with open eyes.

    >At a certain stage I had to resort to counting to keep concentration.

    Not a bad practice in its own right.

    >I'm going to try keeping my eyes open and see how that grabs me

    But be aware that if it's based on like and dislike it goes counter to the aim of meditation, which is training the ability to be present with what is, regardless of like and dislike.

    "The Great Way is not difficult for those who do not pick and choose."

    In fact, the open eyes help train this because of our tendency to attach to objects. We get practice in dropping the attachment.

    Attachment to the external objects is often easier to identify than attachment to dream visions, sensations, or trains of thought: we may think that a vision indicates progress, or that a nice feeling means we're doing it right, or that a certain thought is an important realization; so we abandon the true center of meditation and more easily accept our "inner" attachments.

    Getting distracted by "outside" things is still actually an inner attachment: it's not the object but some thought related to it that hooks us. Ultimately, there is no inside and outside.

    If you're momentarily taken away by a sound or something you see, that can actually be a great moment of clarity. For a moment, the "I" doing meditation is cut off and there is only the door sound or bird call or texture of the rug, or whatever. Without resisting, this can be a moment of deep meditation.

    Keeping eyes open for sure can be more stimulating and agitating. Thoughts of dealing with things and people may appear. The mere fact of additional visual stimulation may stir things up. But this doesn't mean that we should retreat from the open-eye world. Our task is to develop our strong center so that we can be in the world without being lost in it. If we are to develop our compassion, how can we then abandon everything that would need our compassion? If we are to develop our wisdom, how can we then turn our back on the place where that wisdom would function?

    If opening the eyes promotes more agitation, it is our practice that needs to adapt. We build strength in our ability to stay neutral, to disengage with open hands. So opening the eyes is in fact a better training for life; we learn how to be free and happy in life rather than avoiding it. It is this skill of presence that we train.
