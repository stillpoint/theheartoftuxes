    Yes, the notion that emotions, including those we see as positive, are eliminated by meditation is ridiculous. Reactivity, yes. Personal identification, yes. But emotion is very much a part of the lives of great beings.

    What may very well happen, though, is that attachments and negative patterns that have formed part of one's previous identity get purified, which can be taken as a loss. If one thinks of reactivity as an essential aspect of self, losing it will seem tragic. But people surely make up a lot of stories about enlightened masters without ever experiencing their vibrant and creative presence, and people surely make a lot of stories about attainment without having it.

    The great being just doesn't identify with emotion. So sometimes we can distinguish between attached emotion (which is the common human state); reflective emotion, which flashes quickly in response to the situation, with little self-identification; and love emotion, which is only called upon for the sake of others.

    The Brahmaviharas -- the so-called Four Immeasurables or Divine Abidings -- are emotional aspects of enlightened mind: kind-heartedness, compassion, equanimity, and shared joy. The very training asserts an emotional dimension to enlightenment.

    The reasons emotions get suppressed have to do with mental dysfunction and/or misuse of meditation to control one's experience and expression. Addictive or depressive personalities often misconstrue and misuse the form of meditation to achieve an agenda of control. Concentration-style methods are particularly open to this abuse. This can happen consciously or unconsciously. But these folks aren't doing real meditation; they are doing some other mental regimen so as not to feel, or so as to feel something in particular.

    Even just looking at the nature of meditation, we can see that it has nothing to do with suppression; it goes in the other direction: pure, open, non-grasping, non-contriving awareness. It is like open space which offers no interference to the natural unfolding of a landscape.

    In my personal practice experience, meditation has helped tremendously with emotional healing, sophistication, and flow. Trust in myself -- the whole picture of my pure nature and my human nature -- has only benefited.

    I recall an article on a study showing that among major healing modalities, meditation contributed the most toward emotional intelligence (which surprised me a little, as I suspected movement practices could come out ahead). If I can find the article I'll post a link.
