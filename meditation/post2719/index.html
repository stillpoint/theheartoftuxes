    >Does this type of meditation differ much from mindfulness? I've only tried mindfulness thus far and it's the only thing I have to compare anything with.

    Mindfulness is a very good practice. If that's your only formal practice, it's still very, very good.

    Mindfulness employs a broad, open awareness. You remain aware of what's going on, moment to moment. There are many benefits, which I won't get into here. It is a natural way of being present with the actions and experiences that unfold in daily life.

    Concentration focuses on a single object, whereas mindfulness allows all kinds of objects to come and go. Concentration is somewhat sharp; mindfulness is broader, perhaps softer.

    The object of concentration may be a single visual stimulus like a colored disk that you view, or a repeating stimulus like a mantra (om mani padme hum is one of the most often used), and there are many others. Breath is used very often as an object of focus, and provides several advantages if it is suitable for the meditator: it mediates between active and passive, and between physical and mental; and it can be used as an object all the way to liberation.

    One keeps returning to the object, to the exclusion of other stimuli. The mind becomes progressively more focused and subtle. When the mind is very quiet, one experiences one's essential nature. In addition, the concentrative power is used in other practices that develop insight into essential nature.

    Mere concentration is not enough, because the moment you stop the practice the mind begins to return to agitation. Only seeing through to the very nature of being can overturn delusion and the suffering that comes from it.

    Some mindfulness practice use the concentration practices as a foundation, so the mindfulness is deeper, more vivid. One can use the breath, for example, as an anchor of presence as one goes through daily life. Mantra can also be used for both concentration and as an anchor for mindfulness.
