    Seeing dream images (but not falling asleep) during meditation.

    I'm happy to help, if I can.

    The central issue here is application of attention. Applying your attention is like shining a light: you don't know what will appear in that light, you just shine it.

    One of the aims of maintaining a single consistent place of focus is so that you can experience the true empty, ever-changing nature of reality. As you probably already understand, if you keep shifting attention you won't have the chance to perceive subtle, changing events. But maintaining attention on one spot allows you to perceive clear sensation, vague sensation, and lack of sensation. That is important information!

    The same dynamic that generates the dream images we've been talking about is the one that prompts you to seek out and require definite sensations: the psyche fears emptiness, so it tries to fill it in. Your task is to become comfortable with emptiness â€” not to make anything, and not to make an idea of nothing, but just to be with things as they arise, linger, change, and disappear. This gives you direct insight into the nature of the world.

    All of the above is one side of the issue. The other side (or one other side, at least) is that losing contact with sensation may mean the attention itself is more prone to wandering. Since you don't have some definite object to return to you may experience wavering. Some teachers may then recommend or allow you to find a more gross and continuously available object of meditation, like the movement of the belly or other aspects of breathing. I recommend that you consult a teacher in your style or seek out the source of the instruction you have already received, and follow whatever they recommend. It's important to stay consistent and be faithful to your chosen practice.

    Having said that, in my personal opinion if you are doing Vipassana/Samatha practice it is not bad to predetermine the spot beneath the nose as the area of attention, and stay there no matter what. If the attention wavers, great! That gives you a chance to practice bringing the attention back. If it is difficult, great! That gives you a chance to master being attentive in difficult circumstances. It's like a cyclist enjoying a headwind because it helps him develop power.

    I don't practice Vipassana style these days, but the teachers I've trained with and hung out with tend to recommend staying with it. They say that your sensitivity will deepen and you will be able to perceive finer sensations, so, no problem.

    >Is this too advanced for me?

    No, this is not too advanced for you. From what you have stated it just sounds like correct practice. You can do it.

    >...how would you recommend I deal with this feeling of not being anchored enough when focusing on my breath more generally?

    Don't check your feeling. There is a distinction between applying your attention, on the one hand, and feeling anchored, on the other. Your feeling will shift; if you attach to feeling you can't progress far. Checking whether you feel anchored is already not anchored, it's the movement of your thought to (again) fill in space and get a sense of something definite. Become comfortable with emptiness, comfortable with the free movement of things, comfortable with the indistinct, unknown, boundless nature of reality. And let it be. That letting-be is your correct anchor. Just return to the method again, again, again.
