    "Do nothing, just sit" is not so much a 'Zen' response as it is an oversimplification of Zen by people who don't know it well.

    Although one may often be encouraged to work out all kinds of issues on the mat — relying on one's deep Unknowing wisdom rather than techniques or strategies — really the aim in Zen is to express that wisdom in activity, in daily life. The deep, one-pointed purity is to be wielded from moment to moment to moment, in all situations, in all activities, in all circles.

    No longer a separate, incapable being seeking truth and freedom, you use your deep source continually, never falling out of union with momentary truth. This life, this moment to moment unfolding is the magnificent realization. Realization is not some 'thing' downloaded to you, as a small and separate being. If your realization is some spectacular event that you get, that means you are still separate from your practice of open awareness; you are still a ghost, cut off from the universal life.

    So even some spectacular event is not as good as your momentary integrity, your momentary trust of Unknowing. When you trust your before-thinking mind, you and universal life are not separate. That is realization. It's not something you get from outside, it's something you express from within. You real-ize it, you make it real through your own activity.

    > When you know your before-thinking mind, keeping that mind while sitting, standing, walking, or lying down is Zen.

    One of the major difficulties in coaching oneself is that the active, intervening coach is the same person as the student who is trying to let it all go. One gets into situations where one doesn't know whether to let go or to check one's condition or situation.

    Another difficulty is that one's limited view, habits, and misunderstandings are shared by the 'coach'; so the view beyond one's obstacles only gets accessed by happy accident.

    For these reasons and more, studying with a capable teacher is the number one thing you can do for your practice once you've reached the 'what now?' stage. A good teacher will help you learn in many ways, not only by hearing words and thinking about them. In fact, I believe most people discover pathways of learning they had never conceived of when they study with an awakened teacher.

    Also, practicing with a group and spending time in community with other practitioners teaches you in many ways. For one thing, you can get the precious modeling — the living examples — of how the clear mind is expressed in daily life. Our modern society is sadly lacking in examples of integrity. And if we don't witness these qualities embodied in our peers and elders they tend to remain only theoretical propositions for us, and somewhat threatening at that, not actual possibilities that we aspire toward.

    Also in community, we rub up against the edges of our own brittle, crabby, self-centered nature. This is of inestimable value. When we remain isolated we tend to fool ourselves about practice. We think that we're sincere, but actually we list to the side like a boat with a hole in the hull, or like a car with a wheel in the sand: our actual agenda tends to aim at preserving our comfort and opinion rather than moving beyond old patterns. On our own, there's not only nothing to keep us honest, there's also no new reflection of our character, no new challenge that we haven't thought of, and now new possibility. But in community, all of these appear in our experience. We get new inspiration from others ahead of us, others at our level, and also others who are facing what we have already faced. And in community we also discover our stubbornness, laziness, and other weak points more easily, since we will often have to give up our opinion in order to practice and work together. This is very good practice.

    From what you write, your understanding and aspiration are still wavering. Therefore structure is necessary in order to train them and make them firm. Once the moment to moment practice is very clear, and once aspiration is unshakeable, then you will be ready to do more solitary practice if that turns out to be your style; but many people remain in community because they realize that it is what they need to keep them sharp and on-the-ball.
