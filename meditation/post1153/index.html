    Patience is one of the Six Paramitas or 'Perfections': one of the aspects of enlightenment that must be developed. Be pure in your effort; only do it; don't judge your progress. For this to be possible, you must understand and have faith in your chosen method; thus the necessity of learning a proven method from an attained teacher, and learning it completely.

    In any case, you are not the best judge of your progress or what you have attained. You perceive it with your current flawed, incomplete, obscured perception. You haven't yet refined your perception and you haven't yet digested the teaching; so you don't actually know how far you've advanced or what you've attained. But if you judge your progress 'good' or 'bad', already you lose practice â€” you move out of clear, direct experience into the realm of concept and label ('name and form').

    Most of us fall prey to the story of our practice, as if it's a drama playing out across time. We think we have to steer what's happening. This is the ego at work, making its own story. But true practice is completely momentary. Drop it all in a moment and return to pure perception: that is success; that is attainment. Do you see what's in front of your eyes? That is success in practice.

    So there's a famous Zen saying:

    >"Do you see the crane in the pine tree?"

    It's not a secret code. It means "Do you actually see what's in front of you, or are you only thinking about it?"

    You're always experiencing impermanence. If you're experiencing something, that means there's movement, there's change. If it weren't moving you couldn't experience it; everything would just be still and separate and unrelated. In other words, impermanance is a necessary condition of existence. That's why it's one of the Three Marks of Existence. Impermanence and non-self mean that things can function and relate with each other and interact with your senses.

    But you seek some special event; and that's the big mistake in meditation: checking to see how things are going, and comparing your experience against your expectation. It's not that you aren't experiencing impermanence; it's that you expect something other than your experience. You don't believe in yourself. So you check and compare in meditation. Let go of that.

    In order to progress in vipassana practice you need to learn one proper method (there are various styles), and learn it properly, from a proper teacher, and then practice it faithfully. You will get results if these conditions are met.

    Don't try to force your experience into a particular shape. You have to be the clear witness, not someone meddling with outcomes.

    Also, don't equate thinking-knowing with real knowing. Direct experience is knowing. When consistent practice meets and blends with proper teaching, conception eventually develops. That is, the Dharmic teaching must repeatedly meet with your own experience; and this catalyzes the process of growth of understanding.

    But this kind of understanding is a secondary effect. The important thing is seeing clearly, which means don't touch! Don't check or manipulate your experience in meditation. If you've learned the method properly, just do it. If you're not yet confident in the method, find a proper source of teaching and learn the method well.

    You have heard from Goenka that there is a difference between intellectual understanding and direct experience. So at every turn, take the opportunity to let go of intellectual understanding and instead cleave to your direct experience. Without labeling and conjecturing, what do you hear right now? What is in front of your face? Where are your feet standing? Return to this plain, simple world of reality.

    It's not that you're not experiencing the truth; it's that it's more simple than you expect, so you discount it.

    If the stream of thinking is too active to even begin experiencing directly, that shows that access concentration has not yet been achieved, and you need to train samatha/concentration as well as gathering the conditions in your practice and life that contribute to calmness and concentration.
