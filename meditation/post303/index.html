    Should I close my eyes when meditating?

    Your eyes should be gently open when meditating. The purpose is to wake up to reality, not fall asleep into your own thoughts and dreams. So you practice harmonizing with reality -- with the situation you're sitting in -- by opening your eyes.

    Letting in some light will also help keep you clear. People who can only meditate with eyes closed almost inevitably tend to dream (and not realize they're drifting!) and become attached to sleepy comfort rather than training their ability to be brightly awake and just as comfortable with waking life.

    Sometimes meditators will think that it is easier to settle down with eyes closed; and that may be so. But it is wrong to think that easier is always better. As they say in the military: the more you sweat in training, the less you bleed in battle. The more you train with reality, the more clarity and confidence you will have moment to moment in real life.

    Keeping eyes open is actually an expression of compassion. It embodies the will to show up for all beings rather than sink into your own favorite situation. For example, the Bodhisattva of Compassion (in Buddhism) is named â€œThe One Who Perceives The Sound Of The World". His/Her eyes and ears are open to perceive and respond. (Not that this is limited to Buddhism; it's just an example.)

    Having said all that, there are certain exceptions. There are particular sleep and dream practices undertaken with closed eyes, but these should only be performed under the close guidance of a qualified master because we are generally not equipped to open the gates of our unconscious mind and tackle the material that arises without losing our center. The guidance of the master helps you maintain your correct center.

    There are also relaxation practices that are done with closed eyes, like savasana in yoga. These are not true meditation, but they can be useful practices. So in the right situation, like if you're doing relaxation practice or an assigned visualization, you can use closed eyes; but when you're doing proper meditation eyes should be open.

    This becomes ever more important as you progress to longer sittings, to sitting through tiredness, and to attending long retreats and even night-time or sleepless practice. The training you do with open eyes will serve you well on the path.

    It is said that â€œthe sage walks two roads": the true master has eyes open both internally and externally. Neither the inner not the outer world is abandoned, but a harmony is found between them. Indeed, the arbitrary distinction between inner and outer dissolves, and unity and completion emerge.

    Whether you sit on a mat or on a chair, you can keep your face upright (facing pretty much straight forward) while the eyes gaze down at an angle. If you look at images of the Buddha meditating you still see the pleasant, gently half-opened eyes of meditation.

    There is no need to focus at a target like a laser, just let the gaze be soft and relaxed. It sometimes helps to think of being open to receiving through the vision rather than going out looking for something. In any case, the focus soon turns to the method of meditation, and the eyes are just left to naturally receive whatever light may cross their path.

    When drowsy, some meditators find it helpful to raise the line of sight. The eyelids remain gently half-open, but the eyes themselves gaze more forward. This helps to brighten the consciousness, and can be an option from the get-go, if it works for you. But many find the calming influence of a downward gaze to be helpful in most cases. Calming, but not sleepy; so eyes open.

    Good luck, and thank you for your practice!
