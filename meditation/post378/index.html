    Best way to practice metta?

    You can do it either way. Practicing in the morning every day is wonderful, and has the benefit of bringing the mind of metta to your day â€” scenting everything you may do that day with the fragrance of loving-kindness.

    Usually, how your mind is in the morning colors how your mind is through the day. The old image of getting up on the wrong side of the bed and being grumpy all day is pretty accurate. So if you can bring forth a good intention in the morning, and furthermore steep in it through meditative practice, that has great benefit for others and for your own continuing practice.

    Metta and the other Brahmaviharas (the Four Divine Abidings or Apramana/Immeasurables) are practices of embodying aspects of enlightened mind. The most important basis for the practice is the wish and intent to help all other beings with the practice, to have that broad wish for the well-being of others. This keeps the practice pure, untainted by desires for your own advancement or good situation that, although they may be fine and even beneficial in themselves, also plant the seeds of self-cherishing that undo spiritual progress.

    At first, the benefit will be to your own local attitude and activity in life (and hence others will benefit from the change in your character); later the power of your practice may actually radiate to other beings as you sit in meditation.

    You can also, absolutely, do a longer session of metta practice weekly, or daily, or at any time. It is all of benefit.

    There will be many people who attach to the intellectual aspect of practice because of the way Dharma (and fake Dharma) is transmitted these days through books and internet, through online speculative discussions and truth-by-concensus meet-ups. Our society is already addicted to thought, worshiping at the altar of concepts; so it is very automatic for people to make the mistake of thinking that spiritual practice is primarily a conceptual process. This is an unbalanced view.

    I think that people who downplay the significance of metta are not very experienced in real training. It is a way that even a newcomer can quickly embody limitless mind/heart, and it infuses paths of training that may sometimes become cold and dry with a very lively humanity. Furthermore, and no less important, it actualizes your correct direction of practice â€” it keeps you on track. Practice is not "for me", but for others. When you practice for others, you and others are naturally uplifted; when you practice for yourself, you and others are naturally limited. Metta and the other accompanying practices of Karuna, Upekkha, and Mudita literally keep you in your right mind about practice. Brahmavihara practice is highlighted in the ngondro or foundational practice guidelines in Vajrayana traditions, and in fact figures prominently in all the major vehicles of Buddhism.
