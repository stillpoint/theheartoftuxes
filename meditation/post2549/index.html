    Am I doing "empty mind" meditation?

    Whether or not it's exactly "empty mind" meditation is maybe not as important as what you are trying to achieve and why. Why empty mind as opposed to counting breath or anything else? And what does it mean that it "works for you"?

    It's great that you feel better after meditation, but that shouldn't be the goal. Good feeling is a nice side-product, and maybe a good sign. It's just not the main thing. Meditation should help you go beyond your like and dislike, to true happiness that doesn't depend on mood, or feeling, or internal or external situation.

    Actually, checking whether your meditation is "empty mind meditation" or not just constitutes another distraction from your meditation. The best practice is to continue the method, regardless of pleasant or unpleasant, "good" or "bad" experiences. It sound as though your mind does become more still with the breath counting; that's wonderful! But then stepping outside of that and trying to judge the experience that you are having or that you just had is a complete distraction from that stillness. In order to go deeper you'll have to develop the habit of letting go of that kind of checking and judging.

    A teacher, in face-to-face contact with you, will be better suited to judge whether you enter empty mind meditation. Many teachers may see it and not even talk about it, simply encouraging you to continue. If it ain't broke, don't fix it — no need to generate self-centered thinking about meditation; only do the meditation.

    After you have internalized and mastered the habit of letting go of checking, judging thoughts (i.e., after you have a very solid grounding in the correct method of meditation, and in not being moved by even spiritual thoughts), then empty mind meditation may be a good practice for you. But the thoughts that you have about it now suggest that the form of breath counting will be a good and necessary support, so that you can immediately identify when you have abandoned the method and have entered either dullness or thinking.

    In some cases, if gaps occur in the counting of the breath this can mean that a dullness has descended. It's a type of sleepy mind, where part of the mind has become more lax, even though you may still be awake and aware in other ways. This can be very comfortable and pleasant; and meditators who indulge in this state may think they've attained something special when in fact they're basically taking naps while sitting. This is another reason why simple adherence to the method is best. The form — the method and your posture — is your mirror.

    It may also be that you do encounter empty mind during your meditation. The gaps where no thinking occurs may be that kind of clarity. But if you notice that your mind is thoughtless, then that's no longer empty, no longer thoughtless — you are then outside of your experience, checking it. In real empty mind, you and the moment are one; so you aren't aware of it until you come out of it. Thinking about what your mind is doing is a very easy habit to get into, and a very difficult obstacle if you indulge in it.
