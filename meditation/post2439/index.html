    Hey r/meditation, what music do you listen to while meditating?

    What you're talking about is more aptly called relaxation. True meditation means keeping a still mind that doesn't depend on any object.

    Some object-oriented practices like listening to sound, counting breath, or gazing at a candle or colored disc help to still the mind and bring it to the point of meditation. But the problem with music is that dependence on the music to create a mood or state of mind means that your own ability to be centered, present, and okay is not trained. Rather than developing your own strength of mind, you give the power to the music or whatever else creates the mood. When the music is not there or not to your liking, then what?

    This is a big misconception about meditation: people think it's about good feeling. So they attach to feeling, and thus good and bad feeling control them. Keeping a still mind regardless of the feeling or mood means you can be free -- you don't have to automatically chase your favorite and try to escape your not-favorite. You can help yourself and your world instead of being controlled by habit.

    So true meditation doesn't try to manipulate the outside situation; rather, it adjusts and trains the inside situation -- your mind and its relationship with the ever-changing moment. Sitting (or moving) with a mind that is alert and open to the moment, regardless of the situation, is meditation.

    If you are still looking for relaxation and mood music, there's a lot out there. Music created for yoga or tai chi may be of interest. Check out Planet Yoga, for example.

    In the pursuit of more liberated and natural soundscapes, free of human conceptual influence, some musical creations approach and use actual natural sounds like wind, water waves, and rain, or employ white or pink noise. Check out the computer and smartphone apps that reproduce these sounds. Some are adjustable as to the intensity of the natural event (drizzle vs. rainstorm, for example.) Continuous, random natural sounds are sometimes thought to be ideal for encouraging a relaxed detachment from the external world, where the attention is present, but doesn't chase after objects. This may be like the settings of some monasteries and retreat centers, where the natural environment supports the practice. At some point, ones own center must be made strong enough to be able to venture forth into other situations, shifting the focus from self-centeredness to engagement and service.
