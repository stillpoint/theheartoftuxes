    Addicted to Meditation?

    Yes, it's very possible. Meditation itself is neither good nor bad; it's how you use it, and why, that make the difference. So for the addictive personality seeking to control its experience and either make a certain state or feeling arise or avoid certain states or feelings, meditation can be a very attractive prospect.

    It is precisely because meditation functions at the place of great influence (the interface of mind, body, and breath) that it is subject to addictive attachment. Many meditators aren't aware that they are using their minds — using concentration — to avoid seeing or feeling certain things. They go into the object of their meditation happily and blissfully because they are running away from other psychic material. This produces a shadow that controls the person from their unconsciousness.

    Also many meditators aren't aware, or try to avoid being aware, that they use the 'meditative' milieu and lifestyle to avoid certain responsibilities or interactions. Since whatever we ignore and sweep under the rug expresses itself nonetheless, this also produces a shadow. So we see many meditators appearing spiritual in their own minds or putting on a spiritual public persona, but projecting all kinds of judgments, opinions, and images onto others. Whatever is suppressed will tend to come out as projection onto others. Sometimes it's the notion that others are ignorant and we need to teach them what's right; sometimes it's raw sexuality that we can't seem to avoid; sometimes it seems like everyone else is lazy or petty, when in fact our own mind is the worst. This is the activity of the very common 'meditator' shadow.

    Nearly all of us already use our breath pattens and body tension to control our experience and our perception of ourselves and our world. We tense up against projected fears, and thus build an armor that seems to confirm our solidity and selfhood. We breathe shallowly so as not to encounter too much of life, and to keep from spilling over into expansiveness or intimacy. Or we breathe with forced tension in order to feel dominant and powerful. Tension in the gut perpetuates patterns of worry or pride. Tension in the jaw perpetuates stubbornness and oral patterns of clinging. Tension in the hands and eyes perpetuates patterns of grasping and manipulation. It can all be called addiction.

    So when we find a method by which we actually gain more control, the ego can have a field day. Truly, many meditators — among those who have built up some degree of concentrative ability — are addicted to the one-mindedness that excludes other experience.

    This is why — and I always say this — it is crucial to clarify one's correct motivation before undertaking meditation, and to keep clarifying it through ethical practices, study under the oversight of a responsible teacher, and practice together with other sincere seekers. One should not assume that meditation itself will provide the direction. It is a tool, like a hammer, knife, or sophisticated machine, that can be used for beneficial or harmful purposes.

    Therefore: Do you do meditation to get something for yourself? Or do you do meditation to wake up for the sake of others? This is the most significant, influential difference among the countless possible meditative practices. "For me" or "for others"? Doing it only for yourself, to get a good feeling or some worldly benefit or even to escape suffering by yourself, leaves you susceptible to addiction and misuse.
