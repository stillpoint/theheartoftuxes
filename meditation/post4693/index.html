    Where to start on my new practice of meditating.

    You may not be the one to know whether you're getting something out of meditation. First of all, trying just one or two times is usually not even sufficient to learn the method, much less practice it correctly. Second, consistency over the long haul is what brings the most identifiable and lasting results. Third, it is very easy for newcomers to miss or mistake the changes that happen within even a single meditation session.

    What do you expect to have happen? Your own expectation may be off, may be blocking your awareness of results, and may be interfering with the meditation itself. Correct meditation is openhanded; there is no expectation. If you are waiting for something to happen, you are not in the present moment, and you are not attending to the method. If you are wanting something to happen, or trying to prevent something from happening, that is manipulation, not meditation.

    In meditation, you watch; you don't touch. Don't try to make some feeling, vision, knowledge, or mind state. Truth is flavorless.

    Please keep that in mind: truth has no particular flavor. You gain control over your mind not by wrestling it into submission, but by learning to see it without reactivity. You disengage from the habitual reactions that have been ingrained. As your ability to let go of the death-grip on your life's 'remote control' increases, you become more free to choose consciously and healthfully, rather than always chasing something you want or running away from something you don't want.

    So you need to practice open-handed meditation consistently, to train the letting-go, letting-be skill. You learn to let go and let be, and then you can take clear and wise action. If you don't learn this, you will always be controlled by your thoughts and whims.

    If you find the meditation instructions in the book lacking, you can find a good method elsewhere. It's important to find a proven method (which often means a traditional one), and a method that aligns with your aim. Some practices are aimed at stress management, while other practices may have the side effect of stress reduction. Next, you need to learn the method well and follow it clearly. It won't do to dabble or go from one practice to another. Choose a good one and learn it well; then it will shed light on your mind habits and help you to be free of them.

    By far the best way to learn is to study with a master or a capable teacher, and practice in a group setting. Barring that, you can seek out a proven method that is conveyed well in print or online.

    Check out your local meditation centers. There are many sources. But if you can't immediately find a live teacher, or if you want to start sooner, I'd second prajnakaruna's recommendation of Thich Nhat Hanh's teachings (among others).
