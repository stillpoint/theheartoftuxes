    No, not useless! It is good to learn. It's just that book-learning and thinking-understanding can only help you up to a point; and then correct practice is necessary.

    It's maybe something like researching a trip: you get the maps and guides, you gather recommendations, and you make some kind of plan, whether it's very detailed or very open. But in the final analysis, you have to do the traveling; and actually doing it can't be conveyed only in words and concepts. Direct experience is necessary.

    Accessing reliable teaching is all the more necessary when a person is learning and practicing alone, as misunderstandings may continue for long stretches, and commitments and efforts may be based on them, making a lot of mess that has to be undone in the future.

    But having said that, no-one's path is a straight line. Some practitioners are fortunate to access a good teacher early on. Others have to search and try a great deal more. And yet others (a lot of us) come and go, and waver, and get lost, and encounter unfortunate relationships or bad teaching... and find their way through all that. We can't say that one path is better than the other. All those instance of failing, getting lost, giving up, or misunderstanding may eventually unfold into brilliant practice, very clear direction in life, and enlightenment. We don't know.

    What are you interested in? There are many good ways. Also some not so good ways. But if you choose a traditional path and begin to learn it in depth, that will serve you well. Enlightened beings have been produced in many traditional paths -- Buddhist, Hindu, Christian, Sufi, Aboriginal Shamanic.... But it is good to (A) focus, sticking with one path and style of teaching for a while (say, half a year or more) and (B) avoid trading and thinking too much, add it will lead to mental indigestion -- believing in and attaching to concepts rather than believing in your true self, which is beyond concepts.

    You don't have to stay with one style forever; but mixing styles and teachings leads to a lot of confusion (and most people aren't even aware of their own confusion -- the confusion is disguised as knowing), and it leads to a sort of manipulative, egocentric, 'tinkering' mindset that is always busy trying to arrange things and never learns to let things arrange themselves.

    Next, there are often ways to access live teaching If you are willing to try. The thinking may say "there's no opportunity" but sometimes the real issue behind it is fear -- fear of something new, fear of being exposed, fear of losing one's own carefully arranged situation, etc.

    If you try, you may find that some temples and yoga and meditation centers offer work-study deals (karma yoga), some offer free public teachings or teaching by donation (pay what you can afford), and some have programs that are accessible at a lower cost (for example, by attending less frequently). You may also find courses available for auditing at local universities or community colleges, or programs offered as part of medical or mental health initiatives. If you try, many possibilities open up.

    If you want to say something about your area of interest, maybe I or someone else can suggest resources. I don't want to push you into a path that's not freely chosen by you.
