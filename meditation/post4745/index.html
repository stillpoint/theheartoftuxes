    Group meditation advice?

    I've taken and taught classes, done committed weekly or bi-weekly group practice, participated in many retreats, and done many years of residential practice. I've also done a lot of solitary practice, including many solo retreats. So I've seen both the group and the individual side of practice.

    Right now you are learning. You don't have to commit to practicing with a group you don't feel comfortable with; you can just learn the method, try it, have some feedback about practice from the teacher, and get a first limited taste of group practice.

    I always suggest that if your mind and practice are not yet stable, you need to help yourself with the right practice environment and relationships. In some cases, being around other overtly suffering people (and who of us is not suffering and beset by their karma?) who are practicing will be more valuable than being around average people who are not facing themselves. In other cases, a student may find it destabilizing.

    I suppose you could ask yourself where the discomfort lies: are you being triggered because you are also being challenged to face raw issues like vulnerability, habit, loss of control, etc.; or is it really a case where you are being distracted from spiritual focus?

    When we are in meditation, we are the same. Our inner mind is the same. Only the outer story differs. This is the point of meditation: to get rooted in the essential mind, from which equanimity, openness, compassion, and wisdom arise. At a certain point, if we are to accept ourselves completely, we will need to accept anyone who sincerely seeks true practice. We will need to realize that all the world's difficulty is one basic difficulty — misunderstanding who or what we really are — and that is the basic work in life that we share.

    But if the tone or direction of a group is not for you, you don't have to commit to it just in order to fulfill the image of a 'good student'. If you are being sincere, you can trust yourself. Inside, you know whether you are running away from something or whether you are moving toward a clear spiritual goal.

    It's not a question of right or wrong decision; it's about what you need to ensure your proper unfolding, in this most central aspect of life. For sure, proper training and ongoing relationship with a teacher and spiritual group is critical. You won't go far without these. But unless there is an inner certainty (not the kind of adolescent "i'll love you forever" certainty, but a knowing grounded in self-knowledge), you might expect to move around from time to time as you find your way. When you find a situation that takes you beyond your small self, you can commit strongly. In the meantime, no need to either go along with knee-jerk aversion or idealistic clinging. Just see the course through to the end, and move on with your new knowledge and best wishes for everyone.

    ~

    For what it's worth, if an addict can truly move on, the suffering they went through can provide the impetus for great commitment and attainment. Some great teachers have been addicts. (Issan Dorsey comes to mind.) By the same token, some dark chapters in spiritual institutions have emerged around addictions kept secret. In many ways, the addict holds more than their share of the group shadow... and every group has a shadow.
