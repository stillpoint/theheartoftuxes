    You should follow your teacher's guidance on it, because approaches will differ, and the way it fits in to a broader training will differ from teacher to teacher, school to school.

    The main thing in any practice is to be sincere. If you realize when gluttony or craving is arising, let it go. If you don't realize when it's arising, seek a better mirror, better conditions to help you be aware (teacher, group, setting, better understanding of method, different method, more rigorous form, sensory support, etc.).

    Hindu practice streams often tend to aim at dissolution and merging (with the divine). There is a blissful aspect to the practice, and that is not to be shunned. Western conditioning may arise as an obstacle, in the form of shame at any kind of pleasure. So it's important to be sincere and to be clear about your direction. Don't just give lip service or wishful thinking, but know in your heart -- make clear -- that the purpose of the method and of the dissolution and of the pleasure or bliss is to attain the divine. If that is clear, there is no need to either pursue or avoid good feeling, comfort, pleasure.

    In fact, in the right context bliss can be used skillfully as an encouragement to practice. It becomes a far more fulfilling substitute for our usual sensual addictions. The trouble comes when the bliss, rather than the divine, becomes the focus.

    In some teachings there is no distinction drawn between bliss and the divine; and here it is important to understand what constitutes melting into meditative bliss versus attaching and indulging in preferred feelings. There are many good approaches, but receiving consistent guidance within one of chosen approach is important.

    People who learn methods in isolation, getting snippets online or in magazines or books, are often blind to the far broader and more integral path from which the practices derive. Åšavasana is a wonderful practice in the right context; but you can trust your concern about falling into dreaming or only relaxing. It could promote a dull, self-centered, or isolationist approach to life. The broader issue is What is the right function of relaxation, dissolution, and bliss? How are these benefits properly expressed in one's life? How are they applied in everyday situations to help yourself and others?

    For instance, if a toddler is reaching toward a red-hot barbeque, our correct function isn't to do Åšavasana or chant "Om", dissolve in bliss, and realize the oneness of all things! Correct function is to stop them, to protect them. Åšavasana can deepen your experience of unity, bring out your divine nature, refresh your bodymind, and provide a platform for other spiritual practices; but the essential question is how all of this functions properly in life.

    That is what the broader training is for: to help you clarify your direction and function, and to help you attain and practice them. Don't try to do it yourself, because your blind spots will remain your blind spots, and your limitations will limit you. Go to a teacher and course of training that can enlighten your blind spots and help you go beyond your limitations.

    Having said that, when trying Åšavasana as an isolated practice, rather than struggling with the notion of gluttony you might couch it in terms of an encouragement to let go and let what you really are be. What you are settling into is already true, already whole. You are just trusting that. Any good feeling is an encouragement to allow truth to be what it is, without your interference. Any bad or neutral feelings are also medicine, also truth in themselves.

    Consider adding a 'waking up' practice of open-eyed, open-handed attention at other times in the day. (As would be part of a much more complete system of training.)
