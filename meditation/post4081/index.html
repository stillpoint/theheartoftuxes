    I'm want to do 2 hours meditation in the evenings. Better to split it up, or do it all together.

    Being aware of each step in detail is a good meditation for staying grounded and concentrated between sittings. It is simple, and it has a clear form. Dividing it into the various parts — lift, swing, plant, roll/shift weight... lift, swing, plant, roll/shift weight — helps keep the mind from wandering, and provides a good reflection of practice. That is, when the mind does go away, you can know it. When the form of a meditation is less clear or structured, it can be more difficult to identify the times when you've gone away.

    It sounds like you also want a practice that you can do outside of formal meditation time. For moving about the city, running errands, doing work, engaging in entertainment, and all of the things you do where you relate more to the environment and to other people, I recommend just keeping a background awareness of the feeling of the feet on the ground. Keep 20% or 30% of your mind in your feet as you go about your day.

    When walking or standing, keep some awareness of the feet on the ground. When sitting, keep awareness of your butt on the chair and your feet on the floor. When lying down, keep awareness of the areas of contact with the bed or sofa or ground (the "footprint" of your body). In short, always keep some awareness of your contact with your physical foundation — the place where you relate to the Earth and its gravity.

    As you relate to your place of contact, the place where you are, you can give up anything "extra" to that place. If you are carrying physical tension, or feelings that are not necessary, or ideas that are not helping, you can release them at your place of contact. Walking can be a step-by-step letting go; reclining can be your place of releasing and resting. It can be very profound to engage in work or listen to someone while you are deeply grounded and present, having let go of your holding and your opinion.

    It is possible to maintain this background awareness throughout almost any activity. This not only helps you to stay present, it also helps to bring you into your body (and to liberate you from your thinking mind). Finally, it has a grounding influence that is very helpful when energy might otherwise fly upward and outward, as when you are overthinking, worrying, fearful, in a chaotic situation, or going off into lofty or dreaming states.

    Exerting yourself with your whole body helps to settle your energy. I am partial to prostrations; others like sun salutations. These movements circulate and clean your energy strongly, after which you feel much more settled. Bagua circle walking is also a very excellent energy mover and cleaner.

    One activity that I haven't yet mentioned, which I find to be one of the best complements to still meditation is free dance. Free dance has a very unique dynamic, which makes it superior in one area to all of the other practices mentioned: it moves from the inside, out.

    Free dance is a unification of body, breath, emotional heart, intention, and thought. It trains the intuition because it hinges on spontaneity — allowing what arises in you to move as it will. Instead of imposing an external form like steps or moves (as in sports, exercise regimens, Taiji or yoga, or choreographed dance), free dance expresses exactly what is arising in you moment to moment. The more closely you listen to, track, and express your inner movements, the more you connect to the movement of the world. When all aspects of self are aligned with the momentary situation, that is complete integrity. The will to listen and express sincerely comes closer and closer to the unfolding reality of the moment, and can merge with it, becoming pure spontaneous wisdom. It is like an exuberant culmination of Zen.

    Free dance can digest any energy, purify any emotion, and bring healing to any aspect of self.

    If you are a person for whom simplicity is beneficial, then find a simple mix of still, intermediate, and active practices, and just keep doing it. If you are a person who needs variety, establish a foundation practice that you can return to and evolve with, and then add other activities as helpful. If you ever find that the whole thing has become too complex or too heavy, don't throw the baby out with the bathwater: returning to a simple moment is wonderful, and enough.
