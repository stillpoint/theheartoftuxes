    Overcoming passivity and lack of assertiveness by meditation

    It can help, in that -- if performed correctly and consistently -- it can reveal the true nature of being that is uncontrived and undefended, and it can ground the meditator more and more in that reality.

    But often meditation is not the main activity or best remedy for character flaws. We have to be careful not to think of meditation as the cure-all. In traditional paths of training meditation is only a part of a much broader education and practice that includes physical practice, working together with others, meeting challenges offered by the teacher, following lifestyle guidelines, and practicing ethical discipline (among others).

    In terms of social anxiety, often the best remedy is to commit to situations where you have to meet and work with other people. From student council to volunteer work to part-time job working with the public, there are many opportunities to practice being with others.

    Like all neuroses, the root of laziness, social anxiety, and lack of confidence is self-centeredness. The mind continually turns back to one's own situation and considers how to preserve it, or looks with fear to the possibility of discomfort in the future. This is already a very tight, uncomfortable mind. It's just that the self-concern clings tightly to what it knows, and fears what is new, even at the expense of movement and vitality.

    It would be much better to understand that no situation is satisfactory in itself. Only an open, ungrasping mind makes a situation satisfactory. Knowing that everything will always be inherently flawed may spur one to go ahead and try something, if only just for the movement it brings.

    Here's a very useful tip for situations of social anxiety: care more about others. Compassion or even the most basic kind feeling turns around the debilitating self-concern. So putting others at ease can help you be at ease; and doing work that helps others builds the broad, noble self rather than the small-minded self.

    Another tip: do what you love, and love what you do. When you participate in the moment you enter the flow; and someone who is in the flow appears at ease and vibrant to others. Whether you're in the flow because you're doing the thing you would be doing if you were alone (because you love it so much), or whether you're in the flow because you've decided not to waste another moment in putting on masks, enjoying the moment for what it is puts you in command of life.

    Counterintuitively, perhaps, it's surrender -- giving yourself over to the changing situation -- rather than control that puts you at ease.
