    Is it disingenuous for a teacher (Nisagardatta, Watts, Tolle) to claims that 'you are that'..ie..pure consciousness ?

    My friend! Please don't assume that your limited view is anything like the view of awakened beings, or even of relatively advanced practitioners. Many, many, many people have had glimpses of the true nature of mind â€” that pure consciousness. Just because you haven't had a clear experience take root doesn't mean others haven't as well.

    For someone who has seen that the sky is blue, belief has nothing to do with the matter.

    But then you may hear them talk with a sense of certainty about it; and when questioned, they seem to answer effortlessly. Maybe it means something, maybe it doesn't. The point is that you don't know.

    So the central point here, and the vital point, is that you don't know. So never mind whether to believe or not believe. Find out! If it takes a lifetime, find out!

    If you find that a teacher's words are reasonable in your experience, and if you find that their guidance is accurate every step of the way, then you may intelligently rely on their guidance to continue your experiment: you may look where they point. But you have to do the seeing yourself.

    Finding your true nature is not a matter of understanding logical arguments or buying into someone else's strongly held beliefs. Finding your true nature is completely a matter of seeing it for yourself, beyond all doubt. This doesn't come from thinking; it only comes from correct practice.

    So please put down your thinking, don't seek a short-cut to belief, and take steps on the path of finding out for yourself. Follow the guidance of the teachers who have walked the complete path, and who can help you along every stretch; but walk the path yourself.

    Next, besides putting down your thinking, you must also put down your feeling. Your true nature doesn't depend on a feeling. Pure consciousness isn't a feeling. Feeling may sometimes appear within pure consciousness, like a shoe or flower may appear within a mirror; but after the object is gone, the purity of the consciousness and the emptiness of the mirror remains.

    It is much, much better to be honest and know that you don't know than to hold onto a faith that you haven't earned, a faith that is not really yours. People who hold strongly to belief are enslaved by their grasping. We can all relate to desperately wanting to know the answer; but if our wanting makes us accept something less than certainty, it can sidetrack us for years and lifetimes; it can bury our true wisdom. The brutality of human history is made up of people and groups ignorantly clinging to beliefs that separate them from their wisdom. Don't go that route.

    Like Socrates, say instead "I don't know, but where I'm different is that I know I don't know."
