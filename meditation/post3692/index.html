    You can get teachings for free in many cases. For example, some centers or individual teachers hold public meditation sessions that are 'pay-what-you-can'.

    In counting breath meditation your should be natural, not forced. This is important, as breath influences the mind strongly. If you fight or force your breath you will almost certainly experience the results of this imbalanced relationship, with tension or difficult mind states, lack of concentration, pain, or excessive dream visions or other uprising phenomena.

    So the breath should be allowed to go at its own pace.

    Let's start from the beginning, since a good basis is essential -- everything you attain is built on the basis:

    • Why meditate? What is the aim? Counting breath is a concentration-style practice that can develop focus and calmness. It is also used at times add part of Zen training. The aim of your endeavor should be clear, so you always know where you're going with your choices.

    • Have you found a proven traditional method, taught by a qualified source? A proven traditional method should address your aim; it should be able to get you there. Having a reliable method means you can let go of doubt and just do it.

    • Have you learned the method well? Do you know what you are supposed to be doing with your body and mind at any given time? It sounds like this part is not so clear for you, so proper teaching and learning is important. It should not be a speculative, hit-and-miss enterprise, but something you can throw yourself into with confidence.

    • Do you apply the method meticulously? If the above requirements are met, you will be able to trust what you are doing. Don't pick and choose, don't tinker, don't second-guess or try to re-invent the method. Only follow it. In ideal situations and difficult situations, only follow the method. This will bring you to your aim; it will provide the causes that bring about the desired results.

    If it's difficult for you to perceive your breath, other teaching and practice might be necessary. You might need to do some perception-and-relaxation exercises like Śavasana in yoga or Qigong guided breathing. One easy preliminary practice to do if this is the case is to lie down and place your hands on your chest and belly, feeling how breath moves in the body. You can combine this with sounding ('om', for instance) or even counting out loud in time with your natural breath. Both the hands and the voice then help you perceive.

    This is not 'counting breath meditation', though, which is performed sitting up in upright posture, with eyes open. It is a preliminary or companion exercise.

    Sitting up, you can also use your voice to help you. You could also use a mala, as someone else suggested.
