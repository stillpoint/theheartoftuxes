    Coffee and meditation

    I'm not a big coffee drinker, but I very much enjoy the occasional cup — Americano, Espresso, Latté, Cappuccino... especially Moroccan coffee ritual. I do notice coffee's destabilizing effects on my bodymind, and the effects can last for days. (Same with alcohol — the difference in meditation can be noticed for three days.)

    In my experience, there is a kind of jitter. I don't associate it with greater wakefulness or awareness, but as a kind of intensified activity. I've never noticed coffee helping my mind to be more sharp and aware, for instance. Rather, the heart seems to beat faster and there seems to be a buzzing or jittering kind of energy in the body. This tends to appear whether "my" energy is up or down, awake or quiet.

    I've read that some (or most, depending on sources) of the jittery quality that comes with drinking coffee is a result of rancid oils, from coffee that is not fresh. That includes most coffee that has not been recently roasted, and coffee from beans that are not fresh. This bears out a bit in my experience, since I tend to experience less of a jittery quality from Moroccan coffee, which should come from fresh beans that are roasted in your presence, immediately before being brewed. There tends to be a more mellow quality.

    With all coffee, fresh or not, I experience a sort of seepage of energy. It feels expansive on a deep bodily level, after which I go through a more tired or depressed period. This sort of "coffee hangover" can be present for several hours on the day of drinking, for instance, and is still noticeably but subtly present on the next day, and occasionally on subsequent days as well.

    If I weren't vigilant of my body and if I didn't practice meditation, I doubt that the after-effects of coffee would be so noticeable to me, if at all. It certainly doesn't help me to focus, and usually is present as either an over-activity I have to deal with or, later, a slightly sluggish dullness I have to overcome. Occasionally I simply CRASH later in the day, collapsing energetically and emotionally.

    In Traditional Chinese Medicine, coffee is seen to act on the "Liver" and "Kidney" systems.

    • The Liver system governs processing of stresses, distinguishing and filtering toxic versus nutritive substances, and regulating energies — sending food energy, blood energy, thought energy, and emotional energy where they are supposed to go. If the Liver system is overburdened, the flow of these energies can go from smooth to pent-up or flaring. Physically, this can appear as twitches and tics; emotionally and mentally it can appear as an unsettled or frustrated or uncontrolled activity.

    • The Kidney system governs the deep storage of energy and deep patterning of the bodymind. If this system is overburdened, profound exhaustion and lassitude can arise. On a more subtle level, one can become disconnected with one's deep patterning, one's deep sense of root or purpose.

    In meditation, the effect may be the absence of a smooth and settled quality, and possibly shifty and jittery or frustrated movements of the mind (Liver), or sullen or tired — sometimes overwhelmingly tired — energy and difficulty getting rooted (Kidney). Both Liver and Kidney influences can produce headaches.

    I tend to see coffee and tea as substances that press down on the accelerator. They don't fill the gas tank, they make the engine turn faster. That's an oversimplification, but it's a useful consideration for me when I'm deciding whether to partake: will it be useful/manageable to have a brick on my accelerator for a few hours, and then experience the after-effects of that?

    Just FYI, one of my teachers brews coffee for himself every morning (through a gold filter, for some reason). I have never seen him in an altered state of energy or awareness because of it.

    Edit: Ayurveda offers profound and detailed insight into the connection between meditation and lifestyle (and in particular, the things we eat). You may find some very good information in r/yoga, and yoga forums in general.**
