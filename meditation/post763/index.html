    So we might distinguish between 'common' and 'normal'. Attached thinking is extremely common. Utter freedom of mind is normal, but very uncommon.

    Connecting with motivation can make an enormous difference â€” all the difference in the world. In Buddhism, there's a practice called 'The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind'. They are four considerations that actually turn the mind around from its twisted, attaching habit, away from the things that keep us going around and around without getting anywhere, and toward truth, nobility of character, and awakening.

    The basis of the Four Thoughts is impermanence; and this consideration is completely independent of religion or philosophy. It is not dependent on Buddhism or any other tradition. It is just seeing and taking to heart the actual nature of life.

    Typically the view is developed by frequently and regularly contemplating one's own inevitable death. This contemplation can utterly turn around your experience of having to push through meditation or other spiritual practice. It can bring tremendous energy and joy. It can enhance the meditation experience itself, as one realizes all the mental and experiential material that is not worth attaching to (and thus quite deep concentration is facilitated). It can put your life priorities into a very clear order, and one that you understand in a very grounded way. You can be joyful, eager to try more practice and take on more responsibility. Awareness of death can do all this.

    Within meditation, when you have the experience of impatience, you can know that it is based on attachment to a thought. You can bring awareness to that thought, acknowledge the thought, let it go, and return to the method. In the method there is no time. Meditation is completely momentary, not time-bound. Time also comes from thinking. No thinking: no time, no self, no impatience. So the job during meditation is only to return to the method, not to figure or struggle.

    This is the ideal; but of course we will have many experiences that are outside of the ideal, so it is also necessary to be gentle and compassionate with our efforts. If you don't have a support system around you, then you will have to be the one encouraging yourself, consciously thanking yourself for your sincere effort, and remembering the positive aims you have embraced.

    Finally, it's okay to be uncomfortable. It's totally okay! There's an assumption many new (and old) meditators carry around that it's all supposed to be cushy like the middle of a cloud in seventh heaven. That's just not the case. It's okay to be impatient; then you get to witness your impatient mind and become free of it. It's okay to have bodily discomfort; then you get to witness your reactions to it and become free of them. It's okay to have uncomfortable emotions or strange experiences; then you get to practice non-attachment to these things and become free of them.
