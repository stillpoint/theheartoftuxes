    Skip weekends? How important is it to sit every day?

    >Usually I do 30 min every weekday, and take weekends off just because of innate lazyness.

    First: laziness

    Next, your answer is already in your post. You skip weekends because of laziness. So how do you suppose your laziness will ever, ever, ever change?

    And next, you already know what you need to do and why. Don't put it on us. It's not our job to police you or convince you; it's your job to understand the value of your own life, whatever time you have left of it, and find your own motivation. So this is just another expression of your laziness, as you try to shift responsibility from yourself to others.

    You've done a 10-day Goenka meditation course. That's no small feat. It shows an already strong connection to Dharma, and it shows you are very capable of consistent effort. Your post in this thread shows that you already know that laziness is one of your main obstacles. Therefore it is up to you to work on that aspect of your character, chip away at it, admit it and surrender it at every turn, beg the Buddhas for their assistance in uprooting it, and follow up with your enthusiastic and uncomplaining effort.

    My sense is that you are attracted to the Dharma but you are not clear on your motivation. If you conceive of practice only as a way to get a more comfortable situation for yourself, improving your life temporarily or gaining some sort of enlightenment that will supposedly remove all problems, that is a view that will make progress difficult.

    I'm not saying you can't get temporary gains; but if that is your motivation your practice will always be result-dependent, and will thus rise and fall, come and go with the shifting tides of life. It will actually go more easily for you if you offer yourself completely (as completely as you can, from the bottom of your heart) to the Dharma, and to all other beings.

    Instead of practicing to get something, make thousands of offerings. Offer your waking moments to your higher mind. Offer a bit of cool, clear water to the enlightened ones in the world and to your own approaching enlightenment (as if you were putting out roses for a lover arriving from afar). Offer your energy to cleaning your space and your world. Offer your effort to helping others through your work. Offer your humility and gratitude at mealtime, that this half-dead body can be supported for another day to make a positive difference for others. Offer your body and mind to the great openness as you enter sleep, just as you will have to offer them at death.

    This attitude of offering will make all the difference. You will start to like yourself more; you will start to appreciate and honor yourself more. Motivation will come more easily. You won't have to force yourself to do small tasks, but you will take on bigger ones gratefully. You will want to do more and more practice, because you will want to become a better and better vehicle of awakening and compassion.
