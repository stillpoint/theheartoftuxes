    TheHeartofTuxes

    Our ignorance is ignorance of the essential nature of existence: that it is impermanent, interpenetrating (no separate selfhood), and not inherently satisfactory. Since we don't understand the ever changing, co-created, interdependent nature of things, we imagine that there is a 'me' and an external world, and we find that the supposed external world is always creating trouble for the 'me'.

    Buddhadharma teaches that all presumed objects and situations and beings are projections of mind. It is this one mind that is the sacred source. Sacredness is not something contrived; you don't make it up or get it especially in one place and not another. It is revealed when you let go of the concepts that veil it.

    In an unguarded, uncontrived moment, vomit is just vomit; and nauseous reaction is just nauseous reaction. It is all truth; it is all sacred. Your own will to be openhandedly present allows the sacredness of life to come through. It just won't always be as you imagine or hope. It will be as it is.

    So I wonder if "turning cleaning into a more meditative practice" is for you an attempt to sanitize reality, to make it nicer rather than more real. Because if you are more meditative about it your tension and discontent may be released, but discomfort and disgust may not. Discomfort is inherent in life; life includes ugly, difficult, uncomfortable things. Hallelujah! Life is everything! Will you embrace it all?

    Since it is clearly an issue of importance to you, consider whether psychological understanding and healing may be necessary. Meditation isn't a magic pill; it doesn't make everything nice. It is a practice where you call on your own resource to show up for things-as-they-are. Often, other tools and actions are required for our health and progress. If you find yourself more triggered than you feel you ought to be, look into it with an ally like a counselor or therapist.

    Sometimes these patterns are based on a particular event or series of events. Maybe a parent called you 'dirty', for example. And often these kinds of issues, and many more within meditation, are just different ways that we avoid letting go in trust. So trust has to be built.

    If none of this applies and you're just looking for a practice to do, I highly recommend mindfulness of your contact with the ground. "Feet on the floor." When you have your feet under you, so to speak, you can handle anything. When you are sitting firmly on the mat or walking with presence on the Earth, you are capable of facing anything. It's when you lose your basis in reality and allow your mind to waver and waft here and there that circumstances take control over you and cause you to react despite your best wishes.

    At any time, feel your contact with your place of support. When standing or walking, feel feet on the ground. When sitting, feel your feet on the floor and your butt and arms on the chair. When lying down, feel your body's contact with the bed or mat — feel its footprint.

    This doesn't have to be a laser focus. A background awareness is enough to keep you present. At any moment, you can return to that neutral presence and let things be what they are, and just do what you are doing. It will give you a 'root'. You may not like your experience; but you will be alive and present for it; it will have a root.

    When this practice is continued throughout your days you will develop a groundedness; and this will grow and deepen, so soon you will be able to face more and more difficulty, and also be able to freely enjoy more and more unencumbered joy.

    If this practice is not enough — if the mind churns and reacts too violently despite the practice — you can look into devotional work that also engages the heart and the voice/hearing. The whole being is then taken up in practice, and merges with the situation.

    You can, for instance, see your cleaning work as cleaning your own mind of its selfishness, impurities, weaknesses, and reactive patterns, and as cleaning the whole world. When you clean one corner of the world, you clean the whole world; and you can do this with great devotion, offering it to Buddha and all enlightening beings in the same way as you would offer incense, water, candlelight, and your best intentions and generous actions. In your broad heart you can offer cleaning to all beings who live in sickness and pollution. You can turn around your own personal suffering and realize it as the suffering of all beings, subject to sickness, aging, dying, violence, dark mind states, painful living situations... the garbage heap of Samsara. You can offer your cleaning, your willingness to face that disgust and difficulty, as a purification for all beings everywhere.

    Together with this devotional intent you can chant the mantra Om Nam, which is the mantra of cleaning the universe. Repeat the mantra with your voice and keep your devotion alive in your heart. Thus every movement, every activity becomes a bold, generous offering rather than a self-centered retreat. You can completely turn around the direction of the discomfort by moving from self-centeredness to selflessness.

    Mantra uses both voice and hearing to help you. In situations where using your voice is not appropriate you can keep the mantra internally.
