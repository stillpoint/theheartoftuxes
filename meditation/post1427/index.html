    Is this considered meditation?

    Meditation means returning to the natural state of your true mind, which is unmoving and brightly aware of everything. So not-moving mind is meditation.

    Mindfulness is the term used for staying present with what your bodymind is doing in its daily activity. This present awareness can embrace any activity, including knife sharpening, drawing, or jogging.

    If knife sharpening is done with the intent to return to your original unmoving mind, and if it actually does return you there, then it might be considered a meditation. In general, concentrating on something, merging with that object (the mind doing the concentrating and the object of concentration come together as one), and resting in the awareness behind it all, is meditation. But it could be quite dangerous to use the movements of a sharp knife as an object of concentration, withdrawing awareness from other things (like who's in the vicinity, what's in your way, what else is happening around you). Mindfulness, yes, no problem; because the awareness is open in mindfulness, taking everything in as you go.

    Some meditations are both concentrated and open/mindful. Some zen meditations, for example, may allow you to work safely while meditating. You'd need proper instruction from a teacher.

    Organizing your mind's thoughts is not the correct aim of meditation. Organizing and thinking are still movement of the mind. Meditation lets you go to the mind beyond thinking. When you go beyond thinking you can be free of thinking, and free of the illusory world that thinking creates. You can see things clearly and relate appropriately.

    When you are in the kitchen doing all your work, there is a background mind that is aware of everything, regardless of what thoughts and tasks you are engaged in. This background mind is beyond thinking. It is only clear. When you practice meditation you learn to clear away the things that obscure this background mind. Then you can taste and smell and feel and think very clearly — perfectly, in fact, like a perfectly reflecting mirror. The pure, clear, wise, compassionate part of you starts to have more and more influence, and the stories and problems made by thinking have less hold on you.

    So the correct purpose of meditation is to wake up to the dreamlike thought-created life, and use that waking up to help yourself, your world, and all others. It aims at great wisdom and great compassion for the sake of all others. "Great" means not just "for me". It's not just a selfish and temporary means of getting limited relief (although it can also be used that way). The correct motivation is great motivation.

    With great motivation, both meditation and mindfulness become positive and effective. Without taking motivation into account, neither meditation practices nor mindfulness are inherently "good". They are neutral, merely other ways of using your mind. When linked with correct understanding of your human situation and correct motivation to clarify it, meditation and mindfulness become your vehicles to greatness.
