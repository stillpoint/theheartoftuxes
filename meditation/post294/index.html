    Consistently meditating for 2+ years. How to move to next level?

    First and most important: why do you want to do 2 - 4 hour long sessions? Is there something special about such a long period, something that will aid in awakening?

    There tends to be a sweet spot for duration of sitting. To a point, longer sittings can help a meditator go deeper, if he/she is able to sit comfortably. But after that point, a stagnation tends to set in. This can be very subtle, and sometimes it is the teacher that notices it before the student â€” perhaps unity at the cost of clarity, or a more plodding sense. So it can actually be valuable, rather than inconvenient, to get up and move between sittings.

    Sittings of 30 minutes, 40 minutes, or perhaps an hour may be interspersed by periods of walking meditation. Even at an hour long, some meditators will start to experience some stagnation. This is sometimes accepted and even embraced in certain schools or traditions, and these will tend to have a slower, more detached energy than schools and traditions that cultivate a sharp and lively quality. In a very broad generalization, there is a certain divide along these lines between South Asian and East Asian schools of meditation, for instance.

    If your aspiration and interest are leading you, then you will naturally want to deepen practice, and you will naturally find ways to do that. If your motive is more along the lines of becoming a spiritual athlete, that is an ego-driven, lame goal, and should be traded in for something more meaningful and beneficial.

    Bright, vivacious aspiration can bring your sittings easily to 4 hours or longer, but not by applying more effort. There will be a very natural and almost effortless desire or certainty toward maintaining the meditative mind. This tends to bloom together with samadhi, which is a flow of mind beyond effortful concentration. Great aspiration together with sincere effort and a clear method can bring on samadhi; but more often than not this all comes along with quite a bit of experience and training. There tend to be personal obstacles that prevent these profound experiences until the obstacles are overcome â€” usually with the help of a guiding teacher and a disciplined situation over the long term. Left to our own devices, our personal proclivities (our likes and dislikes) tend to win often enough to prevent uprooting obstacles to samadhi. It is a question of surrender, after all â€” a question of giving oneself over; and if there is a motive of control behind the practice, it opposes giving over.

    Do you have a teacher? Have you done any retreats? Without more insight into your situation, these would seem to be the next steps.

    • First, proper motive.
    • Next, proper method.
    • Then commit to a situation where you are required to surrender, where the program is not all based on your own whim (and hence your own ego).

    A retreat will provide the structure and shared energy that can help you move beyond previous limits. People practicing together sincerely help each other. In most retreats, your time on the mat is also balanced with working and sharing, which helps your character grow together with your concentration power, rather than at odds with it.
