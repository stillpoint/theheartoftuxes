    Irresistible urge to move whole body during some sort of meditation

    It's neither good nor bad in itself, but I wouldn't want you to force yourself into any mind state in order to reproduce an experience. I'd guess that you are in the best position — if you can consider it objectively — to know if the practice is okay with you. My question would be: Does this feel to you like something bringing you closer to sanity, or further from it?

    There are a few factors that make your particular routine not so much about meditation and more about slipping into semi-consciousness or opening the boundaries between consciousness levels: First, you do it as a routine just before sleeping, and you expect to sleep imminently. Second, you consciously slow down your breathing rather than letting it do whatever it does naturally. Third, your mental activity is focused on visualization rather than grounded in a sensation; and this may naturally connect you to more of a dream realm than waking life. Fourth, you are lying down as you do this, which almost invariably induces dreaminess. Fifth, if you are doing this with eyes closed, this again almost invariably brings on dreaminess.

    So you may be tapping into and releasing energies from more unconscious levels of your mind — the indistinct, free-flowing, dark, irrational dream energies. Or the body movement may have more of an physical-energetic origin, where senses are withdrawn and certain blockages or nerve energies that were previously covered up with other mental activity come to the forefront. Energy can get pent up or blocked on many levels in a person, and it can be discharged in many different ways — some common, and some strange. Dreams, tics, laughter or emotional expressions, neurotic activity, and excessive talking are all ways that pent-up energy gets discharged. More conscious and healing discharges may involve exercise or dance or bowing practice, singing or chanting, artwork, taiji or yoga or qigong, and so on.

    At the most central point, the mind itself should ideally be relaxed and non-seeking — not trying to get relaxation or any state or experience. Just openly aware. Awake, not drifting off! That openness is healing at the deepest level, not requiring any cycle of buildup and discharge.

    I agree with ReThinkingTheism, who has also commented here: if you are very sedentary and feel disconnected, then doing such a cerebral and dreamy (and self-invented) practice may end up taking you to places where you lose yourself even more. Relaxation is one thing; but breaking down the healthy barriers of your consciousness is another. You may get a lot of benefit from some physical practice, either gentle or vigorous. That will also help you relax and sleep, but it will bring you closer to yourself.

    And you might consider the wisdom in following a time-tested method and consulting with a teacher or experienced fellow meditator, lest the things you do with your mind turn out to be an experiment gone wrong.
