    Results come from consistent application of proper method. Don't expect big changes after only a couple of tries, and don't push too hard.

    Learn a good method and follow it precisely. If you try to invent your own method along the way, that is called 'distraction': following the thoughts that appear rather than following the method. Some thoughts seem important, some seem right, but all are just thoughts. If you follow them, you are basically practicing distraction, making your thought-following habit stronger.

    Meditation turns that around. You say no, I'm not going to follow thoughts; I'm going to follow the method.

    Also, if you keep changing the method, you won't get the results of consistency. It's like swinging an axe once at one tree, then once at another tree, and so on: you never actually cut down the tree. But even if you have an imperfect axe, if you keep chopping at one target, the tree will eventually fall. Find one good method, learn it well, and follow it closely. Then you can experience results.

    Learning a good, proven traditional method and following it closely will help eliminate thoughts like "am I doing this right?" You put your trust in the method, and that empowers you. These mainstream traditional methods have been passed down by enlightened beings and proven by millions of meditators. You can practice with confidence. When you start to see results, you can have further faith in the method.

    You need good teaching. Why try to empty your mind? Where did you get that idea? Worrying about whether your mind is empty or active is not concentration.

    You also have the idea that good feeling equals good meditation. That's not necessarily so. Comparing one experience to another is not concentration. If you start judging practice based on feeling you will soon get discouraged.

    Concern about emptiness of mind is attachment to thinking. Concern about how good it feels is attachment to feeling. Attachment is distraction. These attachments keep you from enjoying concentration and clarity.

    I'll also note that there are many possible causes of distraction and unclarity. Meditation is not always the first or best solution. Sometimes physical/physiological conditions need to be considered. Sometimes emotional conditions like depression cause lack of focus. Living situation and other lifestyle issues could also contribute. So don't put an inappropriate load on meditation, expecting it to do everything, if there are other issues that need to be remedied.
