    title: "Mindfulness in Plain English" says I should inhale and exhale through my nostrils. He also says to not accentuate your breathing at all. How important is this? Do you agree? summary: 10.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2015-07-06 05:40:39 some_url:

    If you're following that method, you should follow it precisely. Every tradition evolves with its own completion, and every method has its own sense, its own context in that tradition. The very reason that you do not know (after all, you're here asking about it) is why you are not in a position to change the method to suit your sense of things. Whether it's arbitrary or whether it's based on a consensus of sundry opinions among anonymous voices on the internet, the change would be based on ignorance.

    Yes, it's important to follow the method exactly. If you don't have enough faith in the method to follow it without question, then you need to learn it better. If learning from a book is not good enough (and it may not be), then find a living teacher who can respond to your doubts.

    Breath is intimately connected to physicality, thought, and mood. It affects and is affected by all three. This is one of the reasons it is such an effective object of meditation; but it is also a reason why breath-based practice can go off the rails so easily. Because the breath is so influential, you must be meticulous in following the guidance of experienced, awakened teachers within a tradition of proven results.

    The in-breath gathers; the out-breath dissipates; the turning of the breath is open. If these are out of balance, some aspect of self becomes skewed. Gulping in breaths more deeply than would happen naturally tends to express and evoke an acquisitive mindset. One may gain a sense of vitality, but it comes at the cost of detachment: duality is subtly implied with each breath: "I" get more power. That is not real power; it is ego-based illusion.

    Forcing the breath in some way has always been an attractive proposition, and many meditators and even meditation teachers don't realize why this is so; they only follow their sense of it. The attraction (or, really, attachment) arises as a psychological shadow. It is the unconscious avoidance of openness. To put it succinctly, one fears the silence and tries to fill it in with activity. One fears the vulnerability and tries to cover it with controlling actions.

    When the breath is very natural and uncontrived, it becomes seamless, gentle, effortless. The feeling of it becomes more and more subtle. The gross formations of self begin to settle as the mind moves more deeply into absorption of pure awareness. But our attachment to these formations — our attachment to what we think of as our self — is very deep and strong. When we begin to lose the grip on the formations, a rebellion appears in the mind. The ego seeks ways to maintain control, to feel itself. So it makes more formations; it stirs something up to comfort itself. It is how masturbation becomes addiction. Or, more related to breath, it is why smoking becomes such an attachment, beyond the chemical dependency: one feels one's existence through the breath. Like sucking on a pacifier, one feels "okay, I exist, I am here".

    So when the breathing becomes more frictionless and silent, notice the discomfort, the anxiety that arises. Notice the urge to fill in the silence, to create feeling that re-establishes a sense of your existence. It keys on the gross sensation of accumulating power through the breath, and it keys on the manipulative relationship to the breath and to the method itself, as your attention turns to 'tweaking' the process rather than surrendering to it.

    The Third Patriarch of Zen said that human beings' shared sickness is our anxiety about emptiness. This is what prompts us to manipulate the breath, manipulate the mood, make more thoughts, stay busy with activity, and so on.

    Some traditional methods do force the breath, for various reasons. The innate imbalance may be used to circulate or build energy or to control the mind in some way. It is not an inherently pure way of practicing, but it can be used positively. It relies heavily, though, on a very clear and strong motivation and/or a structure that keeps the practitioner on the correct path. This structure may include close and continual guidance from a teacher, practice in a group setting, rules of conduct, and lifestyle commitments or vows.

    But that is not the kind of practice taught in Mindfulness In Plain English. If you choose to follow the practice in the book, follow it and don't place yourself above the teacher (by questioning or tweaking the method). Don't let ego get between you and the liberative instruction. You will only understand the meaning of the practice when you fully attain its fruit, after a great deal of diligent effort.
