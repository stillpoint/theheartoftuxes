    First thing is if you have ADD, you should have support from a mental health professional. Get the proper treatment for the issue.

    In terms of meditation, physical practice might be best for you. There are many forms of meditation. Even for people who sit to meditate, physical practice is a very important and beneficial companion practice.

    You might, for instance, try Taiji or certain kinds of Yoga, to help the nervous system settle down, and to establish your connection with your body. Restorative Yoga may be especially helpful to you for settling the nervous system. Taiji also does that, and promotes flow that will help your future meditation practice.

    When you are fully in your body, you have your strongest grounding influence. It is what keeps you present and settled. Running, swimming, dancing, and prostration practice (among others) can all help you to settle down your energy and be more in your body. When it is natural for you to sit quietly, then you can begin sitting practice in earnest. There is some benefit to trying small bits even now, but to force yourself to endure a situation where you feel like jumping out of your skin would just make you resent meditation; you would likely develop a negative relationship to it.

    Walking meditation is also a very good option, if that is not also too 'tight' for you. There are many forms of walking meditation. In some, you basically carry on the very same practice as you would while sitting; only you walk in a path or circuit. Others have a particular focus (for instance, the movement of the feet or the feeling of the feet touching the floor). Also, circumambulation of sacred sites is seen as a way of developing karmic merit, which enables you (for one thing) to undertake meditations that you couldn't previously perform.

    On that last note, there are many practices that can build merit, to help you with your desired practice in the future. In general, generous thought and activity does it. Many skillful ways of practicing have been developed in many traditions. Drumming and rattle-shaking are common meditations among Native American spirituality. Whirling a prayer wheel is an old-school Tibetan way. Working a mala (string of prayer beads) is almost globally common; and you can do this either as-is or with an accompanying mantra (or if initiated into Vajrayana practice, with a mantra and visualization).

    Prostration practice is very highly regarded in many traditions; and I've known very high-level practitioners to do that practice. Sometimes it is done in place of sitting meditation, especially when the mind or heart are too upset to sit. Prostrations have the effect of bringing the mind down very strongly to its resting place; the energy, too, settles in its proper home in the body. It is a much more effective practice than sitting in this regard. In the west, not as many people know about it, and the image of the cross-legged meditator is supreme; but in other traditions of profound spiritual practice, prostration practice is very highly regarded.

    As an interesting side-note, there's a temple in China where you can see one monk's footprints imprinted in the wood, from the many millions of prostrations he performed throughout his lifetime. (No idea if this is legitimate, or one of the many inventions Chinese have come up with to draw tourists.) In any case, there are indeed people who have practiced very deeply with prostrations, and have gained great attainment through that practice.

    Besides the kind of practice, there is a whole discussion to be looked into on the many supporting factors available for your practice. The foremost supports are teacher and practice group. But also, your living environment, diet, and activity have tremendous impact on how your mind and energy and nervous system operate. If you feed yourself with empty, over-stimulating garbage in your food, your entertainment, the way you converse with others, the kinds of thoughts you indulge in and the kinds of activities you perform, then the results will be very clear when you come to face yourself in meditation. These results are always appearing; you only notice them more when you sit down quietly.

    So if you change the causes in your life, you can change the results you experience. Calm, clean, spacious, orderly, natural living space. Images of peace rather than meaningless or tumultuous images. Good, whole, natural food; and a good eating schedule, and peaceful and thankful way of eating. Balanced sleep schedule; regular, at the proper time and length (sleep before 11pm and wake by sunrise). Avoid trivia, gossip, aggression, and lustful or violent entertainment. Limit the frazzling effect of spending time on electronic devices (phones, computers, etc.). The input creates the output. Change input, receive different output.
