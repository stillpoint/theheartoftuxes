    What The Buddha Taught by Walpola Rahula is an excellent introduction to the basic concepts of Buddhism. Although it is written from within the Theravada tradition the main concepts are shared by all branches of Buddhism. (Some practice styles may differ. For instance, some traditions funny emphasize renunciation to the same extent.)

    There are many, many forms of meditation. Buddhism alone has thousands of practices. Hinduism, Taoism, Sufism, Christianity, and other religions all have their own practices as well as shared practices. Then there are non-religious yogic paths, folk-religious paths, shamanic paths, and more contemporary independent methods based on an individual's insight or on psychological principles or interpretations of research. Many ways!

    There's meditation that works with breath, meditation that works with visual or auditory objects. There's mantra, and chanting. There's are many energy practices. There's prostrating, and walking, and standing. There's death meditation, sleep and dream meditation, relaxation meditation, healing meditation. There are many visualization practices (often combined with other methods). There's body-scanning and insight meditation. There's self-inquiry, working with specific teaching questions (koans), and keeping open questioning (hwadu). And there are objectless practices like so-called 'just sitting' (which may or may not be what it sounds like).

    But what is your purpose in meditating?

    Most people start meditating because they want to get something for themselves -- better concentration, stress relief, better health, temporary or lasting peace. Some want to go deeper in their religious experience, to 'know God', so to speak.

    For most, it's only after they've practiced for a while and encountered certain teachings that their purpose widens beyond just gaining something to make their lives better. Developing compassion and waking up from the dream of life may become aims of their practice.

    As a result of their growing compassion and insight, or because they already have a high spiritual capacity, some practitioners vow to help all others awaken from the dream of life and do away with suffering forever. It is an inconceivable vow, embracing numberless beings in numberless worlds, across vast time. Their hearts become that wide.

    And some, having touched pure uncontrived truth, in meditation or just intuitively, may be willing to 'go all in' and devote themselves to embodying that point, to expressing the Buddha Mind from moment to moment to moment, without seeking anything.

    So, many different methods and many different aims.

    Have you encountered any of these modes and methods before? Any thought about what you'd like to try?

    There's no one 'good' or 'best' method. There are many ways to go about it, and there are even many ways to get to enlightenment, if that is your aim. The 'best' way is the one that you learn well and then do. Often it's far more important to find and attend a teacher than to pick a particular method.

    Also important is not to inundate, confuse, and distract yourself with lots of information about lots of different paths.

    > One time doing is better than a thousand times hearing.

    Even within the same path -- say, Zen -- courses of training, methods, and teaching styles will differ; so it's not 'plug and play', you can't just apply any teacher's instruction to your own chosen path. Later, after you get considerable insight in practice and have integrated teaching about the various perspectives of mind, then you will be better equipped to distinguish between paths and methods; but by that point you may be completely satisfied to build further upon their practice you've cultivated.
