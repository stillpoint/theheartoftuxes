    Attaining contentment is a fine aim. But you're not seeing the difference between that and being attached to getting good feeling. Contentment means this is okay, and that is also okay. Attachment to feeling means nothing is okay except what you want; and when you get what you want it soon changes, so again things aren't okay.

    When your mind has equanimity, you can have contentment. When your mind grasps, you have suffering.

    So what makes contentment is your mind, not the outside situation. Seeking good feeling buys into the misunderstanding that the outside situation can give you contentment; but no possession or ingested substance or relationship or outer situation is inherently capable of making you content.

    "Maybe this life isn't for me." Don't be dramatic. If this life is not for you, great — change! The wonderful aspect of suffering — in fact, perhaps the only good thing about suffering — is that it helps you wake up and get sick of suffering and become motivated to change. But if you are very stubborn, or if you have done things to make yourself dull and unable to see the relationship between the causes you create and the results you get, then more suffering is necessary until you realize your mistake and are ready to let go of the thoughts and actions that are causing it.

    I gave you a good reflection for your situation; but you are a bit resistant in your reply. Do you really want contentment; or is some large portion of what you want involved in thinking of yourself as a victim? There are many benefits we look for in playing the victim: we get to think nicely about ourselves while remaining lazy; we get to stir up reactive pity and maybe get some pampering; we get to feel the strength behind blaming outer agents rather than seeing clearly....

    Self-obsession is incredibly uncomfortable. The more you think of yourself, the more you suffer. The more you care for others, the wider and more confident and comfortable your mind becomes. Why? It is because you become the host for others rather than the helpless guest or child. In fact, always thinking about your own situation and your own comfort is such a basic root of depression that when you find yourself lingering on self-thought, it would do well to immediately change the channel and go do something positive: do some pushups, go for a walk, do some deep breathing next to an open window or in the local park, read a good book, make a piece of artwork, dance to your favorite music... or almost anything to get out of self-obsessive trains of thought, anything that's not negative (walk backwards; whistle 'Yankee Doodle Dandy'...).

    There are countless people and animals who are at least as afflicted as you. Perhaps consider them and pray for them, and commit to helping them; and maybe do something today to alleviate some other being's suffering.
