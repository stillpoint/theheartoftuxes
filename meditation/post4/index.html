    >What is boredom?

    Boredom is thinking. When outside things or activities are boring, it means that you are boring; you are maintaining a boring mind.

    The Third Patriarch of Zen said that the shared human sickness is our anxiety about emptiness. Reality is open, boundless, ungraspable. Anything you can touch comes from that which you can't touch. Anything you try to get ahold of, you can't hold for long. This is life. And it is wonderful; because it means that everything can change — there is movement; you can actually have a life!

    But because we have a very deep grasping habit, we are very deeply uncomfortable with the open, boundless, ungraspable nature of reality. Freud recognized this, and saw how we get in the way of our own happiness. He saw that while we want and need to unify with things, we also interrupt the process of unifying because of our fear. He called this dynamic 'isolating': keeping ourselves from being together with something or someone. We go through life as if we are separate, yearning to merge but terrified of merging.

    It is this dynamic that is behind the boredom and dissatisfaction. You keep yourself from merging with things-as-they-are. Boredom is only the excuse; but really, the terror of surrendering is the cause.

    If you want to live in comfort, you have to practice comfort. Wanting is not comfort; it is inherently uncomfortable. You have to practice letting go of wanting (notice that I say "letting go of" rather than "destroying" or "getting rid of" — the wanting may remain or it may return; but the task is to let it be and not attach to or generate it). You have to practice contentment.

    This is a different strategy. Rather than trying to get or make your favorite situation, you decide to go all-in on this situation, this real life, as it is. When boredom arises, you can know that you are still resisting, trying to distract yourself from actually being in your life at that moment.
