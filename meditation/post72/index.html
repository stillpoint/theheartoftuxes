    Why do people want to meditate in cool spots?

    Different meditations have different aims.

    There's a whole vast realm of energy meditation that is not often addressed in this subreddit. In many of these paths, environmental energy is used to enhance the cultivation. Maybe you can relate: pure, cool natural environments feel good. They contribute something to your energy. On the other hand, you might feel drained or agitated by busy, polluted, man-made environments. For those working with energy on extremely — sometimes unimaginably — subtle levels, these differences can make all the difference.

    Waterfall meditation is a famous shamanic practice that appears in several paths around the world. Shamans from many parts of East Asia as well as Japanese Bushido and Ninjitsu practitioners, and more recently Shintaido practitioners have sat under frigid, roaring waterfalls as part of a purification pratice. They find that it can cause big personal energy to arise.

    On a less exotic front, natural environments can provide a calming, grounding influence for the common meditator. Although we are used to our man-made environs, according to our history and genetic makeup they are far more foreign to us, and natural spaces are closer to our hereditary home.

    Consider also that our built-up spaces are built from our ideas. The monotonous forms and straight lines are reflections not only of our need to house and move unnaturally dense populations, but of the straight-line thinking of our egoic minds. And being in these man-made places over time conditions us to think and move even more in those ways. (Not to mention the influence of electronic thought-machines like TVs, smartphones, computers, calculators, scanners, and the like.)

    So we lose contact with the mind that flows naturally and is open to the unexpected, and we become conditioned, trapped in the mind that only sees one goal after another. Consider how you move through the day: do you use your body in countless ways, intimate with your environment — climbing, sidestepping, leaping, crouching — or do you go as straight as possible from one goal to another? Is the journey from appointment to appointment healing, informative, inspiring, or is the journey an inconvenience, something in the way of actually being at the next place, something for which you turn off your awareness?

    Natural settings have much to offer. But that's not to say they're always ideal for meditation. Let's not get caught up in romantic notions, but rather attend to cause and effect. What setting actually helps?

    In order to answer that, we have to be clear on the purpose of meditation. As I said at the top, different meditations have different aims; so different settings may apply. But if we can be so bold, we may say that in general meditation is for the purpose of surrendering to, and becoming, truth. We settle the thinking, imagining mind so that its noise — its dog and pony show — is no longer the thing we identify with.

    This is not a retreat from the real world. Mere silence is not the ultimate goal. If it were, then dead, comatose, and very stupid people would be considered very successful at meditation! Vibrant, live, unwavering awareness within real life is the goal. At least, that's how we speak of it to the uninitiated. To those who have some advanced experience, the experience of being the moment, being all of life, is very possible.

    In light of this goal, an isolation tank is not ideal for meditation practice. Our aim is not to manipulate the outer environment to make it do what we want, but rather to bring our own mind under control. If we always have to shut off the outside world, that is not freedom, and that is not a vibrant and open mind. It is narrow.

    Should we then meditate in the middle of the busy city? If we can do it, yes. But the reality is that the beginner's mind is usually so attached to objects that a busy setting is too disturbing to allow for meditation to even happen, or happen at a deep level.

    Some middle ground is required at first, until the concentrative power is developed. A setting that neither shuts off the world nor shoves it in your face is considered ideal for meditation based on high motivations like awakening. In some cases this may be a natural setting like a forest glade or a quiet garden. In some cases it may mean sitting indoors in a clean, orderly space.

    Online white noise, café sound, and rainfall sound websites attest to the fact that a gently undulating background of activity can help some people to concentrate. We find these kinds of sounds also in distant waterfalls, waves on a beach, trees rustling in the breeze, and — lucky us! — our very breath and movement of blood. Barring the ideal external circumstances, we can get a similar benefit by becoming sensitive to the sounds and tidal movements of our breathing.
