    We don't know what methods you are using, how well you apply them, or what's actually happening when you're practicing them (or when you're supposed to be practicing them). So far, we just know that you've tried a few; so you're probably jumping around too much, for one thing.

    Hedging and shifting and tinkering keep you involved in trying to find a perfect outer situation. The intent should be to settle into the situation as-it-is. After you have learned the method and have prepared a resonably suitable environment and posture, you don't manipulate the outer situation any more; you work on settling down and relaxing with whatever is real.

    If you are doing correct meditation (which we don't know yet) and you don't like it, that means you don't like being with yourself. So practice then is the application of compassionate attention — getting better at liking yourself.

    We can't say what mistake you might be making in the method, but trying to escape the situation creates suffering. The more you resist, the more suffering you have. There's a formula for this:

    discomfort x resistanct = suffering

    The mechanism is thinking. You are not satisfied with the situation because of your thinking about it. If the thinking settles, the situation is just what it is, and you can relax with it. But because the thinking makes ideas about what is good and what is bad, and laments the past, and worries about the future (how long sitting will take, what will happen next, how it will turn out, whether it's worth it) — because of that thinking, your experience is dissatisfaction.

    The method, if it's a proper one, is there to help you let go of dissatisfaction-thinking and return to momentary reality. It's there to help you relax about the way things are. So it's all-important to let go of trying to figure out a better way or a way out, and to just keep returning to the moment by doing the method. Just do the method. Don't switch, don't tinker, don't fidget, don't think.

    Some people — and these days, more and more — are particularly agitated. These people may have especially strong resistance to feeling in general (whether sensations or emotions), or may have an intense skin-crawling anxiety about sitting still, or may be so attached to racing thoughts that settling down only makes the thoughts more dominant. In these cases, still-sitting meditation isn't necessarily the best method; or other supports may be helpful.

    In terms of still meditation, for example, the voice can be used in mantra to help engage hearing, regulate breathing, integrate physical/vocal effort, harness devotion, and so on.

    But physical practice is also very good and often underrated.

    Before making suggestions, we'd need to know what the exact issue is, and whether you've actually learned any proper method, and learned it well. If after a good, consistent try with proper attitude and application you still have difficulty, then you may want to make a calculated switch to physical practice. Or you may want to add a physical practice before sitting.

    All in all, you need clarity; which means you need a proper source of teaching and good and consistent response to what comes up for you. Don't jump around. Don't try to invent your own practice. Don't pick and choose from various sources and various opinions online. Get clear about it.
