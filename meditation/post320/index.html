    Meditation is sold that way? You're hanging around the wrong people if someone is trying to push meditation on you.

    If online culture is your only source of information, that may be your problem; because any doofus can write in a blog, create an app, or pose as an authority in a forum. If you find a proper source of teaching, you'll find a far, far more human approach and a far, far wiser and more true-to-life guidance.

    Some religions and philosophies point out the emptiness of things; and this has truth to it -- no thing or relationship ultimately satisfies us; our happiness depends on what's happening within us. But a lot of people oversimplify and misconstrue this teaching to mean "all possessions are bad and you can't trust any relationship". It's a version of the strawdog argument: arguing against a shallow and false version of the actual statement.

    This wrong thinking is especially true of people who hold strong opinions and who react defensively to what they read rather than looking into it more deeply and learning what it's really saying.

    In truth, it's quite the opposite: to the meditative mind, everything is a benefit. All objects and relationships and events are a benefit, a blessing, to the meditative mind.

    As to the idea that meditation is the 'only way', if you read my posting history you'll find that time and again I stress that meditation alone isn't responsible for happiness or knowing how to live well; sometimes you need to take action, get out and do things, learn how to relate to people, take care of business, enjoy activity and fitness, perform generous and ethical and compassionate acts, and so on. I talk about this very often to people who, like you, have received a very narrow view of healing or spiritual practice.

    Sitting in a room with your eyes closed:

    Don't close your eyes. Unless you are performing a method that specifically required closed eyes, keep your eyes at least half-open. I wonder if you've actually learned a proper method. That's very important, and will of course determine your experience and results. You need to be working with an effective method, not just making your own guesses and assumptions, and you need to have learned it correctly and fully, including the proper attitude of engagement.

    I also wonder if you understand the connection between sitting still and being happy. Meditation doesn't * give* anything to you; it sheds a light on your bullshit so you can let go of it, and it brings you into greater intimacy with your true underlying mind, which is already content.

    If you can't be with yourself, then nothing, nothing, nothing will make you happy. You will always be avoiding yourself. You will always be avoiding situations where you begin to feel yourself -- or in many cases even feel at all. (And so many people fall into a pattern where they chase love and connection but run away from the intimacy and feelings they entail. A recipe for disaster!)

    So meditation is, in part, a process of becoming okay with yourself. In this day and age a great many people are very tightly wound and agitated, and even the prospect of settling down and being quiet for a while is deeply disturbing to them. It takes going through a great deal of discomfort before they actually get to the benefit of meditation, the contentment and mental-emotional stability that ongoing practice reveals.

    Proper traditional paths of training have ways to address this. Narrow online apps and guided meditations can't respond to many of these kinds of issues, much less provide warm, kind, clear feedback, correction, and encouragement. An accomplished and authorized teacher of a traditional path is so helpful, so pivotal.

    If you think this may be the case for you -- that your agitation and resistance make it very uncomfortable to even sit down quietly -- there are other ways of training for you. In traditional courses, body, speech, mind, lifestyle, and relationship are all addressed. In particular, physical practice is an important aspect missing from many books and online sources. Physical practice is not only a tremendously beneficial partner practice to sitting, it is also highly respected as a practice in its own right, especially for those who have difficulty or poor connection with sitting.

    I've written at length on various kinds of physical practice, their forms and their benefits. You can use the forum search utility, go through my posting history (or Google the topic with my username), or follow up with me for more information.

    Best wishes.
