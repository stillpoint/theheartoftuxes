    Each state is a springboard for the next iff the aspiration and character allow it.

    The notion of balance in practice, and the need for mutual cultivation of wisdom, character, and concentration, is far from foreign to yoga. Viz. Patanjali's Eight Limbs of Yoga. One could say, in fact, that ancient yoga traditions came up with the notion of formal balanced, wholistic meditative training first, thousands of years before Patanjali encoded it.

    ~

    Before I reply further, I want to note that different traditions and different cultures ascribe different meanings to terminology. Yoga folks tend to equate the term mind with the appearance of thought-forms — what others might call the "thinking mind" — whereas others might take the word mind to mean "the consciousness beyond" (and including) the thinking mind. Sometimes we are saying the same things in different ways.

    ~

    >The human mind by it's very nature becomes easily dissatisfied when the same experience is repeated too many times, so these kinds of attachments become self-limiting.

    One part of this statement doesn't follow from the other. The tendency for humans to become dissatisfied with repetition of similar experiences doesn't lead to freedom from attachment, but tends toward further and deeper indulgence. We don't seek to give up the sugar, we look for something sweeter and more intense. If it were true that repeated indulgence were self-limiting, the porn industry would be dead.

    Yes, that indulgence can be used skillfully, steered toward the divine — notably in some Yogic streams and in other devotional paths like Sufism — but the basic grasping itself is a basic flaw or danger that does require skill in handling. I wouldn't call it a self-limiting path. Perhaps "calculated risk". And one that hinges on the ability to digest the experiences that arise: through intellect and conception, through wisdom, through moral character, through the guidance of a teacher, through the mutual support of a practice partner or community, and so on.

    Certainly our experience (yours and mine) differs; and that's why it's good to have this dialog and to receive different views. In my experience, I have seen not just a few but many practitioners practice in an unbalanced way and become attached to the states that arise through deepening concentration. The attachment means they can't respond to life clearly but only in terms of their grasping, so they lose their way. To the common person they may seem well off or even superior, because the states they experience (as well as concentration itself) are energizing; but they are unclear and lost nonetheless. It tends to take either a clear-eyed teacher or a life crisis to shake them out of it. I would go so far as to say it is the spiritual shadow-side of our society: an over-emphasis on the mental aspect of practice, and an insidious grasping relationship toward spirit. It seems at first so genuine, so reasonable, but it often ends up taking the same turn we all take through our consumer activity, media, politics: "I want!."

    Our society's alienation from ancestors, tradition, and place goes far deeper than we realize, and its effects reach through us without our awareness. For instance, if we have a nice house or condo we don't see ourselves as deeply wounded by the fact that we have no real relationship to land, to nature. As a society — and some practice traditions play into this tendency — we continue to relate to spirituality as outsiders. Even as we practice, our deep alienation and loss steers us to grasp at experiences rather than letting it be and settling into our essential home. Not much we can do, though, but keep bringing that to awareness and starting afresh.

    Thank you for the conversation.
