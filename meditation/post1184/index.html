    I don't know about that.

    I mean, it is a meditation that has been performed by others, across centuries.

    The question is: why this practice? Why do you want to do this meditation?

    I always cringe when new students mention practices like this, because so many seem to attach to a supposed attainment, some special power that they seek. They've been sucked into the practice by promise of something unusual, out of a sense of greed rather than nobility. That's not really a good relationship to bring into meditation.

    I don't know if this is the case with you, but I'll mention it in case it is.

    The more seeking/grasping attitude, the more duality and tension. It creates the tension that could harm the eyes, for instance; but the bigger issue is what it does to your mental habit and how you relate to life and practice.

    Real meditation is openhanded. It starts with the recognition that there you are already complete, and there is nothing outside that you need to gain. Rather than chasing after a power that will somehow make you superior, you practice to realize your original completion, your original peace and fulfillment. Then you settle into that original mind, learn how it functions appropriately in daily life, and use it to help yourself and all others.

    'Third Eye' meditation is an authentic practice, but it's meant to be done within a very clear tradition and course of training, under the direct guidance of a guru. The people for whom it's intended will already have a context of spiritual intent and other practices, rules of behavior governing ethics and character, a community for whom the practice is very clear, and an attained teacher with whom they work closely. This all serves to clarify and de-mystify the practice.

    On the other hand, people who stumble across this practice out of its context and hear that it's supposed to unlock some higher spiritual realm can get stars in their eyes, dreaming about being special. The westerner's relationship with what they think of as 'quick result' or mystical practice, including all kinds of energy practice, sometimes brings to mind the wide-eyed fixation that children have when they 'make potions' in the bathroom sink, or the way even adults dream about owning a light saber or some other bit of magic. It's not the real practice that I've witnessed bringing people back to their true life.

    If you're concerned with self-harm, you should look into your reason for choosing this practice and whether there's a grasping attitude behind it.
