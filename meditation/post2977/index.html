    Using meditation/mindfulness to stop a daydream

    Meditation is stopping the madness. The very process of meditation involves disengaging from illusory trains of thought and returning to the method, whether it involves an object of focus or objectless awareness. There are countless methods — many good ones, and many made-up, half-assed ones.

    But you may also find benefit in healing modalities other than meditation, and in good teaching, both of which may shine a light on why these attachments arise in the first place.

    Our shared human situation is our illusion about what we essentially are. We think of ourselves as separate, stable, known entities: "I am me over here; and you are you over there." All real evidence we have goes contrary to this view, but still humans and sentient beings share this view. And so we think of ourselves as subjects within a world of separate objects.

    Because of this one initial mistake, we separate ourselves from what we really are; we separate ourselves from the world. Therefore we go through life feeling lost, homeless, and like we're missing something. It is the emptiness that we are all uncomfortable with, like a hole in our being. We try to fill in this hole with possessions, experiences, relationships. We try to complete ourselves by improving in certain ways, or by hooking up with the 'right' person or people, or by getting fame or power. We try to fill the void with food or drugs or sex or entertainment — anything to take our mind away. We look for excitement or we fall asleep and try not to be aware of much at all. If we are a bit more clever, maybe we try to get the right kind of spirituality to make us complete.

    All of these strategies come from the initial mistake about who or what we really are. Because we don't understand our true nature, we clamber and struggle and suffer in these many ways.

    Originally, you are complete. But in your self-view there is a mistake. The mistake we all share gets further molded by the wounding we receive in life. For example, if your father has been absent, or hurtful, or lacking in integrity (—and who is perfect, after all?), then your psyche will almost inevitably look to others for what was lacking in your relationship with your father.

    There are other ways, too, in which wounding makes us feel and see ourselves as smaller and less sufficient than we really are. And then we are driven to find our completion, to fill in those gaps.

    If someone thinks of himself or herself as weak, then strength and strong people will be attractive. If there is a 'mother wound', a distortion of one's ability to care for one's own body, then they will habitually turn to people who exemplify strength and beauty.

    Whenever something about a person seems to attract you very deeply, at a gut level, consider how that quality exists in your life. Very often you will find that it is not prominent in your own life, or that you think of yourself as lacking that quality. So of course your life will seek it in someone else. But it doesn't really help you one bit to seek it in someone else. There may be many good reasons for having companionship with someone, but trying to absorb a quality and make it your own is not one of them.

    You have to find your own original completion. You have to heal the wounds and uncover the qualities that (you think) you are missing. As the qualities emerge, there will be less reason for unconsciously attaching to the qualities in others. Then relationship can be based on something real and positive rather than unconscious and habitual attachments.
