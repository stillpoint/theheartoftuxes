    I think the suggestions I already made still apply. Patterns are all patterns of movement. Something gets built up; something else gets stuck... something is overdone; something is left unexpressed....

    It's all movement, and in many cases can be brought to balance with appropriate movement: If there's stagnation, you invite flow. If there's agitation, you promote calmness. If there's tension and clinging, you practice release.

    This is inflamed by ungrounded contemporary channels of communication, the ways in which many are learning about meditation these days. Online, with no physicality, no spark of life, no presence other than words, we are lulled into the notion that mere accumulation of words, concepts, knowledge, and cleverness is enough to fulfill us. One person's words are as good as another. Good teaching is not well respected, but rather is voted on, as if it were a matter of opinion. Precious wisdom just finds its slot somewhere down the thread among a dozen other voices.

    As Marshall McLuhan said, the medium is the message. The way we are communicating about meditation becomes the teaching. Sadly.

    It plays into our object-consciousness, our habitual attachment to things. We already seek things to grasp, things to manipulate, things to occupy us. And the electronic medium tends to deepen that habit. Even visually on the computer or phone screen, everything becomes straight lines and separate blocks. Where's the flow; where's the dance of interaction?

    So it is easy to get the wrong idea about practice, to think that it's about noticing things. The ego just wants objects, things to control. When it has its things to control, it is â€” well, not exactly happy, but it has the situation it wants. The ego hates letting go of control, hates letting be, witnessing change, opening to unknown possibility.

    But what the ego hates, the soul loves. We need to remind ourselves that the soul is primary. We need to remind ourselves that we are already complete. We need to remind ourselves that "we are Spirit having a human experience". And we need to participate in situations that remind us when we forget.

    Practice in isolation can miss these points. One turns toward what one has always turned toward. One goes by one's whim and preference, and never gets out from under that thumb. One is limited by the same blind spots that have been there for ages. In order to be reminded of our complete and boundless nature, we need to be around people who recognize it. And in order to gain faith in boundlessness and completion we need to see them before us, embodied by someone who has attained the fruit of practice.
