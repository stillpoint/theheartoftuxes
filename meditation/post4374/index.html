    So you're like Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita ("The Song Of God"). In that scripture, Arjuna is thrust into a situation not of his choosing, expected to fight for his side in a war. He is in doubt and turmoil at the prospect, and in fact refuses to participate; but Krishna teaches him about that which is beyond sides, beyond conflict.

    Arjuna learns that to carry out his role as warrior is to maintain the balance of the Universe. It is not done begrudgingly, but wholeheartedly. By completely giving himself over to the role, without thought of victory or loss, Arjuna can win the true victory â€” the victory of Consciousness and Liberation.

    If you give yourself over to the situation completely, without thought of gain or loss, then you will be free... and you will play true music.

    Of course, we are human; we are not perfectly aloof in our activity. There may be some fear for yourself, some fear for your teacher's sake, some compassion for other competitors, some gratitude, some pride, some resentment. If there weren't new hurdles, there wouldn't be new achievements. The pressure is appropriate to the situation; and it can help you to let go of results. If there's just a little pressure, you can bear it while holding on to your usual patterns of relating. Greater pressure, though, urges you to new heights.

    You may find that the performer in you begins to emerge and express itself with these kinds of experiences. The presence and attention of the audience, the pressure of the situation, your training, and your unique life force can all be channeled into the music. It can bring about both focus and abandon or freedom, as you give yourself over. Musicians then become shamans, channeling the energy of the situation into something pure and healing.

    Sometimes you win; sometimes you lose. Either one is okay. But the true winning is keeping your sincere heart through it.
