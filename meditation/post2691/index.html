    First of all, it's not necessary for us to agree. I'm just offering what I can, to help, knowing that you have to find your own way.

    >I resent your opinion that beginners have 'beginner issues' while only experienced meditators have 'real' issues as belittling the cause of anyones suffering seems unhelpful to me.

    I didn't say that beginner issues are less real; and I didn't say that experienced meditators have real issues. You added those ideas yourself, but I'm sorry that what I wrote wasn't clear to you.

    Beginners have beginner issues, intermediate students have intermediate issues, experienced meditators have experienced issues. Originally none of these issues is real; but they may all appear real and significant to the person undergoing them. So wisdom is necessary: insight into the emptiness of things, and proceeding on that basis even if in the moment you can't see it.

    > I also believe that the practice of meditation is completely unnecessary when it comes to enlightenment.

    As regards enlightenment, meditation is completely unnecessary. As regards unenlightenment, meditation is necessary. Insofar as you are enlightened, meditation is not necessary (though you may still practice it for the benefit of others). Insofar as you haven't realized your original enlightenment, meditation is necessary. One could say that true meditation is the expression of your original enlightenment; so maybe we have some area of agreement here.

    >I meditate because I know that the world is internal.

    >...I meditate because these mind states are a source of inspiration and meaning. I also meditate in the hope that I may learn something from them.

    What meaning? Are mind states internal or external? This is a point where it is easy to fool yourself. The direction is not clear, so everything that proceeds from it has unclarity built-in.

    >My practice so far has been to simply sit in a 'meditative position' which is comfortable with my eyes closed. I observe my thoughts and if I am lucky I stumble upon a calm state of being where the world and my body begin to dissolve.

    This method is not good or bad, but it is also very subject to dreaming and self-suggestion. A more structured traditional method would give you better feedback on what you make with your thinking.

    >This 'practice' however has not been working for me at all lately due to my mind state. As you say garbage in = garbage out (are you a computer programmer by the way?). I think you may be right in that sticking to a strict method may be helpful. I will study the approaches of others more closely. Certainly, a more holistic approach is likely necessary.

    >Thank you again for your advice.

    I'm not a programmer, but GIGO is a concise teaching of cause and effect. Good luck with everything.
