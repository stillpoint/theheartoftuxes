    I'm leading a meditative walk this Sunday, and I wanted some tips

    My first suggestion would be to connect to your own experience and interest — why it's important to you, rather than why others should do it. People respond to someone's sincere enthusiasm and energy, not to clever sayings or posturing as an expert. If you are sincere, everything will go well.

    There is the bigger picture of why meditation is beneficial (to help the world, to wake up with all beings, and so on), but the human, personal side is also valid, and people connect to that: "I don't want to waste my life" or "I found that wanting peace of mind isn't enough; I have to do something about it" or "I want to see if this might be a way of connecting with you more closely". Whatever it is, let it be the actual, sincere motive. And if you don't know, then admit that. That kind of spacious honesty is wonderful.

    After the "why", there is the "how": the method. There are many methods, but it's important for the method to be clear. When the method is clear, the practitioner can easily see when he/she has departed from it, and knows what constitutes returning to it. If the method is vague, then the mind can wander and one can imagine that one is doing meditation. It should be clear that moods, feelings, visions, or even special states of mind are not the aim. The aim is to be present with life, as-is. The method just helps you to be present.

    Many walking meditations focus on the movements or sensations of the feet during walking. Choose one object of focus, not two or more. The advantage is that the task is simple and easy to understand (e.g., every moment, stay with the sensation of the foot on the ground; and if the mind wanders, gently and repeatedly bring it back to that task).

    The disadvantage of having a single, simple object of focus when walking in the park is that the situation may be just too distracting, and there may consequently be too much anxiety about keeping the mind focused on the object. This can turn the session into a wrestling match with one's own mind, and that is not good meditation practice. The sights and sounds, the thoughts about walking together and what others in the group are experiencing or thinking, and how one appears to other people in the park — all of these may be too much distraction, unless there is already tremendous sincerity of aspiration and already some depth of concentration.

    The presence of outside observers may tend to make the meditation into a guarded performance ("look at how spiritual I am being") instead of a simple and vulnerable experience.

    For these reasons, I might suggest simply walking in silence without making a big deal about form or method. Merely refraining from talking will already bring a greater connection to the reality present to the senses, and it will likely also highlight the constant activity of the thinking. If there is a desire for an anchor, some alternative to either rampant thinking or having the attention wander here and there, then using the feeling of feet on the ground can form a simple basis for those other thoughts and experiences. That is, rather than trying to focus on the sensations of the feet on the ground to the exclusion of everything, requiring a rigorous focus, it can be a more gentle background connection that embraces the wider scene. Thoughts and sights and sounds and smells can all be what they are, but with a bit of a root, a background awareness of the feet on the ground.

    This approach may harmonize with the situation instead of fighting it. When you are in a more controlled and private environment, or when the intent is very strong and pure, then you can turn to a more rigorous concentration practice.

    Take care couching the walk in terms of "clearing heads". If you try to push away thinking, it will likely come back with a vengeance. It is not that we try to banish or destroy thinking, but that we become free of it — the thinking can be there; we just don't follow it. That is called not-moving mind. You stay at your center, and everything can be as it is. There is no attempt to manipulate the inner or outer world.

    In the end, the best instruction is sometimes the simplest: be momentary. Nothing else is really necessary.

    Good luck, and don't forget to enjoy yourself!
