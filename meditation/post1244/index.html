    TheHeartofTuxes

    Most of us in the west don't understand deep connection with place. In the process of immigrating, and in the process of alienating native peoples from their land, the pioneers may have begun a great cycle of alienating themselves and their descendants. The mindset that says "land is not our Mother but rather our slave" produces a disconnected culture; and that culture then conditions all who live in it, encouraging further disconnection.

    We build a society from our internal structures, and the condensation of those structures into external ones entraps us. We get trapped and alienated by the forms of our own minds.

    Land, culture, heart, and mind grow up together. Those of us without deep roots aren't even aware of the nourishment derived from relationship to a land. How could it even be explained? It is something that can only be experienced, like the love a parent experiences for their newborn child: it is an undeniable knowing or power that flows through.

    As with connection to place, ancestry and lineage also have power, like a river, that gets cut off when we forget or devalue them.

    Though we may give lip service to unity, it's quite a different thing when we get an experience of land-as-heart, or an experience of lineage-as-self.

    Joanna Macy wrote a wonderful book with a wonderful title: World As Lover, World As Self. (Sometimes all you need is the title. You read it and then, "Well, that's all I needed. I'm done.")

    From an online article on (North American) Aboriginal Medicine:

    >Much traditional healing centers around group ceremonies, including prayers, the sharing of a meal, the use of traditional medicines and practices such as sweat lodges. Healing also involves feeling part of a shared culture, of being outdoors and in connection with the land and with nature.

    Korean culture is, at heart, every bit as connected to nature as North American native culture. The emotional experience of nature is emblematic of shamanic roots, where everything is recognized as spirit. (And especially in Korea, this recognition is often integrated through profound, poignant sadness, as evident in Korean Sijo poetry and Pansori drumming-and-storytelling.) If you're interested, OP, perhaps you could seek out a Mudang (Korean shaman) to facilitate your re-connection with the land of your birth.
