    First of all, many people can attest to being overwhelmingly uncomfortable when sitting. It is an interplay of the struggle happening within and between the mind and the body, as well as the simple result of having bodies not yet accustomed to sitting still and upright.

    Your pain may not be any worse than that of other meditators; so it's possible that your weight is an extraneous issue. It may only be another layer of the mental tension and inner struggle you bring to the table.

    ~

    You don't say exactly what the issue is -- what kind of pain, where in the body, appearing in what circumstances, etc. So it's difficult to speak to the problem with focus. What have you done so far to learn about beneficial meditation posture?

    (When we say 'good posture' we may subtly entrench the notion that there is one external shape we're supposed to fit into. That idea itself may cause a lot of pain and struggle. The aim is to realize our okayness, which is fluid, and to embody it with the whole being.)

    ~

    You clearly have a lot of motivation to meditate, and that will serve you well.

    I would recommend walking meditation, or any of a number of movement meditations like Yoga or Taiji.

    I came across an inspiring story of a Korean monk who had physical issues that prevented him from sitting the long hours other monks could sit. Far from giving up, he gained respect from the other monks as he practices at all hours, walking the paths outside the temple, devotedly training in the way that worked for him.

    If you're set on doing sitting meditation, find a way that is comfortable enough.

    The two requirements regarding posture at this stage are that it be relaxed enough to allow some periods of mental calm, and upright enough to support wakefulness. Relaxed and awake. Make these two qualities your aim when setting up posture, rather than trying to conform to particular shapes or rules. Forcing body and mind into set forms will create resistance and rebellion within you.

    Although it's ideal to be self-supporting when you sit, it may take some time to work toward that ideal..So really what's ideal is to do the best within the context of your actual situation. That may entail:

    • getting live instruction and feedback from a meditation teacher and/or an expert in posture and physical ease (qualified Yoga teacher, Alexander Technique, Feldenkreis Technique, physiotherapy, etc.)

    • learning how to set up the body with supports like a firm mat or chair for a base, firm cushion(s) to raise the pelvis to the proper angle, supporting cushions under knees and arms to relieve pressure from various joints, and other possible supports like meditation belt, back support, etc.

    • looking into a dedicated meditation chair that gives extra support (some have dips in the middle so legs can be cradled, and support for the small of the back)

    • doing shorter sittings, interspersed with periods of walking or gentle mindful movement (especially movement while lying on the floor, to relieve the body of much of its weight-bearing tension)

    • spreading sittings (or standing) through the day rather than concentrating the effort all in one session

    • practicing mindfulness methods throughout daily activities instead of only sitting

    • adopting a method with more complex form, that occupies your mind more, relieving you of the vicious cycle of mental checking and growing frustration

    On this last point, consider that you somehow do okay sitting and standing at other times, but that there is some kind of mindset or assumed set of rules you've adopted that makes sitting in meditation somehow more problematic. If you were watching TV, for instance, would you have the same kind of pain? So, often it is the tension building up in the mind that contributes so much to physical pain. A meditation method with more engagement and less opportunity to harp on your woes could be helpful in that regard. Edit: Practicing with others can help tremendously too.

    Good luck!
