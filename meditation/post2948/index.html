    It's not necessarily the wrong place, but meditation may not be the first or best assistance.

    Have you talked to a mental health professional about it? That should probably be the first step.

    See a doctor, ideally someone well trained in old-school diagnosis, to make sure there isn't some physiological or lifestyle-based reason for the overthinking and headaches. Also get an opinion from a psychotherapist (or a few) as to the cause and best course of treatment.

    You are not expected to go through this suffering alone, and you are not expected to have the resources to end it on your own. There are many healing modalities that may ease or completely eliminate the problem.

    If you are at a loss about where to begin your search, you can call your local community information (311 or 211 in many cities), or a mental health crisis hotline (Google it, or find it in the front of your local phonebook), or a local hospital's mental health support group.

    Within the framework of understanding the cause and being guided in healing, meditation can be a useful tool. But if you expect it to completely solve all problems, that's expecting too much. You could end up disappointed, and you could miss finding and addressing an important cause of the difficulty.

    Given that you follow up on the above points, you can use your breath to help you settle down for a moment. One method recommended for anxiety attacks is square breathing: inhale, hold, exhale, and hold for equal counts. If your breathing is slow, maybe you do each part for 6 or 8 counts; if your breathing is fast, maybe you do each part for 1 or 2 counts. As you settle, the breath can become longer and more relaxed too.
