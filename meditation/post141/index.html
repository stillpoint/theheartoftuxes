    Many (not all) monks train very deeply in meditation. There is a state of profound and effortless concentration, a state of unified mind, that is extremely clear and aware but unaffected by any objects that come and go, be they external circumstances, sensations, or mental objects like thoughts and images. It is a completely still mind, unmoved by anything, and with no effort to remain that way. This is called samadhi (it is one type of samadhi; another type engages with objects).

    There are also levels of transcendence beyond physical, energetic, and even what we would commonly think of as mental realms. But in general, we can say that this monk and others like him were engaging in samadhi. Some monks who have performed self-immolation were recorded preparing for it meditatively, bringing their minds to that utter stillness. If they have helpers, the helpers are very careful to let them fully prepare before setting them on fire.

    Short answer: yes, you can reach a point where all kinds of pain are completely ignored. Samadhi is not even that rare; and many advanced meditators access it fairly frequently, if not regularly.

    Not only that, but time and space themselves — which are made by thought — can disappear. People go into samadhi and when they come out of it, it is as if little or no time has passed for them, whether many minutes or hours have gone by in the external world. Some legendary masters sit in samadhi for months or years. A recent news story spoke of a monk in one kind of near-death samadhi, who was mistaken for a corpse. There are also stories of masters who remain in samadhi constantly, radiating blessings to the world for decades or centuries.

    Once perception moves beyond the gross physical form and we begin to understand some of the other levels our being operates on, the limits we previously assumed to be final drop away like mirages. People often first interpret stories like these as tricks, or at best some kind of self-numbing — because that is all these people can understand; it's the only level they've ever perceived. But the monk was actually connecting with something more real than the physical body. More real, more stable, more blissful.
