    Having no 'location'.

    Pretty much anyone who practices sincerely over the long term will experience many different kinds of experiences like this, as well as intuitive or psychic experiences, emotional manifestations, visions, and so on. And depending on the kind of practice you engage in and depending on the forces at work in your psyche, the experiences may crop up much sooner or much later, and at various intensities.

    Concentration practice, where you're focusing on an object (like the breath) can especially tend to induce "special" experiences, because of the way forces move in the mind: when the usual noise is cleared away, something else rushes in to fill the space. When pathways are opened, energies will rush through them. And whatever pressure one puts on oneself in grasping and holding on to the object of meditation also influences the force and quality of those energies, or rather of how they express themselves.

    ALL of that — the objects of meditation, the pathways, the energies moving, and the experiences that arise — all of that is the projection of your mind. It all has the same substance, and so in reality there is no experience more special than another. It's just that to you — to your personality and your thinking — some experiences seem more or less... something. More or less significant or special or wise or whatever.

    If you can use the comfort that arises, or some other aspect of the experience, that's great. But the more non-attachment you can muster in dealing with meditation experiences, the better.

    The ego (our idea of ourselves as something in particular) always wants to exist; and it will do whatever it can to maintain its existence. When you merge with an object of meditation, the ego may become terrified that it will die, so some of these experiences may be ego strategies to stay involved in a drama. Because if you're in a drama, then you exist, right? "I'm a great meditator" or "I'm a bad meditator" or "I'm suffering" or "holy cow I'm disappearing" — all of these have in common the fact that the thought of oneself is regenerating. The ego claps and jumps with glee at that.

    Pure practice means you only do it no matter what arises, and without thought of past situation, present condition, or future result.

    But all of that is not to denegrate any of your experience. It is very often the case that special experiences in meditation convey something "more true" than our common experience. But it still is just a momentary projection of mind, and your job is still to ungrasp it and return to the method. You won't lose the significance of the special experience by doing so, but you will gain the freedom of nonattachment to it.
