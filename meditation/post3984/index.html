    If you want to check, you need an objective standard, like a plumb line or a vertical line in a mirror. You could also have a practice companion or friend check with a yardstick or long level vertically along your back.

    What your position feels like is very often quite different from what your position actually is. That's one benefit of sitting under the guidance of elders: they can check you, and you don't have to fret or otherwise abandon your practice; just, if they correct you, follow their correction.

    There can be various reasons why you might feel out of balance, whether you really are physically off-center or not. These reasons include physical tension, energetic tension, restricted flow of blood or energy in various parts of the body, underlying injury or skeleto-muscular imbalance, and transient mental phenomena (makyo).

    One of the more common causes of these sensations is imbalance in the base of the posture. That is, the legs are not level or are not crossed in a balanced way. One hip or thigh may be tighter than the other, with a resulting compensation in the torso. It can be surprising how compensations show up. Usually it works in a sort of zig-zag: one knee may lift a bit, so the waist tilts the trunk in that direction, so the opposite shoulder drops for balance, so the head tilts to the other side. There are all sorts of variations.

    Other tensions sometimes cause an out-of-vertical posture. If one shoulder lifts, for example, the resulting tension may pull the head to one side; or compensation may pull it to the other side.

    Make sure your basis is firm. The more firm and even your foundation, the more easily the rest of the body can stack up and rest on its structure (rather than using tension to hold it in place).

    If one knee lifts off the mat or carries less weight, you can put some padding under it for firmness until that hip stretches enough for the knee to rest more firmly.

    That takes care of the firmness of the base, but not the asymmetry. So you may also want to place and equal padding under the other knee, to keep them level.

    At other times, you can practice with a bare-bones setup, keeping the body very vertically aligned so that flexibility and strength can be developed in support of that posture. This is kind of an old-school approach, but it works for many. Just don't do it to the point of exhaustion, because then the posture will collapse and you'll just be learning and ingraining a bad slump or twist.

    Rocking back and forth in progressively smaller arcs is indeed the oft-recommended way of finding center. You can also focus on a feeling of the crown of the head floating lightly upward. This tends to tuck the chin down and back, which should happen without effortful pulling.

    It is also important to get the hips in a neutral position. Stability of the triangular base (two kees and the butt) firmly in position will help release the hips; but they should be balanced, with the sitbones being vertical. This neither slumps the lower back out (backward) nor collapses it in (forward); there is usually just a firm, gentle inward curve. (In some people the lower back is fairly straight.) When the hips are neutral like this they can more easily find their balance.

    Once you've set up your sitting posture, you shouldn't obsess over it. Actually, it will follow your mind once you get in the groove. When you achieve a smooth, deep concentration, the energy and the physical body will tend to follow. The energy flows more powerfully, and the body finds a support in that, allowing it to rise to an easy upright posture.
