    How to level up ?

    1. Get clear on the correct purpose and function of meditation.

    2. Offer your practice to the benefit of all others, in all circumstances.

    3. Submit yourself to the guidance of a master and within a formal practice situation.

    4. Learn a proven method well, and follow it meticulously.

    Get clear on the correct purpose and function of meditation.

    What makes you think that not feeling the body is necessary or positive? I mean, maybe it is and maybe it isn't; but where do you get that idea? Next, what do you think is 'beyond'?

    Beyond means this reality, this actual moment. Being in the scene where you are just now and reading this comment: that is 'beyond'. If you seek to attain something outside of that, you are moving toward unreality. There may be many different kinds of experiences along the path, including the disappearance of the world; but these should not be conceived of as outside of or different from this plain reality. The aim is to be free of all concept and habit, functioning well so you can be happy and complete, and so you can show up fearlessly and practically for all others, helping them be happy and complete.

    Offer your practice to the benefit of all others, in all circumstances.

    Many meditators seek to level up, to get greater power of concentration, or special experience, or peace or bliss. But they carry along with them their same old self-centered habit, which taints every effort and every achievement. You can't go beyond your small, illusory self if every effort is made for the sake of that self.

    True maturity in spiritual practice begins when the central motive is no longer centered on the old, petty ego (which seeks control and greatness), but instead opens to the suffering that others have, and seeks to relieve it. Practice to get clear in your life so you can respond appropriately and compassionately in all your relationships, in all kinds of situations. Just the turning of your attitude in that direction is a greater achievement than any special state of trance or feeling.

    Submit yourself to the guidance of a master and within a formal practice situation.

    If you are serious about progressing, you need a proper teacher. One can't move beyond one's own blind spots just from one's own effort: the blind spots are woven into the practice. Whatever you reach for is limited by your own narrow view. So you need someone who has attained the Great View, and who can reliably hold it — a constant beacon as you move through the storms of mind and heart.

    This is also the embodiment of the path beyond the small self. You give up the old habits, preferences, and views because you realize that they are not serving you; and you put yourself in the hands of something greater, something that can ferry you to that which you don't yet see and know. The ego hates this kind of commitment, but the soul loves it.

    Learn a proven method well, and follow it meticulously.

    At higher levels of practice it is absolutely essential to be free of doubt. When the shit hits the fan and you are faced with boundlessness and groundlessness, how will you embrace it and surrender to it if doubt arises? At any moment, you need to be completely clear on what to do with your mind.

    Therefore it is necessary to be clear on the training and the method. Many things can't be conveyed until others are first attained — a foundation has to be laid. So you have to take the time to research and try and give reasonable effort to a teacher and course of training, after which you can reasonably give yourself over to their wisdom.

    This means that one method has to be learned correctly — the why and the how of it — and it has to be applied correctly, without wavering. Vagueness is a hindrance. Picking and choosing (which is based on your habit and blind spots) is a hindrance. Complication and confusion arising because you try to combine teachings or methods are hindrances. If you want to progress, study one method well, with a teacher who has attained the fruit of that method, in a practice situation that can hold the beyond-self practice which you are not yet able to hold.

    ~

    The great Tibetan master Tilopa gave a teaching that points out the correct approach not only in meditation but in spiritual practice in general; it is an extremely clear and succinct teaching that in its original form appeared in only six words:

    > Tilopa's Six Words Of Advice > > Don’t recall. > > Don’t imagine. > > Don’t think. > > Don’t examine. > > Don’t control. > > Rest.

    In other words:

    > Don't concern yourself with the past. > > Don't concern yourself with the future. >
    > Don't make concepts about what is happening now. > > Don’t try to figure things out. > > Don’t try to make, hold, or avoid anything. > > Relax the bodymind and let the whole universe rest as-it-is. >
