    Would you rather..

    Are you asking about others or about yourself?

    There's no single solution right for everyone. It depends on your bodymind and how you sit.

    The base of your posture is important, as a stable base allows the rest of the body to relax in alignment. If your physical base is unstable or not under the rest of the body in good alignment, you will have to put other body parts out of alignment to keep your balance. That creates tension and pain, and hinders the free flow of breath and energy.

    If you are flexible enough to sit on a mat (or carpet or blanket), that's great; it's nice to be close to the ground when you sit. But it's also fine to sit in a chair or to stand if it allows you to be more stable and at ease. Reclining or lying down are not good unless you have no choice.

    The main thing in sitting is for the knees and butt to form a strong triangle. The knees should be solid on the ground. That almost always means the hips need something under them to raise them, like a cushion or combination of cushion and wedge or support cushion, or kneeling bench.

    A zafu / round meditation cushion is versatile. It can be used several ways: flat, on edge, cross-legged, kneeling. A kneeling bench won't give you many options for changing postures. Some people use kneeling benches to relieve pressure on their knees, but sometimes it works the other way, as the body may tend to slide down the angled seat and thus transfer more weight to the knees on the mat. This depends on your particular body configuration; you need to try it out for longer than just a few minutes. (Many discomforts only start to appear later in a sitting.)

    Another issue with kneeling benches is that some meditators find them a bit 'tippy'. There can be a feeling of sitting too high; and aside from sliding slightly back or forward on the seat there's not a lot of recourse for the sitter. Again, it's a matter of preference.

    The angled seat of the bench gives a clue to the right setup of the pelvis. Usually a bit of a tilt to the seat or cushion helps the pelvis to sit in a balanced way. The pelvis and lower back shouldn't collapse or lock forward or backward. That is, there shouldn't be a slump with a concave lower back, and there shouldn't be a slump with a protruding belly. The sit bones should be balanced fairly vertically so the spine and torso stack up on them without muscular effort keeping the body in place. This often, but not always, creates a slight forward curve in the sitter's lower back; but you should focus on ease and stability rather than trying to put the body into arbitrary positions that someone else demonstrates. Work with your body and your situation.

    If you use a cushion, sitting on the front edge rather than on the top may help relieve pressure on the nerves at the back of the legs, making it less likely that your legs will fall asleep. Some find that difficulties lessen with experience; others find that certain discomforts are just part of the practice. As with the posture itself, you should find the right balance between self care and acceptance of outside circumstances.

    Have you received instruction on how to sit? This is a good first step. A lot of meditation centers just pass on standard instructions without much insight into individual body mechanics, but it's a start. A yoga teacher experience in meditation or a knowledgeable bodyworker may be better equipped to show you how to live up without causing stress and pain.

    If you don't know which support to buy, maybe try some out at a meditation center a few times to see what works for you.
