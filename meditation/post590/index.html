    TheHeartofTuxes

    We might say that the purpose is simply to develop kindness. It's part of a body of practice that develops the boundless qualities of enlightened mind (the Four Boundless Qualities, or Four Immeasurables; also called the Brahmaviharas or 'divine abidings'). It turns around our usual selfish, grasping, poverty mentality, and calls on the boundlessness we already possess. That is, it calls on our true self rather than our habitual self-image of being a victim or of needing or deserving some imagined external treasure.

    Thus the practice affirms that your true nature is already kind, already limitless. It's more what you are than your usual self-image.

    Yes, eventually we're aiming at unconditional kindness like that from a perfect parent; and beyond, becoming unconditional and boundless and reaching everywhere.

    But we are not expected to attain that immediately. It begins in a simple way, by connecting with some small bit of sincere kindness and helping it to be alive and grow.

    It's like when you light a handful of tinder with a small spark. All you need is the smallest spark. Then you cradle the tinder closely and encourage it. You protect it, but don't stifle it. You let it breathe, but don't lose it to gusting winds. Then it grows into a small flame... then larger. Soon you feel its warmth radiating. It reaches a point where it takes on a life of its own: you continue to tend it, but it grows and radiates without struggle.

    So all you need is a small spark, just the first little kind of kind feeling. Then stay with it and nurture it. You'll know when it becomes a warmth that you can start to radiate to others.

    No, I will not describe the feeling. Because it is your feeling, your kindness, not someone else's.

    If I describe a feeling, you will then check your experience against my description. But this practice is not about checking. You are not to step outside of the practice, look back at yourself, and start to analyze and judge. That's not the practice. The practice starts from your center, and radiates outward. That is all. Be sincere; and share that sincerity.

    Don't check. Be pure in your practice.

    That means that you train in trusting your own true nature. Don't trust my measure. Don't doubt and judge based on comparisons. Find your own sincere kindness, and share that with all beings. It is already complete; it is already boundless; it is already enough.

    So this is the point: if you are sincere, you won't have to check. That is the principle of all practice; and that is the principle of spiritual life. This is where Buddhist practice differs from some dogmatic or moralistic paths that always check and judge and blame according to imposed external standards. Essentially, and from the beginningless beginning, you are already complete.
