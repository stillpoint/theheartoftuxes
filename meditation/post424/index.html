    That's all heavily dependent on the researchers' limited vision of mindfulness, their willingness to create exclusive categories based on their assessment of stereotypes, the narrow-minded methodology of the study, and the self-reported basis of the data (i.e., the subjects' estimate of what 'mode' of thinking they were using, from a limited and fixed list of possibilities).

    I mean, let's see what kind of relationship the researchers have with creativity. How do they define it for the purpose of their study and report; because defining it already imposes an arbitrary boundary on it -- a boundary founded on the conception of the researchers.

    >Mindfulness and mind wandering can be viewed as opposing constructs

    Why assume that? The assumption conditions all the findings that follow. Mindfulness means not losing oneself in the wandering, not following it. But one can be utterly present and still allow the mind freedom to do what it does best: take leaps and make connections.

    If you start with a definition of mindfulness that doesn't include creative flexibility, your conclusion is a self-fulfilling prophecy.

    It wouldn't surprise me that some forms of 'mindfulness' practice are stilted and stifling. That depends on the method and mental attitude being taught. It also hinges tremendously on the practitioner, their level of attainment, their ease with the method and trust of uncontrived, undirected awareness. With certain understandings of practice, and with certain levels of attainment, a creative freedom emerges far beyond the common.

    The most brilliantly, spontaneously creative people I know are Zen Masters and Zen artists. I know an artist who has written at least six books, has had his art shown in exhibits and collections around the world, has illustrated dozens of books and magazines, has held many live art performances, has taught scores of classes and workshops with lively spontaneity, and has collaborated on a symphony -- if that's not prolific creativity, I don't know what is! And I've never for a moment seen him falter or lose presence. It's just not an object-attached mindfulness. It's at another level.

    Certainly, if you're constantly occupying yourself with single objects, movement will be curtailed. But that's not the only type or definition of mindfulness.
