    Why are you stopping?

    What's your aim in meditating?

    Have you learned one clear method, and learned it well from a proper source?

    The aim should be clear; the method should serve the aim; the method should be clear; and the method should be applied according to capacity.

    It's a chain of cause and effect. You strange the causes that achieve the effect you seek.

    Then, if something's off you can look into why: what cause is not being achieved?

    Sometimes quitting happens because the aim is not clear -- one's motivation is vague. The cure is to look into the reason for meditating, and in fact the reason for offering time and effort to anything in life. If the aim actually makes sense in your life, and you see that it makes sense, then motivation will arise naturally.

    Sometimes quitting happens because there is doubt -- one didn't understand the method well, or one doesn't believe in the method. Proper teaching and living example from someone who has achieved the fruit of practice are very valuable in this respect. At the least, you should study one proven method rather than trying to mix or invent or guess. Then doubt will be reduced.

    Sometimes quitting happens because the mind is too agitated. All of the above cures address this in part. If you know why you're doing the practice, and when you understand the cause-and-effect of the practice, and when you know the steps and requirements of the practice, and when you have faith that performing the practice will achieve the desired effect, then you will be more empowered to keep returning to the method rather than following agitated or defeatist thoughts.

    Another remedy for agitation is physical practice. Physical practice brings the energy down out of the head and into its proper seat in the body. Most traditional paths of meditation include guidance on physical practice, lifestyle changes, and other conditions that support meditation. Live teaching is very helpful. Practicing with others odds very helpful.

    When you're building a practice, consistency is important. It's better to do shorter sittings that you can finish than to force yourself to do something beyond your capacity, get discouraged, and become very erratic or quit completely. Sometimes meditators develop an internal battle that is not necessary, because they try to impose an image of perfect on themselves rather than go gradually and logically, step by step.

    When you complete a short sitting, you can have a satisfaction and self-gratitude that gradually builds and becomes a stable joy. When the good associations with practice have become stable, then you can take on more and more difficulty. But most people who take on great difficulty without first developing a consistent positive association with practice become discouraged.

    Discouragement comes from an urgent, grasping attitude. But if you do short, consistent sessions with a sense of easy enjoyment and patient dedication, then results will eventually appear by themselves. A big part of the practice is returning to correct attitude time and again: openhanded, compassionate, sincere, rather than the arrogance of thinking you can force your mind into some particular form.

    Small, simple steps make a journey.

    Having said that, some things will only get better with more practice. If you get good teaching, then some aspect of progress is completely a matter of doing the practice; and quitting can't help. But if other conditions (as above) have been met and mental agitation is still too overwhelming, then you have to look into a different approach and get other support besides meditation, like therapy and lifestyle changes, possible even medical treatment.
