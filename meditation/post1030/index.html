    Meditation for Letting Go of Control?

    There's no special way to let go; you just do it.

    I should think most meditation methods involve letting go of the need for control as you describe it; but again, it is something that you have to apply. The method itself has no special power, it is a framework that helps you show up in the right way, which is openhandedly.

    So now that you understand more deeply your need to let go, that is something to work on. It will get clarified over time if you keep returning to it sincerely.

    This points to an entire course of training as the support, not only one meditation method, but a whole lifestyle context that conveys the issue deeply and in many different ways. A course of training under the guidance of a teacher, and within a framework of place, schedule, tradition, study, and relationships — that all point you back to practice — is the best support for your learning to let go. The container helps you let go. Strong practice container tends to invite the deepest letting-go. This can be found in retreats, residential practice situations, and long-term commitment to a teacher and practice group.

    Understanding very clearly the reason for letting go is also most practically helpful. You let go, you surrender, because what's originally there before anything is made or held is already complete, already perfectly functioning, already pure wisdom and compassion. Letting go of the need for control is, then, an embodiment of faith in yourself, in your original completeness — what Chogyam Trungpa called your basic goodness.

    Note that we let go of the need for control, we don't necessarily lose control. Sometimes it may turn out that the whole world gets turned upside-down and you realize there's not a single thing you can grasp. How terrifying! How wonderful! But often you will be in everyday circumstances, interacting with the world, doing your work or other practical activities; and there will be the need for both control and giving up the need for control. That is, you perform your activity with full attention and investment, but without clinging to results. This is called "100% action" or "complete activity" or "just doing it". It is also called Wu-Wei or non-doing. You do what you're doing without any residue like checking, judging, or attaching to results. (Most meditative traditions point here; among them, perhaps Zen and Taoism aim at this most directly.)

    So as a practice, work practice or other very active practice can be a very wonderful way of learning and embodying the letting-go mindset. You still have to provide the letting-go; but understanding it with your activity (rather than your thinking) will become a great benefit during meditation. You can call on that embodied participation in the moment, without reserve.

    You ask about contemplations. Any contemplation on impermanence would inform the process. Vipassana, (as you say) noting transient sensations, death meditation, contemplation of bodily processes, and The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind would all fall under this category. The more you can take to heart the ever-changing and ungraspable nature of existence, the more ready you may be to let go. But you still have to do the letting go yourself.

    I see many people involved in breath meditation having difficulty with control issues. The very reason breath is so effective — its extremely subtle bridging of body and mind — also makes it very susceptible to the controlling habit. One gets into a feedback loop between thought and breath. For this reason, although on paper breath meditation is effective and accessible to many different kinds of people, I often don't recommend it.

    Consider the circumstances in which anapanasati was originally taught. A lasting connection with a highly achieved teacher and a group of sincere practitioners (and even monastics) was assumed. There were many external supports providing the practice container, bestowing a profound sense of safety and grounding. When we learn meditation on our own, some of these supports may be missing or diminished, so we try to wend our way between guiding ourselves and yet giving up control, between trusting practice and not yet fully understanding it.

    So if you want to continue with anapanasati and letting go, you may need to gather more supportive conditions to provide a strong practice container. Or you might consider whether another method might be more helpful at this point, one in which the feedback loop is not so likely to appear.

    With any still/sitting meditation, active practice is a very good balance. In monastic settings this would often mean doing physical work gardening, cooking, maintaining the monastery, etc. Some traditions include very vigorous physical practice, which offer numerous benefits besides just balancing the stillness aspect. Prostrations, for example, strongly settle the mind and energy. After 108 or 500 or 1,000 prostrations, believe me, you will be ready to sit down and let go!
