    You'll get a lot of responses based on 'premature absolute'. They'll say that the issue is your mind, not the internet. It's true that form is empty, so we should seek to be free in our mind first and foremost. But to use this as an argument against finding our best, most supportive conditions is premature.

    When one is stable enough not to be at all moved by external situations — which is a stage quite far down the road of practice — then we can be around internet, difficult relationships, trivial or negative activities, and so on, without the need for retreat. But before we have achieved that stability, our mind requires a degree of protection from negativity and a degree of support in returning to what's important. Thus part of good training involves committing to controlled environments.

    With progress, one gradually opens to greater and greater difficulty; and with stability of mind, the difficulty is no longer harm but medicine, honing our clarity, devotion, and most of all bringing out our compassion and being digested as our wisdom.

    For example, in the classic Ten Oxherding Pictures of Zen, describing the stages of the path, 'returning to the marketplace' (also called 'being in the world') comes at the end, after all the other phases and attainments. That's not to say that the phases always come one after the other in a neat plotline; but there's something to be said for taking care of the process until it's complete.
