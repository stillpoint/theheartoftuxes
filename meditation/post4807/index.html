    I have no doubt that many good encounters have been had with your method and similar ones. I'm passing on a warning based on my experience and second-hand accounts. Rather than denigrating your practice I'm honoring it as worthy of care.

    In response to your question:

    Qigong and Taoist Internal Energy Arts often teach that young people and especially young men should not initially focus on centers above the heart because it brings too much volatile energy upward and unbalances the person. In performing standing meditation, for instance, young men and hot-headed people are often guided to keep their arms lower and focus lower in the body to help settle the energy.

    [My encounters with these teachings have been numerous, both in person and in print, but I don't have many citations for you right now. I've included one quote further down in this post.]

    Zen traditions and most overtly the Korean Zen tradition instruct on the difference between energy "up in the head", which relates to overthinking, energy around the heart, which relates to over-feeling, and energy down in the belly, which helps one to be centered.

    >Energy: When we let our energy drop to the Dan Tien our mind becomes quiet and our center becomes stronger. - Dharma Mirror, Kwan Um School of Zen

    Gopi Krishna is one of the more famous examples of energy practice gone wrong. He regarded his kundalini experience as a fast evolution, a transformative upheaval, but it went through some agonizingly painful and life-threatening territory. Krishna wrote his autobiographical account so that others could avoid his mistake — as an informative warning, not as a condemnation of energy meditation or kundalini awakening.

    >Though Krishna's development is often held with the highest of praise, he instead felt only depression and anxiety. And it was the beginning of a nearly 25 year struggle to regain his mental, physical and psychic facilities.

    >Before telling of his struggle with the aftermath of his Kundalini awakening, Krishna gives us a description of Prana and the Nadi (yoga) indicating that Kundalini results in an increase of prana, or the Subtle Energy which controls and regulates all bodily functions. The danger of turning up the voltage soon became apparent to Krishna a few days after his awakening. He portrays his sensations with painful images like "a jet of molten copper...dashed against my crown". He was sick and tired and felt awful. About two months after awakening Kundalini, Krishna believed he was about to die. He had been in agonizing pain for hours, and had barely eaten or slept since his first experience. In his last throws of pain he had a thought; perhaps Kundalini had risen up the pingala instead of the central nadi sushumna. Focusing all his attention he forced Kundalini through the sushumna, saving his life. Krishna felt the burning pain cease, and burning flame in his skull was replaced by blissful radiance.

    TL; DR: There's no denying that different people have different results in different situations. We all tend to recommend what we think has worked for us. There are many different paths that provide benefit for the many types of character and constitution possessed by humans. But let's take some care and responsibility in urging or recommending activities that could have serious repercussions for those taking our advice.

    It's all too easy for us to throw words to the wind in this anonymous forum. We can easily scurry on, while the listener is left to deal with the results of any recommendations they adopt.
