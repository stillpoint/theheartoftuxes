    Meditating while Tired

    First, you don't need to check your condition in meditation. The outer situations -- the conditions that appear in your mind -- come and go. What's important is your sincerity. Just keep your mind sincere in the moment. Present with sharpness, present with dullness, present with clarity, present with thinking: it's the application of open, momentary presence builds your strong center.

    If you practice with closed eyes, please open your eyes. This counteracts drowsiness and dreaming. Furthermore it embodies a more compassionate, engaged relationship with life. (Rather than retreating to your own inner world you open to the whole thing, all reality.)

    Next, you can use a timer so that you can let go of concern about duration, and can take the process out of the hands of your like and dislike. If you leave it to your own whim, your like and dislike will control the process.

    If you really don't want to use a timer for some reason (and in any case), I recommend beautifying the ritual. Create an altar to the symbol of your true mind, whether it's a Buddha image or a calligraphy or empty space. Beautify it with the freshness of flowers or cool water that remind you of your fresh mind. Light a candle that remind you of your bright awareness and warm-heartedness. Maybe light incense that remind you of the best teachings and encouragement wafting to you, and your good qualities spreading out to the world.

    You can also make a commitment that comes from the heart. It's very lively and beneficial to end the day cleanly, acknowledging your negative actions and giving them up (you can give them to the ground through bowing, or give them over to the one represented by the symbol on your altar), and recalling your goodness and committing to it.

    To clean away the daily negativity and remember your goodness is a very beneficial way to end the day. It also programs your sleep, planting seeds of goodness and aspiration that sink into deep levels of consciousness -- deep into the field of your mind, where they will grow.

    Making a beautiful practice place and ritual has its own benefit, but in your case it can also engage more of your being, counteracting the tiredness. You may feel more alert; but even if you feel tired or dull, your heartful expression, your physical and sensory contact with the altar, and your day to day repetition of the ritual will all help more of you to be present and to be heading in a good direction.

    It is a very lively, lovely, organic activity; but on a psychological level it also happens to be a good way to support your practice.
