    TheHeartofTuxes

    It's impossible to make it up. Time only moves in one direction, from future to past. And if you fret and push and struggle you could make things worse.

    The only real life is just now; so just now is the only time when you can have any effect. Even a life of murder and mayhem is to be released in the moment, as you return to momentary open awareness. There is no benefit to self-blame or actions compensating for guilt. There is only: how is your mind just now?

    To say "It's a big deal" or "It's not a big deal" are both wrong. These opposite polarities are illusory thoughts, judgments that appear in the false Opposites-World. Your practice is not in that realm. Your practice is in Truth-World, the world of this moment.

    There is originally no good and bad. These judgments are part of Opposites-World. There is just cause and effect: you do something, you get a particular result; you don't do something, you get another particular result. So if you miss a day, all we can say is "You missed a day. Now what is your correct relationship to this moment?"

    We can distinguish between worry and regret. Worry is not useful. In fact there's a way we trick ourselves through worry and guilt, a way in which we make deals with ourselves: we decide that it's okay to slack off or do bad behavior as long as we make ourselves feel bad about it. The feeling bad only distances us from clearly seeing the results of our actions.

    But if you have regret, that is an energy that can go toward purification, toward clearing obstacles from your path. You feel the regret, you recognize clearly the cause-and-effect relationship between what you have done and its results, you use the energy of regret to cut off the negative action, and then you proceed strongly and clearly â€” essentially, you are reborn, with a completely new chance, a completely new life.

    This kind of renewal is very important in practice. If we don't have sincere repentance and renewal, the mistakes that we inevitably make will keep us off the beneficial path for too long. That is not the way; it makes no use to cry over spilt milk. Clean it up and start fresh, each time!

    > The whole spirit of practice is to start fresh, just now.

    Traditional paths of training have repentance ceremonies and purification practices built into the training. They provide ways to quickly clear the thoughts and emotions of worry, shame, guilt, self-blame, and so on, so we can quickly, quickly return to correct relationship with life and practice.

    Beginners don't have the capacity of seasoned veterans. People with many responsibilities don't have the same opportunity and leisure that others do. People with heavy psychic wounds or addictions, or with otherwise difficult living situations have much to deal with. So in many cases, days will be missed, precepts will be broken, people will be let down, aspirations will fall through the cracks. All the more reason why a way of quickly returning is necessary.

    When you miss a day or mess up on a promise you make to yourself, you might take a minute or two to consider the repercussions, remember your best intentions, give it over (let it out of your hands), and rededicate yourself to practice. You can light a candle or stick of incense, or offer a little cup of clear water, or some other ritual to represent your return to sincerity and the burning or washing away of mistakes.

    If you don't clean away the mistake and return quickly, it will tend to grow and mistakes will pile up. Then there really will be cause for regret.

    In practice places like meditation centers and temples, renewal rituals happen every day, sometimes multiple times. In many Buddhist centers, for instance, the practice of 108 bows first thing in the morning serves this purpose: it gives one a clean start for the day. Prostrations are a body-mind-heart way of 'putting it all down, and standing up again'. You might find that washing your face or going for a run have similar purifying/renewing qualities. Then you just start anew. Since we are always making mistakes, it gives practitioners a chance to frequently refresh themselves and return to balance.

    It is said that the only difference between a master and a regular person is that the master corrects her/his mistakes much faster.
