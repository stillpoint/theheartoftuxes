    Starting with meditation - mindfulness and breath focus

    You are trying out some concentration meditation by focusing on the breath. This is sometimes called "listening to the breath". If you are following a video course that you trust, you should just continue following the course without trying other things from outside of the course. Any teacher you are with: follow that teacher's instructions and eliminate distractions. (Different methods have different effects and purposes, and some are better or worse for different types of people. Some techniques are wide and can be easily used by many people; others are specific to certain mind formations.) There are many good ways, but if you try all of the good ways at once they will interfere with each other and be bad ways. So just return to your method for the duration of the course.

    Following or listening to the breath is a good technique for helping the mind to calm down. Sometime people with a lot of thinking are better served by a technique with more of an 'anchor', but there's no problem with trying a method even if it's not the perfect one for you. Use your own power to overcome any difficulty! Then you become free!

    But it's important not to keep checking on how the mind is doing ("Is my mind calm yet? How about now? No... not yet; what should I do? Should I change something, or should I try harder? How is it now?...") That kind of checking is the opposite of mind becoming calmer, so you have to let it go.

    It's kind of like skiing. At first you want to look at your feet and see everything they do; but that makes it hard for you to relax and move naturally. You need to trust and commit to the method, then the skiing will flow. So in meditation, only return to the method. It is very appropriate for your mind to wander here and there. As a beginner, that is quite natural; and even advanced meditators have a thinking mind that goes here and there, but advanced meditators know how to quickly come back to the present moment. Don't make a drama about going away; instead, make it into nothing, and just quickly return once you realize you've departed.

    So you need to strengthen your ability to come back to the present moment, to come back to just following your breath. At first you will be able to do it a little bit. Then if you keep trying you will get stronger and be able to do it more. But "being able" is not your job. Trying is your job. Okay? Very important! Only try, and don't judge your effort. That will help you progress.

    The upshot of this is that you are not put off balance when different results appear. Anything appears, you can still keep your balance. But on the other hand if you keep checking how your mind is doing and keep changing in response to that, every time you don't get your favorite result you will be put off balance. (And when you get your favorite result you will also be put off balance!) In effect, you are practicing distraction if you think about how you're doing. So whenever your thinking goes away from the method, once you notice it just gently and immediately return. It is this gentle and immediate returning that is your great power.

    This has a sort of beautiful, pure faithfulness to it. "I don't care about how my monkey-mind jumps around. I vow to return to the present moment as often as necessary... a million times... forever...." Once that vow is clear, the mind settles tremendously. But you don't take on the future (that's too big a burden), you just do it very plainly in the moment. Just now, return.

    Hopefully the course teaches correct posture. An upright, noble, relaxed posture helps the mind to have an upright, noble, and relaxed posture as well.

    Yes, it is possible to be clear continuously. It is very possible. And in fact, it is closer to who you really are than your distracted, flitting thinking is. But you should not try to make that happen; you should just return to the method simply and faithfully.

    If after having finished the video course you still have difficulty, you can post again or you can send me a private message and get advice on making changes to the method.
