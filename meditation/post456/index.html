    Especially since so many people these days struggle with anxiety, the grounding effect of feet-on-the-floor can be so helpful. It can be taken as a comfort rather than a burden: "It's okay. I am here."

    It also often happens that a sort of feedback loop arises with the breath, where people fixate on the quality of breathing, and it makes them agitated, and then the breathing becomes tight and agitated, which makes them feel even more tense, and so on. Bringing the background attention down to the feet bypasses this kind of loop.

    >I find myself getting anxious and frustrated when I catch myself not being mindful....

    What are you telling yourself about mindfulness? This is a psychological issue, an issue of your relationship with your own mind and how it is languaged.

    It's funny/tragic how easily we take a lovely, gentle, open-minded practice and make it into the ego's agenda for 'world domination' — the agenda to be in control of everything, to be seen in a particular way (powerful, the 'good student', the 'expert', etc.), to hide and disown vulnerability, uncertainty, and other parts of ourselves that we see as flawed, and to suppress or destroy certain feelings. Very tight and uncomfortable! But it happens so automatically, based on preceding causes: our upbringing and family culture, our social conditioning, and the ways we most frequently use our mind and activity.

    So this beautiful and essentially free activity — just being openly attentive — gets all wrapped up in our inner politics, the ways we try to arrange our mind and experience.

    Fortunately, meditation and mindfulness themselves — if performed correctly — can help us see our patterns. Seeing them, we can start to be free of them. If we are to let these patterns go, we have to first be conscious that they exist, and we have to admit them. Besided meditative practice, you can bring your patterns to consciousness through introspection, dialog with a counselor or therapist, journaling, or art that is focused on admitting and expressing those ego-based fears and agendas.

    So that's the whole big, ongoing project of bringing the ego to light. It doesn't happen overnight; it is a long-term project.

    In terms of method, one of the best pieces of advice you'll ever get regarding meditation and mindfulness is this:

    > When you realize that you've gone away in distraction, return immediately and gently.

    Immediately and gently. Do it as if it's nothing, as if you're flicking a piece of fluff off your sleeve. Often the method of meditation or mindfulness is set up as a battle, a conflict between good and evil or a fight between parts of ourselves. That is not what is intended! Meditation and mindfulness are meant to be unifying, to embrace all of us and all of experience with equanimity.

    So it's important to return to presence without making a drama. Don't make a story about how hard it is, or how great you are, or how bad you are. Don't make any story at all. Don't label the experience. Don't judge the experience. Don't say it's good or bad, better or worse. Don't compare one moment to another. Any time, only return to the method immediately and gently.

    Have you seen how smoke from incense or a candle shifts effortlessly in the air current? That is how easily we ought to return to presence. No big deal; only return. If you take this advice to heart it will serve you throughout your meditation career; and ten or fifteen years down the road when you get great fruition of practice you can look back on this and say, "Yes, that was precious advice".

    Next, in order to return to the method quickly and easily, without hesitation, struggle, or doubt, you need to be clear on the method: why you are doing it, and how it is to be done. That means you need to have learned a proper, proven method, from a good, attained teacher, and to have learned it completely. Then, apply it meticulously, without hedging or tweaking the method based on your whims and preferences. These steps will assure that you can proceed without doubt.

    I recommend studying with a living teacher. There are so many reasons for it; but one that comes to mind in relation to your question is that you will have a living example of how to apply the practice without anxiety and frustration. The living example is every bit as necessary and valuable as the theoretical explanation.

    Some (many) people struggle with anxiety so much that they can't even be that close to their thinking minds. In these cases, physical practice (like prostrations) tends to be more helpful; and often other things have to happen first (like psychotherapy or other healing modalities) before meditation and mindfulness can be taken on in their pure forms.
