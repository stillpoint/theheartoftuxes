    As you continue practice it's important to understand the guidance -- why it's given. What are the reasons why doing practice first thing in the morning is said to be ideal?

    • It establishes the direction of the day, and expresses your priorities. Regardless of what else you may do during the day, centering yourself and working on the great issue of life and death is the most important and essential activity. The way you start your day strongly influences your mind and how you relate to things for the rest of the day; so this is a good time to establish what's most important in your life.

    • It can access deeper levels of consciousness, as your awareness is applied to a mind that has not yet fully formed a distinction between waking and sleeping. In this respect, it's not necessarily so important to be sharply awake all the time; but by the same token, waking up is the essential direction of the practice. If nodding off causes you to lose your practice, that's no good.

    • It can be the most convenient time to dedicate to practice, as thoughts of other tasks may not be clamoring for attention.

    • It can bring greater peace and centeredness to your day's activity, helping you think and act in beneficial ways. If mindfulness and other spiritual practice throughout the day is important to you, starting your day with clarity gives you a big advantage.

    That said, your own situation may make another time better. It's not a rule written in stone; it's a recognition of cause and effect. That's always the basis in matters of form: recognizing cause and effect.

    We might also acknowledge that people who practice in community have a lot of help in practicing early in the day: they have a very regulated work and sleep schedule; they have a very clear set of expectations for practice; and they have the collective effort of their companions in waking up (both literally and metaphorically).

    ~

    In traditional practice communities, physical practice (and often also chanting and devotional practice) is commonly performed before morning sitting. Starting the day ten minutes earlier with prostrations (a very vigorous, energizing whole-body-and-breath activity) or Taiji, Qigong, Yoga's Sun Salutations, a brisk walk, or short run can perform the function of walking you up brightly and getting the energy flowing, without the negative impacts of caffeine.

    ~

    Some practitioners do use caffeine in the morning. If you find that it is helpful without becoming a crutch or leading to exhaustion, you might take it in the form of fresh green tea, high in antioxidants and containing fewer toxic substances (like the oils from the coffee bean that may go rancid and contribute to jittery energy).

    Sometimes, what the body craves and needs in the morning is actually just the warm liquid, which adds energy to the body. A very healthy morning drink to try is ginger tea. This may be enough to wake you up, and actually supports the body's energy rather than just 'stepping on the accelerator'. You can make it by boiling slices of ginger in a pot for 10 minutes (and up to half an hour).

    Some efforts recommend scraping the skin off of regular ginger root before slicing it, to limit ingestion of toxins from non-organic sources. If you can buy organic ginger you can use the whole root. It is said to have many healing properties, including improving digestion, adding energy to the body, and reducing systemic inflammation.

    Ginger tea is considered a staple of health in East Asia, and in some households it is the main 'medicine', used for many different conditions. You can drink it straight or add lemon, and/or honey. You might find that this helps you wake up in a more gentle, supportive way.
