    It depends on what you want. If you want to have a regular up-and-down life with some triumph, some tragedy, and then you die, then going in and out of awareness is no problem. You can get temporary benefit from certain practices, then try to arrange your ideal recipe of showing up sometimes and numbing out sometimes. Or only numbing out a little, and with other nice benefits in the mix. This is no problem, and many people do it.

    But if you want to wake up from the dream of life, and if you want to show up in selfless compassion for all others, consistent and penetrating awareness is necessary. That's just the causal requirement.

    So it depends on what you want. There's a lot of territory between the two scenarios as well.

    Next, getting 'lost' in a movie, say, is not necessarily lack of presence. Really, if you merge with the moment, without resorting to a notion of a self experiencing it, that's a kind of presence or awakening. People sometimes think that presence means you are aware of yourself experiencing presence, but that's not necessarily the case.

    So it's interesting to do radical mindfulness practice and begin experiencing ongoing unity, as one 'getting lost in...' blends with another in a continuous stream. For example, one teacher said that clear mind is the mind you have when you're dying of thirst in the desert and come across water: there's no thought, no self-reflexive notion "now I am drinking", there's only pure drinking — 'getting lost' in drinking.

    Different paths and different teachers will have different definitions of mindfulness. Some say it is a constant self-awareness ("I am breathing... I am walking... I am watching TV... etc."). Others may say it is more direct than that. Yet others claim mindfulness is more 'meta', that it's adherence to ethical behavior and positive mind states, and not so much tracking the external world.

    The main thing is knowing what you want. What is your life for? Why eat breakfast, lunch, and dinner every day — for what purpose? This will dictate how important it is to maintain presence from moment to moment.

    One way of organizing thought on the issue is to consider lost mind, one mind, and clear mind. Lost mind means you are controlled by objects, not present with reality. One mind means you are present, but only present merged with one object, to the exclusion of all else. Clear mind means you are present, non-attached to any object, and can function appropriately as the situation requires.

    When someone imposes on you unreasonably, lost mind reacts according to conditioning. You may react with aggression, or passive-aggression, or flaccid passivity; but it happens from conditioning, not from wisdom.

    In the same situation, one mind only has one object. Maybe you're concentrating on breath, or on a mantra; so one mind would be only breath or only mantra, with no other awareness. You are not affected by the imposition, but neither can you relate appropriately: you are just in unmoving peace because you are one with the object of your meditation. With this mind, you can die, no problem; but you can't help others.

    Finally, in the same situation, clear mind addresses the imposition. Maybe you set aside your personal agenda and generously offer to the person imposing. Or maybe you hold a very clear boundary and communicate a very clear and strong 'no!' In any case, your action is engaged with the situation rather than either reactive or narrow, it is spontaneously appropriate, and it comes from a basis of compassion.

    In actual life, we sift in and out between these modes. The mind comes in and out, wakes up and falls asleep.

    Those who have experienced deep, ongoing clear mind have some knowledge of what it takes to be clear. They know that a half-hour of TV or chatting can destroy the effects of a week of mindfulness practice. They can perceive with sensitivity what supports the mind's clarity, and what undermines it. But those who have not experienced deep clarity don't have the sensitivity to understand the ramifications, so they are likely to think "Ah, what's the harm?" It's similar to someone who smokes and drinks regularly not being able to perceive how various lifestyle changes contribute to vitality or bright awareness: it is all only theoretical for them; they can't actually perceive the cause and effect until they purify their perception. The issue with this question is similar: do you have the refinement of perception to understand the consequences? And do the consequences even matter to your level of motivation, your awareness of life's purpose?
