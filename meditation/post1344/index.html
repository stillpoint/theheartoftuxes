    LOL Thanks for following up on this.

    When I say "clear" I mean nothing gets in the way of things-as-they-are. You can hear clearly, you can see clearly, you can think clearly. Chocolate tastes like chocolate. The blue jay song sounds like blue jay song.

    When we are not clear, our concepts get in the way of our experience. We see what we want to see or expect to see. We make the same mistakes over and over because our beliefs block our true experience. We ignore the taste of our food because our mind is still arguing with the grumpy check-out clerk at the cleaners. We fail to hear what's behind what our partner is saying because we're thinking of sex. We miss ten rounds of breath counting because we're figuring out how to get better at meditation.

    The mind reflects thought and experience. When the mind doesn't move, it's like a clear mirror. It perfectly reflects things just as they are, and perfectly responds to them. "When hungry, eat. When tired, sleep."

    The unclear mind doesn't take things in well, then it thinks something else about them, then feels something else about the thinking, and then tries to hide or arrange the feelings. The being and the situation are all disheveled. Being able to put it all down and return to zero means truth can appear by itself, and your innate intelligence can function by itself.

    So if you are clear — if an object arising doesn't throw you off — you can respond freely. If you are not clear — if your mind attaches to objects — then objects will control you according to your patterns, such as the patterns of like and dislike.

    The Eight Worldy Winds are ways in which we get tossed about by objects that arise:

    > Loss and Gain

    >|

    > Praise and Blame

    >|

    > Pleasure and Pain

    >|

    > Fame and Shame

    >|

    These are groups of "content" that basically enslave us because of our attachment to them. They push us around and tell us what to do. Being able to disengage and return to a not-moving mind frees us from the effects of the Worldly Winds.
