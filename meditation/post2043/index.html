    TheHeartofTuxes

    The thinking mind naturally jumps to objects, handles them, and makes connections. That is its function.

    In human evolution, if someone's mind were solely focused on one thing, unseen dangers and new possibilities would not be accounted for; and there would be a severe limitation of leaps in consciousness like the ability to project future events or the synthesizing of separate events into new outcomes (like making the connection between lightning, flint sparks, and firemaking).

    In our modern lives as well, the thinking mind's function of jumping here and there and making seemingly random connections is very important. So we don't want to destroy that ability. But we can avoid being controlled by it. We can gradually develop stability — command over the attention.

    In part this is done through concentration practice, in which we repeatedly withdraw attention from countless objects and apply it to a single chosen object. This develops the key skill in concentration, which is relinquishmnet — disengaging from subject-object relationship.

    Another important part of developing stability is clarifying life direction. We need to keep touching base with the central point of life. Even if others around us walk around as if in a haze, we need to keep returning to awareness that soon enough we are going to die, and that only ongoing compassion and spiritual practice can help us resolve the great question of life and death.

    When this direction is clear, and when it sinks into us, then objects will not have so much power. The reason so many objects catch us is that at some level we believe they are going to help us — we look to get something from them. Awareness of the one important point helps us let go of all other less important things.

    So when your life direction is clear, you will have a degree of relief from distraction, even if your concentration is not yet fully trained.

    Daily rituals are helpful to ensure that we keep in touch with the important point. In Christianity, one may do morning and evening prayers, and say Grace at meals; and then once a week or more, one may go to church; and several times throughout the year, one may take an accounting of one's attitude and behavior. In other paths, different rituals may be used; but the common point is that some kind of external structure helps us remember the important point in life (if we don't just follow the ritual in a rote manner, making it empty and meaningless). Because if we don't have these external supports we are well likely to be... distracted.

    Your motivation to help yourself and get clear for the sake of others will see you through. Whether you find your good supportive structures in a matter of months or years, the main thing is that you eventually find them and do good practice. Sometimes it takes time. Keep your clear direction, and eventually the way will be clear, like persisting through dense forest and then emerging in open land.
