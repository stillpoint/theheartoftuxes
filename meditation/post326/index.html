    Shying away from mysticism often means lack of openness to what one doesn't yet know. If that is the case, it's an emotional, egocentric reaction rather than practical stringency.

    The ego that needs to feel in control and be seen as all-knowing is like a petulant brat, fighting against being exposed. In this respect, maturity is not found in knee-jerk reactions to things not yet understood, but in fully experiencing and admitting one's own ignorance and vulnerability. The child who knows that he doesn't know is the wise one; the child who stands on top of a sand castle proclaiming his authority over the beach is caught up in his own fantasy, despite how empowered he may feel.

    By the same token, attachment to common materialistic view is emotional and immature in its basis. The 'materialism' of the common person is a vague and baseless view that just happens to be widely shared. When we actually look into the scientific view we see something far, far more vast, nebulous, malleable, and consciousness-driven than the common view of static objects and beings. In effect, real science is far more mystical than people generally like to admit.

    In your post you are not really asking any particular question, but rather broadly labeling a category of offerings as "magical nonsense". Since you don't point out any actual method or teaching, it seems very likely that this is a psychological distancing technique on your behalf. You are not pointing to any concrete thing, but attempting to distance and control a vague category of experiences.

    In psychological terms, this is called your shadow — the parts of yourself that you disown and push into unconsciousness. When someone has a pattern of strong recurring reactions to a particular part of life it usually indicates that their shadow is at work: what they are experiencing is not so much a real-life situation (though there is often some real trigger) but the reflection of their own disowned nature. The fact that they are eager to disown it — to refuse the truth that this is part of them — charges the encounters and causes them to be perceived as loathsome. The fact that they are pushed into the unconscious causes them to seem to appear 'out in the world' repeatedly rather than be known as one's own psychological material.

    Fundamental to our ability to distinguish reality is our psychological wholeness: we must admit and know all the ways that we project ourselves onto the external world, forming it in our own (mental) image.
