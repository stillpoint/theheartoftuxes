    One thing that comes to mind is that you yourself can start practicing a martial art along with meditation. Take a minute or two at a time to practice a kung fu exercise and then sit and bow. Don't try to draw your son in, just model the practice for him.

    The example is far more influential than your words or efforts to steer him. The son digests the image of the father â€” what he does, the actions he performs, the relationships he keeps, the ways he reacts and responds. This informs the range of possibility in his being. He will grow into that range in his own way.

    The other thing is that the father is the teacher of the heart. The mother is the teacher of the body: how to clean oneself, how to eat properly, how to express love through touch, and so on. (Not exclusively, of course, but in general these are the pathways. The mother's influence dominates in the first years, and the father's influence takes over more strongly in late childhood.)

    The father shapes the way emotions will be experienced and expressed, whether they are sincere or manipulated, whether they are repressed or spilled out of control or expressed flowingly, and whether there is an inner sense of truth and stability among them. This all comes from your own daily activity, the way you are from moment to moment and from situation to situation. So your best gift to your son is to do good practice yourself, to work through the shadows and sticking points that would otherwise get passed on.

    The way you relate to your son's unfolding spirituality will be the way you relate to your own. Will you be clear and consistent? Will you give your mind and heart space to be what they are? Or will you try to mold them into a certain shape, and thus invite their rebellion?

    I think the best kinds of questions for your son are the ones that you yourself have. You can wonder out loud; you can get interested in the mysteries of life. You can seek out questions that lead to other illuminating questions. This is a trickster style of teaching: teaching by inviting rather than telling. You can find a good example of trickster teaching in the Tom Brown series of nature guide books, where he writes of his profound connection as a young boy with an Apache scout who became his teacher. You'll also find there a whole world of exploration and ways to practice meditation without necessarily sitting down cross-legged.
