    Difficulty breathing in lotus position

    As often happens, there's a lot of speculation among the responses in this thread. So let's first acknowledge that we don't necessarily always know why events like shortness of breath, panic, or laughter come up, much less know why they come up for a stranger who has provided very little history and context for their situation.

    It may help to take an analytical approach, looking at the cause and effect relationship of sitting in lotus position for you. For instance, what do you know or perceive to be different in lotus, as opposed to other postures? Is there pain somewhere? What happens to the rest of the body? What do you perceive about positioning and tension?

    There's a sort of chicken-and-egg situation here. If something about a particular posture — whether tension, alignment, or physical impingement — blocks the belly and diaphragm from free movement, the shallowness of breath can contribute to emotional and energetic states that go along with shallow breath.

    On the other hand, if something about the posture — pain, tension, or blockage of energetic flow — triggers feelings of being unsafe or pent up, the breath can respond.

    For example, if you huff in short, shallow breaths for a while you can set up a feeling of claustrophobia or fear. And on the other hand, if you watch a suspenseful horror movie you might experience shallow, huffing breaths.

    Lotus is not so appropriate for many people, especially westerners. It takes more hip flexibility than most of us have, and if that flexibility is not there other structures bear the brunt. Aside from the possibility of, say, slow damage to the knees or ankles triggering a defensive response from the organism, you might look into the connections deep in the body that originate in the pelvis and communicate along the lumbar area to the diaphragm. Notice if there's something tugging or freezing inside when you take the posture.

    Jittery panic, laughter, and certain kinds of crying can be very similar in terms of their energetic and physiological expressions. They all involve a buildup of tension that is released in that sort of bumpy way (and often not completely released). To me this points to an energetic posture where the energy is up high in the body, up in the upper chest and shoulders and head rather than settled down into the lower belly and physical base. This is contrary to the ideal embodiment of the lotus posture, which normally should help the energy come down.

    So the question here is whether there is something keeping your energy up high, like physical tension or overthinking or a manipulative attitude, or whether the lotus posture is actually facilitating release of energy that is already held up high. As happens throughout much of one's early meditative career, the jittery release may also be the bubbling up of old habits and patterns, as they're suddenly offered space and open attention; but if this were the case I might expect it to happen as well in other postures as long as the mind is similarly engaged.

    Of course, this all begs the question as to whether you are actually sitting in lotus posture as classically described, with the proper alignments and subsequent open flow of energy, or whether you're pressing yourself into an inferior version of the posture.

    If you are not experiencing any tension or pain from the posture, and if listening deeply to what happens when you sit in lotus doesn't bring any answers, you might do well just to continue following your method — if it is a good, proven method. The self-making habit is so deep and urgent that it will set up all kinds of interesting puzzles or dramatic events to distract you from the activity that will ultimately dissolve it. These manifestations amount to illusion, and there is usually no need to dally with them.

    That bit of teaching is nothing new. "Never mind, just continue" is offered at every turn, and for good reason. Just don't be a slave to it. Take care of yourself; few people will value your well-being as much as you can, and talk is cheap, so you have to do it.

    If the breathing continues to be a problem, feel free to consult about options. If you have a teacher, I suggest bringing these concerns to her/him.
