    Yes, most people find this to be the case. For instance, we may close our eyes when performing a mathematical calculation in our heads.

    But easier doesn't constitute correct or better. We want to train the ability to be concentrated, grounded, compassionate, and at peace within daily life, not only in the seclusion of special sheltered circumstances. If we don't practice with waking life, how can we develop ability in it?

    It goes to show the drawback of practicing based on one's own 'best guess', which almost always means one's own existing preference. We inevitably opt for our easy way, our favorite way, and thus never transform from what we already are. The preference that we've always had rules our behavior.

    That is why, when we realize this issue, we adopt a form that can take us beyond our old self, beyond the way we've always preferred to do things. That goes for having a teacher and practice group and schedule as well: these support us in going beyond our old way of being. Left to our own devices, we just have our own views, assumptions, and blind spots guiding us.

    So this is another meaning of opening the eyes: moving beyond our limited circle, being willing to engage with and take in the whole of this vast world -- in the context of even-minded awareness.

    Having said all that, there is some value to going into concentration and leaving the world behind. It has a specific purpose. It is not the correct direction of practice, and it is not our correct overall approach; but it does have a specific function, and that is to promote the experience of unity with an object. When you and your object of meditation become one, you can get a glimpse of your clear, silent original mind. This glimpse can be very important, very influential in the unfolding of your life and practice.

    Therefore some paths may do 'incorrect' practice for the sake of getting this experience quickly, and then later be concerned with giving the experience the proper context and bringing the proper open, non-grasping attitude forth. In my experience this is only possible within a very specific guided course of training.

    What happens with people who chase -- and even get this quick experience -- is that their priorities are skewed from the start, and a lot of attachment builds up around the experience, which of course all needs to be sorted through. Much better to start by clarifying the attitude and motivation, and then when the experience comes naturally it will already have its proper context and will function properly in your life.

    If you don't have a teacher to guide you through this territory, here's something you can try: Do a timed sitting with eyes open, maybe five minutes of practice; and then do another timed sitting for a minute or two with eyes closed. This is not for the sake of comparing! Don't make ideas about which one you like better! Only use the correct function of each: With open eyes, develop equanimity and the ability to return to your practice within real life. With closed eyes, go deep into concentration.
