    Looking To Become A Certified Teacher...

    This attitude is surprisingly common, and it comes from a sincere place, no doubt; but it is quite premature. You need to devote yourself to a practice overseen by a competent teacher; and you need to offer yourself to that practice over time so things can be built up step by step in natural order; and you need to practice closely with others so that your own opinions and obscurations can be worn down.

    It's not about becoming something (a teacher, or whatever); it's about being able to let go of becoming something, from situation to situation.

    When we're on our own, our practice goes very much according to what we like and what we know. Having our own favorite situation becomes the rule of the day; furthering our own opinion becomes the agenda. That is not a fruitful circumstance for the development of wisdom and compassion. You may think you know something; and you may actually have some knowledge; but it hasn't been trained and tested in relationship, so in fact this phase can be 'worse' than a beginner — you think of yourself as having something, where a beginner would at least be free of that delusion.

    Once you come face to face with a proper teacher and teaching situation, you may actually find yourself being torn down for a time — the things you've been holding on to may start to get taken away. It is far, far better for everyone if that happens in your training rather than happening when you've installed yourself as a guide for others. If the breaking-down phase arises when you're trying to teach others, will you still try to hold on to your public image, living a lie? Will you break down in front of others and leave them stranded? First practice without thought of becoming something.

    Next, presumably you wouldn't want to share vagueness, uncertainty, and shallowness with your prospective students. If you are to share clarity, sincerity, and depth these qualities must become part of you — not just ideals, but woven into your very being through the strength of your training. A teacher and group practice situation are indispensable, so you can learn the things you didn't think of in self-training, see the perspective you haven't yet come to know or appreciate, and discover how your practice functions in relationship and under pressures that you haven't submitted to on your own. One of the best things that can happen to you in formal training is seeing how far you have yet to go.

    It will also go better for you in your practice. Any role or self-image you create can easily become an obstacle. Give yourself the benefit of a clean slate, so you can explore training and devote your energies to the proper aim — toward deepening your practice and clarifying your character, rather than toward bolstering a role or lifestyle that soon won't fit.

    If teaching is part of your karma, it will appear later as your practice bears fruit. But if some other expression is part of your karma and you instead try to become a teacher, you will ruin that wonderful and natural expression. Be open-handed about how the path opens for you, submit to the instruction of a good teacher, and your life will go well and be of service to others whatever you do.
