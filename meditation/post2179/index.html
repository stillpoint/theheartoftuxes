    Yes, as you already surmise you're confusing unwinding for meditating.

    The game takes your mind away, for the most part. That's all well and good, but how will you do it for yourself when there's no game? Meditation means you make the effort to let go of your mind, to 'put it all down'.

    Lifting weights and other exercise are actually helpful beyond just relieving stress. Physical activity helps settle the mind and energy (and has the obvious health benefits). Video games engage the attention; that's what they're designed to do. People feel this as a relief because for a while they no longer have the whirring loops of dialog that usually occupy the mind. But the nature of the activity itself isn't conducive to settled mind and energy. If anything, agitation is drawn out to the point of exhaustion.

    Much is made of the notion that "anything can be used as meditation". In theory this is almost true; one can try to stay present and aware while doing almost anything. But that's far from how it actually unfolds in real life. Some activities support us in training our ability to return to presence, and some activities distract us and fool us into thinking we're being present.

    I suppose the litmus test is how well you do without the activity. If you stay mindful of your feet touching the ground step by step, can you continue that attentiveness when the walking stops? If you think that playing a video game is meditative, how does it go for you when you turn the game off?

    In general, good objects for concentration-style meditation are stable and neutral. They don't incite desire, aversion, anxiety, or passion. They don't encourage trains of thought or prompt habitual reactions (like the fear, frustration, and pride that make up the emotional content of a video game). In short, an object of meditation provides a simple structure for you to practice coming back again and again. It doesn't reach out and grab your mind, it is merely there so that you can come back to it.

    It is your coming-back, returning to the real present moment, that is significant. Your peace and your freedom are utterly dependent on your ability to return to the real moment â€” not fantasy, not the world of thought, not a virtual realm or dream realm, but the real feet-on-the-ground living moment.

    If you learn how to bring your attention back to the moment reliably, then you can 'take it on the road' and apply it in everyday activity. It will be easier to do with some activities than others. Simple walking in a safe place is a good way to train presence in activity. Some people practice a mantra constantly throughout the day, and that is what they keep returning to as they wash, eat, travel, work, and so on. One is already a little bit advanced (let's say intermediate) if one is able to maintain mindfulness without a supporting structure (like a background awareness of the rhythm of breathing, or of footfalls on the ground, or a mantra, or keying on tactile engagement, etc.). And even very advanced practitioners keep the structure that they started learning with. Thich Nhat Hanh, for example, famous for the breath meditations he teaches, still practices mindfulness of breath himself from moment to moment.
