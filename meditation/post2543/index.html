    Living a more 'Monk-ish' lifestyle

    Yes, mind and life are not two separate things.

    I don't see those positive activities as particularly “monkish", but rather as examples of clear, beneficial living. Cause and effect are clear.

    For the common person, mind conditions life, and life conditions mind. For the person who has glimpsed the nature of life or mind, the fact that there is no separation emerges: the very substance of life is mind itself.

    If the mind is to be calm and clear, correct behavior and correct environment are necessary. This is very significant for someone starting out on the path of meditation. But mind is primary. As one's center grows stronger, the influence of environment becomes less critical, until a person can go anywhere and keep a clear mind. This is demonstrated by such luminous figures as Thich Nhat Hanh and Maha Ghoshananda, who have each done a lot of work for peace in war-torn situations.

    As to conduct, it Is not possible to keep a clear mind when anger, desire, guilt, shame, and other negative influences are present. The cause and effect relationship between meditation and behavior in daily life are thus quite clear. You can't abuse others or generate repeated attachments and then sit down and experience peaceful silence in meditation. If a person could do that, it would be severely compartmentalizing his mind, making it less whole, less sane.

    Early phases of spiritual practice almost always require a set of rules and guidelines, to change one's activities from negative influences to positive ones. As one progresses in both insight and power, the rules may open up: the practitioner begins to see all experience as awakening, so both positive and negative dissolve into simply transformation. One's action becomes spontaneously beneficial. At the highest levels, even transformation no longer becomes necessary, because the practitioner sees the essential awakened nature of everything -- there is nothing to transform. At this stage, one is a clear vehicle of truth, a constant source compassion.

    So the need for rules and guidelines of conduct inversely parallels insight and attainment: the greater the attainment, the fewer rules necessary. The greatest masters simply have no need for rules. Having said that, there have been many great masters who nonetheless choose to maintain rules of conduct, either as a sort of devotional offering, or for the sake of others who still required rules.

    Short answer: yes, definitely; these lifestyle choices have a big impact on clarity of mind. But one shouldn't obsess about them, thinking they are the sole source of awakening. They are part of the whole picture of training.

    Edit: I should mention that it works the other way too: as one becomes more clear and mindful, one sees cause and effect in greater detail. One may begin to notice not only how sleep routine and electronic entertainment affect the mind, but how leaving a fork unwashed or failing to acknowledge someone's good work may prompt one to fall out of integrity. Momentary changes in posture and attitude may be spotlighted.

    A sincere student with good motivation endeavors to perform even the smallest good action, and uproot even the smallest negative influence. “A single spark can burn down the entire house." Like OP, we can notice how one negative action can lead to a whole series of others, soon snowballing out of control. Positive actions, too, can build upon each other, carrying one along on a stream of practice.
