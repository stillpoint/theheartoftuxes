    Is it still effective to meditate sitting on a chair versus sitting on the floor?

    It depends on the method and the aim. For most common meditation forms the most important issue of form is to have an awake and a relaxed posture. That can mean sitting on a cushion, or on a bench, or on a chair, or standing up.

    The main points would be to have a firm and stable base, and an upright posture that doesn't create undue discomfort. For the sake of both overt and subtle awakeness, the eyes should be kept open. (This, again, depends on the specific method and aim, as some scanning, visualization, or relaxation styles do better with eyes closed. But otherwise, closed eyes promote attachment to dullness.)

    There needs to be a middle ground between forcing oneself to sit through pain, and obsessing about comfort. Part of the process is to find one's inner peace despite outer difficulties; but self-harm won't do, and pain that makes meditation impossible (at one's current ability to concentrate) also won't do. The mid-point of balance between forcing and indulgence tends to be more rigorous than many westerners are used to.

    Knowing your purpose -- your best purpose -- for meditation, and keeping to a clear, proven method will help eliminate doubt and distraction. You should be clear on points of form and why they matter.

    In some forms of meditation, a particular posture is much more important, even critical to the success of the practice. This has to do with the flow of subtle energies, for example, and other reasons beyond the scope of instruction for beginners. But if you are starting out on such a path it will be important to maintain proper form from the start, so you should follow the guidance of your teacher and not let your own assumptions and delusions get in the way.

    Mental and physical posture are not separate. Each conditions the other.

    You should also realize that you are a living being, and that your situation changes from day to day and season to season; so what works for you on one day shouldn't become a dogmatic imposition on a subsequent day. Therefore inquiry and sensitivity tend to be more valuable than generalized statements about hour things should line up in the body. This is another reason you need to be clear on the aim and method, so you can be wise rather than arbitrary about the changes you make.
