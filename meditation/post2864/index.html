    Meditated for an hour- felt worse. Is this normal?

    Thank you for your comment. That's a really good point, that words get thrown around and we don't necessarily know what they're pointing to.

    When I talk about reality here, I mean whatever is here before you make anything. I don't have any big ideas about it. Before you make any idea whatsoever, and before you throw around words, what is actually in front of your face? When you let go of all making, wanting, holding, avoiding, what are you left with?

    So when I talk about reality I mean the thing that's not a concept.

    Often we're so caught up in concepts that it's not even clear to us there's a world that doesn't depend on concepts. So we're often living in our thought world, not the real one. Can we see clearly? Can we taste clearly? When we splash water on our face, do we get its wetness? When someone is suffering in front of us, do we get that they are suffering? That's all, no special idea.

    What's in front of your face right now is not independent from you. All experience is completely intimate with you, and you with it. The only thing that makes us separate is our own mistaken assumption about what we are, and our own ongoing effort of separation through thinking. But really, the place where this internet conversation is happening is you.

    I agree that just closing off from certain experiences or parts of the world won't lead to insight, though sometimes we need to quiet some things down so we can listen to others. But correct meditation isn't shutting off or avoiding. Quite the contrary, correct meditation is showing up for whatever is here. I hope you'll be happy to know that!

    >I get the impression that mediation is not about coming to know the definite, but accepting the indefinite.

    Meditation is just open-handed. Definite and indefinite both have their correct place and correct function. But definite and indefinite are concepts; like all opposites, they arise from thinking. Originally, there is no 'definite' and 'indefinite'; everything is just clear, as-is.

    So the meditation path lets go of the world of opposites made by thinking, and returns to the clear world, as-is. You trade your idea for direct experience. That's a good trade!

    I didn't say anything about being without preference. I said you can be free of your preference. This is something that is not well understood by theorizing. When you do a practice of putting it all down, moment to moment, situation by situation, you can not only understand freedom from preference, you can embody it. The name for that is equanimity (upekkha in Pali) and when it is offered to the world it is compassion (karuna).

    At the perfection of practice, yes, some beings are completely liberated from all personal preference. It is difficult for us, in the clutches of our own ignorance, to conceive of the mind of an awakened being. Rather than speculating about what is possible or not, we should take practical steps to clarify our own lives and learn how to live well with reality.
