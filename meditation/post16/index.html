    Very interesting talk. But hate the way it's promoted.

    I'm so done with the 'brain-hacking' schtick. Also, "I'm going to share some secrets with you" — yeesh!

    Look, this has application in our performance in the workaday world. It goes along with so much of the personal development field: very good information for a particular, limited purpose.

    We might ask (among other questions) what a lifetime of 'brilliant success every day', and then dying, will get us. These kinds of talks tend to beg the question, and expect us to tacitly agree on the value system behind them. It's not wrong or bad — this is actually useful information — but it's not getting to the more fundamental issues. Although he revealed layers we don't often consider, we might say that there are still layers left unacknowledged by the speaker.

    The correct, intended purpose of Vipassana is far broader and deeper. In fact, it has a radically different aim, which is to reveal the true nature of life itself. Rather than trying to control outcomes within a dream, it aims at waking up from the dream. Once one has woken up to the actual nature of the dream (i.e., seeing that it is all made of your mind), ultimate control is possible and the dream can be used appropriately, rather than being your only realm of activity.

    So your doubt about whether to abandon Vipassana just goes to show how important it is to understand the aim of any method or course of training, and to be clear on what you are on about, what the bottom-line purpose of your life and effort is.

    The method shown in the video is not at all new. It's essentially controlled breathing, which has been taught for thousands of years. In this case, it is also informed by biofeedback, which has been investigated and used since the turn of the last century (initially studied around 1865 - 1900), and which gained popularity as a meditation aid in the 1970s. (Oh, and the ancients had biofeedback as well: some of the asanas and mudras, for example, are meant to indicate the state of the bodymind, as do various other signs passed down from meditation master to student.)

    The effect of controlled rhythmic breathing has been known scientifically for perhaps 8,000 years or more in India; and Indian yogis (and practitioners in many other traditional systems like Taoist yoga, tribal shamanic practice, and so on) also developed a great deal of insight into how different rhythms have different effects. The speaker here seems to think he's passing on some amazing new information, but really he's not even touching the tip of the iceberg as far as ancient biofeedback technology and knowledge goes.

    One of the aspects not taken into account in this talk is that control implies a controller. While it is true that regulation of breath can regulate energetic, chemical, anatomical, and emotional layers of being, it also perpetuates the sense of selfhood — and maybe we should say the delusion of selfhood.

    The higher level of refinement found in the breathing recommended by many traditional methods of meditation also embraces release of control. There is regulation, but in concert with the dissolution of a supposed controlling being. One doesn't regenerate the delusion of selfhood.

    So not only are emotional and performance attributes enhanced, but one is gradually freed from the limited field of activity and contrived (and perhaps unconscious) value system that go along with merely seeking performance enhancement through control.

    There are many other considerations, including why the breath is followed at one place or another... or left alone completely. People can train in these studies for many years, so this is beyond the scope of one post or thread on Reddit; but suffice it to say that the collective body of knowledge already out there for centuries is vast.

    In general terms, consider the difference in 'brilliance' or 'success' between a Zen Master and a champion athlete or successful businessperson. The attainments are on a completely different scale. The athlete or businessperson has found temporary success; the Zen Master is free beyond life and death.

    So breathing technology is just a tool that can be used in shallow or deep ways. Both shallow and deep can be positive; but which one do you want? The aim and intent are all-important.
