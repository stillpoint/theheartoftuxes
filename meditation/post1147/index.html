    Many things will come up in meditation, for a few different possible reasons. But most meditation is about remaining stable and not attaching to objects, whether they're external or mental objects — getting sucked into things. That's the practice of being clear and free. So, short answer: yes, it can be ignored.

    One of the things that happens when you start calming and relaxing your relationship to the mind is that the levels of mind find more connection. Previously rigid and arbitrary boundaries become more fluid. This is part of the process or realizing things-as-they-are. Previously, the patterns and perspectives you've been given by your parents, family, culture, and peers determined how you see the world. Now, little by little, those rigid ways of seeing are being relaxed, and things are allowed to be what they are rather than what you think they are.

    So as these mental structures soften and become more natural, material usually gets released and finds its way into your awareness. It's just dream stuff. Its origin may be recent or very old. But it's just stuff that is finding its way out, so you can be clear. Maybe it's like washing a dish and leaving it in the rack to dry: the water finds its way out into the atmosphere. As far as you're concerned the main thing is that the process continues.

    So for the most part you leave it alone. This is very important in meditation: sticking to the method. Most people have some degree of doubt that creates an obstacle in meditation; they don't fully believe in their own basic mind. So when certain thoughts or experiences arise, they have more faith in them than they have in letting-go and letting-be.

    For some people, it's negativity that grabs them. They think they have to 'deal with' any supposed negative thing that arises. For others, it's positive or special thoughts or images that grab them. They become self-fascinated and like to think of themselves as special for experiencing something unusual or 'spiritual', and so on. Either way, it means that ego is functioning rather than meditation. The ego is finding something to do to keep itself alive and in control of everything.

    So if you attach to things that come up, realize that at that moment you are choosing to depart from meditation and coddle the ego. "I just had this experience. Wow. Poor me." Or "I just had this experience. Wow. Wonderful me." Or "I just had this experience. Wow. What does that mean for me?"

    In some paths that deal with dreams and dreaming, certain material that comes up is reflected upon as a message or invitation from the deeper mind. This is a very different approach from most meditation. Regardless of your chosen method, you should be clear on the method. What is the method, and why are you doing it? Then you will know in an instant what to do with objects that arise in the mind. In most meditation, you must refuse to get caught up — you don't take the ego's bait — and you return gently and instantly to the method of meditation. This is the power you train, the power of returning. When your power of returning is strong, nothing can take you away from reality for long.

    Some visual manifestations also arise because of firing of optic neurons (a phenomenon called phosphenes), either randomly or because of stimulation or in some cases lack of stimulation. (This is sometimes related to the mind's/ego's story-making activity, which becomes more prominent during sensory deprivation — an event sometimes called 'the prisoner's cinema'). In the dark, or with closed eyes, these perceived lights become more noticeable. In fact, meditating in total darkness or with closed eyes is not recommended because it greatly encourages sleepy and dreamy mental state rather than clear meditation.

    Phosphenes can appear in a sort of psychedelic eye-shaped pattern, simply because that's the shape of the retina and optic nerve where the play of light appears. Even more reason to keep the eyes open and refrain from getting caught up in the event. Only return to your method of meditation.
