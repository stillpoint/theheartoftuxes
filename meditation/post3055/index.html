    Next, you can realize that it's just another mental object among other mental objects, and keep spring yourself to the method. If you trust the method, only do it.

    Complete silence is not required. For instance, sometimes thoughts will keep nattering away in the background, or sometimes the sound of traffic or construction outside could be continuous. Tinnitus is not different in its essence; it's just another thing that appears on the movie screen of the mind. The point is to identify as the stable screen rather than react to any of the appearances projected on it. Don't move either way, toward or away from whatever appears.

    You emphasize relaxing the body and letting go of the mind's urgent arranging, and then return to the object of meditation.

    Difficulties and distractions are actually very wonderful in the context of meditative practice, because they show is where we're still clinging and reactive. They help is being forth greater depth of concentration and greater equanimity with the coming and going of things. So if we have a clear direction, 'bad' conditions help us to be unconditional.

    >A bad situation is a good situation; and a good situation is a bad situation.

    If you do truly make your best effort and still can't maintain reasonable concentration, then you can help yourself with other practices, or you can change.

    Physical practice can help the mind settle drastically. When the mind is settled, outside things become no problem.

    Next, you can often use supports, generally touchstones that employ the senses to support the meditation: you can use your voice to help you (counting breaths out loud or voicing a mantra); you can use a mala to help bring you back with the sense of touch; you can train and regulate the breath (a very powerful support, though often misused). In some cases you can even incorporate physical action, like walking or bowing or (if the method allows it) rocking. Some practices combine all of these elements, with the body, speech, and mind all engaged and involved in purification. Going further afield, shamanic practices more involved with trance than wakefulness often use rhythm in the form of dancing, drumming, or using a shaker; and traces of those practices remain in traditional religious forms like chanting while hitting a precision instrument.

    The other alternative is to change practices to something that you are naturally more involved with. This is dangerous ground as far as practice goes because it begins to invite the picking and choosing mind, and encourages the false notion that progress is a matter of finding a magic technique that makes things ready for you, rather than using responsibility for your own mind and putting in the effort to break through. Those with great confidence in their practice (and in themselves) are usually those who have broken through obstacles with their effort, not those who keep seeking short-cuts.
