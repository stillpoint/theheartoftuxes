    Physical practice is an important and effective tool for ADHD/anxiety constitutions. Rather than coming in direct contact with your thinking mind in meditation, bring awareness to your body and get active. The body is your major grounding influence.

    Prostration practice is very highly regarded in many traditions. It can bring your energy down very quickly from being stuck up in the head to is more stable, settled place in the body.

    Other rhythmic, whole-body-and-breath movements can also be performed mindfully to help the anxious and overthinking pattern settle. Swimming, running, rowing, cycling, and free dance (these days called 'conscious dance') are all good options. Some (such as running and cycling, which may happen on city streets) are more prone to distraction.

    Walking meditation could be a great option for you. Coming back to the sensation of feet contacting the ground is an excellent grounding influence that doesn't involve struggling with your thinking mind. It has the same rhythmic, physical aspects that are so useful in times of agitation.

    Walking can be performed very slowly as a concentrated meditation; and it can also be used more openly as a mindfulness practice during the day -- that is, not going deeply into silence but just having the feeling of the feet to bring you back to what's happening in the present moment, in the scene you're in.

    Walking can also change with your mood and energy. You can calm down by consciously taking slower steps, out you can match and soon guide frantic energy with quick, vigorous walking. In addition to all of this, it is a healthy practice physically, and it can provide a healthy change of scenery of you take it outside.

    Yoga, Taiji, and other movement meditations can also in some cases be complete paths, including important lifestyle training in addition to the in-studio practices. (It depends on the teacher and the score of their experience and training. Traditional practices tend to be more wholistic, more inclusive of all of one's life and being.)

    As to having piano music playing, it is not a problem. It helps you relax. If you want to be able to relax without relying on music, then you will have to practice without it; but in itself is neither good nor bad. It's playing in the background, you aren't distracting yourself with it (you aren't listening to it instead of doing meditation, as some people do) so no problem.

    People are usually more calm around ambient background sound than in complete silence. In old Buddhist writings, for example, the ideal meditation space is described as a natural setting, where you might hear the flowing of a stream or a breeze moving through trees. Many people find it easier to work or study with the sound of rain or the murmur of unidentifiable conversation in the background.
