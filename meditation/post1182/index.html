    That's a valuable experience, if you don't get stuck there. It's important to also know that things are as they are. Emptiness doesn't mean the universe of things is completely non-existent. After emptiness, lemon still tastes like lemon, and lime still tastes like lime.

    In fact, emptiness is the taste of lemon, and of lime... and the blueness of sky, and the cold and wet pelting of spring rain. Emptiness is spring — it doesn't unfold without emptiness — and you, and your moment to moment life.

    Emptiness means that things can be what they are — they can co-create, interrelate, move, express, arise and pass. If things weren't empty — if they were only solid and stable within themselves — no event could bring about another; and no thing could change or interact with other things (and perhaps more to the point, no thing could interact with you).

    Thus, as the Heart Sutra says so concisely, not only is all form emptiness, but emptiness is all form.

    This critical point is why so many masters and traditions constantly point the student back to simple groundedness, even after tremendously transformative experiences. It's like that Zen saying: "After the ecstasy, the laundry."

    Without this very wise grounding influence of proper training, one's experiences, which in themselves may be significant or at least harmless, almost inevitably fly skyward into concept and story and capricious fantasy and self-congratulation.

    One of the best things you'll ever learn — if indeed you take it to heart and learn it — is not to make much of any experience, no matter how thrilling or reframing. Don't attach to anything, but make it a well-founded, absolutely consistent discipline to keep moving on to the next thing.

    As you develop skill in moving on, you let go of the need to even frame or name these kinds of wonderful experiences as anything (even 'wonderful'). Thus you let them be completely what they are, without interference or limitation. That is great wisdom.

    The great haiku master Basho wrote:

    > how admirable to see lightning

    > and not think "life is fleeting"

    How wonderful not to add a single thing to reality, to the way things unfold.

    If you learn this one thing, it will serve you throughout your meditation and spiritual career. 100% guarantee.

    ~

    Edit: There is another point here. Without making the mistake of opening your mouth and making something of your realization, you may not have received this teaching. So we should also not expect perfection to mean never making a mistake. In fact, if you make a mistake and keep moving, that is perfection, as pure and empty as your realization.

    When asked "What is Enlightenment?," Shunryu Suzuki Roshi responded, "One continuous mistake."
