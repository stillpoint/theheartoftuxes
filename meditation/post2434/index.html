    Is meditating crossed leg superior to meditating sitting on a chair?

    >Is sitting crossed leg in anyway superior to sitting on the chair and is something worth spending time to get good at?

    Yes, the traditional cross-legged posture has many benefits over other postures. It is the product of -- and has been verified by -- thousands of years of spiritual technology and the experimentation and insight of tremendously evolved beings.

    I will get into some of the 'superior' benefits, but first: the salient point is whether the posture is superior for you in your current practice, in your current situation. The great benefit of sitting on a chair is that you're doing it. It is helping you to continue with meditation, and to put your attention in the meditation rather than fussing with other things. This is the first and foremost benefit, and mustn't be underestimated.

    At higher levels of meditation, where subtle states of mind are accessed and subtle energies are circulated, the cross-legged posture becomes more critical. Hindu yoga, Tibetan Buddhist yoga, and what amounts to Taoist 'yoga' all describe in detail the effects of very specific points of posture, from the way the chin is held, to the position of the chest, to the way the hands and feet are placed. Each point of posture has an effect on the way energy is circulated through inner channels. So when, in the more subtle or so-called 'secret' Tantras, where all of one's resources are brought to bear in order to achieve a meditative goal, the smallest change in posture can make the difference between success and failure. That's not to say that posture is a make-or-break issue. Mind is primary, and the right mind can overcome wrong posture. It's just that posture is often the support a particular meditator requires in order to succeed in a particular situation.

    Even before these levels are reached, the cross-legged posture is beneficial as a very stable and grounded foundation. Long sessions can be undertaken in this posture, more than any other. It provides the ideal balance not only physically, but mentally as well, between rest and wakefulness. It makes best use of proximity to the ground, gaining physical and energetic stability. The posture itself induces meditative stability; so much so that even when the mind is racing, sitting in the correct posture can subdue it. And it provides a framework within which the belly can relax and the breath can circulate deeply.

    But again, for you these benefits may not be as significant as the fact that sitting in a chair helps you actually do meditation. (In long retreats, I usually sit cross-legged about half the time, and on a chair half the time. I prefer cross-legged, but need to be realistic about my physical/energetic situation.)

    The right balance needs to be struck between comfort and vigor. Changing according to whim only increases the hold whim has on you. Forcing the body into positions it's not ready for is also an ego-based activity, and brings harm to the bodymind. For deep practice, the balance is usually quite a bit more rigorous than we'd adopt on our own; due to our comfort-seeking habit we're far more self-indulgent than we realize. Thus following a teacher and practice group is nearly always necessary if we are to break out of self-indulgent patterns.

    If you are sitting (or even standing) comfortably without moving, and your mind is on the meditation, I'd suggest continuing with that posture -- in your case, using the chair. As you progress in meditation and it becomes clear that you will be moving into more subtle practices or sitting for longer periods, you can add a supplementary practice to prepare for cross-legged sitting. (Also, find a teacher as soon as possible!)

    Don't think of the new posture as something you're lost without, but rather as an adventure into new territory, expanding the range of both body and mind. In any case, physical yoga practice is a very good sister practice for meditation. An open, flexible body supports an open, flexible mind, and vice versa. Closed or locked body parts nearly always reflect a related closure of the psyche (hips with personal power and sexuality; chest/lungs with sadness and expressivity; shoulders with fear and mental tension; and so on); so opening these parts can open broad new territories in meditation as well.
