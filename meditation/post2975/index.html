    meditating on a thought vs. mindfulness meditation. can someone explain?

    Eckhart Tolle's book A New Earth: Awakening To Your Life's Purpose is very good. He doesn't give a lot of meditation instruction in it, but helps you to start looking into the 'why?' part: Why meditate, or why do anything in life; for what purpose?

    Tolle offers meditation instructions online and in other resources, but the method described below is very good and will work with his teachings or on its own.

    If you don't have a preference and you're looking for one practice you can do for health, stress relief, clarity, and calmness, I recommend counting the breath.

    Here are instructions:

    • Sit in a comfortable upright position with head relaxed upon the neck.

    The posture should be both attentive and relaxed. It is a noble posture, without forcing. You can rock back and forth, and side to side, to find your center balance where the body should rest upon itself without much strain.Feel your weight sinking into the floor as you relax.

    • Let the eyes relax, half-open. Gaze down softly at the floor in front of you.

    If you wish you can also face a wall and gaze softly in that direction. There is no need to either try to see or try to avoid anything; it may be helpful to have a plain surface in front of you that doesn't tend to grab your attention.

    • Take a few deep breaths, after which you should breathe naturally.

    The pace and depth of the breath may change, and that's fine; no need to control it, other than to relax and allow the breath to come. If at some point you feel the breath is unnaturally fast or shallow, you can invite it to deepen and slow; but in general don't bother trying to manipulate the breath in any way.

    • Inhale naturally, and count "one" (to yourself) on the exhalation.
    • Count "two" on the next exhalation, and so on up to "five."

    • After you reach "five", return to "one" and start the count again.

    • When your attention wanders, as it will almost certainly do, simply return immediately and effortlessly to "one". It is not failure to return to "one"; it is success, because you have realized your wandering and you have returned.

    • Sometimes you may lose count by going well past "five", or you may just not realize where you are in the count any more. Just as with any other distraction, as soon as you realize it return to "one" instantly and without a fuss. This may mean you return to "one" often, or even repeatedly. This is the correct form.

    (One teacher says that she knows when a new student is being dishonest about his/her meditation when they say they can reach "five" consistently without having practiced much. Either the meditator doesn't realize when his/her mind is wandering, or they give themselves permission to break the form.

    • If you surrender to the form it will allow you to be deeply relaxed and clear, because you won't be competing with anything and you won't be trying to decide what to do â€” you will only be following whatever happens according to the method.

    Try five minutes of this every day. If you want a morning routine, you can splash cold water on your face, do a couple of minutes of vigorous, rhythmic whole-body-and-breath exercise, and then sit for five minutes of breath counting. Use a watch or timer, and follow it.

    • At the end of the session, take a couple of deep breaths, rub your hands together vigorously to generate some heat, and cup your eyes (letting the darkness and warmth of your hands soothe them) and massage your neck and skull. This will help keep your energy flowing when you start doing longer sittings.

    • Feel whatever vibrancy and clarity have developed, and from this centered place wish well-being for all living things.
