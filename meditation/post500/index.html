    I agree; and I'm sorry this happened to you, and that it happens to a significant number of people.

    I'm sorry that in oversimplified teaching, the attempt to go beyond attachment to the body becomes, in some cases, disrespect for the body. It seems that certain puritanical views and cultural norms bleed in where they don't belong -- unconscious notions of the body being impure, inferior, an obstacle.

    If we get more political about it, we might identify an agenda of dominance of mind over body as an embrace of patriarchal, 'God The Father' spirituality that descends from the lofty heavens to bless us heathens; and rejection of earthy, embodied Feminine spirituality that nourishes from below. It is perhaps not a coincidence that these attitudes are more prevalent in very formal schools where authority comes down with an iron hand, and in male-dominated schools.

    We can also consider what gets 'lost in the translation' when Asian forms are introduced to Western audiences. For instance, a course of training may originate in a land where the general population is very used to sitting on the floor, with most people never losing the ability to do so after infancy. A teacher from such a culture may then think that someone complaining of pain is talking about mounting tension and soreness, or getting caught up in mental agitation that increases their suffering, rather than reporting the fact that their joints are separating and ligaments tearing.

    We can add to this dynamic our idolization of teachers rather than respect of them. In the West, we are conditioned to be agog at movie archetypes and quizzical about memes; we are not taught to respect and revere teachers and elders as humans -- as equals, but exceptionally devoted and accomplished equals.

    And furthermore, for all our flag-waving liberty and individualism, we don't generally have a strong sense of self and confidence in our own experience. We are disconnected in our society and families. We are fragmented within ourselves. We are ever more disembodied through our e-lives, i-selves, 'smart' worlds. Even if we could feel what's going on within us, there is not much precedence in our lives for standing up for it.

    So it's a perfect storm where we can be browbeaten into disregarding what our bodymind is saying, and taking a teacher's guidance (or rule) as the final word.

    And I realize that it's not cut-and-dried. In practice we often find that we can endure more than we had imagined possible, and that it's okay, and in fact it's liberating. And we perceive more closely the tidal movements of pain and pleasure, their coming and going; and we may be forgiven for holding out hope that "this too will pass". And sometimes we are so engaged in the practice -- wonderfully engaged -- that the gradual increase of pain can pale in comparison. We can be like the cold hiker who hunkers down and doesn't do anything to save himself, just getting colder and colder, until it's too late; or like the frog in the pot of water that heats up so gradually the frog never jumps out.

    I think we need to be intentional, conscious advocates of integrity, including the physical. We need to get out of our heads and into our bodies. We need to practice trusting our movement as much as we practice trusting our stillness -- so when it's time to do so, we can jump up and say "Sorry, the knees say no." (Or maybe we don't exactly jump up. Easy does it. ;))

    We need to take steps toward the feminine side of spirituality: the strong-yet-soft, the Unknowing, the nurturing. We need to know when the masculine side is getting out of hand: the hard-line, the Knowing, the pushing-through.

    If a teacher confronts you about attachment to the body when -- within the context of the teaching -- you reasonably respond to pain, I wonder where the practice that relieves suffering is supposed to start!? Do we refrain from saving the rainforest because it might represent an attachment?
