    Meditation for tolerance/patience?

    This seems like something you'd want to look into with a therapist or cousellor, since you recognize a growing anger in yourself. It's very wonderful and fortunate that you notice this. That means you can do something about it to return to balance. While meditation can definitely help develop patience, it seems that there is a pattern in your own thinking or feeling that is reacting to the sounds of the kids outside; and that's something that might be easily and usefully addressed with the right psychotherapy.

    There's nothing inherently annoying about the sounds of children playing. All sound is just sound. What makes it blissful or annoying is your own mind. When someone is in love, the whole world seems wonderful; when someone is bitter, even things that are usually delightful seem like problems. The world takes on the quality of your own mind.

    So your annoyance doesn't come from the sounds around you. Instead, you might want to look into what the annoyance is really about within you. May there be something about children or play or their sounds that trigger a reaction rooted in the past? This or any online forum is not the place for speculation about personal issues. Just rest assured that we all have our patterns â€” reactions that were put in place by fears and hurts and difficulties we encountered in the past.

    As to meditation, breath-counting meditation (as recommended in many online and offline resources) could help you disengage from thinking that goes around and around, getting more and more upset. Many other meditations could also be effective; I just recommend breath-counting because it provides a very clear and neutral object that you can return to (i.e., the counting), helping you practice letting go of upsetting thoughts.

    But I fear that you may make meditation into a battle with yourself. Since you have tried ignoring and drowning out the sounds that upset you, you may likewise try to escape or drown out the sounds of your mind. This is not correct relationship with your mind, and it is not correct meditation. Correct meditation means you keep your center without moving toward or away from anything. So if you are doing breathing meditation, only return to counting the breath; and don't try to force thinking to disappear or make sounds go away. And if you are so upset that meditation itself increases your annoyance, then you should do something else, like perhaps walk, run, swim, sing, etc. Moving to a quieter apartment is also an option.

    If you find a guided Upekkha meditation, that may also help. Upekkha means equanimity â€” balanced open-handedness with all things, whether they are pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral. But again, this is not something that you force on yourself or use to escape something in life. If you can have the right approach to it, Upekkha practice can help you. (Upekkha meditation is sometimes presented as the fourth part of a set of practices also including Maitri/Kindness, Karuna/Compassion, and Mudita/Joy.)
