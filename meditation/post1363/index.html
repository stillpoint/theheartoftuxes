    Meditation teachers

    How well do you see beyond your own view?

    How well can you move beyond your own like and dislike, and the ingrained patterns built on them?

    The teacher provides the awakened perspective, the perspective that you can't even conceive of much less move toward unerringly. It is very clear how we defeat ourselves, how we give up when the going gets tough, or retreat in fear, or deny what doesn't fit in our comfort zone. The teacher keeps us moving on course when we go off.

    It is much better, and for the sake of some parts of training necessary, to have face-to-face contact with a teacher. There are many things that can be checked and guided from a distance; but for the intimate meeting of minds there is no substitute for face-to-face meeting. So whether you live with a teacher or visit one occasionally and then go away to work on your homework, personal contact is important.

    In all of history, even extremely exceptional beings have required a teacher to draw out their light. One in some billions may self-awaken (Sri Ramana Maharshi comes to mind), but if you look at the context of the person's life and circumstances and motivation, you'll see that the constellation of causes for self-awakening are also very rare (only occuring once in several billion instances). Even among self-awakened beings, there is often contact with other masters to confirm and hone what has been realized. A much more common story-line is of an awakening experience that fades and fritters away because one lacks the foundation and guidance and correct practice to ground it and build on it.

    But all that doesn't mean the entire spiritual career is spend under the wing of a teacher. It may be quite viable and appropriate to spend much time seeking or practicing on one's own. Sometimes this amounts to developing enough suffering experience to take the plunge: when the pain of living gets big enough, clear enough, you become more certain of what you need to do to get free of it.

    In the old days in China, say, a meeting with a true teacher was extremely rare. Most people may never have met a master. Others would travel on arduous treks to meet a master once, have a face-to-face experience, and then go away to digest that experience in practice for maybe several years before visiting the master again.

    These days, it is much easier to meet all kinds of good teachers. Maybe because it is easier we think it is cheaper, less meaningful. We tend not to value what is easy; but we (in modern society) also tend not even to attempt what is difficult. Meeting a true teacher is every bit as valuable as it was in the golden age. It is wise to value that which is valuable.

    >You cannot observe what happens in my mind.

    Why make this assumption? This is a mistake. Already we can see what happens in your mind, from the topic you bring up, the way your write, the assumptions you make. We can see where there is energy and interest, and where the mind turns away from looking. With closer contact, a true teacher will see deeply into your mind even from the way you move and interact with people and objects. The seeing is not just from the eyes. A master is open to the moment, and digests the moment as his own mind. She or he is like a mirror, reflecting whatever arises in you. This is precious not only because it helps you to train properly, but because you get a living example of your true mind, your original awakeness. Immeasurably valuable.
