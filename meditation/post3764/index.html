    I was meditating tonight and just taught myself how to breath from my abdominal muscles because I never understood it before. MIND BLOWN!

    This is tricky territory, which is why it can be very beneficial to study closely with a teacher who can guide not only your method of connecting with breathing, but who can guide your relationship with breathing. Having a knowledgeable and responsible guide can save you from going down blind alleys or establishing bad habits that can mean, for some, years of wrong practice.

    The reason why breath can be so influential on practice is the same reason it can be so problematic: the breath is intimately connected to the emotions, the thinking, and the chemistry of the body and brain. So while learning to release stuck patterns that hinder breathing can liberate energy and bring meditation to new levels, once one gets a glimpse of how influential the breath can be there can be a great temptation to then manipulate the feeling, the thinking, and the physical/mental setting through breathing.

    In the extreme, this becomes total reliance on the breath to create certain states. In a book that made the rounds 30 years ago a Japanese Zen teacher, for example, recommended "bamboo breathing", where tension would be held in the belly and breath would be forced out in segments (like the segments of a bamboo). This would simulate near-death breathlessness, and a more intense (extreme?) concentration would supposedly result. This notion of tension and force has also been found in Hindu pranayama and some Taoist energy practices. The problem isn't that it doesn't have the concentrating effect advertised â€” it does. The problem is that it sets up the wrong relationship with oneself, the moment, and the direction of practice.

    Practices like these have a place in traditional systems, but in the context of the tradition: the course of training, a great deal of foundational work to develop correct view and ethical behavior and tempered ego, and the absolute responsiveness to a watchful teacher. "Forcing" techniques of breathing are only part of a bigger picture of surrender, which, with our modern western mindset, has close to a zero percent chance of happening if we build our own practice based on our own whims and opinions.

    Tools (like, in this example, breathing techniques) are useful. That is why they are tools. Tools are not beneficial in themselves, but are beneficial when used with correct intent and skill. Our inclination, our mind-habit, is to always seek more tools, more control. Just think back to when you were a toddler, how fascinating and necessary it seemed to you to push the elevator button, play with the TV remote, manipulate the doll or toy car, and so on. If anything, that impulse to control only gets more ingrained with our conditioning over time: we buy more gadgets, learn self-help technologies, do more to change our situation with our money.... But meditation goes in the other direction, or should. Correct meditation lets it all be. Everything is allowed to return to itself.

    Our controlling habit is what sets up our poor breathing. Below our usual awareness, we are trying to control our experience of the moment. We put up armor in our belly so that we can feel self-contained; we make our breathing shallow and rigid so that we can limit the merging of inner and outer. So some assistance in returning to natural breath and breaking down those controlling patterns can be very helpful indeed. But once we are there, we must let go and let breath do its own thing. We shouldn't ask our breathing to make the moment different than it is.

    So before gaining tools or developing technologies (including meditative ones), we must have a very clear direction. We must understand our correct relationship with the world, with the moment. Then the world can breathe through us, live through us.

    If we understand our correct direction, then even if we don't do traditional meditation we will be helping ourselves and all others. If we don't understand our correct direction, then everything we do has that unclarity, even if it takes the form of meditation.
