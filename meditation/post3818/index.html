    Full Lotus Danger?

    Pain is a sign that something is being strained. The question is how much. A little pain over a long period may indicate a situation that is gradually injurious. How do you know? I think there is no way to convey where the line is drawn. If someone has a way, I'd be very interested to know it! Rather, you have to learn about your own body. You are in a good position to make the determination, since you already study yoga. You know that some helpful postures involve pain; and you train in applying your awareness to the cause and effect of posture.

    I would just suggest that you don't make it into an intellectual exercise, which could block your intuition. Trust your own sense of it first; consult your teacher for feedback as you go along.

    With lotus — and hopefully your teacher has conveyed this — it's important for the top foot not to be arced upward, with the ankle bent inward. Usually effort has to be applied to keep the foot flexed strongly at a right angle to the shin, so the sole of the foot faces to the side rather than twisting up. This is a strong position of the ankle, protecting not only that joint but the knees as well.

    Interestingly, the strongly flexed foot is a feature in some martial arts. For example, much of Sun Mu Do's intense stretching and movement regimen is predicated on the flexed foot protecting the knee from damage.

    In these kinds of cases, going slow is valuable, to prevent lasting damage happening before you get the chance to understand the cause and effect relationship of various things you try. I've seen and heard of several people with knees blown out by cross-legged postures, or with otherwise compromised physical health resulting from long sittings in postures they weren't ready for. I myself have had joint injuries that lasted months and years, arising from sitting.

    I think that many teachers encourage a perspective that students aren't ready for. "Are you attached to your body?" They may give a nod to self-care by saying you need to know the difference between the pains of harmless and harmful situations, but their other speech, body language, and subtle cues may seem to look down on "too much" self-concern, and thus "too much" self-care. It is for the most part teaching offered from a sincere and helpful intent, but the teacher, having developed a degree of strength and ease in their own sitting, may be somewhat divorced from the vulnerability student's physical situation, or from the willingness of an eager beginner to sit through intense pain. Some teachers come from cultures where facility in cross-legged postures is developed at a very early age, and so really may have little personal experience of how vulnerable another's body may be. And then there are the (admittedly more rare) cases of a very strict, gung-ho system that tends to deny the body in order to advance quickly in spiritual attainment: these systems do have their place, but are not for everyone.

    This is an area where I would advise practitioners to trust themselves and follow their own sense of things first and foremost. (Actually, this is the case in general in practice; but our habit if replacing intuition with opinion is the pitfall, where one taking this advice may then discount the teacher's guidance unnecessarily.)

    The result of long-term injury incurred in practice is often enough resentment and alienation from the teacher or practice; and that's not what we want.
