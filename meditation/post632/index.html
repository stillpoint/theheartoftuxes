    All of what you say makes sense to me.

    I don't think anyone can or should tell you what to do. I just hope that you trust yourself and act out of deep self-trust. Sometimes it can feel like diving off a high cliff to trust your inner sense of things (whether you stay or go or find some other option); but there can also be the exhilaration of deciding to follow your inner truth as best you can, the happiness of being your own good friend.

    There is no perfect way to go, no right way that will avoid difficulty or make everything good happen. But nevertheless, regardless of difficulty, you can choose to trust yourself. You can choose the kind of difficulty you welcome — whether it is pointless or whether it has a clear direction to it.

    I just want to say that it can be easy to get into situations and and difficult to get out. There's a very limited amount of time and energy in life — sometimes far less than we realize — so be clear about where you devote that precious time and energy. I support you in that. If you are finally ready to move out of your own limiting attitudes and behaviors, what makes it worthwhile to then linger in someone else's limiting attitudes and behaviors? Maybe there's a reason. If so, what is it? What is the benefit to you and him?

    But there's no good reason to abandon clarity or your highest priority just to avoid rocking the boat. It's possible to act out of love for everyone involved; and that doesn't mean avoiding decisions. Acting out of love for all parties can give you the strength to set clear boundaries and get busy following your path. It doesn't mean you're abandoning the other person. It doesn't mean you think you're better. It means you have no time to waste not being yourself.

    Seeing the eagle's-view of life, we realize that death will inevitably come, that we don't know when it will come, and that there are a limited number of activities and relationships we can be involved in. Devoting time and energy to one thing makes that time and energy unavailable for another. Sometimes your efforts can embrace more than one activity or relationship (for example, when you have a similar life direction as someone else); sometimes not. With that view, what is important to you?

    In all these kinds of situations, communication is key. Don't be afraid to say what you mean, and also keep your partner's best interest at heart when you speak and when you listen. If you can agree, fine. If you can't agree, then at least agree to disagree and support each other in your individual choices.

    If you need structures for communication, I recommend Non-Violent Communication. Another link. A third link.

    Thich Nhat Hanh's Four (or Five) Loving Mantras can help you start loving conversations. Another link.
