    If the aim is to make it easier for yourself, that's a good option.

    If the aim is to become free of preference, mental patterns, and attachment to external situations, it makes more sense to practice unconditionally: not having to change the external situation in order to feel better. Instead, one puts the onus on one's practice: rather than blaming the timer for the agitation, one realizes that the agitation comes from thinking, and that therefore it is important not to give in to wandering mind.

    If you quit whenever you feel like it, your whim and preference are leading you rather than your independent mind. Continuing in this way, whim and preference are indulged and strengthened. On the surface, this indulgence feels good; but it doesn't feel good in life when one is always controlled by one's whim and preference, never free to be independently happy.

    So one alternative to having no timer is to keep returning to the method moment to moment instead of speculating about time. Don't blame the timer, blame the distracted mind. Speculating and fretting about time are signs that you're not meditating. Only changing the external situation allows your attachment to externals to keep controlling you.

    That is why we have form: to become free. People tend to see form as an imposition, so they compromise and leave themselves 'outs', to avoid completely surrendering and committing. Therefore the avoiding and manipulating habits continue. But when form is very clear, one is left only with the option of finding freedom within form. Tinkering with the method or duration are typical ways of avoiding clear form.

    Freedom doesn't mean getting everything your way; it means you can have contentment and presence regardless of what is happening in the outside situation. The discomfort and difficulty in the last few minutes of sitting are very precious; they give you the opportunity to assert your freedom, your unconditional mind.

    But if freedom isn't the aim, certainly a looser form is possible.

    For those looking for alternate methods of keeping time, here are a couple of ways:

    • An incense stick burns for a fairly set duration. Small Japanese sticks typically burn for 30 - 40 minutes depending on the incense and the conditions (like whether it's burned vertically or horizontally). Large Tibetan sticks may last for an hour or longer.

    You can calibrate the duration and trim incense sticks to suit your desired time of practice. You become aware of the end of the season when the aroma becomes less intense; and if you burn incense in a bowl of ash there is a distinct avoid smell for a few seconds when the stick is extinguished by the ash.

    • You can use a mala (string of meditation beads) to count repetitions rather than go by clock time. Figure out how many breaths or mantra repetitions you want to complete per session, or how many will take the amount of time you want to sit for, then use the mala to count and alert you to the end of practice (e.g., after one complete string has been completed, or ten, or whatever number).

    • Although it's not ideal, some might try to wean themselves of worrying about time by keeping a watch dial in sight. After checking, practice letting go and returning to the method. Gradually develop the courage and concentration to not check at all. Pure practice means not checking anything, from moment to moment.

    The Enso Pearl meditation timer has an option to appear only as a circle that gradually fills in for the duration of the sitting.

    That Paul Simon song "Slip-Sliding Away" is so apt: "the nearer your destination, the more you're slip-sliding away". The very point at which our practice could be transformative is often the exact point where we fall off, avoiding the transformative effect. Just at the point where surrender would bring us new freedom, we often give up and settle for our old pattern of control and avoidance.
