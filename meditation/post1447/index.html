    How could I “watch” my drowsiness without falling asleep?

    The questions were rhetorical, for your benefit.

    Vipassana/Shamatha practice is wonderful. It is often presented as eyes-closed meditation, so no problem. In this case, motivation, setting, and posture will help you stem drowsiness as I set out in my comment.

    You have a good motivation for practice. It is categorized as low-level beneficial motivation, the aim to gain benefit for your life. If you keep connecting to this motivation it will help. Medium-level beneficial motivation is to gain complete awakening. High-level motivation is to gain complete awakening for all beings; i.e., to bring all beings to full enlightenment.

    Death contemplation and meditation are indeed very compatible with Vipassana. It is one of the forty subjects of meditation recommended in the old Theravada scriptures (in three variations, actually — the inevitability of death, death as murderer always stalking us, and death as the ruin of any success we gain in life). The Visuddhi Magga offers eight different ways of being mindful of death. Monks were instructed to make their robes from the clothes of deceased people, and to spend time meditating in burial grounds and crematoria.

    The simplest form coming from Theravada sources is to simply recollect death, realizing that it will come. This practice is sometimes reduced to considering the reality of death while repeating the word "Death... Death... Death...." The practice can be too dry and jarring for some people.

    Vajrayana Buddhism offers a very clear and systematic analytical death meditation, along with other practices that vary in subtlety and complexity. Vajrayana also embraces Vipassana/Shamatha as a foundational practice, and the death meditations are also very compatible.

    In the Nine-Point Meditation on Death the practitioner considers these points in order:

    First root: Contemplation that death is definite.

    1. because death will definitely come and therefore cannot be avoided
    2. because our lifespan cannot be extended and diminishes unceasingly
    3. because even when we are alive there is little time to practice

    First decision: I must practice.

    .

    Second root: Contemplation that the time of death is indefinite.

    1. because our lifespan in this world is indefinite
    2. because the causes of death are very many and the causes of life are few
    3. because the time of death is uncertain due to the fragility of the body

    Second decision: I must practice now.

    .

    Third root: Contemplation that at the time of death nothing helps except practice.

    1. because at the time of death our friends are of no help
    2. because at the time of death our wealth is of no help
    3. because at the time of death our body is of no help

    Third decision: I must practice non-attachment to any of the wonderful things of this life.

    ~

    One of the benefits of the Nine-Point Meditation is its clear formula. It can be useful to 'touch all the bases', so to speak. But one has to fill in the formal structure with one's own understanding and willingness; one has to breathe life into the practice, letting it hit home.

    A practice that tends to hit home with more immediacy is to actually visualize the process of dying in great detail, including the realities after death (burial, unresponsiveness, decay). Vajrayana provides much supporting knowledge for this practice in terms of the dissolution of the various elements of the bodymind, and their disassociation from the essential mindstream. One can actually rehearse death, not only deeply realizing its reality and thus prioritizing one's motivations, but also preparing to enter death with the consciousness and equanimity that result in a good rebirth or even enlightenment.

    Here is an excellent video introduction to this meditation on death. This is the practice I would recommend above the others mentioned here.

    Some variations continue the process and include awareness of the bardos or intermediate states after death, to allow one to gain eventual control over the entire process of death and rebirth. The Tibetan Book Of The Dead (Bardo Thodol) is all about this knowledge and practice.

    Yet another death practice is Phowa (transference of consciousness), the yogic preparation of the bodymind to make best use of the opportunities available at and after physical death. It is said that rebirth is determined in relation to the orifice through which the consciousness leaves the body; exiting through lower gateways results in animal or hell rebirths, exiting through higher gateways results in human or divine rebirths. So in Phowa one learns to manage the gateways and subtle energies, and direct the consciousness to leave through the gateway at the top of the skull. This practice should be done under the guidance of a qualified teacher. At the time of death the process can be facilitated for you by some masters.

    The Tibetan Book Of Living And Dying, by Sogyal Rinpoche (not the same as the Tibetan Book Of The Dead) gives many great teachings about dying, death, and supporting those who are dying. It includes some basic instructions for both the dying visualization mentioned above and Phowa (in a preliminary way that can be practiced without harm, according to the author).
