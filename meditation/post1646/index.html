    It is very much okay. You don't have to micro-manage your mind, so to speak.

    There are various approaches. In some formal and monastic courses of training, specific instructions will be given for a great many life situations. There is the tradition of the gatha or poetic verses about practice that apply to all kinds of situations, from how to start the day, to how to cook and eat food, to how to walk, and so on. That is a very meticulous approach, but when you do the practice you find the broad, overarching heart and mind that embrace all of these details.

    Other approaches — and I think, more appropriate for our modern everyday life — have a less formal approach. Mindfulness really means non-distraction. You can, and should, relax and go about your day naturally. Just avoid getting caught up by things.

    Our original mind is already bright, open awareness. That doesn't have to be made up. No struggle is necessary to create awareness. But we do make concerted efforts to follow objects and trains of thought, and in the process we give up on, or trade in, our original awareness. Because this object attachment is so usual and familiar we think of it as our basic state of being — we think distraction is natural and awareness has to be found through struggle. But it's the other way around. If you are relaxed, if you let go of clinging to things, your natural state is already aware.

    So one of the primary instructions offered in Dzogchen Buddhism, for instance, is to remain relaxed about whatever is going on. Don't grab so much, don't resist so much. The state that remains is already awareness. If this makes sense to you, you can try it.

    Don't exhaust yourself. If there's a sense of you over here and your situation or practice goal over there, that's the separation that leads to effortful making/grasping/avoiding and ultimately exhaustion. In any situation, you are not separate from the situation, and the situation is no different from your practice. You don't have to put a practice on top of the situation.

    Okay? You don't have to put a practice on top of the situation. I mean, some training may do that; it may create a special activity in order to bring about something special. But essentially it's not necessary. Whatever you do, you just do. Stay relaxed about it and just avoid getting taken away by trains of thought.

    So the famous Zen saying "chop wood, carry water" is not poetic or symbolic. It doesn't have a secret meaning. It doesn't mean there are things like chopping wood that should be done and other things not mentioned that shouldn't be done. It's like saying "brush your teeth, ride the bus" or "at work time, work; and at eating time, eat". No need to make special ideas about anything. Don't 'make special'. Only do it.
