    I prefer to meditate with my eyes open. When I meditate, my eyes start to drift one way and my vision gets blurry and darker unless I blink. Why is that?

    I fully agree with the importance of realizing Sunyata — what you have called the Illuminating Void. And it seems that you agree that one must also carry that realizing-of-Void back into daily life activities and relationships. This does not have to be an all-or-nothing proposition. One part does not have to be cultivated to the exclusion of the other, only at different times. Correct (or shall we say 'complete') meditation already embraces both sides.

    Your take on the necessity of closed-eyes meditation is not commonly shared with many meditation traditions, though. As kafoo said, ten thousand zen temples disagree. In my experience, all of the many, many zen masters and teachers I've encountered have taught open-eyes practice. (So it is ironic that you invoke Zen Master Huai Jang.) The other Mahayana teachers I've met have taught to practice with open eyes, as have most of the Theravada teachers. Some Theravadins will indeed practice with closed eyes, but most of those will teach that keeping the eyes open or closed is a matter of personal preference.

    One should keep in mind that the teacher you are quoting on the topic of The Illuminating Void, Samael Aun Weor, was teaching based on his own personal, unverified experience and his own personal ideology, unconnected to a wisdom tradition (though drawing on many). Clearly he experienced something profound, but one should take into account that his teaching was at once very individual, eclectic, and arcane — and offered out of context from the several traditions he did apparently contact. In addition, there is some controversy over his legitimacy.

    Individuality, eclecticism, or controversy are not in themselves necessarily signs of, say, a cult figure. Many legitimate teachers are couched in these, for better or worse. I'm just saying that one should note where the teaching one's relying on is coming from. Just because S A Weor strongly felt something to be true in his experience doesn't mean that the many thousands who say otherwise can be discounted.

    Maybe we should take a cue, as you wrote, from Zen Master Huai Jang, and carry on with our own illuminations: "Whatever is said misses the main point."
