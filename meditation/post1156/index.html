    You can't always tell. A lot of people — actually many, many meditators — who do dull, sleepy practice have little or no idea that's the case. They think of it as good practice because their mind is relatively sedate or they don't notice their thoughts, and because it feels comfortable. They may even feel better after a sitting. It's because they were practically napping.

    Two things come to mind that help you identify whether you're falling asleep. The first is a keen-eyed teacher. With a teacher you have the clear perspective, the awakened perspective, which you are not yet capable of providing. It also means that the mind that's falling asleep is not in charge of assessing the situation (sort of like a corrupt police force policing itself).

    The second thing that helps is to have had the experience of bright, clear practice, and to know it well. Unless one has had very fortunate and fortuitous unfolding in one's study, this also means a teacher has been there to teach the right method and attitude, the supporting factors, and the difference between right practice and lax practice.

    Certainly if you catch yourself nodding, that means you've been alseep. If you drift in and out of awareness, or if you jolt back to awareness, it means you've been sleeping. If the form of practice continues vaguely in the background but otherwise the mind is sort of soft and foggy, it indicates a dull or sleepy practice.

    One question I would ask is what changed about a month or so ago. What's different in your life or in your practice? That might point to a causal connection. (Or it might not.)

    But your job during meditation isn't to check your condition. So you shouldn't be stepping outside of your practice to judge and label your state of mind. First of all, you are not in the best position to know. Secondly, it breaks practice. Your job is to only do it, only sincerely express the practice, moment to moment.

    I've written previously on flagging practice and motivation, as well as factors contributing to sleepiness. You can do a subreddit search or search my previous posts.

    In terms of 'losing time': it can happen through sleep or it can happen through concentration. As concentration deepens, outer stimuli including the sense of separate outer world and separate time abate. This is a common experience, especially for people involved in concentration-style (rather than awareness-style) practice. Sometimes it can be a combination of concentration and sleepiness: the mind has calmed down through concentration, so it doesn't jump around between objects, but the quality of awareness is dull.

    Meditations with a clear object (like, say, breath counting) make it easier to identify sleepiness or dullness. In counting meditation, for instance, the count should progress and return in the right order. If you count breaths to ten, you should return to 'one' once you reach 'ten'. Going beyond 'ten' or losing count indicates distraction. But each individual number should also be clear and bright in the awareness. Losing the clarity indicates dullness. The counting aspect represents compassion; the clarity aspect represents wisdom.

    If the concentration is very clear and bright, one can enter samadhi, which is a radically different state, a state of non-separation. There is no longer separation between subject (meditator) and object (counting the breath, or whatever the object of meditation). Since the subject-object dichotomy no longer exists, there is no longer effortful practice, no longer any attempt to bring oneself back to the moment and the object.

    In samadhi, the sense of time can be very different. Time comes from thinking; so when thinking is gone, time is gone. Minutes or hours can seem like no time at all. Very profound samadhi has been demonstrated by yogis who remain in the state for years.

    But if the cause for losing time is samadhi, it would come from completely bright, clear, momentary, effortless presence.
