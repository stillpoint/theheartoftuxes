    Question from a beginner. Can I meditate while listening to music?

    You can listen to music for relaxation, enjoyment, or diversion, but that's listening to music, not meditation.

    We have problems with our own minds. Our thinking kidnaps us and tells us a particular version of the world, and we believe it. Usually we only look to avoid this when it becomes uncomfortable -- when we can't stand what's going on in our minds. So we look for external situations to take our minds away.

    We watch TV, go to movies, take in sports events... we surf the internet or look for sex, or both... we fill the void or distract ourselves with food... we veg out or sleep... and most of all, we make more thinking and take in more thinking and talk about and fight about more thinking -- all to avoid our minds.

    These external situations can indeed take away the thinking mind, but it is only temporary. At the end of the TV show we are again faced with the uncomfortable thinking mind, which we again habitually avoid. Maybe we stick with the next show on TV, and the next, until far later than we had intended; or maybe we go to music or chatting or sleeping to try to escape the mind. There is no peace to be had from external objects and situations.

    Furthermore, the more we engage in a strategy of escape, the weaker we become. We depend more and more on external things to take our mind away, losing our innate ability to focus and to experience contentment with what is. Our avoiding habit deepens, and the backlog of mental junk -- the stuff we've been consuming in order to avoid what's there -- piles up.

    Meditation turns around in the other direction. Rather than avoiding or trying to destroy the mind, we face it directly, confidently. We exercise our natural, innate contentment, and regain the ability to apply it across a wide range of situations. Whatever arises in the mind, we don't let it move us. We don't grasp and we don't run away.

    This is a very noble posture of body and mind. It is deeply human. And it takes courage. Being present with the mind is truly a warrior's path: the activity of a spiritual warrior.

    As you practice, your ability to stay present grows. Thus contentment grows. The mind is not moved as much by circumstances, because that is exactly what you practice -- not-moving mind.

    But you have to understand these points:

    • Meditation has to be correct. Grasping at mind states or results cannot train the non-grasping skill. So you need to learn a proper, proven method, and you need to follow the method meticulously, and you need to follow it with a correct open-handed attitude.

    • Meditation has to be practiced consistently. It's all cause and effect. For 99% of your life you let your thinking drag you around, controlling the way you perceive and the way you act. How will you counteract those causes, built up minute by minute, year by year? The effort has to be similarly consistent.

    • You are the worst judge of your own progress, because you are still looking through the filter of your ingrained thoughts and habits, still controlled by what you like and don't like. If you give you because 'it didn't work', that's just giving in to another of the many trains of thought controlling you. (Just like the trains of thought that say this moment is unbearable on its own, without the addition of music or entertainment.)

    It is all just thought, just illusion, that's controlling your behavior. But you can decide strongly to sit still and assert your freedom. Nothing need have the power to turn you away from open presence and contentment.

    • If you find that even after making the most courageous and consistent (not necessarily heroic or overblown) effort you still can't seem to develop stillness and concentration, you may need external structures as support.

    Before, you used external situations to affect your mind in unconscious and habitual ways. Now you can use external situations to bring you back to centeredness, calmness, self-possession, health, wholeness, contentment.

    The most important of these supports is regular contact with a capable teacher or other wise and centered guide. Others include practice with as meditation group or community, lifestyle changes (balanced diet, exercise, and sleep), ethical and generous behavior, and changes to your environment and acquaintances that help you return to your true, noble being and centered mind.
