    Double Shirt?

    Double v-neck or other t-shirts are, in my opinion, fine if intention is there. That will usually mean that the shirts — essentially layers in a layered outfit — will be of a different color and/or pattern, and that the difference in their cut or size will allow at least the borders of the one underneath to show. This can be done with t-shirts, with t and polo, with t and s/s button-up, with t and henley; and if you're quite young it can be done with t-shirt on top of collared shirts. (In this example, the contrast between the two shirts is too great, the top layer is too tight, and the collared shirt should be undone one more button down; but the principle is there.) Henley as under- or over- layer is almost as versatile as a t-shirt.

    T-shirt under long-sleeve shirt (and especially graphic tees) is by now quite cliché and should only be done if you really mean it. In addition, make sure that any t-shirt or undershirt don't show through a long-sleeved top layer.

    And we all know the difference between t-shirts and undershirts don't we? A white t-shirt might be okay as an undershirt; but an undershirt does not fit the role of a t-shirt. A t-shirt will be of thicker/beefier fabric, and the bottom hem will usually be higher, for wearing untucked.

    Layering has become the standard of casual style in the past couple of decades. Shirts are even being sewn with false layers these days. It doesn't mean you have to be 'that guy' with the seven popped-collar polos. (In fact, don't pop your collar unless there is an eminently practical reason for it, such as needing to protect your neck from strong wind or sun while sailing.) Rules are there to point to what's appropriate in any given situation; follow the situation, check the mirror, and decide for yourself if it works for you.
