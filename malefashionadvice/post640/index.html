    Henley/vest/blazer.. thoughts?

    If it's not the waistcoat, it's the fedora. These seem to be the two go-two pieces used by guys looking to "switch it up", but who don't have a solid grounding in how to put together an outfit.

    It is, I suppose, our modern-day materialistic curse that leads us to place our hopes in objects — and in this case a single stylistic object — rather than building skills and sensibilities and character that, over time, unfold into a truly integral expression of who we are.

    As vqshjfkjxfh writes, there is a mish-mosh of the levels of formality in your outfit.

    But that's not the half of it:

    • Is that a blazer or a black suit jacket? In any case, a black jacket doesn't go with jeans (outside of Nashville, anyway); and a suit jacket only goes with the rest of the suit.

    • That much black in a sharp-casual context is usually quite boring and unsophisticated. There's no color, no happiness, no expression of rhythm or sensuality; there's just darkness. And then the grey layer becomes too obvious and separate from the rest. Black is difficult to match, for several reasons. You'll have much better luck by starting off with a true neutral as the basis for your outfits.

    • The black jacket just makes everything else unworkable; the black shoes are not far behind. Some will accept black oxfords with jeans, but to my eye it's a very cloying pairing unless the rest of the outfit sets the stage for them (this outfit doesn't).

    • Henley with vest is just a "no"; henley with sportcoat requires sensitivity to the overall context; henley with both is... I don't even want to say.

    • If you soften your vision and look at the outfit as a whole, you will see the elements that stick out: hands and head, vest, and belt buckle. It's too disjointed visually. For example, the belt buckle is too eye-catching within the dark fields, and that only highlights it as a piece that doesn't belong with jacket and waistcoat; it exacerbates the cheesiness that comes from different levels of formality being forced together.

    The statement that I get from this outfit is one of confusion. It's as if you've tried — valiantly and courageously, no doubt — to bring in more "style" without understanding the actual style moving through you and your situation. That is why I say that you should go back to square one and start properly; because if you start properly then everything else will fall into place naturally; but if you start wrong, then everything that follows will have a wrong basis.

    Starting properly means understanding your own situation — your personal character and qualities, your aspirations (what you want to become, and what parts of yourself you want to encourage and express more), your actual lifestyle and activities, and your form (body shape, skin color, proportions, etc.). When you understand your own true situation, then you won't have to look for the magic item to bestow "style" upon you, your style will grow organically from what you are, and will make sense to those who see you.

    In addition to understanding your own style, you need to learn more about putting together an outfit. Fit, color, contrast, pattern, matching style, and matching levels of formality are all areas of study. Read the sidebar guides, especially the Getting Started section. My recommendations are offered in a more orderly manner in this post from an earlier thread.

    If we disregard the expletives that ran through my mind when I first saw your posted pic, let's just say you should start from scratch and never again think of wearing a waistcoat without a suit.
