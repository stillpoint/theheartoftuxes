    Collar too overwhelming?

    I hope you take this post in the spirit in which it's intended, not as criticism but as critique for the sake of improvement. There are a lot of learning points here; I hope it helps.

    Color & Shade:

    Red shirts are problematic from the get-go. This one has the dual difficulty of intensity and darkness. Unless you have something to tone it down with, like large fields of neutral solids elsewhere in your outfit, the red tends to be too vibrant. And dark shirts often look cheap or cheesy unless they are (A) of a luxurious fabric and design and (B) part of an over-all dark evening look. Dark red is not a good color next to the face for most complexions.

    This red shirt is only intensified by combining it with the complementary (opposite) color blue in the sweater. And then the red is reinforced with a red tie. All in all, color-wise this is too strong.

    Style:

    There is also a mixing of styles here that doesn't work for you. The high-collar red pinstripe shirt has a conservative, vintage or old feel to it. The flower pattern of the tie, while not lavish in its presentation, is inherently more flamboyant. While the shapes of the patterns are not bad together, their symbolism is in opposition: the thin stripes speak of careful business-related precision, the flowers speak of charming artistry. Add the safe, casual sweater, and there are too many mixed messages.

    Shape & Proportion:

    A high, tight collar shape like that is usually best for balancing a short, thick neck and/or wide face and frame. On a longer neck like yours the length is over-accentuated.

    The point collar shape, with narrow-angled downward-oriented points and a narrow notch below the chin, does not accept a large and wide tie knot. If the over-all look is slick and thin, a four-in-hand may do. But the collar is quite high, so while I do prefer an asymmetrical knot I would think that the four-in-hand would only accentuate the lengthening and thinning effect. A smaller wide-angled knot like the Pratt or Nicky may provide more balance.

    Fit:

    Another clash appears in the inconsitency of fit. The high tight collar provides a very controlled, almost suppressed energy. This can be used consciously in, say, professional outfits that express control and dominance, or in chic evening outfits that play between control and display (with, for example, certain elements in a controlled monochrome and close fit, and other elements providing flow, splashes of color, or showy patterns). But in this outfit, the uptight nature of the collar is rendered completely out of place by the loose-fitting sweater and pants. (You may in any case want to take in the fit of your sweater and pants.)

    Overview:

    Style moves from the inside, out. You shouldn't try to shoehorn a piece into your wardrobe just because it fell into your hands. You will look much better with a bare minimum of pieces that fit you well, enhance your coloration, and most importantly suit your personal style and lifestyle, than with many pieces that don't suit you or that create confusion in your look.

    Start where you are, looking into your lifestyle needs, your personality, and what looks good on you; and then gradually add carefully selected items to support you. Your clothes should serve you, not the other way around.
