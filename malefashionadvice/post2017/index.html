    I've read the sidebar. I've read the links. I've read frugalmalefashion. My internship starts next week, and I need to dazzle. Can you help answer some of my specific questions?

    Grey suit, no pinstripes. Pinstripes are too identifiable and are an assertion of power that may be taken the wrong way; and in any case the stripe pattern will soon be emblazoned in people's memory if you wear it every day, or even every other day. A solid (or very subtle pattern of thread color variation) is more versatile in both matching and range of formality. A navy suit would not be terribly wrong, but not as workable as the centerpiece of many different outfits (i.e., not quite as easy to match with many different shirt and tie colors).

    You would be wildly lucky to get a suit that fits perfectly off the rack. Always budget for a tailor. And in fact, buy from a store with a return policy and bring the suit to an independent tailor so he can give his opinion on how well the suit can be altered. That will give you the best chance of getting a fantastic fit.

    Never mind fancy socks or pocket squares. Focus on fit, and on proper matching. When you can consistently put together outfits that have integrity — with all the pieces sending the same message, harmonizing, and making sense according to the situation — then other flairs are open to you. But even then, being an intern in situations that shift between business casual and professional doesn't sound like the appropriate place for dandyism: it's too much "hey look at me" for the role of the intern; it's too dressy for business casual; and it's too expressive for professional.

    Wingtips may also verge on too fancy for some business settings. You may want to get some opinions from people you'll be working with. If they do go over, let the rest of your outfit be appropriately... reticent. The business setting is seldom the place to show off your unique style, especially for an intern. When you run the company, then you can wear all the clashing socks and bright pocket squares you want.

    Brown is a more elegant and versatile choice for shoe color, and generally the first choice, with a couple of exceptions: As the shade of grey in the suit approaches dark charcoal and black, one starts to lean more toward black shoes. And some (American) conservative traditionalists will insist that black is the color for business, as it is more dispassionate and serious.

    Another option you may want to research is an outfit of separate dress pants and blazer. Some dress pants also do well in sharp casual outfits; you'd just need to find out how formal the 'professional' phase of your work has to be. Usually, 'professional' means a full suit.
