    MFA what do you think about these as multi-situation shoes

    We definitely need a lot more information about the context before we can give focused advice: Which multiple situations? What is your personal style? What will you want to wear with the shoes? Where do you live? What is your age; and who will you be hanging out with?

    Next, check the footwear guides in the sidebar for lots of good advice.

    I actually like the profile of this shoe and the crossing swaths of leather; but there are some big problems with it. First and foremost, a velcro closure is never an elegant choice; the two main associations with velcro closures are of toddlers who haven't learned how to tie their shoes and old people who now have difficulty with it. It's also by nature an overly synthetic and technical feature in an article of clothing from which one hopes to derive as much character as possible.

    The velcro closure also has a problem in its visual aesthetics: it cannot lie flat -- there will always be a gap -- and the stitching used to hold it in place complicates the strap. Aurally as well, the ripping sound of velcro doesn't signify 'class'.

    The synthetic and (at the heel) slightly techno-styled sole adds to the man-made, factory-made feel of the shoe and just magnifies the velcro issues. Again, just in terms of silhouette, I do like the sporty profile; but the welt of the sole also juts out around the toe and disrupts that a bit.

    The overall genre of this shoe is sporty. It heads in the direction of clean, slick Euro outfits (tight, unadorned fits; high-sheen, fine fabrics; sport-based sharp pieces like track jackets and moto jackets or techno pieces with metallics, zippers, and epaulets); so even if the features were done better and it had less of a synthetic feel, it would still be limited in its range -- definitely not a multi-situation shoe.

    • If you want something that can function as a dress shoe with a suit, but also be dressed down with sharp jeans or chinos, the monkstrap shoe can be good, although it is a bit heavy on the style -- in cosmopolitan areas, it's a stylish choice, but in some circles the buckle may be considered fey or even feminine. The rest of your outfit, and maybe your attitude as well, may need to play up to a certain level.

    • Slightly less slick and more comfy, but also able to work in that sharp-casual to dressy range, is the brogue or wingtip. The amount of detail in the decoration may take some getting used to. You can also seek simpler brogue designs that will take you a bit further into the dressy-formal end; not as formal as plain balmorals or cap-toes, but more versatile.

    • If you don't need anything more formal than for use with a sport jacket (i.e., no suit involved), suede oxfords and bucks are versatile, and have a lot of character. They won't stand up to weather quite as well, but are still surprisingly durable.

    • Perhaps the most versatile piece of footwear across both casual and formal ranges, and one I'd recommend for you (knowing nothing else about your situation or needs) is the dress chukka boot. These come in many different designs, in both full grain leather and suede. The full leather will allow for use on more dressy occasions, but can also go deep into the casual side of things.

    Look for leather soles, if you can. If that's not within your price range, then seek the least conspicuous, dark sole you can find. Choose a low profile rather than a high sole or high chunky shoe; the low profile will work better in dressy outfits, and while some chunky designs are good for casual wear others are simply bad on any occasion. Avoid square toes. There is a difference of opinion as to the acceptability of stitched toes (the two lines of bicycle stitch, or the moccasin stitch that parallels the toe shape, for example); so best avoid stitched toes other than cap toes until you know what you're doing. Avoid hybrid styles that mix dress shoe and sport shoe. The newbie or amateur is usually inclined to buy a hybrid in attempt to 'cover all the bases', but it really looks quite bad, and will not perform either role well; and anyway, good dress shoes should be comfortable enough on your feet for extended wear. A brown leather shoe will be far more versatile than black in terms of matching colors, and is almost always the more stylish option because there's more character in the color. Finally, in shoes you most frequently get what you pay for.
