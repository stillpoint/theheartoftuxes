Another guide might suggest pulling the pants up over a straightened shirt rather than pushing the tails down into a loosened waistband. Also, the picture on the left is as worthwhile as the picture on the right, depending on the situation.

    I do, however, take issue with any of these guides that suggest folding-tucking methods as ways to draw up loose fabric. That idea is a vestige of a previous millennium. 'Classic' cut shirts are still sold, which have more loose fabric around the torso; but if you're into that look you should be happy with the billow it creates.

    If, on the other hand, you opt for the contemporary cut which is closer to the torso and emphasizes the shape, physicality, and fitness of the underlying body, then for Pete's sake buy your shirts in the right size and have them tailored to fit.

    In neither case is it necessary to try to hide extra fabric by folding. All that does is flatten some areas and create more radical wrinkles and bulges in others.

    The wrist-length rule for hems of shirts that are appropriate for untucking should be taken with a grain of salt. (Sometimes we say halfway down the zipper; sometimes we say between halfway and all the way down to the crotch of the pants.) Firstly, arm length can vary no matter how tall you are. Secondly, your height and the proportions of your upper and lower body need to be considered. Thirdly, it's disingenuous to suggest that only the "indie rock star" (no capitalization necessary) look will accept untucking; untucked shirts are seen in far wider public use, and there is some acceptable variation in length of exposed shirt tails. Fourthly, the allowed length of exposed tails varies according to the situation.

    Context is important.
