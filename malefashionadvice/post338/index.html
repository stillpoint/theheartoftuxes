    Redditors of MFA, how did you find your tailors? More specifically, those who are more experienced in menswear, as opposed to dresses and female-wear?

    I looked in the Yellow Pages, Googled "tailors" in my city, asked on online forums (you can ask here specifically, with Huntsville AL in your thread title), and went by word-of-mouth. I tried out several tailors and a few seamstresses, over the course of a decade, in addition to the alterationists employed at local cleaners that can be used for simple tasks like hemming pants.

    Over the course of my search, I encountered some bad, bad tailors as well as some good tailors who seemed to have lost their concentration or will to take appropriate care. I've had several disasters along the way. One place had to replace a no-longer-available $300 shirt with a duplicate I was lucky to find on eBay. Another terrible tailor ruined three shirts in the process of doing the (seemingly) most simple alterations.

    You learn; you move on.

    Right now, I'm pretty happy with who I work with. I use a local expert seamstress (who does work primarily with women's clothes) to take in the waist on pants and to work with some casual knits like sweaters, hoodies, and t-shirts. Until I found tailors I was happy with, I also brought casual shirts to her to have darts installed; but looking at those now, I find that they are satisfactory but not perfect.

    I have to travel a ways to get to the tailor I use most often. I bring him a lot of work, usually several pieces at a time. He is very knowledgeable and skilled; but two things that set him apart are his willingness to listen to what I want, and his willingness to keep trying until we get things right. There have been times when I've brought pieces back twice because they're not quite perfect, and he always cheerfully says "you're the boss; I want to do it right for you". That kind of attitude is rare. He's a keeper.

    There's another tailor that I use for most of my dress shirts. He does them in Italian style, the way I like it, very close to the body. And he always gets it right the first time; I've never yet had to bring a shirt back to him for adjustment.

    It has been a long process of trying and learning before I found the craftspeople I feel I can trust. Part of that process is learning what to ask for and how to ask — I myself have to be willing to communicate what I want, to ask for items to be re-pinned if they don't feel quite right, to ask to be educated about why certain alterations don't turn out the way I envisioned, and to also invite the tailor's view on how things look.

    Another important point: I do pay for their skill. If I want to pay a low price for alteration, I can take a garment to the local cleaners, but there is less skill, less consistency, and quite a bit more potential for hassles. At this point, I'm willing to pay more to get it right. It may take longer to get my whole wardrobe in perfect shape; but I experience fewer frustrations in the process and end up knowing that, whatever I wear, it's going to fit perfectly.
