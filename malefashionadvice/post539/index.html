    What can I change?

    You know what? You look 100% better with the suit jacket, light shirt, and different-shade tie. (Just use a knot that fills the space between collar points, and tighten it to avoid exposing the collar behind the knot.) A grey suit will accept a black shirt; but don't wear a tie. As to whether your shirt will look good with the suit, we should see photos.

    Compare the above outfit to this black-on-black one. Here, George Clooney is using the sheen of shirt and suit, and the subtle pinstripe of the suit, to provide interest. But it still fails. It's boring, (literally) monotonous, and it's all too dark for him. He looks like an old man here, whereas the grey with black linked above provides at least some interplay of tones, and hilights his elegance. This black-on-black is awkward, even on George; it only barely works because he's very handsome and confident, and it will look even worse on most others. Besides, it's unimaginative and clichéd. Seeing these two outfits side by side, you can practice seeing the whole of the outfit, which includes the person. Notice the energy of the whole look, with the clothes merely part of the entirety.

    Aubergine is the french word for eggplant; it's a dark purple with a hint of brown in it. My suggestion is to completely forget trying to match a tie to a black shirt; so never mind the aubergine tie. Focus on using black in small areas, in strategic ways to increase the sharpness of an outfit that involves other shades, colors, and patterns.

    The grey fitted chino should do well with the grey shirt, if the chino is not too dark. You want that contrast, which is even more critical in that case because your major pieces will lack any color whatsoever. Where will the interest — the life — come from? With regard to wearing the black belt, again the black shirt paints you into a corner. Most other colors will be too stark or will clash in combination with black, so a black belt is one of your only options. But it will then blend with the shirt, providing no contrast other than a difference in texture. You'll get some sheen from the leather, and the chrome buckle in the midst of the monotone will — bam — be an eye-catching focal point. A belt is called for, but because of the black-on-black you lose the opportunity for the belt to contribute to the interplay of shade and color.

    When one repeatedly encounters these kinds of issues with awareness, one may realize why black is a limiting option as a basis for outfits, and why brown is much more versatile: brown in itself offers color, character, warmth; and it combines much more easily with a wide variety of colors, opening up a whole new world of possibilities. You'll have to develop your eye and use some cleverness to start bringing color and contrast into black-based (or grey-based) outfits.

    The other issue with that belt is that it is quite an 'urban fashion' design. Depending on the style of pant and shoe, it may or may not match well. If the chino is casual — soft cotton, without a crease — the belt may look too slick. It's another situation where you should check the mirror to see for yourself, or post photos of the complete outfit.
