    How can I avoid buying clothes I end up never wearing?

    A lot of the advice in this thread has addressed the end of the purchase process; but it doesn't get to the original and pivotal point, which is how to know what is a good purchase in the first place.

    Your clothing should work well for you.

    • It should suit you — who you are and who you want to be

    • It should suit your situation — the activities you engage in, the settings you enter, the people you encounter, and the goals you want to achieve

    • It should combine well with your other clothing — the color, pattern, design, style, and level of formality should be easily used with much of the rest of your wardrobe (what you already own or what you intend to acquire).

    We run into problems because our society is so object-based. We tend to think that the thing we get will solve our problems, and so we focus too much on the thing and not enough on how we ourselves use what we have. The first step should not be checking out what looks good on someone else, or what is hot in the blogs or online ads; the first step should be self-knowledge: who are you, what movements are arising through your being, and what does your life require? When you know these things, you will know which acquisitions will help you and which will be useless.

    So first do an overview:

    • What are your most common activities? Be honest. If your life is mostly about hanging out at the ballpark, then get the best clothing for that, not 3-piece suits or hip-hop clothing.

    • What activities may not be so common to you, but are very important nonetheless? (For example, job interviews, etc.)

    • What is your real personal style? This is not necessarily what you have always worn in the past, but rather your personal and energetic qualities, whether they have been expressed well or not. What comes through you naturally?: calm; intense; bright; classy; easy-going; urban; outdoorsy; active; home-oriented; elegant... the possibilities are infinite, and each of the infinite possibilities are all valid, if they are true.

    • What do you hope to achieve? If you have a wish or intent, might as well help yourself with what you wear, if you can.

    These questions help you to know what kind of clothing you need (or what kind will help you).

    The second part of the overview should focus on your current wardrobe:

    • Separate out everything that doesn't fit properly. Of these, if a piece can be tailored to fit, it can move on to the next step (considering whether it's worth keeping); if not, get rid of it.

    • Also cull whatever is in a color or pattern or design that doesn't make you look great. The piece itself may be expensive or well-designed or may look good on someone else; but if it doesn't help you yourself look fantastic, it's not for you.

    Once you have your wardrobe narrowed down, you can create your checklist of basics — the clothing that will be at the center of your life.

    • What kinds of clothes will you want/need to wear most days? These will have the best $$-per-wear value.

    • What kinds of clothes will make the most impact on your life? (Job search; work; dating; important events....) These will have the best $$-per-life-change value.

    • What are your best (and worst) colors? What colors make you look healthy and vibrant, and what colors make you look 'off' or sickly? With a mirror, a camera, and/or some friends' opinions, try many, many different colors next to your face, and see how they affect your look. This has nothing to do with your or someone else's favorite color; it has to do with how the color interacts with your skin tone and eye and hair colors.

    Finally, learn the basics of combining color and pattern (so that the pieces you buy can go well with most or all of the rest of your wardrobe), and combining style (so your outfits make sense, and all the pieces in them match the 'type' of look you're going for).

    Then you will have a much better idea of exactly what piece of clothing you need; and you will also be able to identify very clearly why you need it.
