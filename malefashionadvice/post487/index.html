    how should a stocky man dress?

    A good fit is important for all body types, but perhaps more so for yours; and that means clothing that skims close to the body, but is not skin-tight. Definitely stay away from loose sweaters or other layers that billow away from the body.

    You'll usually want to emphasize the vertical dimension. There are a few ways of doing this. First, you should have pants with a long, clean line. On dress pants, wear a full break; that is, let the front of the pant rest on the arch of the shoe, and let the back of the pant come to no more than about an inch above any heel. This will produce a nice break or buckle at the front of the pant. The point is to have as much length as possible of a single fabric and color. For the most part, avoid the no-break and shorter cuts that are in vogue these days. Also avoid cuffs or anything that will break up that one long line.

    For jeans, stay away from any kind of skinny cut or taper that will emphasize the difference in width between hip/thigh and ankle. A break or one or two 'stacks' may be okay; but if you have several stacks of wrinkles at the ankle, it will break up the long line and visually isolate your thighs. You may want to try a straight cut that has a wider pant leg, so that the leg drops straight down from the widest part of the leg; and even consider very slight boot cut to balance the width above. (Note that many guys are resistant to boot cuts. In any case, the curve should not be an obvious flare.)

    Don't worry about a close fit in the seat or in the lower waist and lower back; it's an athletic look. You can get pants and shirts that fit closely there (it's a case of "if you've got it, flaunt it"). Shirts can be tailored to fit more closely at the waist and small of the back. Again, just skimming the body, not skin-tight.

    Avoid wide or colorful accents around the waist. Keep belts minimal, not wide and chunky; and some outfits will be good without a belt at all, so that you can maintain the vertical flow of attention rather than divide it. Try to avoid anything that will cause the eye to focus or rest at the waist. If you really want to get technical about it, minimize the contrast between top and bottom parts of an outfit. Rather than a deep color below and a complementary color or white above, wear a shirt in the same color family as the pant. This will create a more visually homogeneous effect, preserving the vertical line.

    With suit or sport jackets, let the hem be slightly shorter than average. Especially avoid a long suit jacket, as it will shorten the legs. Make sure the jacket sleeves are proper length as well, not too long; and you may choose to expose a bit more shirt cuff. A good tailor should be able to educate you about how best to tailor a suit to your body, and this knowledge will translate into knowledge about buying other articles of clothing.

    Narrow (not skinny) ties in a solid color will be better for you than wide ties or ties with a large print. That goes for shirts too: with a wide body, focus on fine patterns and colors that don't hit people over the head. Large prints and strong colors will emphasize the density of the body.

    Maybe the guiding principle is that you don't need to be apologetic about your body shape. You can minimize extremes without actually hiding your body. It's a reflection of who you are, your activities, and the work you've done; so wear it well.

The jacket is a bit shorter than the classic length, and the pinstripes contribute to the vertical dimension.
