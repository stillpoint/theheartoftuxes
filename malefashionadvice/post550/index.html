    bag for dress clothes

    When traveling, I try to wear the suit or sport jacket so I won't have to pack it. The garment bag is, in my opinion, the best way to keep a suit from wrinkling when traveling. But these days I far prefer the One Bag style of travel; so no garment bag or checked luggage for me.

    Garment bags come in shoulder carry and roller formats.

    If I must pack a jacket, I try to fold it around the outside of a bundle of other clothing. This minimizes the sharp angles formed by folding, and so minimizes creases. The softness of the core bundle also helps minimize wrinkles.

    Other jacket folding advice has one turning the jacket inside out and fitting the shoulders one inside the other before flipping the bottom up (basically, folding in quarters). (Alternate method here.) (Other step-by-step instructions here.)

    The thin plastic shrouds that clothes are covered with when they come back from the cleaners are very useful in packing sensitive items of clothing, serving a function similar to tissue paper. A dedicated suit bag may do the same work, and will be more durable but also more bulky. (Google "non-woven suit bag" or "plasic suit bag".) The theory goes that wrinkling happens because packed garments rub against each other and hence bunch up. Over time, and with compression, the bunched areas become set in wrinkles. If you can keep items from rubbing and bunching, you can prevent wrinkling; so two strategies will be:

    • reduce friction (by using plastic sheets or bags)

    • stop items from shifting (by rolling or stacking them)

    One tool I use, which draws on both of these strategies is the modular container -- the packing cube or shirt folder. By keeping the garments stacked more tightly in a separate container you can limit the amount of shift, rub, and wrinkle.

    Some travel experts will recommend rolling all your clothes. Pants are a natural target for this strategy. In my opinion, this works if your luggage is filled to perfection -- neither too stuffed nor too empty. If there's too much space, items will shift and the rolls will loosen. In addition, disorganization ensues. If things are too tight, the parts of the garments at the center of the rolls get too compressed for my liking. Of course you can (and I do) roll items for inclusion in packing cubes or ziploc bags. (I find it easier to roll my t-shirts than fold them flat.)

    Another popular method is the stack, where items are laid flat over each other, with each new item crosswise at right angles to the previous. The stack is capped with some non-wrinkly items like socks or underwear or towels. That 'cap' becomes the core as the entire pile is wrapped up in a bundle. Any suit or sport jacket can go on the outside of the bundle and benefit from the soft curves and cushioning. The great disadvantage of this method is that you're screwed if you need something from within the bundle mid-trip.

    Discussions on rolling vs. bundling are here and here. Note that the second link is on a suitcase manufacturer's site and promotes their bags along the way.

    I prefer the modular system, which gives me great, organized access to my clothing but still gives me the option of wrapping a jacket around one of the packing cubes. With the addition of a plastic shroud from the cleaners or a sheet made from a sliced-open garbage bag, I'm confident my jacket will survive without being deformed.

    For a different take: The SkyRoll is a unique garment bag in tubular, rather than flat-folded, format.
