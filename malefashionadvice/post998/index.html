    Pocket square advice

    Thanks. Sorry, I don't have any particular books or blogs in mind. For me, it's a long, gradual, and ongoing process. Interest and engagement are most important. I like to see the qualities of things — their energies and movements — and try to figure out why things work and why they don't. Both good and bad outfits can help you learn, if you have the interest to look into the reasons why they have the impact that they do.

    I recommend having a lookbook: a scrapbook, file folder, or computer file with images of all kinds of outfits. Include sections on styles that you like and that you want to try, outfits that are exceptionally well put-together, themes that interest you (dress according to season or locale, based on a particular color or on color matching, texture, history, etc.), various looks offered by different designers, and especially include styles and outfits that perturb you or that you can't yet relate to — that's where your area of growth is.

    Let your lookbook be very fluid, not so rigid. You'll learn a lot through the interrelation of things. For example seeing Italian clothing design among images of the Mediterranean Sea, Italian city and country life, other (industrial, architectural, and product) design, and the history of Italy can bring up many enlightening associations.

    You may also want to explore a theme in depth, like say focusing on pocket squares and all the magnificent and crazy ways they are worn. If you see a hundred examples, all within the context of an outfit and a situation, then you will get a much deeper and more instinctual sense of what is possible than if you just go by someone's rules.

    I also always recommend certain 'tools of style':

    • A full-length mirror in good light (ideally by a window), for checking a complete outfit and seeing how the pieces relate and how the proportions work. (Tip: Besides looking at color and pattern and proportion, try seeing the outfit as a blank neutral within a framework of lines, as if you were using a marker to trace the outline of every element — the outer profile, the collar, button placket, pockets, tie, cuffs, etc. This will help you to see the relationships of shape and line.)

    • A digital camera, for quickly getting a more objective view of an outfit, and for recording the combinations that work very well and the mistakes that you never want to make again.

    • A bed or large surface on which you can lay out pieces of clothing. This is a much faster way of trying out combinations than having to put on and take off each outfit. For example, one pair of pants on the bed can be tried with every shirt in your collection to get a sense of the possibilities. (But always check the mirror as a final step to confirm.)

    • A well-dressed, ideally style-conscious friend who is willing to give honest feedback. It doesn't mean that you have to take another person's opinion as the final word; but having that outside perspective and the opportunity for discussion is valuable not only in terms of particular outfits, but for building a whole look or wardrobe and for growing your awareness of style.

    • A good tailor — one who is skilled, who listens well, and who can offer advice. It's rare for those three qualities to all be present; so you have to be aware of the weaknesses and work with the strengths. Also know that tailors have their own style of tailoring, and may not be open to doing things a different way; or at least, you may have to be clear or even forceful in your requests if their initial, habitual action is in the way. And know that while tailors may understand the properties of fabric and the geometry of the body, they may or may not be interested in or knowledgeable about style.

    Useful knowledges:

    • Know your context — your lifestyle, the activities you engage in the most, the places you go and the people you interact with the most, and your own personal energy. Knowing who you are will allow you to express yourself sincerely, wholeheartedly, and effectively. This can, and should, include who you want to be, as long as it's base in reality.

    • Know your measurements.

    • Know your best and worst colors, the ones that make you look great and the ones that make you look worse; i.e., ill, or 'less yourself'.

    • Understand the basics of color combination.

    • Understand how to match styles and levels of formality; or at least be aware that it's an issue.

    • Learn how to use contrast, pattern, and texture.

    • Know that risk and error are essential to growth.
