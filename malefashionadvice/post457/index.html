    Grey suit to a wedding, white or black dress shirt to go with? Also, do trim fit shirts still allow enough room to be tucked in?

    If you're going without a tie, you lose one of your accents and an element that could combine and play off other elements. You also take a step down in formality; not the worst thing at a wedding, depending on the setting. (It's your choice as to what image you project, but the rule of thumb for most is to risk overdressing rather than underdressing.) However, going without a tie will give you more leeway for expression in the other elements of your outfit. Where you might have concern about employing too many patterns or colors 'with-tie', without it you have one less focal point to worry about.

    Also, given that you'll be wearing a grey suit, and that pants and jacket will be given over to a single neutral block of tone and hue, by all means add some color with your shirt, socks, and pocket square.

    Salmon and lavender both look absolutely super with grey, and both tend to be flattering to the complexion. If your skin tone is pink, choose your shirt with caution or you may end up looking ruddy. Identify the difference between warm pinks and cool pinks, and check in a mirror in natural light to see which looks best on you.

    Yellow also looks great with grey, but it's very difficult to match it with most skin tones. Other colors can also easily go with grey, but may look more contrived (such as lime green, popular with Le Chateau club-goers in Canada).

    Avoid the black shirt. While it won't exactly clash, a black shirt with grey suit is 'evening', it's 'club', it's dour, and it's a bit clich√©, harkening back to the '80's and even '70's.

    White shirt with grey suit won't be wrong; it'll be a nice crisp look. But it won't be very celebratory either. High-key accessories (silver or gold watch/glasses or minimal jewelry, and high-shine shoes and belt) could compensate, if you want a look that's more rich than celebratory. Take note that one of the most common frustrations women have about men's dress is the avoidance and apparent fear of color and, by extension, verve and expressivity.

    It's not clear what the trimness of a shirt would have to do with its tuckability. The length of the tails is the main thing, and that is a dimension independent from trimness. A properly fitted trim shirt may actually become untucked less often than a classic fit, since there will be less blousing -- less material puffing out around the waist and tugging on the shirt tails. But it depends on your individual geometry; and there's no substitute for actually trying the shirt in action.
