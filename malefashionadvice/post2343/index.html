    I don't know where you're at now, so any thoughts I could offer would be quite general. Here are a few:

    • Review the elements of style, and stretch the envelope. For example, with contrast, try very low-contrast, muted combos; try medium-contrast combos; and try very bold color-blocking, power-clashing high-contrast combos. Likewise for all the other element of style (color, pattern, texture, fabric, line, cut, fit, design features, flow).

    • Learning color combining, or at least learning some new color combinations that work for you. There are apps for both iPhone and Android that pick and save colors from your environment (including outfits that you or others wear), and show you how to use them in new palettes. Try both overall color and pops of color on neutral backdrops.

    • Get involved in the senses; explore texture, pattern, even fragrance.

    • Put together a lookbook of styles that you either like or might like to explore. Looking at images with an analytical eye can kickstart your creativity. Notice what's done well, and why, and consider how you could make the look your own.

    • Taken even further, look to popular culture, art, current events, and history for creative ideas.

    • Use your bed! Try new combinations by laying them out on the bed. That way, you can quickly have a look at many new possible outfits, and you don't have to fear the mistakes. Whether you find a combo that works or one that doesn't, consider why â€” learn at ever opportunity.

    • Try. Try on even the outfits that "don't work", and see how they could work in a different context or with different pieces. When you go shopping, try on clothing that you otherwise wouldn't. And, even see for yourself whether ideas downvoted on MFA can be made to work for you or in another way.

    It sounds like you have some of the basics. "Formulaic" isn't necessarily bad; and it can be exactly the right spot to start branching out. Guys who go all eclectic without getting the basics down may not learn as much, and may not be in control of their look. But if you start with an outfit with classic pieces, based on the rules of style, you can then look into how to change it up, in a systematic way so that you can develop control.

    For example, starting with a chinos, t-shirt, and blazer outfit, see how it can be changed by swapping out one piece at a time: a few different shoes or boots; then the same shoes but a few different pants; and so on. See how layering affects the look. See how adding or subtracting accessories changes things.

    Then you might check how you can use classic pieces to convey a whole style or theme. Urban, nautical, western, bohemian, Euro.... Even if you don't have the pieces that convey a specific genre, how close can you get with what you have?
