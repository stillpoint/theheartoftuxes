    Are boot leg jeans a thing of the past here? Suggest some good affordable alternatives I might try.

    Boot cuts aren't going away. The widths and angles may change, but the basic cut is by now a classic. Even if only due to its strength in women's wear, it will stick around in the fashion vocabulary. Clearly there's a strong resistance among many men to the notion of bootcut styling; but shape-wise, it's the best balance for many body types. The bootcut adds shape to angular frames, and its width balances wide shoulders or hips, in both athletic and round body shapes.

    People often don't realize that their perception of bootcuts may come from poor examples — floppy flares that ruin the line of a pant. The wider bottom leg of a good bootcut is often very similar to a straight leg with a break; and unless the pant is pulled to its full length one may not at first notice the difference. (The difference being that a flattering leg shape -- wider thighs and calves, narrowing at the knee -- can be enhanced.)

    Online advertisers don't seem to know how to showcase bootcuts. They're often pictured too long for the model's leg length, and with sneakers that get swallowed up in the leg opening. It just goes to show that you can't take all your cues from advertising; you have to understand proper fit and appropriate use yourself.

    Rather than asking if the boot cut is a thing of the past, let's ask when, oh, when whiskering will fall by the wayside! I think the issue in the marketplace is that a boot cut is seen as a special feature, rather than a staple; and bootcut jeans will unfortunately often come laden with other features like complex washes, radical distressing, and UGH, whiskering. I'll distress and whisker my jeans my own way — by wearing them, thank you very much.

    At higher price-points:

    • 7 For All Mankind offers a plain dark wash bootcut, Hallelujah!, along with one or two faded versions.

    • Citizens Of Humanity has a looser-fitting bootcut with the dreaded whiskering (albeit subtle) and a higher rise; and I get the impression that this jean is targeted at the older upper-class man looking for a taste of youth without the need for slimness. You may find a few bootcuts in this brand; but it'll be hard to avoid imposed faded wrinkles in their jeans.

    • Paige Premium Denim offers a bootcut in an ever-so-slightly faded and whiskered design. If you're looking for a pre-fade, this may be your most reasonable option. (Note that here, as with the links above, is an example of the advertiser displaying a bootcut poorly. It's too long for the height of the shoe, with the hem sinking to the bottom of the heel; and the low profile of the sneaker is overwhelmed. A chunkier shoe or boot would be better.)

    At medium price-points, Abercrombie & Fitch and Guess always have a bootcut option. It's just a crapshoot as to how much crap will also be foisted on a perfectly good basic jean -- fades, embroidery, abrasions, tears, and faded whiskers that hilight natural wrinkles at the hip or completely do their own thing independent of how the denim behaves. Don't get me wrong; I love totally distressed jeans in a certain look; but please give us a plain option as well. In their eagerness to create both youth-targeted and definitive designs, chain stores sometimes go overboard with the features.
