    Tie bars/clips don't always have to be shorter than the tie.

    Clips and bars don't have to be shorter than the tie width, but these guys may be missing the picture when it comes to line and visual flow. Or it may be, I suppose, that they're aware of the issue, and have consciously chosen to place a full-stop in the middle of their outfits.

    There's a saying that if all you have is a hammer, everything starts looking like a nail. I find that many North American guys are still so object-focused in their style. Rather than looking at harmony, flow, line, and movement, guys focus on how they can show off a certain piece, and maybe in a clever way. I wonder if that's what's happening here with the tie bar.

    You can try tie bars and clips that extend wider than your tie, but there's a cause and effect relationship. The clip extending all the way across is a forceful punctuation. It's like a paragraph end right in the middle of the tie. Or actually, since they are bright metal with straight edges, it's a bit like placing a knife across the tie. It gives the impression of cutting.

    The all-across tie bar is bad feng shui, as it stops the vertical flow of energy; but it's not in itself good or bad. If one is conscious of the effect, one can use it. Maybe the men pictured want to project an image with some cutting to it, some resistance, contention, or even threat. (Like the throat-cutting sign?) Maybe it's a little bit of "go to hell". But then again, maybe it's just unawareness of the elegant line that's being tampered with.

    The all-across tie bar is mitigated here and here because it's at the bottom of the gorge, where the line of the tie also ends. It's only interrupting the imagined progression of the line. It is also softened here because the many angled lines in the plaid reduce the sense of one object cutting across.

    On the other hand, the slightly graceless move of opening the jacket in this example and this one, or the wholly unrefined combination of removing the jacket altogether and wearing the tie too long as in this example both compound the sense of a long line that is being cut all the way through. (The cutting effect is repeated in the clips on the braces, and the interplay there may actually be a saving grace, even if a bit heavy-handed.)

    Don't cling to rules that command "always" or "never", but be aware of how things are affected by the moves you make.
