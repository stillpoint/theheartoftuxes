    Me in Russia. Am I doing it right?

    Thank you for your wonderful reply. I'm happy not because you agree but because you are open. That openness means that you will be able to find your own way because feedback from others and from the situation will enter you.

    It sounds like you are aware of some of the issues we've mentioned; so you can make your own choices. Truly, the mirror (and / or digital camera) is one of your most important styling tools. You can check the difference for yourself on many of the suggestions. Photos can help you to be more objective, as you see yourself from a different perspective.

    Sometimes collar spread can be mitigated by buttoning another button or by ironing (sometimes with starch). Understandably, each of those has advantages and disadvantages. Meticulous guys will sometimes use magnetic collar stays. But button-down collars will usually stay in a good shape even when the shirt is opened two or three buttons down.

    My comment on the hat comes from my (North American urban) perspective. You may want to get feedback from others in your area to see what they think; but in any case, consistency and integrity of style is going to be a big part of your growth. When laying out an outfit or when checking the mirror, consider the style and level of formality of each piece, and have all the pieces chime in on the same expression, telling the same story.

    It shouldn't get to the point where it's a costume (like, head-to-toe sk8ter gear or all-out cowboy cliché); but all of the items in an outfit should make sense with each other and especially with you — your qualities and activities. No harm in trying out new things; but at the same time, be open to feedback both overt and subtle.

    One helpful technique may be to just brainstorm or word-associate keywords that come up with respect to each item. For example, the trilby hat and dark sunglasses bring up associations (for me) with the beach, guys who'd hang out at the racing track from the 1930s through the '60s, jazz and ska musicians, and the Blues Brothers and that 'secret agent' kind of look. But when going through the same word-association with the shirt or jeans, very different words and phrases come up. So if you were wearing a beach-going outfit with say light chino or seersucker shorts and a light polo or t-shirt, a trilby and sunglasses might make more sense; just maybe not so dark.

    The trilby will still almost always have some eccentricity to it that for many will mean dorkiness. The hat is associated with a kind of punky counter-culture look, and unless it really suits your personal style it will look costumey. Even on the beach, the hat's ironic aura still comes through. If that suits your personal style, fine; but then the rest of the outfit should match (for example, slim, short-hemmed chinos; plain or patterned flannels or bowling shirts; leather lace-up shoes with lots of character; 'grandad' cardigans or sport jackets or patterned suits with bowties).

    Just also be aware of how bad it can get. Remember to distinguish between what you envision and what you really look like.

    Read the sidebar guides and consider (A) what would be an upgrade on current items and (B) whether an outfit all makes sense together visually and in terms of style and level of formality. When shopping, try on many different items if only to learn and to stretch your sense of style; no need to buy.
