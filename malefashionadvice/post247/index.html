    Debating between a few fabrics/patterns for a new suit, are any of these "too much" for a job interview?

    An interview situation requiring a suit would also require discretion in its design and pattern. Some subtle visual variation among the threads of a fabric is not a bad thing; but always keep in mind the difference and appropriate balance between dressing to express your style and dressing for business success. Any but the most subtle Prince of Wales (aka Glen Plaid) is too loud and brash. A sharkskin with a sheen is too stylish in a slick way. Not that these couldn't be worn in some liberal, creative work environments; but for an interview where you want to show your sense of responsibility, flashy style isn't going to help you out.

    Regarding option #1, the 100% wool suit, it really depends on the type of fabric. A solid grey suit should likely be your first suit. Something on the darker side, like a charcoal color, will be most versatile. Charcoal grey will serve you well for serious formal occasions and professional situations, and can be brightened up (with choice of shirt, tie, socks, and pocket square) for festive occasions.

    A fairly thin wool blend fabric will likely be best for you since, as transmetrospider mentioned, you may need to wear it in warm and hot weather. In cold weather you can add (under the suit) undershirts, thicker shirts, and sweaters, and (over the suit) insulated vests and coats; but in hot weather there's only so much you can remove.

    There is a very wide range of patterns for suit fabrics, especially when you take into account the intensity of pattern. This is what some people mean when they talk about a sharkskin suit. By that definition, sharkskin is clearly too slick for business wear. But sharkskin can also mean a (non-shiny) solid grey with some variation in the shade of individual threads, which is more subtle and wholly appropriate for business (sharkskin on the right). A matte sheen is okay; shiny reflection is not. This sharkskin, though on the light side, would still be appropriate for an interview. This sharkskin and pinstripe is still too slick, and may give off an air of self-involvement or even sliminess.

    There are also degrees of boldness in the Prince of Wales pattern. A loud pattern like this could mark you as a dandy -- flaky and unprofessional -- in business settings. (And there are much louder PoW patterns out there!) This one is still too defined as well. This pattern is getting close; but there's still the optical effect of vibration that could be distracting. This pattern is called a "Glen Plaid" at Indochino, but it seems entirely invisible at a distance; though on close inspection you can see the pattern: this one is subtle enough to be very appropriate for interviews and work, and it still has a depth and visual interest (which contributes to a sense of depth and interest coming from within you and your character).

    Note that pattern is only one parameter. Even if the pattern is appropriate, the design, cut, and fit also have to be right. Roped shoulders, very narrow lapels, and very short (trendy) jacket hems should be avoided if you want versatility.
