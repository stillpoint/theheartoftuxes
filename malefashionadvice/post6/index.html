    Besides missing belt, any advice?

    Please take to heart that I'm giving feedback in your best interest, since you asked for advice; because several corrections follow. But that doesn't reflect on you as a person; when you know better, you do better. You look like you have the brass to take the advice and roll with it.

    While it may seem right to pose casually for a photo, there are two strikes against this pose here. First; a suit is designed with a structure to show your form off at its best, and the jacket needs to be closed to function properly in this respect. Unless you're sitting down, close the jacket leaving the bottom button open; and keep your hands out of your pockets. Your air of ease should come from your actual confidence and general comfort in dressing up, whereas hands in pockets is an immature and submissive posture. Practice by dressing up in a suit even when you don't have to for a certain occasion, so that eventually you're completely comfortable wearing a suit.

    Second, with the jacket open and hands in your pockets we can't judge the cut and fit of the suit.

    This suit looks at least a size too big for you, if not more. The puckering at the shoulders and under the arms shows that the shoulders are too wide. Whereas many other discrepancies can often be addressed with good tailoring, this can't easily be corrected.

    In addition, the sleeves are slouchy — too wide and too long — and the pants are slouchy — too wide, worn too low (indicating that the waist size may be too big), and possibly too long (although you haven't shown us the length of the pant leg or the shoes worn with the suit).

    I'd like to see you in a well-fitting, contemporary suit. If it's a youthful design and a very good fit, then a young man can buck tradition and go without a belt. The standard, though, is to wear a leather dress belt that matches the dress shoes.

    Not only does a poor fit and boxy cut look messy, it is not age-appropriate. An ill-fitting suit makes it look more like you're dressing up in someone else's clothes (your dad's?) than shining on your own, which is a shame, because you'd look great in a proper fit.

    Next, the tie is also conservative and 'old'. You should seek a tie in a different solid blue, a more expressive color, or more dashing pattern, and one that is a bit narrower (around 2.5", not skinny; i.e. not under 2").

    A narrow pindot tie would work, in either the navy or purple background. | Grey with contrasting color stripe. is both respectable and expressive.| Lighter blue chambray is a bit more casual but still sharp, as is a dark blue knit tie. | And there are many other possibilities. Check out images on the internet.

    The point of the tie should hit right at your waistband, right at the middle of where your belt buckle would be. Some say it should be at the top of the waistband, and that's okay too; it will change with small changes in posture anyway, but the way it is in the picture is too high.

    I'd like to see a color-corrected photo. It's not clear if your shirt is yellow or white. If yellow, that's not going to be a good color for you. A white dress shirt is a basic. After that, a pattern of stripes, tattersall (grid pattern), or gingham (small square checks on white) will be good for a shirt that is sharp but not as dressy. Especially the gingham is a good vibrant pattern for a young man, and it will add some movement and life to an outfit that is otherwise dominated by the solid color of the suit. When wearing large fields of a single solid color, consider adding a pattern or at least another color besides neutrals somewhere; and on the flip side, if you want to wear a bold color or pattern, limit it to one piece and ground it with solid neutrals elsewhere in the outfit.

    The shirt's collar is terribly curled. That just won't do for dressing up in a suit. If you were wearing a sportcoat or sweater and had a casual Oxford-cloth shirt on (a thick fabric that is meant to be worn in a laid-back way and look good even when rumpled), then it would be okay. But when you're entering a situation where you need to look sharp, you have to take care of details like this. Iron the collar and install collar stays. Most dress shirt collars will have channels under the collar points where stays can slide in. (Remove the stays when you wash and iron the shirt.)

    Without other images I can't speak to the fit of the shirt, but I can say that the point collar (AKA straight collar) is not the best for you, as it emphasizes the length and slimness of your neck. A spread collar will work best with your physique, and is also a stylish combination with slim, well-fitted contemporary suit and tie. Look for "slim fit" or "extra slim fit" rather than "classic fit" in all your clothes, and take them to a tailor to have them adjusted even when they're the right size. Your shirts and sweaters should just skim your body without being skin-tight.

    I also agree that a shorter hairstyle will add to the sharpness of the look when the other issues are fixed. But then, when the outfit is rocking the long hair (if washed and styled well) doesn't necessarily have to contribute to a scraggly look as it does here, but it does require a good style and grooming to get there.

    Good luck!
