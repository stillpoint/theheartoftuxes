    Why does this happen to me EVERY time? (I'm on the right)

    The three major reasons why a shirt may untuck and billow are as follows:

    (1) The shirt is too large, and the extra fabric drags and inches out of the waistband.

    (2) The armholes are too low, causing the shirt to be pulled upward when the arms move. Note that the attachment point of the sleeve is a lever; the lower it is placed, the smaller the range of movement the arm has before it starts to tug on the side seam.

    (3) A convex belly pulls the shirt out when the body moves.

    A further, minor, cause may be that the shirt is too short; or in other words, the tails don't have the length to be tucked and held in place with friction.

    There will almost always be some shifting of the shirt and consequent untucking of material at the waist; but this can be minimized. The first cause is cured by having a shirt made to fit your body, or tailored after the fact. The second cause can only be avoided by buying shirts with high armholes. Since many off-the-rack shirts have low armholes, this often means having shirts made to measure. The third cause is cured by dropping body weight and losing the round belly.

    In your case, that shirt is too big for you. You can, and should, take your button-ups to a tailor to have them fitted to your body, but a shirt that is altogether too large will not be able to be adjusted well. Buy shirts that are a better fit to start (check the sidebar guide on How Clothes Should Fit), and then take them to a good tailor.

    A good tailor will be able to:

    • shorten sleeves and sometimes narrow them

    • take in the side seams to create a tapered shape from the front view (while maintaining a double-stitched lap-felled/folded seam)

    • add darts — wedges of fabric gathered into a new partial seam — to the back to make the shirt fit more closely to the hollow at the small of the back

    • add smaller, secondary darts for an even closer fit at the waist (Not many people know this. I've even seen darts at the front, but that disrupts the clean look, and is usually only seen in tailoring women's tops.)
