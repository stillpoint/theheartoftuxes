    Simple and quick question. Are undershirts cool or uncool?

    This question has been asked many times before, so checking the search function will turn up more threads for your reading pleasure.

    We distinguish between undershirt and t-shirts. A visible undershirt in your outfit means you're displaying your underwear. That's on the same category (though not as egregious) as pulling down your pants and showing your underpants.

    The underpants move is insulting, which of course is why it's done -- to advertise that you don't give a f@#& -- which of course is why it's childish and lame. Although showing an undershirt is not nearly as bad, you should be aware that it still belongs to the same family of stylistic choices.

    That stylistic choice is sometimes made as part of a very casual and somewhat brash expression, and it can be pulled off with confidence. The message sent is that the wearer is so comfortable and in command of himself that he doesn't feel the need to augment his outfit in order to rise to a more public formality. Note the number of open buttons and the other supporting elements in the outfit shown. If this is not carried off with the support of matching character and ease of being it just looks like the wearer doesn't know or care how to dress properly; and that's the perennial difference between stars and wannabes.

    When using undershirts, in general a deep-v-neck undershirt should be worn with an opened button-up so as to be invisible, and a crew-neck undershirt should be worn with a closed button-up so that a vee doesn't show through the shirt.

    Thick and colorful t-shirts can be worn more legitimately as layering pieces. This is a somewhat young look with limited viability past the early years of college, and it will still carry some echoes of the undershirt, but can be distanced from that look and it's mistake. Any crew neck showing under a button-up will limit the outfit to a certain casual level, so care should be taken to understand the situation one is entering and align with it. The button-up worn with a t-shirt also must be casual — say, a flannel plaid, not a broadcloth (gag) or even an oxford. This outfit avoids the "undershirt" problem since the t-shirt is colored and the button-up is opened all the way; but it is otherwise a fashion disaster that marks the wearer as a stylistically clueless high-school kid.

    If the situation calls for it — backyard barbeque with a beer in your hand — you may a times want to wear a button-up completely or significantly unbuttoned, in which case you should be wearing a t-shirt or undershirt to avoid overexposing yourself. Like the example above, this still marks a style that has not evolved out of high school.

    You should know that an extra (t-shirt) layer that fits close to the neck and covers the upper chest is a move toward the shy and conservative end of the spectrum. It is a less confident and free look than simply leaving a couple of buttons open on the collared shirt, without anything underneath. This may speak volumes about your level of ease with your body and sexuality. The other cause for caution is the shape of a crew neck itself; i.e., is one sure that it's a flattering shape for one's neck and head and the outfit as a whole? The crew neck becomes even more of a problem under v-neck and cardigan sweaters, forming an awkward ice cream cone shape. (This example also makes the "undershirt" mistake.) If you must expose a t-shirt under a sweater, the collars should match: v-neck with v-neck, crew-neck with crew-neck.

    Wearing the same color of t-shirt and button-up will usually look awkward. T-shirt color can be chosen to bring a flattering color near your face or inject some interest into the outfit with harmonious or contrasting hues. Since only a sliver of fabric is shown, it may be possible to be more adventurous and to use more striking or playful combinations than if a whole shirt front were visible. This example works because the shirt and sweater are both casual styles, the t-shirt is distanced from an undershirt because of its color, and the muted neutral color of the t-shirt doesn't rankle within the combination. One might argue that the t-shirt adds some interest to a combination that would otherwise be too dark and lacking in contrast; but it also contributes a boyish, conservative feel that one may not want to project.

    A great alternative for layering under button-ups is the henley shirt. The buttons and opened collar shape harmonize much better with the opened button-up, and avoid to some extent the shy and reserved quality of the crew neck. A henley can also sometimes act as a buffer between a crew neck t-shirt or undershirt and the rest of the outfit (though in this example I don't know that the white undershirt is even necessary). Take note of how casual are the button-ups in these examples, and consider the situations in which they would be appropriate. It's a look that's not generally thought of as "well put-together", finding its place instead in a rugged outdoorsy or home-bound weekend niche.
