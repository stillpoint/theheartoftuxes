    Pock square advice needed

    Properly worn, pocket squares are often in a completely different color, pattern, and fabric than worn elsewhere. The advice to complement the tie color without matching colors exactly is also fine; but if you wind up too close to the tie color the effect can be amateurish or cheap. Better would be to coordinate just one among a few colors in both pocket square and tie.

    Although many high-profile men will wear matching colors in tie and pocket square, it's really the sign of an unrefined sense of style.

    White cotton or linen pocket square (cotton versions will usually be more appropriately named a handkerchief) are hard to get wrong, especially with a white shirt. The drawbacks are that the look is ubiquitous and not very creative. And that's fine; if you prefer to play it safe, a white pocket square offers a crisp, clean feel with a bit more flair than wearing no pocket square. It is the white pocket squares very strength as a standard accessory that has made it so popular. But if you prefer to exude an air of style, display your command of the sartorial options at your disposal, or have more of an alpha-male take on self-expression, then look into wearing a more colorful or patterned pocket square. To have it both ways — classic and creative — you can wear a white square with noticeable texture or patterned weave, or fold it in a more expressive way. Silks will be more adaptable to stuffing; linens will provide a greater range of folding options.

    Some anchor points for matching the square's color can include a minor color in the tie (navy in your example), cuff links, socks, or contrast stitching. Obvious matches can go two ways: Bold color or pattern matching can serve as the equivalent of the 'f&%# you' pants, signalling your willingness to say it loud and proud with color; or it can appear matchy-matchy and immature, like bad prom-night rentals.

    Taking it to the next level, you will consider the message or qualities conveyed by the pocket square's color, pattern, texture, and fabric. Your personality should be honored and hilighted as you opt for understated, classic, bright, sporty, 'power', or any number of style options that suit you and your persona. You will take care not to compete with the frame created by lapel, shirt, and tie, which is the most important relationship in a formal outfit. The pocket square, in fact, can be seen as a second tie; and there should be asymmetry in the relationship between the two. That is, tie and square should not compete, but rather one of the two should take the lead. You will also take care not to overload the outfit in general, but instead will maintain a balance between loud colors or patterns and the fields of solid neutral tones that support them. Always check in a mirror. And if a boutonniere will be worn at the wedding, this will figure into your strategy.

    Maybe the best rule of thumb with the pocket square is not to think so much of coordinating as expressing yourself in an off-the-cuff way.
