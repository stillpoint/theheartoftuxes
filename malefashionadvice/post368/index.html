    I just tried tailoring my own shirt for the first time. Here it is after the first temporary stitching. Thoughts?

    There's still quite a bit of material to come in at the side. I'd want to see a photo with your arms out; because the lower the armhole and wider the sleeve, the less leeway you have taking in material in the body (you have to curve the stitching out to meet the armhole junction). If the sides are fitted close on a shirt with a large armhole, fabric will bunch up at the front and back of the underarm, and may even create a billow across the entire upper back. I can't see if that's an issue here without more photos.

    If the shirt is more fitted to your body shape by taking it in more at the waist, it will look much better. As it is, it still looks boxy; and when tucked it will likely bunch out.

    The fit at the hips looks like it is approaching too tight; or maybe already there. The bottom of the shirt should just skim the hips. This is essential if you are going to wear the shirt untucked; because a shirt that is loose elsewhere and gripping your hips looks disastrously bad. But it will also help you out even if you tuck; because proper fit at the hips will keep extra material from pushing upward and bunching at the waistband of your pants.

    You can't always take in the sides to completely fit the shirt to your shape; but the side alteration is the most important. This is because the side alteration sets the shape that people will see when meeting you from the front. Darts can take material in at the back (usually the lower back); but if the sides are simply straight, people will still just see a boxy shirt.

    So do as much as you can while staying in proper relation to the armhole; and then if there is still extra material at the waist or blousing out at the lower back you can put in darts.

    I've never altered the arms on a shirt myself (I like to preserve the double seam; and have only done single-seam alterations to the side seams of casual shirts); but if you need to take considerable material in from the side, it will usually be necessary to 'turn the corner' and take an angle of material from the arms as well. This alteration doesn't usually go all the way down the arm, unless the entire arm is too wide. Instead, it usually starts at the armhole, at the same distance as the amount taken in from the side seam, and tapers into the arm seam a few inches down near the top of the bicep/end of the underarm.

    If you want a temporary way to see how the shirt will fit after a second try at alteration, do this: flip it inside-out, put it on and button up, and pin the side seam to your liking. Straight pins will be easier to sew past or remove while sewing; but you can also use safety pins. This will give you a chance to see how close you can bring in the seam while retaining a comfortable range of motion. Having someone else help you out with pinning can be much more convenient.
