    Backpacking through SE Asia for a month in September... What should I pack to have good form but also good function?

    Synthetics are the way to go, for their wicking and quick-dry after washing. One pair of the right design of lightweight shorts can do triple duty as walking shorts, swimsuit, and sleepwear. Three undershirts and three undershorts: wear one, wash and dry another overnight, and have one in reserve. Wool and wool blend socks will be a godsend, and will keep your feet cooler and more dry by far than cotton. I use smartwool liner socks; again, three pairs only. Light as can be!

    Plan your wardrobe so that all items can work together with each other, and you can eliminate half of what others might bring. Remember that in many places you can buy what you need when you get there; but check travel forums or ask ahead at the hotel where you'll be staying, because essentials can also be curiously unavailable at some locations.

    Carrying only one backpack on the trip is a great idea; but with some prior planning, it doesn't have to be a big backpack. Ever since I converted to one-bag travel (with the Osprey Porter 65 -- great bag!), my travel life has totally changed for the better. I experience more ease and more freedom with a single bag. I have confidence that excess luggage will not be the reason I miss a connection or turn down an exciting side-trip. And for the most part, I don't fear the loss of important items in checked bags that go missing. I was on one flight where I was required even to check my carry-on; and that is why I pack a minimalist essentials kit in a grab-bag that's easy to pull from within the carry-on.

    The Osprey Porter is well-designed, convertible, and comfortable. Semi-firm side walls help with comfort when carrying less than a full load or when carrying awkward items; but it sometimes takes a bit of a push to stuff into small bins and corners. If you prefer a soft-sided bag, check out Tom Bihn's Aeronaut and Western Flyer. Red Oxx bags are well-designed and tough, but they are already heavy even when empty, and those ounces add up when walking all day.

    For a daypack, I carry the very lightweight and versatile Patagonia Lightweight Duffel. The old model has a much better design than the new model, especially in terms of the convertible backpack straps and the compression system. The Lightweight Duffel, or an ultralight hiking backpack (Google it -- there are many) can double as a stuff-sack for sleeping gear or clothing within the carry-on until you get to your accommodations.

    Check this page for things to look for in a bag; and check the website for all kinds of info on one-bag travel: http://www.onebag.com/bags.html Ultralight hiking principles can also be applied to general travel: http://home.bresnan.net/~swultralight/Principles.htm

    My best travel shoes (no longer made) were very classy-looking trail runners by Montrail that were sleek and neutral enough to dress up with jacket and tie, but were very comfortable for city or wilderness walking. Current trail runners have thick soles and 'technical' design that eliminates them from any role in dressing up. Ecco makes some very comfortable shoes that will suffice for dressing up. Another option would be workboot-style, although they may get too hot for tropical climes. One Two

    Never mind the fanny pack; it's dorky and makes you a target for theft. Instead, find a secure way to keep money and passport under your clothing, such as money belt, neck wallet, leg safe, etc. Some experienced travellers do away with these tricks and just keep their cash in a zippered pocket. It may very well be that maintaining a low profile and blending in with the situation will keep your possessions safer than secret stashes. I do like to have a cable lock with me to secure my backpack around a chair leg when I need to close my eyes, or as impromptu locker or door lock. Keep copies of your ID, traveller's cheques if you take them, and important info separately in your backpack and with someone at home. (If mugging is a concern, fold together some low-denomination bills and a couple of useless keys and keep them ready in a pocket as quick throwaways -- throw in one direction and run in the other.)
