    Tie bar guidelines?

    It may be that a tie bar that spans the entire tie looks neater locally; but if you take in the whole picture you can see that it cuts the vertical line of the tie — the very raison d'etre of the piece. It is the verticality of the tie within the angle of the jacket's gorge that defines the upright, dressy image; and that line is defeated if a tie bar or clip cuts all the way across. Do what you like; but visually it is more proper for the clip not to reach all the way to the other side of the tie.

    Flourishes and accessories have more authenticity if they are connected with an actual function. For example, the flipped-up polo collar is regarded as smarmy; but on a sunny, windy day out on the yacht, it's perfectly acceptable. Likewise, the tie bar is most fitting when control of the tie is an issue. It should never be worn with a waistcoat (where tie control would be very redundant); and care should be taken with flairs near the face — a tie bar can be quite bright and eye-catching, so the additional presence of a pocket square, pin, boutonniere, eyeglasses, cufflinks, or watch, etc. can quickly get out of hand.

    As to how high to clip, guidelines are derived from the actuality: look with a discerning eye, and see how proportion and line are changed. See for yourself where the eye goes first, and what the feeling is. Too high, and the feeling is cramped; too low, and the feeling is slumpy. Knowing the difference, you can play with it. But if the eye travels to the tie bar before the face, get rid of it. The focus should be on you, and your attire should support you and your message. If your possessions start stepping into the limelight, you lose your soul.
