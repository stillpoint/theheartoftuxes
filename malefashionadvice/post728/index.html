    What does malefasionadvice think of leather driving gloves? Also, how and when should they be worn?

    Driving gloves are visually and stylistically specific, so they're problematic. It's hard to wear them out of context without appearing douchey.

    The common driving glove design has a lot of eye-catching detail: ribs, vent holes, open back, snap or buckle, and knuckle cutouts may all appear on the same glove together! Simply from a design standpoint, this tends to be too fancy for one accessory. Remember that greater density of decoration tends to be associated with increasingly fey or dainty qualities. Besides that, the eye-catching quality of the contrast that open back and cutouts create propels the common driving glove into statement piece rather than accessory, meaning that rather than a simple flash of color and texture that relates to other parts of the outfit, this item will tend to speak louder than all the rest.

    It is the association with the auto racing genre that bumps the piece further along the spectrum, from statement piece to novelty item. In context (i.e., worn in and around an appropriate car â€” a Saab Sonett, not a Subaru Tribeca), driving gloves make sense, and become sharp and stylish and evocative of an exciting lifestyle. Out of context, they easily become a toy for the try-hard, making a guy seem like a boy who's playing "let's pretend".

    The gloves you [OP] linked to are not really driving gloves. They are patrol gloves, used by law enforcement and security personnel for better grip on tools and weapons, and for hand protection during searches and scuffles. Some patrol gloves come lined with fireproof or anti-stab/anti-slash materials, or even loaded with plastic or lead armor. Although they don't sport the same level of detail that driving gloves do, their super-tight fit and cut-off wrists mark them also as gloves from a specific genre; so stylistically they can come off in a similar way; i.e., as gimmicky and immature.

    A couple of ways you can integrate driving gloves into your look:

    • Seek out a hybrid version with a simpler design. You can find many so-called driving gloves that don't have all the cutouts and ribs; many will simply feature an elastic back or buckle across the wrist. These are simple and generic enough to fit in with many styles of dress. The tight fit is still a slick statement, but it more easily fits in with most sharp outfits if it leaves behind all the racing design features.

    • Commit to lush high-style outfits with features that won't be outdone by the gloves. In this image, the scarf texture, the pocket... er... treatment, the lapel piping, the similar color leather strap, and the moleskin coat itself all present themselves with a volume that doesn't leave the gloves stranded by themselves. A new context is formed â€” not racing, but styling â€” and the gloves' genre becomes an interesting echo while the color, shapes, and patterns within them take the driver's seat (pardon the pun). Context is of utmost significance.
