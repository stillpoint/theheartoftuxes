    You're travelling for three months and you want to pack smart and travel light, yet still look sharp. What clothes do you take so you don't look like a backpacker... while living out of a backpack?

    A few resources for you:

    • One Bag (all about travel with a single bag)

    • Key principles for traveling light (this site also has a forum)

    • Rick Steve's Europe what to wear (the site has many articles and a forum)

    Wool (thin knit merino or cashmere) is extremely versatile across a wide range of temperatures and conditions. It dries fairly quickly (though not as quickly as synthetics), and is naturally antibacterial and thus odor-resistant. One of my most useful travel pieces is a merino polo sweater (sweater with a collar). I can wear it on its own, sleeves pushed up or down, or over a t-shirt, or under a sportjacket; it works with shorts on the beach or with sharp slacks at a nightclub.

    Chinos will be more versatile across a wider range of formality than jeans, and in many cases are more comfortable. Those made with stretch fabric will be most comfortable when sitting or walking for long periods. Avoid dark colors in summer, as you'll find your legs burning when you sit outside on park benches or café patios. Khaki/dust/stone colors will still look good after picking up some city grime, as opposed to very light or clear colors. There are many performance-fiber pants now made that feel great, dry quickly, adapt to vigorous activity (trekking, athletics, dancing), and look quite sharp. Check brands like Outlier, Rapha, Proof NY, SWRVE.

    If you are going to be attending semi-formal events or venues, you can usually get by if you have a sportjacket and nice leather lace-up shoes. These are the two pieces that stand out the most, and that will upgrade a casual outfit. You can be wearing a (clean) t-shirt or henley, but with some nice shoes and a light summer sportjacket you're good to go out on the town. The jacket should be quite breathable. Synthetic blend will help it stand up to use and abuse, so it can double as an everyday jacket. Unstructured sportjackets can be stylish, packable, and breathable (but if unstructured, the cut must then be good on your body). The shoes, of course, must be very comfortable. Desert boots can be a happy medium, ranging from trekking (depending on the sole) to nights out.

    Shoes are quite heavy, so trim down to one pair, or wear leather shoes for most traveling and pack ultralight runners (like Onitsuka Tiger or Puma) for knock-about. Sandals are not classy in town; but ultralight flip-flops may be useful as indoor slippers, shower shoes, and beach shoes.

    Choose one color palette, and have all your pieces conform to it. Functional layers that conform to one color scheme will provide the most versatility for the least amount of weight and number of garments. Packing a matched set of clothes will also allow you to throw together outfits without even thinking about it.

    Once your color scheme is established, keep the basic pieces basic — solid neutral colors. You might throw in one patterned item, like a button-up or jacket; or let your accents appear through accessories like a colored or striped scarf or belt, or the metallic shine of sunglasses and watch. When the outfit makes sense together, there's already a stylish foundation. Then a little goes a long way in terms of spicing up the look.

    Short short hair is easiest to travel with. Buzz cut is just wash-and-go, and regardless of how your day goes your hair never looks disheveled.

    Remember that you can buy fantastic fashion pieces in Southern Europe. No need to pack everything. If you're missing something you can pick it up later; and you'll want to leave room for those purchases that will not only become part of your wardrobe but will also serve as mementos of your trip.
