    V-Neck shirts. Yes or no?

    The V-neck is a common and classic neckline on t-shirts and pullovers. It can look great; but — like anything — not always. Reaction against v-necks often amounts to simple narrow-mindedness.

    Much better than any rule of thumb is to understand the properties of a certain piece or stylistic element. Instead of asking whether something is good or bad, look into its qualities — how it relates to the forms around it.

    The angled opening of a v-neck can be both literally and figuratively sharper than a crew neck. A crew neck can sometimes visually cut off the neck and head, in an abrupt manner, from the rest of the profile. A v-neck, on the other hand, provides more of a transition. It also tends to expose the neck muscles (sternocleomastoid), collarbone, and notch of the sternum in a flattering manner.

    Check photos and check in a mirror to see the difference between v-neck and crew neck, in how the neck and head relate to the rest of the body. It can help to soften or even blur the vision, to take in the whole rather than fixating on small areas.

    The transition-forming quality of a v-neck can also be a disadvantage in some cases. Since the whole line of the neck is longer, and the line of the neck muscles is exposed to the point where they converge, and there is literally an arrow encouraging the eye to continue moving downward, a long or thin neck can be made to appear too long or too thin. The formula for balancing is the same as for collared shirts (spread collar vs. point collar): a shallower vee offers balance to a long and thin neck, and a deeper, narrower vee offers balance to a short and thick neck.

    The v-neck shirt is also a much better shape than crew neck behind a v-neck or cardigan sweater, or even under the vee of a collared shirt. Some do find that the doubled vee is an excess of anglularity — and it is something to keep in mind — but the problem usually arises because of disharmony between the vee shapes, not simply because they are doubled. In basic outfit building, the neckline of the inner layer should follow the neckline of the outer.

    As to hair showing, that comes down to the convergence between your own take on the issue and the contemporary trend. There is a bit of a swing now from the just-so grooming of the metrosexual to a more emphatic and natural masculinity. There is more of an integration between primal masculinity and refinement of style, rather than an either-or dichotomy. We're no longer accepting the uncontrolled hair of the hippie era or the in-your-face chest exposure of the disco era; but we have also moved on from the technical '90s and the almost neurotic precision and emasculation of the '00s. A rebirth of masculinity (see /r/everymanshouldknow) is the new trend — looking to the old, but with new sophistication.

    All this by way of saying yes, you can participate in the trend integrating masculinity in style, by showing a reasonable amount of (trimmed) chest hair. Know that those who balk at it are behind the times.

    Take this in context, though, and pick your spots. For sharp or formal occasions, cover up.
