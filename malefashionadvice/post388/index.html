    Checkered pattern on inside of cuff---what's their purpose and how do I make them show?

    Yeah, the patterned or contrast inner cuff is a gimmick that became popular a few years ago. I agree that it looks out of place; but of any of the various inner cuffs out there, the ones on that shirt are pretty much the best you'll get because the revealed pattern is very similar in shade to the shirt itself.

    The most common way I've seen of rolling these cuffs is the method shown in the video onebigcat posted: drawing the cuff up past the elbow, then rolling the remaining sleeve from below to cover just part of the cuff. This can be more easily performed before you put the shirt on, and the result may be more even and flat.

    When the cuff lining contrasts with the rest of the shirt because one is a solid and the other a (loud) pattern, or because the cuff is a different color or shade than the shirt, this method of folding creates an awkward and misplaced border â€” two focal points that seem arbitrary and contrived. It's a look reminiscent of card-dealer's armbands; decidedly smarmy. Some cuff linings are more restrained, and minimize the border effect.

    The way you have folded the sleeve is too far down the forearm. It creates poor proportions, with the sleeve being too long and the exposed forearm being too short. This end point is only ever employed in the most casual of contexts, like scrunching (not folding neatly) the sleeve up during manual labor or when walking on the beach. In your picture, the move emphasizes the billow of the upper sleeve, making the whole thing look messy as well as out of proportion. This method tends to require full, muscular forearms to balance the length of sleeve above (and a better-fitted sleeve).

    Another method is to flip the cuff once and scrunch the sleeve up to some point below the elbow. Whether it's high or low, the crisp, flared cuff tends to look mismatched with such a casual scrunch, and the contrasting lining only adds to the mismatch. Again, the gimmicky or contrived feel takes over.

    Simply flipping the cuff once (no men's image available) without scrunching eliminates a couple of problems â€” the border effect, and the mismatch of crispness and scrunching. There is still an awkward proportion with the exposed wrist, but it's not as bad as the half-forearm proportion. The flared, undone cuff is still not nearly as casual and nonchalant as one might hope â€” again, especially because of the obvious intent to expose the "special" lining. It's fancy-schmancy, and because of this it will tend to look better on women than on men.

    Although I have seen the rolled contrast-lined cuff look good, I'd have to say it's most often not the case.
