    Does this top work with the jeans?

    In most outfits, the belt should harmonize with the footwear. For casual outfits this is a more relaxed guideline, with a greater range of color and material available. Here the belt color should usually be in the same color family as the footwear, and the style or genre should not be too different.

    Some examples of the range possible in casual outfits: With dark brown leather casual shoes you might use a belt that's medium-to-dark brown leather, or an olive green military canvas, or even a khaki quilted belt. A light jute belt might work with canvas sneakers; and a nylon striped belt might work with taupe suede "buck" shoes.

    Note that in all of these examples the style of belt and shoes remain closely related.

    For more dressy outfits, there should be a much closer match between shoes and belt. For example, with dark brown dress shoes the belt should be not just any dark brown, but a dark brown similar in hue to the leather of the shoes.

    I can't see the belt in your image well, so I can't critique it well. I'm not enthusiastic about black leather with denim. Some people like it, but I'm not a fan. If you're going to stick with black boots in a rugged casual style, you're probably looking at wearing a wide, thick black or off-black belt with a fairly chunky pass-through ("frame-and-prong") buckle. The kind of style you're investigating here won't do well with a big fancy plate buckle; it's too showy.

    • You'll probably be looking at a belt about 1.5" wide, and thick enough to appear sturdy. This belt from Wrangler is a small step up from the most standard flat leather belt. The raised central ridge adds interest, first because it adds a design feature to something otherwise flat and plain, and next because the ridge provides a feeling of heft or substance. The buckle's rounded brushed steel keeps it from appearing too sharp, both visually and in terms of its formality. The keeper ring adds another bit of substance as well, and closes out the frame of the buckle.

    These types of features aim at one of the foremost and sought-after qualities in rugged/workwear/outdoorsy casual styles: the lived-in feel. Fading, creasing, fraying, burnishing, tarnishing, scuffing — whether artificial or natural in origin, these features go beyond adding visual interest and evoke a sense of history and a particular active, experienced engagement with life. Look for these kinds of features; but don't overdo it. When artificially aged or distressed features become too prominent, or when their artificiality becomes too overt it enters into cheesy territory; it doesn't look lived-in, it looks like you're trying to look lived-in.

    Black is the most dour color. It's feel is a retreat or absence of feeling. Just as it absorbs light, it absorbs character as well. This can contribute to outfits that are intense and brooding, but it has some inherent difficulties. One of these is the almost reverse of expressivity in character; another is the lack of reflectivity — it's harder to get a read on black. So other elements of style have to come to the forefront: line, texture, design features, and so on.

    • This next belt from Wrangler has some texture to it, a stamped pattern that reflects light differently in different places. This could, in a small way, help keep the belt from disappearing within a dark-on-dark scheme (black belt on dark indigo denim).

    • Proceeding further along these lines, might consider a completely textured belt like this woven leather belt from Dockers. The buckle is not as chunky, so this belt is hovering between rugged and sharp casual styles.

    • This belt by Neves brings in some of that distressed, lived-in quality discussed above. The color contrast brings the belt into visibility even more than mere texture, yet it will still match with a black-based outfit.

    • Compare this John Varvatos design, at a higher price-point, with the second Wrangler offering above. Notice the similarities in pattern, and the difference in refinement.

    • Note the difference between the casual belts shown above and this plain black leather dress belt, with its narrow width, tapered edges, thin and angular buckle, and design free of embellishment.

    Now check the difference in character that can be achieved with brown leather. Suddenly there's warmth and color contrast; the greater reflectivity of light means features are more visible, and subtle features like dye variation and natural differences in color and texture can enter the picture:
