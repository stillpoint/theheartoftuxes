    Just decided to look a little nicer today, what do you think?

    Not sure if you're kidding; but I'll respond as if this is a serious post.

    You have a good starting idea: khaki chinos, a t-shirt, and a sweater. The colors of the basics are good: khaki, white, and blue. You can keep the look young, even wearing a hat if you want. And you can add a pop of color, as in your sneakers. The outfit does needs some refining, though.

    Yes, this hat makes you look like a hood rat, which is what it's kinda meant to do. But you want to look nicer; so lose the hat. If you really want to wear a hat, try a plain hat in a neutral color. A stone-colored slightly distressed cadet cap is young but still classy, not tacky. It will go with a lot of different outfits. For more interest, look for a neutral pattern like grey plaid.

    A nice hairstyle with a bit of movement in it (a textured cut with a small amount paste, clay, or wax added) will look way better than a 'statement' hat. It will look not only more put-together, it will also look more confident; because you won't be relying on an article of clothing to make you look cool or stylish, it will come from the totality of what you present.

    Anyway, you already have bright sneakers. One loud attention-getting piece is enough. More than that will usually create discord because the attention is being called to too many places at once (and in your outfit, the triangle of white within the cardigan is yet another bright spot). One of the secrets of style is how to be bold without being loud -- to be subtle in using those vibrant pieces.

    Check the How Clothes Should Fit guide on the right sidebar of this page. The pants are far too baggy. It seems that they are both tapered and much too large, a disastrous combination. You don't need tapered. It's not that good a look. Just get pants that fit.

    The low vee on the dark cardigan with the white crew neck underneath it make a large and high-contrast area that is distracting and unappealing. The white tee would be better with a medium-color crew neck sweater; or a white tee under a medium-color cardigan or v-neck sweater. With the dark cardigan, a grey or medium brown shirt would be better, preferably a v-neck. A polo shirt with the top couple of buttons open would also provide a nicer vee to go with the cardigan.

    Here's perhaps the all-time greatest iconic sweater photo. Note that there is contrast between the grey and white, but it is not too great; and also notice the harmonious shapes created by the sweater and shirt openings.

    Since you have all solid colors (and the top half is all intense color), you could wear a patterned shirt, and especially a patterned collared shirt. (Again, top button or two open would make a nicer vee with the cardigan.)

    The cardigan can be worn with the bottom button(s) open, and with the top buttons open as well if you choose. That, plus pushing the sleeves up a little gives less of an old-man look to it.

    You don't need to try for a fashion look or adopt the uniform of a subculture. Looking better means dressing with regard to how an outfit displays your form and your energy, not fitting in with someone else's image. It's true that kids take a lot (most?) of their style cues from pop culture; and it's true that this time of life is a time to experiment and try things on, even if just to discover whether it's 'you' or not. But you are not 'hip hop' -- from what I see in the picture, that's not your style, not your energy. If you try it, you'll only be doing it half-assed, because it's not who you really are. It may take some experimenting to find out who you really are and how to express that with your clothing; but staying true to your own character and supporting it -- bringing out your strong points -- should be a priority.

    Children dress in costumes; and that carries over into adolescence, as teenagers categorize each other according to the styles of clothing they wear. It's more comfortable for teens to be able to quickly identify with a certain group or connect with a certain part of culture. But there's maturity in finding your own definition, and not requiring a stereotype to do it for you.

    So I encourage you to go beyond trendy logos, visible brand names, and pieces of clothing bought only because they are from a certain line or store. Those things all hide who you are; but a clear, stable basis of essential pieces will provide you with a sort of stage upon which you, not flashy clothing, are the star.
