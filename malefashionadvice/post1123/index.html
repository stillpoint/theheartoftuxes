    I've had this same bland haircut for awhile, and I was looking for some new ideas for a new one. (Ignore my workout clothes)

    Elements of style and design are almost universal. The ways to generate interest and character are the similar for a hairstyle as for an outfit. Those elements include contrast, color, texture, line, flow, and pattern.

    Contrast

    More dramatic variation of length will add some interest. For instance, you could keep the top at the same length or longer and shorten the sides and back. A sharp defining line between short and longer areas will be both more interesting and more "out there"; a compromise could be to have the shorter areas taper in to the longer ones.

    Color

    Coloring hair has always been a way of bringing more energy and uniqueness to a style. This category includes highlights, lowlights, dyes, tints, and paint-on colors. These options tend to go in and out of fashion quickly. The more obvious the change, the more it can be perceived as contrived and affected. Subtle over-all changes of hair color are possible, but likely not worth the effort and expense to maintain, especially for short hair. Spending time in the sun can bring about a natural color change.

    Texture

    If you have your hair cut with a buzzer it creates a flat, uniform look. More texture (and thus more interest) can be achieved with a scissors or razor cut; and that usually means using a skilled stylist instead of a barber. Ask for a more chunky style. "Product" is maybe the easiest way to get some texture in your hairstyle. From paste to pomade to gel, hundreds of different products are available, each with its own properties of hold and moldability. Products like American Crew Fiber or Got 2 Be Molding Paste can create an attractive (slightly) tousled or "just woke up" look — rub an almond-sized dab between the hands and smear it over the hair, then tease a few ends up, especially at the crown of the head. Alternatively, use the product rubbed between your fingers to just twist a few points of hair up strategically.

    Sheen

    A sub-category of color and texture is sheen — how the light reflects off of the hair. Wax pomades like American Crew or Murray's can create a separation and flexible control together with a reflective sheen. These tend to work better with hair that's at least slightly longer than yours, otherwise the hair tends to stick up. Sometimes products can be combined, with one (a clay or paste) used to create volume and hold, and a second (a lighter wax or a serum/spray like Aveda Spray-on Shine) used to provide sheen.

    Line

    The profile and defining lines of a hairstyle can also be altered to create interest. Your style is quite round and simple; bringing on more angularity or otherwise changing the shape is an option. With short hair, this has to be done with contrast of lengths (as in the first category above). With longer hair, you have the option to cut the ends to different shapes — for example, having bangs hang asymmetrically, or having some longer pieces hang over the ears or neck.

    Flow

    Longer hair can move, which is inherently eye-catching. A good cut will look good no matter how the hair falls (so you don't have to worry about keeping it glued in place); but this requires the work of a skilled stylist.

    Pattern

    It's a radical look, but patterns can be cut or shaved into the hair. Gimmicky and not recommended.

    ~

    My recommendation for an easy fix that can evolve into something even more stylish is to have the sides and back tapered shorter (preferably with scissors) for contrast, and to use product to style some texture into the longer top area. As the top and front grow in, more scissor- or razor-cut textures are open to you.
