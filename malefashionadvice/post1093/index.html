    Feedback Request - Casual & Cocktail Attire

    You're a young, fit guy -- wear more fitted clothes! Find shirts and pants in slim (not skin-tight) fits, and whenever possible get them tailored to fit your body well. Some tailors are old-school and conservative, and are used to altering suits for larger and older men; pass on those and find a tailor who will help you achieve a contemporary look.

    The green, khaki, and brown colors of the casual outfit are good together; but I want to see something more age-appropriate in the cut and perhaps more daring in the style -- slimmer pieces, a plaid or interesting print shirt, and open another button at the top.

    Unless there is a specific request or key word in the invitation, a Christmas party doesn't necessarily require a tie, or even dress pants. If 'Cocktail Attire' is specified, traditionally that means formal wear just below dinner jacket/tuxedo in formality. (This is also called 'Informal' on traditional party invitations.) In the evening, that means a dark suit and tie; in the afternoon, lighter colored suits can come into play.

    These days, the term 'Cocktail Attire' can offer more latitude; so you have to find out whether the intent is traditional or modern. If the latter, a suit, blazer, or sportjacket can be worn, with or without a tie. For the festive occasion, a jacket and dress shirt, without a tie but with a bright pocket square, will do well. The less formal the occasion, the more options you have, down to a 'sharp casual' outfit like a nice sweater and dress shirt.

    You can sometimes guess the formality of the event from the method of invitation. A phone call or email would usually signify a more casual get-together, whereas a printed and mailed invitation is more formal.

    As mentioned by wip3out, a v-neck or cardigan (or sportjacket) should be worn when wearing a dress shirt and tie, and especially with the starkness of a white shirt. Pockets on a dress shirt make it more casual, and also make it appear older and more conservative. Find something smooth and unadorned, to emphasize the slickness and youthfulness of your look.

    The tie is not bad, but high-key bright red and white stripes is very loud against the plain black and white. I'd like to see a jacket or sweater and then determine what color, in a solid or in a tasteful pattern, might go well. The tie is also wide, which also makes it an older look. A tie closer to 3" in width would have more dynamic energy and more of a contemporary feel.

    The tip of the tie should rest at the top of the belt or down to the middle of the buckle. It shouldn't extend below the belt or end much higher than the belt.

    The shoes go along with the rest: they are not bad, per se, but are a bit 'adorned' and a bit of an old look for you. For a Christmas party tomorrow, no problem; but I also can't help but think that you could at some point do something more interesting than black pants, black belt, and black shoes.

    In the prime of your youth, you should be trying out colors and patterns and styles. You may find that some outfits you try are 'not you', but that's okay; then you'll have learned that about yourself, and your own style will start to develop. Just as this is the time to be trying all kinds of new experiences and activities, it is also the time to be trying new kinds of expression with your clothes. Not necessarily weird, not necessarily what someone else is doing, but simply venturing into new territory.

    Read the sidebar guides and learn about fit and combining colors and patterns. And perhaps most to the point, start thinking about what your clothes are expressing and what you want to express. That is, how your clothes convey who you are -- your character, your values, your quirks, your assets -- and who you want to become.
