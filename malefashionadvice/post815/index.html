    Modern Tailor shirt fit check

    Definitely reduce the waist (or half waist) measurement. If you want the shirt to conform to the shape of your body at the small of the back, you'll almost certainly have to get it darted anyway as well, either by Modern Tailor (if they do darts) or later by your own tailor.

    There does seem to be extra fabric all the way up the torso; but you will be in the best position to tell how much loose fabric you have at the level where the chest measurement is taken, right under the arm. Either you or a friend can pinch the fabric there to see how much leeway you have. It shouldn't be a skin-tight fit, but you probably won't want a lot of extra material in a made-to-measure shirt.

    Likewise, you are in the best position to say whether the shoulder seams line up with the crest of your shoulder â€” at the end point of the shoulder bone. There is a bit of bunching in the sleeve; so if you need to reduce the shoulder width a touch, that might help. If you leave the shoulder dimension as-is, you may want to shorten the arm to reduce or eliminate the bunching. You can open the cuff of the shirt completely and let the sleeve hang freely, then measure how far past the base of your thumb the cuff falls; and that's the number to reduce by.

    If it were me, I'd slim the sleeve a tad. These sleeves look normal, in that they're no different from many other shirts you see in public; but the fact is that most guys have shirt sleeves that are too wide, and narrowing the sleeve will add to the overall picture of a great fit.

    From the new views, the armholes look okay. You want the armholes, or scyes, as high as possible without binding, since that will give you the greatest range of movement and keep the shirt tails from pulling out when you raise your arms. (A low armhole is like a lever that attaches lower on the torso. The lower the attachment point, the smaller the range of arm movement before the lever tugs on the shirt's side seam.)

    Good luck!
