    Can a trouser crotch be tailored?

    The distance between crotch and waistband is called the 'rise' of the pant. Dress pants will typically have a higher rise than casual pants; but some dress pants are lately being marketed to be younger and fashion-forward, and those may have a lower rise than usual.

    The seat can be taken in on most pants by (the tailor) opening the seat seam and bringing the two sides closer together before re-sewing.

    The seat can also be let out in many dress pants, if there is sufficient extra material. Sellers used to commonly make dress pants with extra material in the seat for just such a purpose; although more and more are skimping on this now. Even if there is extra material available, if you want to have a seat let out, consult your tailor as to whether the previous needle holes will show.

    Pants waists can also usually be shortened at the waistband. This may be done by just cutting the extra material and re-sewing the waistband to the top of the remaining pants; but if there is a significant angle at the waist it may also involve taking in the seat to keep the waistband size the same by changing the angle of the waist material.

    For example, some jeans are designed for people with some extra... let's say 'gluteal mass'. These will typically have more material at the back of the seat and waist; and on average-sized people the waist can end up looking like dad jeans, even though the rest of the jean fits fine. In that case, have the tailor reposition the waistband by cutting a wedge of fabric from the back of the pants, so the waistband and belt won't ride so high in back.

    Finally, the cut of pants will differ. Some pants are meant to be form-fitting, and others are meant to have spare room. Going against the design of the pants is often a losing battle, and in that case you may be better off just looking for a different cut.
