    What does MFA think about turtlenecks? Yes/no? How to wear them?

    Turtlenecks have been 'out' for quite a while, but apparently they're coming back 'in' this year with a presence on designers' runways (example from the Hermes spring 2011 collection). Sometimes you'll find them sold as "roll neck" sweaters.

    A fine, close-fitting t-neck can be worn under a sportjacket, windbreaker, leather jacket, or coat. A fine-gauge t-neck can go under a denim shirt or denim jacket, which may help take the seriousness out of the look. They'll also dress down a suit, while making a clear style statement. As a fall/winter look, a plain or rib-knit turtleneck is best paired with thicker or textured wools.

    Keep the neck fairly tight to the chin, and avoid mock-necks. Some t-necks can be worn unrolled to good effect. When worn with a collared shirt as an intermediate layer, a turtleneck can give the effect of an ascot. It's an old-timey, self-conscious look.

    T-necks can also be worn as the only layer for a clean and simple look, whether they are thin or thick, plain or patterned. The thinner and slimmer the turtleneck, the better the proportions of the underlying body had better be; but you soon reach a point of diminishing returns with thick and loose sweaters. They are not a good look for larger men.

    The benefits of turtlenecks are their clean look, the line they create for the neck and between lapels (one field of color, from gorge to chin), and their cozy feel. The risk is that even as a more casual alternative to collared shirts and v-neck sweaters they may appear serious and self-concerned. Their neck construction itself may convey discomfort or conservatism. At worst, they could bring to mind Eurotrash and spy clichés.

    To see how turtlenecks were done in their heyday, check Clint Eastwood in The Eiger Sanction (several examples) and Steve McQueen in Bullitt.
