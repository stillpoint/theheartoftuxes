    I have ~$300 to spend. In the interest of pragmatism, what articles of clothing do I buy to maximize my net fashion gain?

    Assuming no major gaps in your wardrobe but rather an overall upgrade, two items will have the most impact: shoes and coat.

    Beyond that, basics will have the greatest use value. You'll want something that fits your lifestyle, the situations you'll be entering, and who you want to be; and you'll want something that is a good color for you (makes you look hale, hearty, and radiant when you wear it) and that combines well with the majority of your expected wardrobe.

    To determine for yourself what will maximize your gain, start from the inside and move outward; start from what is most important and essential, and then branch out.

    • Who are you and what do you want to achieve with your attire? The pieces you buy should serve that purpose.

    • What activities do you engage in most of the time, and which ones are most significant or will be impacted the most by an upgrade in style? Buy for those areas first.

    • What color palettes and styles will you be working with? (And do they suit the person you want to become?) Narrow down the styles and colors you'll be buying.

    • What pieces could fill in your wardrobe the best, both from a practical standpoint ("I need to be warm this winter") and from a style standpoint ("I have nothing to wear for evenings out"). Some pieces, like a sport jacket, will take on many roles and work in a wide range of outfits. Identify and prioritize the pieces you should be purchasing first.

    • Don't think that buying a single amazing piece will put you over the top! It's more about the integrity of your outfits and the ease of combining than it is about a single stand-out piece. In fact, if the integrity isn't there, the standout piece will look out of place and insincere.

    Shoes are the foundation of the outfit, and signal your 'bottom line', both literally and figuratively. Women especially key on the shoes you're wearing more than any other item. Great shoes can often bump up a middle-of-the-road outfit even without any other change.

    Your coat will be the first thing many people see of you when you're out and about and when you enter a building. Like shoes, a great coat can also set the tone. Jeans and a t-shirt look much different under a ratty old puff jacket than they do under a crisp pea coat.

    The thing that will maximize your buying power is not a specific item or set of rules, but the time and care you spend looking into your own life and what simple changes can help make it work better. Sometimes the best change is to wait -- spending money isn't the cure for everything. When you've identified clearly the pieces that will help you out and that will combine the best with each other, then you can act.

    We often see something we like in ads, in the media, on the street, and we'll develop fantasies about it that aren't well thought-out. Just being a fan of suits, for example, doesn't help you out if you'll only wear a suit once every two years. No matter how wonderful a thing is, it's still sometimes better not to have it.
