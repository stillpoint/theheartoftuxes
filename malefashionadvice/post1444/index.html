    Darting casual flannels

    I prefer a fitted profile in nearly all my shirts, dress or casual. I usually have my tailor take them in in the Italian style, very close to the body. Some casual shirts I leave looser, but still with shape in them. This usually means having darts installed, and only occasionally involves taking in the side seams as well.

    Both thick and thin flannels work well for me with a tailored look. The fabric/style that I need to be careful with is oxford. The strong point of oxford cloth is that its body allows it to look good even when rumpled, and creates and easy-going drape and flow (which is more apparent when the shirttails are untucked, but which is still significant when they are tucked). Installing darts structures the oxford and reduces its flow; and if the shirttails are not long enough, the shirt's easygoing nature is completely undone. Instead, it looks uptight; and this clash of natures causes it to look off. (I'm not talking about royal oxford, a completely different fabric for elegant dress shirts which does very well with structured tailoring.)

    I find that flannel has enough flexibility to overcome the structuring effect of darts; but I also wouldn't assume that a conflict would never happen. So the first point is that it's up to you to know and choose the look you want from a flannel shirt: is it laid-back and rugged, or is it chill but put-together? The next point is to understand the fabric and the shirt design: know the properties of the fabric, how much body and flow it has, and what can and can't be done through tailoring. (Talk to your tailor and ask a lot of questions.)

    As you'll hear often on these boards, fit is king. Perhaps no other element of style is as significant in influencing the quality of your outfit and your appearance to others. Closely tied to fit is line. I put a priority on line most of the time, and much prefer a "perfect fit" over perfectly matching pattern on the back of a shirt. If I can have both, so much the better; but for me, fit is first.

    If you need a better compromise, think in terms of taking your shirts in at the side seams instead of installing darts. They won't accomplish exactly the same thing, but there will be some overlap in effect. That is to say, some of the extra fabric around the waist can usually be taken in through the side seam, but unless darts are installed, a billow of fabric at the small of the back will almost always exist, and the side-view line of your outfit will tend to be sub-par. Taking in through the side seam, though, preserves the regularity of the fabric's pattern on the back.
