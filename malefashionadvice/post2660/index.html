    What hat / glove combo would look good would work with this?

    Down is great if used in the right way for the right thing. There's nothing that we know of (as far as I know) that provides as good insulation per weight.

    Another great strength of down is its compressibility; but it's also a weakness. It's great if you want to cram your down coat away into a stuffsack, but if something presses on the down while you're wearing it, you lose insulative value. You already mentioned the problem with wet down: it becomes almost useless. So for mitts, which are likely to both be compressed often and come into contact with snow or wet objects often, I'd avoid down. I'd only do down mitts if I were sure I wasn't going to need to use my hands much in the mitts.

    Much better for the cold would be a wool mitten with a windproof weave or fleece liner, and a leather palm pad. Tightly woven wool is fairly traditional, but a dense wool felt is much better. If they're not windproof, they could be supplemented with a gauntlet shell. The very best deep cold mittens will likely be leather with shearling, with the cuffs reaching well up the forearm. You'll see some of these with articulated or extra index finger, for use with a rifle. Those with an extra finger will allow you to slide the finger back into the mitt part for greater warmth.

    But if you're not in deep, far-north cold, there are a lot of ski gloves and ski mitts that are more functional and not as massive as the polar mitts. Go to a ski shop and ask for their assistance in choosing. They'll be familiar with the various conditions the gloves will be expected to withstand.

    Another option that I find an interesting possibility, and that I'd like to try at some point, is the handwarmer muff. The insulated tube clips around your waist and keeps your hands warm until you need to use them. Then, no need to find a pocket for mitts or to holding them under your arm while you perform tasks. Muffs are available in some hunting stores and at sports supply stores (check the NFL handwarmers that the quarterbacks use).

    I do like a down hood. I used to have a down hood that I salvaged from a Goretex parka. It was absolutely fantastic! I would just wear a knit cap or toque most of the time, but in blizzards I would pull the ultralight hood out of a tiny ball-like stuffsack that I'd carry in my day bag. The hood covered my head, neck, and face, offered a visor against driving snow or sleet, and was perfectly waterproof and windproof. Very cozy even in a blizzard. For winter weather emergencies, I'd recommend something like that, if you can find the Goretex version. Non-waterproof versions will be very useful in significantly sub-zero conditions (no worries about rain or wet snow). Not at all stylish, though.

    For versatility in a range of winter conditions, the trapper hat is very good. Being able to flip up the ear flaps is great, so you won't overheat. Wool is quite water resistant, and with either synthetic, shearling, or fur lining you don't have to treat the hat so delicately. When choosing a hat, do the breathe test for windproofness: press the hat against your lips and puff or suck air through it. You can tell -- if it's too easy to pass air through the hat, it's not going to help you in gusting winter winds.

    I just ordered a leather trapper hat with rabbit fur lining. Leather, of course, is extremely windproof, and will stand up to quite a bit of moisture. The fur should be more than warm enough for anything but deep wilderness excursions (in which case a huge, dense shearling or wolf or beaver fur hat might be wanted).
