    Does the whole "coordinate your colors with your skin/hair" argument have any merit?

    There is some merit to rules of thumb that match clothing colors to your own coloration; but you will never know how much and in what instances they apply until you get your own direct visual experience. That is why I recommend having a full-length mirror near a window that lets in good outside light, and taking the time to try many different colors next to your face to see what they do for you.

    Other points you mention are also significant. Appropriate contrast adds interest and implied sophistication to an outfit. Without contrast, the outfit is a single shade, and the viewer's eye has nowhere to go; the attention rests without moving. In a subtly fashioned monochrome outfit, this produces a sense of stillness or calmness; in a less conscious effort it makes for a feeling of boredom, stagnation, and mental dullness, and these become the qualities associated with the wearer.

    When contrast is added, however, there are now clearly differentiated areas between which the eye can travel. Instantly, there is interplay, relationship... because of the movement between one spot and another, a story develops (first this now that), and within that there is now the possibility for rhythms and echoes and clever references. Greater contrast implies a more definitive character (the issue is very "black and white"). All of those interplays and manipulations of attention suggest the wearer's mastery over energies; he has gone from boring blob to sophisticated manager of mood.

    One starting point is "dark bottom, light top; and vice versa". It shouldn't be taken as a hard-and-fast rule, but if you keep it in mind it will help you to construct outfits that include contrast. You will have to learn through experience how much contrast is enough and how much is too much.

    Yes, some colors pair very naturally with others. Brown and blue. Light brown and pink. Green and rust/orange. Tan and black. Navy and white. Check out photos online and in style magazines, and you'll be able to catalog the combinations that are rock solid together. One very useful standard is the brown-grey-blue triad. Rotating the pieces of your outfit among these colors is almost guaranteed to keep producing well balanced, interesting outfits. Increase the interest by including contrast; increase it yet again by finding more rich or intense versions of the colors (e.g., heather grey chinos, turquoise blue linen shirt, terracotta brown sportjacket, and tie it all in with matching walnut brown leathers — so nice).

    The thing is, you can't get all the knowledge you need in one shot. No one is going to write the overview that will immediately provide you with top style. You need to put the work in to (A) gather knowledge, (B) develop your eye, and (C) attain direct experience that makes the knowledge firmly your own.
