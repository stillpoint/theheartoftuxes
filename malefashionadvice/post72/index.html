    Simple Questions - Mar. 1st

    You are right; black is limiting for several reasons:

    • It is colorless, meaning that the creation of interest within the outfit then relies more on design features and contrast with other pieces. Being a solid, black also lacks the movement and rhythm of a pattern.

    • It is dour and serious. In the colder and darker seasons when you'd be wearing a coat, this can be oppressive.

    • It creates a contrast problem. With black pants, the whole look suffers even more from the above two issues. With dark colored pants the possibility of clashing is increased, since black can be an unpleasant combination with some dark browns, blues, etc. With light colored pants there may be too much contrast because of black's extreme intensity. As the outermost layer, which is seen most often and dominates the outfit, a black coat is somewhat hampered in its versatility and ability to uplift the whole look. Not that it is not possible, it just requires a special treatment.

    • It may not work well with your coloration. Some skin tones are completely overpowered by black, and some colors that would mitigate this if worn near the face don't go well with black (as mentioned above).

    A grey coat is one possible answer. You may solve some of the issues such as contrast and compatibility with skin tone. Grey will definitely go well with many more colors. But depending on the grey, a dull/dour energy may persist.

    Contrast should also be considered carefully. Will you be wearing mostly light or dark pants? With dark pants, a lighter shade of grey will provide better contrast and thus more interest; and vice versa. You might think that a medium grey would be most versatile, but it can paint you into a corner in terms of contrast, providing some contrast with very dark or bright pants and little contrast with intermediate shades.

    A better, more stylish, and possibly even more versatile solution is to go for a patterned coat. The addition of texture, rhythm, and internal contrast within the pattern adds a tremendous amount of interest. You are freed up to use all kinds of solid colors in your pants (and thus you are freed up to use many different options for combining the rest of the outfit). And if the coat pattern is somewhat subtle and uniform from a distance, like herringbone, birdseye, or small houndstooth, it can also go well with patterned pants.

    A coat that has some color may be similarly versatile by making a neutral-based outfit immediately more interesting. The drawback is that it again limits the use of some secondary colors; but the bonus is that if you have a coat color that works well with your personal coloration you can look fantastic in public at times when most others are just getting by.

    Coat colors don't have to be intense hues to add interest. Camel, tan, khaki, olive, navy, taupe, cream, and other neutrals can work very well if they happen to be a good color for you. And the higher echelons of style do call for some boldness, some willingness to (literally) wear your emotional and expressive heart on your sleeve; so you may also dare to commit to hues like reds, oranges, clear blues, and so on. The greatest expressivity, of course, will be achieved with both color and pattern.
