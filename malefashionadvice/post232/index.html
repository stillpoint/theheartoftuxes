    Are trench coats a faux pas?

    Old-style, classic trench coats are very dated. They make young men look like they're playing dress-up, and make older men look older still, and out of the loop. The contemporary style, which is completely viable, is much more trim â€” close to the body, and hemmed above the knee or even mid-thigh.

    The Burberry coats you linked to bring up a couple of points. The first is that most trench coats, like double-breasted suit jackets, are meant to be worn buttoned closed. If they hang loose as in the first link, there is too much fabric creating a misshapen, awkward line and profile. The sleek drape of a trench drawn in at the waist (usually by the belt at least tied at the back, if not tied completely around the waist) establishes a nice broad-shouldered (accentuated by the epaulets), slim-waisted, long-legged shape. Leaving the coat to hang open loses this shape, and with double-breasted versions the excess fabric will exacerbate the destruction of good line.

    Some exceptional fabrics may drape well enough to overcome this somewhat; but if you check the line on the left and right sides of the first link's front view, it's terrible. If it's this bad on a male model, in an advertisement photo where the conditions are controlled, you can bet it won't look good on you out in real-life activity.

    Second, black is usually not a great choice for a trench coat. Not only do the usual color-matching and skin tone issues arise, the black trench brings with it a lot of pop culture baggage, from movie spies, to Matrix wannabes, to kids that go on shooting rampages. Khaki (what Burberry is calling "taupe"), actual taupe, grey, tan, brown, and navy, are all usually better and more versatile colors for a trench.
