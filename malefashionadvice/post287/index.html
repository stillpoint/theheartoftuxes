    What is the difference between a $500 suit and a $5000 suit?

    The type and provenance of the fabric can be one of the big differences, which include availability of certain patterns, colors, textures, fibers and blends, and quality of weaving.

    The cut and construction of the suit is the next big difference. In order to cater to a larger market to make the most of the $500 price of a suit, the manufacturer may make it a very generic cut — the kind of thing that will exclude the fewest people from wearing it, but will fit none of them well. Cutting some corners (literally and figuratively) on the tailoring is another way cheap manufacturers recoup cost. The more expensive suit should have higher armholes and slimmer arms, among other features, that will exclude the suit from what some men can wear, but will make it more functional, comfortable, and better looking for the ones who can wear it. So the price goes up partly because of the extra care and skilled work involved, and partly because a segment of the market is excluded.

    Construction features, design features, and detailing also make up a large part of the cost. Full hand-stitched canvassing, specific shoulder features like roping, functional cuffs (working buttonholes), button design and materials, hand-topstitched edges, unique lining and interior pockets, under-lined collar, besom or patch or barchetta pocket features, and correction of aberrations before the piece even goes on the rack are all aspects of craftsmanship that may possibly be included in the more expensive suit.

    After that — or before all that, really — the concept and design of the suit according to a designer's unique vision and/or according to the leading edge of fashion trends is part of the investment. Although designer names in and of themselves may significantly increase the price, there is also a reason that the designer and his/her house is paid a premium, and that is for you to access his/her guiding vision and sensibility — the way they shape forms and qualities to bring something marvellous into the world — that special, almost shamanic talent that may have brought the designer to prominence in the first place.
