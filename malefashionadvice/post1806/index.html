    Body shape.

    As far as pants go, if you want to minimize the apparent size of your hips and butt then buy straight leg pants that drape from the hip. That is, find pants that fit close to the hips and fall straight down from the widest point, without tapering or widening. Resist the urge to buy big overall, as a clean line at the hips is much more attractive than anything oversized.

    If you are of average to tall height, you could also do a slight bootcut on occasion, but likewise with the fabric draping straight down from the widest part of the hips. Some guys can't even stand the thought of a bootcut; but we're not talking about a flare, just a bit of width to balance the width higher up in your silhouette. Take or leave the bootcut; but past a certain age, leave it.

    To maintain that clean line, keep your pockets flat, and avoid any stitching or additions that bring attention to the area or add bulk. Keep your belts thin (i.e., flat so they don't add width, but not necessarily narrow) and under the radar as to color. Forego pleats.

    Although following these guidelines limits your purchases, you can indulge in fabrics with movement and texture. Check the classic style icons to see how they wore 'drape'. Gene Kelly wore loose cuts with lots of drape not only because it was the style of the time, but also to accommodate his larger butt and thighs.

    Avoid pants that taper in any way, as the width of the hips is then accentuated. Slim-cut pants will also tend to wrap the hips and show the curve there; but if for some reason you want a slimmer cut, you might partly camouflage that curve with a jacket that falls straight to the hips and a little bit past, such as a leather car coat, or a canvas field jacket. No elastic waists!

    The same principle could apply to jumpers and cardigans: let them fall to the hips and a bit past rather than hug the waist. A good fit is helpful in the torso. Bulky jackets worn with slim pants may look unbalanced, massive above without much support below; but you'll have to check the mirror to know.

    You could choose shirts and jackets with features that bring more visual mass and attention upward. Have reasonable shoulder pads in sport jackets, and opt for a structured sleeve that turns the corner at the peak of your shoulder rather than a slouch shoulder. Roped shoulder will enhance the shoulder even more. Shirts should also have the shoulder fitted to the peak of your shoulder; if the yoke is too narrow, the shoulder seam will fall on the top of your shoulder rather than the outer crest, and the shirt will have a slouch to it. If your style and the occasion warrant it, epaulets on both shirts and jackets can help bring the visual weight upward. There are many other strategies toward this end, including bright shirts, eye-catching ties and scarves, peaked lapels, and even wide-brimmed hats.

    (Let me just note that a bright shirt exposed within the vee of a jacket gorge can bring the attention upward to the face and give a sense of lightness; but when wearing shirts without a jacket more muted tones are recommended to downplay weight in the body. Vertical stripes and, if you can find them, diagonals that converge at the waist are your friends.)

    Some style consultants recommend a slightly lower rise to the pant for wide hips; but to tell the truth, I haven't seen that work. I think the intent here is to avoid having too great an expanse of fabric at the hips and waist; but a higher waist with a perfect line can actually make a larger backside look attractive — rather than hiding it, you'd be flaunting it. You might find some success experimenting with lower or higher rise in the pants, but this is risky business. Too low a rise can emphasize the belly and encourage it to hang out; and low-rise can in any case just look so wrong past 'that certain age'. And with a higher rise, in addition to adding visual weight to the area, if it doesn't conform to a good shape beneath it could become a 'dad pant'; or worse, a 'grandad pant'.

    If you find that you have to go to larger sizes for pants that fit the hips, the waist can almost always be taken in by a tailor.
