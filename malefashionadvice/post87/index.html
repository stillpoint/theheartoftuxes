    What alternatives are there

    The poor guy is begging for some non-preppy suggestions, and he gets penny loafers and chukkas. Oy!

    It's great that you know yourself well enough not to be overtaken by the style of others. Don't be dressed by the internet! The basics of style still apply, the first of which is fit: see to it that your clothing fits you properly, and you will already have vaulted ahead of the pack.

    Color combination still holds true as well. Most of the time you won't be using as bright colors as in the 'preppy' palette -- those salmon colored chinos, pastel shirts, and madras ties -- but learning your own best colors and how they go together will still be important. In front of a mirror with good (preferably natural) light, hold items of various colors next to your face, and see which ones make you look great, and which ones fall flat. Compare colors to learn specifically which tones work best for you. "This... or this?"

    Here's a good primer on color matching.

    Start putting together your outfits from the shoes up. Engineer/shop boots, biker boots, and hiking boots will all be possibilities for you. The Engineer boots with a rubber sole will likely be most versatile across a number of different activities and locales. On certain occasions, no doubt regular sneakers will be in order.

    Once you have decided your shoes for the day, work upward choosing items that fall within the same style and casual/formal level.

    A good, rugged jacket or coat will serve you well, since it will be what most people see of you most of the time. A leather bomber or field coat, wool barn coat, parka, quilted coat, or canvas military jacket can define your look while being comfortable both in function and style. Don't underestimate the impact of this one piece. A good outer layer will radiate a message about who you are, and will allow you to be simple and laid back with the rest of your attire.

    So shoes and outerwear are the first priorities. Attend closely to fit. Don't avoid the tailor; because even outdoorsy clothing will look much, much better when it fits you well. Learn the colors that suit you, and that go well with each other.

    In terms of fabrics and textures, look at thick cotton bottom layers, whether classic white t-shirts, thick button-down collar oxford shirts, or cotton or wool flannels. Sweaters can be chunkier, with more texture to the weave. Layering with henleys and shawl-collared cardigans will appear more outdoorsy than crisp shirts and thin v-necks. Scarves can also be thicker, with patterned wool or crocheted cotton.

    The look can be more casual; but your standards can still be high. Certain items can be purchased at lower prices -- your t-shirts and henleys -- but others should be bought with a discriminating eye. Something that fits well, works for you, lasts a long time, and looks great will be a better value than three or four sub-par items.

    And as far as jeans go, dark wash will be a bit more formal and a bit more versatile with a range of outfits in the city; but you don't have to shy away from lighter washes and a bit of wear -- just don't go overboard (avoid the light blue '80s look).

    In fall and winter, corduroy pants go well with the outdoorsy look as well.

    Check out online catalogues like Eddie Bauer and Land's End. Ralph Lauren will have, among the preppy, examples of higher-style outdoorsy outfits; in particular, go there if you want to see how to use plaid well. Another source for ideas is movies, either on screen or online. Check out what the guys are wearing in action/adventure/nature movies; their outfits are given a lot of thought by the costume designers.

    Consider how items will go together; and don't just buy a single piece hoping it will create the look for you. Article here.
