    Though this discussion is beyond the scope of both this thread and this subreddit, understanding the point here could possibly prevent injury or, if one loses the ability to walk, death.

    You note that the waterproof boots should be loose fitting, and also that the liners get dried out by the fire. This shows that you understand the importance in extreme cold of releasing moisture-laden vapor and dealing with the buildup of water within the boots. Depending on the conditions and level of exertion (and sweating), there are various systems for dealing with the issue.

    Full-waterproof boots with removable liners are sometimes used. It's assumed (and important) that the liners be pulled out and dried at the end of the day. If moisture buildup becomes too high, either due to exertion or because snow, slush, or water has made its way into the boot, the lack of an option to release the moisture becomes a danger. Some people bring an extra set of liners, stowed in a waterproof bag, for this reason. Of course, you need a way to keep your feet warm while boots and liners are being dried.

    Many expeditionists use pac boots, with waterproof bottoms and breathable tops (often including gaiters to keep snow out). This is an attempt to keep some of the benefits of waterproofing with some of the venting ability of permeable materials. There are still moisture release issues with the waterproof foot. An extra set of liners is recommended here as well.

    Vapor barrier systems are also used, where a thin liner sock collects moisture next to the foot, and stays wet. This is supposed to signal the feet to stop sweating. A barrier outside the liner keeps moisture from invading and compromising outer insulating layers of wool socks and wool boot liners. If the outer layers get wet, it's a problem; and keeping moisture next to the feet has its disadvantages in terms of skin health.

    Mountaineering boots tend to use a combination of waterproofing, water-resistance, and ventilation, plus removable inner boots.

    Modern variations on traditional mukluks have synthetic soles with leather uppers, fabric gaiters, and criss-crossing ties to keep snow out. These are eminently breathable. These types of mukluks, as well as permeable desert boots, can be used with multiple wool socks in a system that will constantly release moisture even when soaked. They tend to be preferred for high-exertion expeditions involving snowshoeing.

    Boreal forest survival expert Mors Kochanski recommends 3 pairs of socks (and no boots!) in emergency situations where the feet have been soaked. The wool layers insulate well and allow moisture to be transported away from the feet, with snow absorbing much of the water; and the outer layer freezes and forms a flexible boot. The ability to move the foot easily enhances circulation, a further benefit in the cold.

    Just saying that for those expecting to keep their boots on for several hours at a time in extreme cold, knowledge about moisture management is critical.
