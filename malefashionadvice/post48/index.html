    Is there something wrong with wearing black?

    Many people seem to think that 'black goes with everything'; but in practical wardrobe-related reality, black is not really a neutral. It's a whole color unto itself. It's very intense, the most concentrated color; and it makes other colors look more intense. And the depth that one may seek to project with black attire is easily perceived as dour, unimaginative, depressive.

    Unless you've well established to others your facility in putting together an outfit, one that's dominated by black (easy enough to do with even a single item) will tend to appear as if you just don't know how to use color. It has something of a 'dumbed-down' quality. Yes, black has associations with profundity and intensity -- the brooding artist, the simmering club-crawler. It has associations with wisdom and power -- the priest, the wizard, the zen monk. And since black by definition can provide the greatest contrast with lighter shades, it can be used to project an image of decisiveness and sharpness -- the 'slick' look of '80s New Wave, for instance, where the world was black and white.

    These intense, all or nothing qualities are also what make black easily appear immature. To someone who's lived a while and negotiated a wide range of experience, the world is not just black and white; and in terms of attire, the mature person will have more subtlety at his command than merely 'dark' or 'light'.

    Used consciously, according to one's situation and attitude, black can be suitable and good looking. Principles to investigate include contrast, and tonal and textural variation. Here is an example of contrast that, I think, works. Dark top, light bottom. But if the pants were stark white, that would usually be too much (a '70s/'80s look: think tropical resort or disco). When top and bottom are both dark, a belt can provide the defining contrast. |Belt as contrast 2. Separating contrast can also be achieved with layers (but in this case, the button-up will have to remain open so the t-shirt will show; and that has its own problems). This is too much contrast. The black shirt floats on the outfit by itself, without relationship to the other pieces.

    All black, without contrast or tonal variation is very often associated with extreme self-concern, trying too hard, immaturity (eg, the inability to use tone and color either in dress or in relationship), and 'douchbaggery'. Of course, there will be a portion of the population who respond to and admire those qualities.
