    In addition to the great reflections already in this thread, I'd like to add my list of top style tools:

    • Buy a full-length mirror, hang it where there is good light and enough room to turn around (preferably near where you dress for the day), and consult it often.

    • You can use your bed to quickly lay out combinations for viewing without having to actually wear and take off each piece -- faster, and therefore easier to experiment with new combos

    • Use a camera with a self-timer and a tripod to take photos of your best and worst outfits, so you can remember what works for you, what doesn't, and why.

    • Consult MFA and other sites, but make the effort to investigate for yourself (you'll never learn well just by taking advice from others -- it has to become your own knowledge through your own experience).

    • In a computer file folder or in a physical scrapbook, create a lookbook of outfits, styles, color combinations, and motifs that interest you; and include examples that perturb you, that you don't (yet) understand, and that stretch your sense of style.

    • Make use of well-dressed friends, especially women. Ask for advice and feedback, possibly even support as a second pair of eyes when shopping. Just don't let them have the last word on what you should be wearing.

    • Get a tailor to measure you, and carry a wallet card with your measurements on it, or memorize them. Include your ideal measurements for different styles of clothes. For example, you may prefer to have absolutely no break on summer chinos, half or full break on dress pants, and extra length on jeans for stacking.

    • Spend an afternoon determining your best colors -- the colors that make you look healthy and vibrant. Also learn your worst colors, the ones that make you disappear either by overpowering your skin tone or by softening it into dullness. Try as many colors as possible right next to your face, and take photos or ask a friend for feedback. Your clothes should not be the main thing that people see; they should bring you to the forefront.

    • If you find it useful as a shopping tool, you can also create a swatch book of your best and worst colors, using pieces of fabric or paint chips on a keychain. Some people also find a color wheel useful, along with some knowledge of color theory.

    At the root of it, training yourself in style and fashion is part knowledge of design and cultural history and part tantra -- engaging life through the senses.

    On the knowledge side, broaden your horizons and learn what others enjoy about the looks you're leery of. Make the connection between what's going on in fashion and what's going on in the cultural era and current events. Music, art, writing, and politics will all have connections with fashion. Especially track clothes associated with pop phenomena like hit movies and tv shows -- guaranteed, you'll start seeing those pieces in the stores in coming seasons.

    On the sensing side, enjoy more of life, through your eyes and ears and nose and tongue and body. Fine wine or plain apple juice: taste it deeply. The texture of rags or the sheen of an haute couture dress: breathe it in, and enjoy it for what it is. Don't bow to preferences. Don't just love particular colors; love the fact of color. Don't just hope for particular sounds; love sound itself.

    Edit: I'm revealing a personal secret here as one last tip that can bring even experienced MFAers to the next level: When studying an outfit, picture it (at first) as pure outlines on a blank background. Eliminate color, texture, and dimension and just concentrate on the shapes formed, by tracing thin imaginary lines around the outer profile, collars, pockets, zippers, openings, folds, and other features. This is one of the best ways I know for seeing both overall proportion and internal relationship between shapes in an outfit. It's similar to becoming aware of internal rhyme and rhythm in poetry -- a whole other level.
