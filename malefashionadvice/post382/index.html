    What are some small, cheap fixes that make a big difference?

    Others can suggest items to buy that may be a good start; but to help yourself in the long run you have to know how to buy and how to wear clothes. Just owning a certain item doesn't give you style.

    The following two "fixes" are very important and influential to your appearance:

    (1) Fit

    • Learn proper fit.
    • Buy items only in your size.
    • Forego items that don't fit you, no matter how much you wish you could wear them.
    • Consider buying slim-cut pieces rather than more voluminous 'classic' cuts.
    • Spend the extra few dollars to have your clothes tailored to a better fit.
    • Improve your existing wardrobe by getting rid of clothes that are not in your size and that can't be tailored to a good fit.
    • Establish a relationship with a good tailor (not a seamstress or cleaning-shop alterationist), and learn what kinds of alterations can or can't be performed.
    • Learn when to give up on a garment. Some items are lost causes — a tailor could do work on them, but it will still not make them fit well enough to look good.

    (A) A style that suits you

    • Understand yourself and your lifestyle.
    • Do an overview of your most common and most important activities, to understand the types of clothing that will support your actual life best.
    • Consider and acknowledge your own personal qualities — the ones that are already at the forefront, and the ones that you would like to bring out more. Express these qualities in proper measure through the clothes you buy and wear, and in the way you wear them.
    • While being 'on trend' can be important (especially to teenagers looking to fit in to peer groups), avoid putting trends ahead of integrity. Your style of attire should unfold from who you are.
    • Guys in their teens and 20s are, and should be, exploring many possibilities (in lifestyles, values, activities, vocation); and their style can also change to reflect those movements. To make this more financially viable, build a core wardrobe of basic, classic pieces that can combine easily in many different looks, and can be added to or adapted over time.

    After these two points, learning the basic elements of style and cultivating your sensibilities will help across the board:

    • Learn color and pattern combining
    • Learn how to use contrast
    • Learn the difference between well made and poorly made clothes
    • Develop your 'eye' and stylistic sensibility (not only as applies to attire, but engaging with all aspects of culture: design, music, cuisine, movement, ettiquette, etc.) Engage with interest, and study many examples of good and bad style choices (in magazines, online images, in movies and tv shoes, and out on the street).
    • Besides fit, color, pattern, and contrast, learn the other elements of style (material, texture, cut, line, drape and movement, cultural and historical reference, etc.)
