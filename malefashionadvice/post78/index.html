    Thanks for being specific. I was responding to OP's question asking about the limitations, so the answer was weighted toward the limitations. I did say that the important point is to understand the properties of a color or design element, to know how it can be used and how it can't, where it belongs and where it doesn't.

    I'm happy to talk about what's great about black and how it can be used well.

    I've already provided a couple of examples in my previous response to MAJhacks, including David Beckham's very casual outfit; so, yes, black can be worn casually too; but its intensity can't be escaped, only modified or used for effect.

    Black's intensity requires a similar intensity of personal style, attitude. In general terms, the more black used, the more it has to be backed up by the wearer's character or presence. For those who can live up to that requirement, it can be a very bold look; for those who can't, it falls flat and can look ridiculous or "douchey". Here's an outfit from the Ralph Lauren collection, of all sources.

    The sharp, defining contrast that black provides does find its wheelhouse in sharp looks, which often translates to more formal but not necessarily. It is used here with white to create an uncompromising image. In this use of black and white, though, the starkness is completely at odds with the casual style of both the attire and the situation.

    That stark contrast is similarly present with bright and light colors, bringing sharp intensity to the sharp looks or greatly emphasizing the starkness of casual ones. That level of intense color contrast tends to be more accepted in women's mainstream fashion than men's, where it finds its place mostly in active wear.

    If stark color contrast is not an option, then outfits featuring black will rely more on line, pattern, and texture to create interest and avoid a monolithic, one-note appearance:

    • This man's all-black outfit is all about the line, the profile created by the way the fabric is cut and the way it hangs on the body.

    • A great deal of the interest in this outfit derives from the elegant interplay of pattern.

    • This otherwise utterly boring outfit is redeemed somewhat by the inclusion of pattern.

    • Although these outfits are charcoal grey monotone rather than true black, the use of texture to create interest still applies.

    You have to understand the properties of the color, and see for yourself how it looks matched with other pieces, and especially how it looks on you. Compare these two simple outfits that use black:

    • In the first, George Clooney is looking great — sharp, vibrant. The outfit and his own presence are unified, and the focus is on him and his face.

    • In the second, George is washed out and looking like an old man. The man and the outfit are disconnected, and the focus is on the huge mass of black rather than on the person wearing it.
