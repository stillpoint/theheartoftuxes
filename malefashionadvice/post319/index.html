    Any "tuxedo etiquette" I should know about before prom on Saturday?

    Yeah, that's a tough one for a lot of us. First of all, practice makes perfect, and the more you familiarize yourself with formal wear and proper posture the more comfortable you'll be down the road. Actually, hands in pockets, arms crossed, hands on hips, and many other postures are fine if you don't get stuck there. Keep it moving organically.

    It could even be worthwhile to try out postures, some of which may be new to you. What postures are relaxed without slumping? Arm over the back of the chair... arm around your date (with permission).... Hiding your hands means hiding the part of you that interacts, shapes the environment, and reaches out. In the context of a confident attitude, this can be a coy or cool look; in the context of an uptight attitude it looks passive or defeated. (And either way, it disrupts the elegant lines of a suit or tuxedo.) If you can't get away from hands in the pockets, then instead of both of them hidden away in a posture of powerlessness, try just one, and let the other one move.

    One good remedy is to keep engaging outward instead of thinking of how you look. When your energy and attention are moving out and engaging with the situation and with your companions you will be more natural. This can also involve using your hands and body more, in general. Holding a glass of punch, using hands expressively when talking, or making more physical contact with others are some ways you can engage with the hands.

    Here's a trick that's good for both body posture and mind posture (attitude): whether standing or sitting, open your palms slightly outward. If you're standing, this means that instead of your palms facing toward your legs or slightly backward when you stand, the palms rotate out maybe 10º so that they begin to be open to what you are facing. If you're sitting, this means that instead of your palms facing down they are facing slightly or completely up.

    The effect on the posture is that it sends a subtle message to your body to rotate the shoulders back, lift the head, bring the chin slightly inward, and move the chest up and forward. You can do this consciously, but you might also find it happening automatically — you may tend to be more upright when the palms are rotated out.

    Your mind also tends to be more upright when your posture becomes upright. When you are still and hunched you become smaller and more afraid, and when you lift yourself up and move you become more confident and natural. (One reason why you should take every opportunity to dance or move, even if it's just enjoying some back-and-forth movement.)

    When you are talking with someone you can open your palms to them slightly, bringing yourself to greater openness in general. "I'm here with you and I'm attending to what you're saying." This "I am here" is very strong, and it can also be comfortable. When the intention to be more present and open comes from you it naturally builds confidence. And your presence is a gift offered to others.

    If you are present and open with someone you like, it creates a bond. If you present and open with someone you don't like or someone who threatens you, it makes you more attentive and self-possessed, more able to take appropriate action like speaking up, steering the conversation, or moving away. It is fascinating how the palms turning out and the chest and head rising can bring forward both strength and care.

    A sincere smile has similar effect.

    The best posture is the shape of a true person — self-possessed, present, ready to respond.
