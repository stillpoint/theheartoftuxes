    Here are a couple of tips I wrote on other threads:

    Top advice: get real about who you are, what you do, and what you want or intend to do. Your clothing should support you in being your best self. Function, appropriateness, comfort, and expression should all be taken into account. An overview of your most frequent and important activities will provide the context and guidelines for all of your purchases, and will help you be most efficient with your money. 'Frequent activities' means what do you do most of the time, and with whom: obviously, going to class is going to be high on the list. 'Important activities' may range from going on dates to interviewing for university scholarships or jobs -- you may not do the activity frequently, but its upshot could impact your future.

    Absolutely vital to your process is a full-length mirror. Also very helpful will be a clear bed or large table, and a friend who knows what looks good, and usually that means a girl.

    You're trying to determine what items of clothing will suit you (fitting your body and aligning with your persona), will support your most frequent and most important activities, and will be most versatile in pairing with other pieces and dressing up (more formal) or dressing down (more casual). The bed is for laying items together to get an idea of what goes with what -- how many outfits can a single piece create? By laying shirts and pants together on the bed, you can quickly try new ideas; and you can also quickly discover the pieces that don't help you out much. The mirror is for checking whether an outfit in fact looks good on you. This is a vital step. The friend is for offering suggestions, telling you honestly what looks great and what looks like crap on you, and encouraging you to step up your game. Don't settle for merely 'okay', unless that's the life you're looking forward to.

    Rather than risk tossing out clothes that you may later find you actually want or need, shunt the ones you're thinking of getting rid of to a sealable bin or clothing bag (XXL ziplocs are available for clothing storage). Store the bin or bags out of sight for a year; and if you ever find that you need one of those pieces, you can retrieve it. This can actually help you clear out more of your old clothing, since it makes it safe to be ruthless about purging the wardrobe. (The reason you store the clothes for a year is so you can know that seasonal requirements aren't an issue -- if you throw out the bathing suit at Christmas, you may miss it once summer arrives.)

    I agree not to do a massive purchase all at once, but to take your time and see what you need through the course of living your life. You don't necessarily need a lot of clothes if they are versatile. (For the past week, I've been completely comfortable with a single pair of chinos, a white t-shirt, and sneakers. I'd just change the look day to day by perhaps adding a collared shirt, or a scarf, or sport jacket or windbreaker.) Versatility in your wardrobe means you'll first be grabbing a few basics, and then expanding with other items expressive of your own style. Be open to suggestions, but don't let anyone else tell you what your 'basics' should be; determine that from the context of your own life.
