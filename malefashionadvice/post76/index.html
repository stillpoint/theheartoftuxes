    Is that outfit okay for a ''blazer friday'' at school?

    Ugh! No, man; don't untuck. You have a good start there. Don't undo it.

    The untucked shirt is the youngsters' last gasp, their rebellion against a formal structure that is seen as an imposition. I know, I did the same when I was a teenager. I didn't want to be told what to wear or how to wear it; and I really wanted to be thought of as easy-going and confident. Unfortunately, the untucked shirt is not the way to do that in this situation.

    The rules of dress are thought to be burdensome, forced upon you by the powers that be, and so you try to show that your free will is still intact and that the rules don't hold sway over you. But that act of resistance marks you as a youngster digging in his heels, resisting being dragged into more mature forms of dressing and relating.

    You can turn it around, though. You can be the one in the driver's seat, by embracing the form and displaying your comfort within it. Your individuality can be expressed through your skillful use of the rules; but more importantly, the rules (and the seriousness of the outfit) can fade into the background once you embrace them. It's not a big deal.

    If you resist (halfheartedly at that) by untucking the shirt or leaving some part of the outfit messy, then you just bring attention to your discomfort with the rules of sharp dressing. Young guys think that they look carefree when they dress sloppily; but they just look sloppy. It's pretty obvious that they're concerned enough to make an overt statement with their clothing — one that is supposed to protest or rebel against formality. That's how young guys all end up looking the same: in an attempt to look carefree they shine a spotlight on their nervousness.

    But once you embrace the form, you can let the outfit just be the backdrop for you — you are the center, not the outfit. The more comfortable you become in the form, the more freely you can express yourself.

    This is the secret to your freedom in life. Instead of resisting, become flexible and masterful — try to gain mastery of every form so that you can be comfortable within it. On blazer day, do "blazer" magnificently. When it's casual time, do "casual" wholeheartedly. When you visit a new culture, blend in and enjoy. When you're with new people, let go of your opinion and act together with them. Then no situation can defeat you; you will be able to go anywhere freely. If you only try to hold your own way, you will always need your own special situation in order to feel comfortable.

    ~

    In terms of the outfit: It's very hard to see the color, lines, and fit of the jacket and pants.

    I like the choice of a colorful shirt, and if the blazer is blue the green of the shirt should be nice with it. The blue-grey foulard tie is also fine, and looks like it might actually be tied to the proper length (the point should end at the top of your waistband or just within the belt buckle).

    The shirt should be tucked in and the jacket should be buttoned whenever you are standing. Unbutton it when you sit down. I can't see how many buttons the blazer has; but if there's more than one, the bottom button is always left unbuttoned.

    Are those jeans you're wearing? Sharper pants are in order, to match the upper part of the outfit. Wearing jeans would require the upper elements to be more casual: maybe a button-down collar oxford shirt and knit tie, for instance.

    Keep your hands out of your pockets. I know this may be a complete turn-around from how you feel, but try out what it's like to stand with confidence — not having to hide or shrug or weaken parts of your outfit; just standing as if you own that spot. This is as much a part of style as the clothes you wear. Wear your clothes and your body proudly. You don't have to act up or put on airs, but do practice "upright and comfortable". If you stand as if you believe in yourself, then you will start to believe in yourself, and others will too.

    In the future, I'd like to see a slightly narrower tie, and perhaps even a knit tie, for its more jaunty and youthful look.
