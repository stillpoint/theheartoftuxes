    Suit or no suit to casual interview

    There's the saying that it's "better to be overdressed than underdressed", but let's be reasonable and follow the situation. It's just not clear how reliable is the advice of your friends in the company. "Whatever you do, don't wear a suit": is that the final word, or is that just another opinion. It's not clear what "differently-thinking" means; and some progressive companies are now moving (with the popular trend) away from sneakers and jeans toward a sharper dress code.

    Have you received, or can you receive, any guidelines from the HR department on what kind of dress is expected at the interview?

    If you are sure that business casual is in order, remember that the central aim is to be presentable rather than stylish. If you were vying for a creative position like graphic designer, you'd be expected to show some flair, but for most other jobs let the focus be on a neat, clean, cohesive outfit â€” no aberrations. (And please, no vest and matchy-matchy shirt!)

    'Business casual' is just a shade more casual than 'professional'. From the bottom up:

    • Either dress shoes or sharp-casual shoes like wingtips. (Although loafers may suit the look, the association with loafing around is to be avoided in a job interview.)
    • Dark neutral dress pants or sharp dress chinos. Jeans are avoided except when it has been communicated that they are acceptable, and only with the sharpest dark wash in combination with a sharp outfit.
    • Colored or minimally patterned dress shirt. No loud patterns like plaid; but stripes, windowpane, tattersall, and gingham are possible. Solid muted color is also fine.
    • Merino or cashmere v-neck or cardigan sweater, and/or...
    • Not-too-loud sport jacket.
    • Tie is usually optional, but in this case it sounds like you may want to forego the tie.

    A suit can also be 'business casual', if it is casual in its features (like patch pockets) and fit, and worn with a more casual shirt and without a tie. If you're comfortable wearing suits, it's an option.

    It may be a good strategy to wear a sport jacket. Then you're sharp and presentable at first glance, but can take it off the moment you get an indication that the interview is at a more casual level.

    At the sharp end of business casual, you can do a black-based, tailored outfit with more expensive pieces. At the casual end, you can use more earth tones and color, with more casual pieces. Lighter pants also make the look more casual.

    It sounds like you'd enjoy the help of a personal shopper or style consultant. Nordstroms in San Francisco is a good place to shop for business casual items, and they provide personal shopper services for free. You can also do a search for style and image consultants in your area if you want to drop some money on it.

    Again, please don't wear a separate vest. Not only is it an awkward style move, it is too far-reaching for an interview.
