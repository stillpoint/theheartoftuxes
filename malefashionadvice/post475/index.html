    MFA, how do you battle sweat?

    • Many close-weave dress shirts will encourage sweat because, I surmise, they don't breathe well. Cheap, no-iron shirts are particularly implicated in this. You may notice a big difference in sweat-inducing properties between the shirts that are crisp and stiff and the ones that are softer: the softer ones, even if they are thicker, tend to manage the body's moisture more comfortably. Oxfords, poplins, peached fabrics.... You may need to experiment with fabrics; but once you hit on the right one, seek it out and gradually switch out your wardrobe.

    • Your physiological constitution and fitness will have a lot to do with regulation of sweat. Even some very fit men sweat a lot; but when it comes to spontaneously bursting into sweat at a very cool 60ยบ (12ยบ below normal room temperature!), you may need to take a serious look at your health.

    • In Traditional Chinese Medicine, it is said that the lungs govern the external protective energy that controls perspiration. If that external layer is compromised, or if the lungs are weak, moisture can leak out of the body irregularly. This is why we sometimes sweat quite a bit even in cold situations: there's no further need to cool the body, but the cold has drained the external protective energy and sweat starts to pour out. If it's a fitness issue, a proper exercise regimen is necessary. If it's a constitutional issue, the right herbal medicine and/or acupuncture can do wonders. Cinnamon twig is one common herb that is often prescribed in remedies for excessive sweating. See a good Traditional Chinese Doctor.

    • A thin wool or technical synthetic undershirt can help in both hot and cold situations, for different reasons. In the heat, it can help to wick moisture away from the body where it can evaporate, before it collects in wet pools. In the cold, the shirt helps preserve the microclimate around the body, preventing the clammy sweat from being triggered. Merino wool underwear is the best base layer known; no man-made technology as yet has approached what nature has made in terms of insulative and wicking properties; and the ability of wool to provide comfort across a wide range of temperatures is nearly miraculous. Specialized synthetic (usually polyester) athletic base layers can wick as well, if you don't need quite the same temperature-adaptive properties.

    • If you know you'll be sweating, avoid the colors that show it the most: the medium shades, especially medium grey, but also including most chambrays and other shirts in the midrange of colors.
