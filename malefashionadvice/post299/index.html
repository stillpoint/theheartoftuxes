    Is this a good business casual outfit?

    The belt may be brown; but it looks black in the photo and may read as black in person. So, yes, swap it out for one closer to the shoe color.

    The pants are a light neutral solid; the shoes are mid-brown: It would be hard to find a shirt of any color or pattern that would clash; and light blue solid is especially safe. In fact, it's so safe that you have achieved the typical business casual uniform: solid blue button-down, solid tan or khaki pants, brown shoes. I'd like to see more interest.

    To achieve that greater interest you could, first of all, change either pants or shirt to a darker value so the outfit as a whole would have more contrast. A slightly darker pant might even bridge the current tonal gap between shoes and belt; but you'd have to check the mirror for that one. With more contrast between top and bottom pieces, the viewer sees relationships (and thus movement) between the elements of the outfit. As it stands, what the viewer sees is a bland field in a single tonal value.

    You could also let go of playing it so safe with color, and try a shirt outside of the mid-to-light blue range. Almost any color would go with the neutral pants; so base your decision on what is most flattering to your skin tone. Lavender, rust, greens, buff... there are many options. More movement and interest can also be attained with a pattern in the shirt or pants, and with the addition of accessories. In a business casual situation, pretty much the only accessories that are acceptable are the watch and the messenger bag/briefcase. A sharp watch could bump up the outfit. In an otherwise safe outfit, contrasting or patterned socks can also be expressive of one's willingness to say something definite. (But if you're going to go 'out there' with any of your pieces, let the rest of the outfit remain calm, or it could appear too loud or flaky.)

    What constitutes 'too casual' depends upon the culture at your workplace; but it's better to aim higher than be perceived as a slouch. Adding the right sport jacket would both add interest to the look and spiff you up as well -- you wouldn't fade into the background; and if your attire does register on the radar, it will be noted for its sharpness rather than for being too casual. Since you are checking on MFA, it seems that you want to take your public image seriously; so a jacket would be in order, in my opinion. If you stay with basic solids for the rest of the outfit, seek out a pattern or texture in the jacket -- glen plaid, subtle windowpane, herringbone, houndstooth, marled, etc.

    And if you're in doubt, you may want to go for a slightly more dressy pant. It doesn't have to be a 'dress pant' per se; but you may want to save the wrinkle-showing chinos for occasions that are a step down from business casual. You can find many chino-like slacks in fabrics that have more drape or resist wrinkling, and many of those may be available in patterns or subtle weave patterns such as herringbone or striped weave fabric. Dockers offers what they call a herringbone (really a variation of corduroy) as well as other patterned weaves.

    You already know that the shirt should be taken in to make it slimmer, or replaced with a more fitted one. Under a jacket, you could try opening another button at the neck. I'd prefer it; but you have to decide within the context of the tacit company dress code whether that's an option.

    Attention to those details can change how others perceive you. It may not appear as a conscious thought; but in the split-second, unconscious way people categorize each other, it could make the difference between being pegged as someone willing to fade into the scenery and being seen as someone of substance.

    Edit: I completely neglected to mention the wide range of alternatives available with sweaters, either worn by themselves or under a sport jacket. V-necks and cardigans, in both button and zip version, can add an element of interest when worn over a basic outfit. The variety of textures, colors, and patterns in both color and weave, is vast indeed.
