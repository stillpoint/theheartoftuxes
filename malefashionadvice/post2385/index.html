    23 year old College Senior trying to slowly, cheaply upgrade my wardrobe

    Hi; good luck in evolving your look. When modeling clothing for assessment here, roll down sleeves and stand with your hands relaxed at your sides so we can see how everything sits on your body and fits. (Also button any sportcoats or suit jackets.)

    My critique:

    • Two moves will make you look 100% better: focusing on the fit of your clothing, and getting a shorter haircut. The hair doesn't necessarily have to be cropped short, although that could also look great; but having it hanging straight down isn't doing you any favors. Consult a good stylist (may require $$), who should be willing to sit down with you and discuss the kinds of styles that would look good on you. You could also start a separate thread on Reddit asking for examples.

    • The t-shirt is too big in the body, and the sleeves are far too long. Check the How Clothes Should Fit guide in the sidebar. The sleeves of any short-sleeved shirt should come no lower than the middle of the bicep, and no higher than the top of the bicep/bottom of the deltoid.

    • Black is a bad color for you, for several reasons. First, it is not flattering for your skin color; it is too intense and makes you look pale. Second, it is both dull and a difficult color to match -- it creates the greatest contrast of any color, often intensifying bright colors too much, creates a morose look with many darks, and itself has a dour and often boring quality. Third, it looks quite bad with light jeans as well as many other shades of blue jeans.

    • You can do much, much better with jeans. As suggested by others, a dark wash, straight-leg jean in a slimmer cut would be much better. Although that's the general recommendation, if you don't want to do something quite as sharp as a dark wash, at least let it be a medium wash or dark with fade. The light jeans hearken back to the '80s, and the vibe they call up is immature and tacky. Only perhaps on a sunny day in California, and in the context of a very sunny-Cali outfit, might jeans that faded be appropriate.

    • The long-sleeve t-shirt fits well in the body. We obviously can't see the sleeve length; but if the sleeves don't fit, you should still be able to find a t that does fit in both body and arms. The gray color is slightly better than black, and may combine better with other pieces; but so far it is also a bit dour and boring. I'd bet you would look good in a henley, in a medium neutral (medium grey or brown) or an actual color.

    • Wow, you like your black and gray. The button-up shirt is not a bad fit, but I'd like to see it tailored to come in closer to the body. Again, because of the rolled sleeves and posture we can see neither the width nor length of sleeve nor the way the shirt sits on your body, nor the overall fit. The color is again dull and a bit depressing; but now at least you have some movement, some energy with the check pattern. In combination with some color in your pants (light brown chinos or wine cords, for example) it could look good; but check how the colors look against your face. My sense is that black and dark grey are making you appear sallow and lifeless rather than happy and vibrant.

    If you want to gradually upgrade your wardrobe, you should have an overview and plan. Your wardrobe is an expression that comes from your inner life; it should be an extension of you and should serve your purpose, not be some idea you try to cram yourself into. So you should list the different types of activities you engage in and the kinds of clothing you will need for them (school, work, evenings out, hanging with friends, active wear, 'about town', etc....). Consider who you are and who you want to be. What values are important to you, and what do you want your clothing to do for you? This will give you a good direction for planning your wardrobe. Then you have to fill it in with the regard to color, pattern, cut, style, and especially versatility in combining among pieces in your wardrobe.
