    What makes sense to get tailored or not tailored?

    • Are the pieces magnificent, aside from not fitting well?

    • Do they combine with your wardrobe in a versatile and useful way?

    • Do they represent your personal style that is current and alive, rather than just going along with the same old look from last decade? (Not that there's something wrong with that, if it is still current and alive for you.)

    • They may be special pieces of clothing in themselves, but much more importantly, do they make you look great?

    These are the pieces I would bring to a tailor first. Bring them with the intent to figure out what is worth tailoring; don't just decide ahead of time, but be open to the tailor's advice. (Some tailors are more able to give advice than others, and some will be more in tune with contemporary looks than others as well.)

    Learn the alterations that can and can't be performed on a piece of clothing, so that you can understand why some pieces will look good after tailoring, while other pieces will never achieve a good overall fit.

    If your t-shirts have side seams and aren't knit in one continuous form around the body, then they should be easy to alter. In fact, they can usually be brought to a cheaper alterationist like you might find at your local cleaners, or to a seamstress. If you are handy with a sewing machine, you can even take in your own t-shirts and polos (search in this subreddit as well as in /r/sewing).

    Jeans can be difficult, for a few reasons:

    • Their thicker denim fabric makes folded seams more difficult to sew, sometimes breaking standard sewing machine needles.
    • The legs are cut in a curve, and the back panel of the leg is wider than the front; so some delicate balance is necessary, and sometimes both leg seams have to be opened in order to narrow the leg while keeping it hanging the same way.
    • Double seams as seen on more rugged jeans will be more difficult to reproduce.

    This doesn't mean that it's impossible to tailor jeans; but some tailors will shy away from or refuse the job. If you're in the NY area, DenimTherapy does all kinds of tailoring and repairs on jeans. A search in this subreddit should turn up other similar services.

    Whatever you choose, whether it's to tailor none, or some, or all of your clothes, it is recommended to establish a good ongoing relationship with a good tailor. Not only will you have an important sartorial resource, you also stand to learn a lot about clothing. It is expected that you will talk with your tailor or any alterationist, to explain your vision and to take in their advice; both sides of the conversation are necessary.

    And it is also expected that you will bring back any items that don't fit perfectly, to get them readjusted or to get an explanation on why the adjustment can't be made. You need to bring the items back in good time, though; especially when the seasons change, a considerable amount of weight can be gained or lost over the course of a few weeks. But any so-called tailor that refuses to adjust his work is a scam artist; even a master tailor shouldn't assume that the first fitting will always be perfect.

    Finally, second and third opinions never hurt. I have gone through several tailors before finding the right group for me. (And I've experienced my fair share of disasters with overly ambitious tailors or outright swindlers.) I currently use two wonderful tailors, a very skillful seamstress, and my local cleaner's alterationist, each for very specific projects. If after visiting a tailor I am still in doubt about getting an alteration done, I'll take it to a different professional for a second opinion.
