    blazer fit questions - too much buckling? one-vent weirdness?

    > I'm a lady, but not very curvy, so I typically fit into mens clothes fine.

    You ask if either of the blazers is okay. The question is: okay for what purpose? Men's blazers are constructed very differently from women's, emphasizing the broadness and bulk of the upper torso over the (women's) inward curve of the waist. The proportions between body parts -- such as the relative distances between hips, waist, and shoulders -- are also quite different. The issue here is not whether you can fit into men's clothes, but how you will look and how you want to look.

    The two blazers fit you fine, but flare a bit at the hips. This can be given a 'better' form by going with it and taking in the waist; or it can be covered up with a different jacket, made of a more flexible fabric that doesn't require a structured cut. The present jackets are long in the arm: shirt cuffs should reach just past the wrist to the base of the thumb; jacket arms should be short enough to reveal 1/4" to 1/2" of cuff. (Yes, cut open the vents and pockets.)

    By your post in MFA and your name, can we assume that you want to de-emphasize feminine qualities and hide what curves you do have? If that's not the case, then look to women's clothing that takes cues from menswear. If it is the case, look into whether you want to present yourself as a man or as a woman, because it will make a difference as to how you relate to yourself and others.

    Going in drag with a complete man's outfit will stand out, both for the incongruous nature of the outfit as well as its incongruous fit. The fit will be a bit off because of the difference in your body's proportions; but if that doesn't bother you, you're all set with what you have.

    If you just want to borrow some pieces from menswear and have them work in an otherwise stylish look, the key will be in balancing the look -- choosing one men's piece, but retaining some feminine qualities in the rest of your outfit. For example, a fitted blouse, slim tapered jeans, and strap shoes with a bit of a heel might support a men's blazer if it were tailored close to the waist.

    And a very stylish look is possible with clothing that is made for women's bodies but borrows from men's styling. Even the 'boyfriend blazer' is structured for the female form.

    The tailored extra-slim blazer will fit you better before alteration; and I would guess that it can be altered to a closer fit than the herringbone. The herringbone, on the other hand, will be a more versatile pattern in casual wear.
