    Need help with basic summer attire

    First take stock of your lifestyle and what you might need to support it. What items would be most useful to you? What pieces are missing from your wardrobe? (See the Building A Basic Wardrobe guide.) From there you can decide your priorities, whether the items you'll look for are more practical or whether they will be meant to boost your style (or both).

    Consider especially how things will go together, so you can buy pieces that interchange well and can combine to create many outfits rather than being useful in only a single situation. For the greatest bang for your buck, choose a single color scheme ahead of time (not black and white, and not all-black, please!!). Blues and browns is a good theme for building a core of basics. Depending on your skin and hair color, other possible color schemes might be warm earth tones, bright ice colors, cool blues and greens, and so on. Women's style articles tend to have more articles (and more sophisticated ones) that teach about color schemes; so if you can stand to look at women's clothes in addition to men's you can get some good ideas about combining colors.

    Just taking some time to think and plan will help you start to learn more about clothes. Shopping and getting advice from your mom, salespeople, and well-dressed friends can also help. When shopping, try on lots of pieces (even if you're not sure if they'll look good).

    Know your measurements so that when you're shopping you can quickly eliminate things that won't fit you. And always insist on a proper fit, whether it's perfect off the rack or whether it has to be altered by a professional.

    For better or worse, experiencing whether your purchases serve you well or not will be part of learning. So if possible, it may be most beneficial to spread your purchases out rather than buying it all at once. That way you can take time to consider carefully what else your next purchase should be — what would be most useful and work best with the rest of your wardrobe.

    Other ways to develop your style sense include studying pictures of outfits from online and magazine ads, articles, style blogs, and even movies and tv shows. You can copy or clip pictures of outfits you like and put together a scrapbook or lookbook as you begin to envision your own preferred style.

    You can also try outfits and post them for feedback here on MFA. You don't even necessarily have to even buy the pieces in order to try them. If you buy from stores with a good return policy you can take a whole bunch of items home and see how they mix and match with the pieces you already own or intend to wear; in a similar strategy you can also take the items to a tailor to get his advice before having them altered; then just return all the pieces that aren't fantastic.

    Finally, many stores that sell their own line of clothing will offer a whole lineup of items that all go together. Check the ads in Club Monaco's online store, or see the mannequins in the actual store for ideas. (J Crew is another store like this.) These higher-end stores also tend to have trained salespeople who can help you put together outfits, but in any case almost anything you pick out will go with almost anything else from the same seasonal lineup.

    Also, read and re-read the sidebar guides.

    Happy Shopping!

    (P.S., remember: it's only a bargain if you wear it — and it helps you.)
