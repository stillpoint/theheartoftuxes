    How does this look?

    Very nice; but it's so promising, I'd love to see a change that would make it spectacular.

    PasDeDeux: I'm going to disagree with the shirt criticisms. I think if you had a bold, dark, solid tie, it would bring together the look better. Right now, the tie and shirt are similar brightness and saturation and similar in pattern shape and size, which is too "bland and busy" at the same time.

    Blooster: I won't say that the pattern matching seems off, just that the colors are all very dull in the background of such a bold jacket.

    Agreed. Three-pattern matching is a sophisticated statement these days; and the wide check shirt with pinstripe jacket is a modern look. If we keep the shirt, the tie should change.

    There's room for improvement with the tie. First; the type and scale of pattern in the tie are too similar to the shirt. When mixing patterns, type and scale should vary between all elements. If staying with one motif throughout the pattern, as you are with the thin lines, this rule becomes crucial. A bolder and more filled-in plaid, or a broader stripe would have worked better. But dots or foulard in the tie would have been altogether easier to take. I wouldn't do madder or paisley unless the paisley were a particularly stylized and modern version. Or, let's say only approach that option with your eyes wide open. The antique, bohemian quality of madder and paisley work against the geometric modernity of the shirt.

    Second; as mentioned, the tonal value of shirt and tie are too close, and in tandem are too light for the depth of the jacket. While the pattern mixing, spread collar, and wide knot show that you are trying to make a powerful statement, the paleness of the tie undermines it. Any number of deep or bright colors would have put this outfit over the top (in a good way). Solid, textured solid, or the patterns recommended above would all work well.

    If you changed the shirt, the tie would still be a bit pale with many alternatives; and in most cases the proper color combination (that tie with a pink, lavender, or green shirt) would only make it good enough, not excellent. The tie would rely on the shirt rather than pulling its own weight in the outfit.

    The tie knot may be seen by some as ever so slightly wide for the collar; and by others seen as admirably hiding the loop ends. There's absolutely nothing wrong with the dimple of the tie! To insist on a perfectly symmetrical dimple is, in my opinion, a regimented and boring habit. Sprezzatura is expressed by deviations from rigid alignment. For example, some Italian men at the height of style will shift the tie slightly to one side under the jacket, or allow the narrow tail to peek out. Those anchored to a primitive, pedantic, and rule-bound approach mistake this for sloppiness; but to the sophisticated eye it's one of a thousand ways that playfulness, ease, and self-expression can be employed within the classic theme of the man's suit.

    There will be times when you may want to forego the dimple altogether; for example, when you are putting together a very slick, sleek look with luxurious silk, and shiny suit and shirt fabrics; or when the number of patterns used (!) calls for more structural simplicity. Here's Errol Flynn wearing a tie without a dimple.

    If you perfect the other elements of the look -- color and pattern matching -- the slight gap at the top of the knot can be taken as intentional, as this kind of sprezzatura move. Until then, the epitome will be achieved by holding the knot up at right angles to the shirt while pulling the tie tight to the collar. This will eliminate the gap, and create a nice arch that will be noticeable in profile. The arch can extend even this far, or not quite as much.
