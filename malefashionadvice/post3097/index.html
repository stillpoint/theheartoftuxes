    What can I comfortably wear for 23 hours on a plane and still look somewhat decent (if tired and unshaven) on arrival?

    Meeting new employers and co-workers means you'll need a smart look, even if you will be in a casual setting. Though you'll be arriving in a tropical climate, your long airplane ride and possible the venue where you'll be meeting will be air conditioned; so you'll need layers. Linen and certain weaves of cotton are prone to wrinkling; and 23 hours on a plane plus the activities associated with travel (such as lugging shoulder bags, draping/piling jackets over the arm, etc.) will be a tough test, wrinkle-wise.

    With these criteria in mind, let's start with the shoes and work our way up: Comfortable lace-ups (rubber soles are a compromise here) are versatile for travel, if you are okay wearing them in the heat. More breathability can be gained by going with woven leather. Loafers can also be both comfortable and multi-use; and suede will tend to enhance both comfort on the foot and breathability. You can cover all the bases here with woven suede loafers (but maybe in a better styling than pictured).

    Be aware that in some corporate cultures, wearing loafers can get you pinned as a 'loafer', even in a casual setting. It may be a very old-school prejudice, but it still crops up here and there.

    For pants, lightweight no-iron chinos (Land's End), light cotton blend dress pants (Banana Republic) that include some spandex fiber, and light wool blend dress pants (Banana Republic) are all comfortable, versatile, wrinkle-resistant options. Don't make the mistake of thinking wool can't be worn in the heat. Not only does wool wick moisture far better than cotton, it is amazingly adaptable to a wide range of temperatures, and can be twisted into very fine strands for light, airy weaves of fabric. A few people can't stand the feel of wool in the heat, while others swear by it for its versatility.

    Shirt can be your choice of button-down collared long-sleeve or polo short-sleeve. Oxford cloth shirts will look better than other button-downs once wrinkles appear, but the thickness of the cloth might be undesirable in tropical heat. No-iron button downs seem like the obvious alternative, but often the no-iron quality is derived with a fine weave that, even if thin, renders the shirt less breathable. Polos in piqué cotton have the advantages of short sleeves and open collar for the heat, and the knits will usually wrinkle less than weaves; but the thick cotton can soak up a lot of perspiration. (Avoid middle shades, especially middle grey, as armpit perspiration will show up as dark spots.) If you can find polos in a synthetic blend, go for it. Another perhaps risky option is a v-neck t-shirt in a moisture-wicking synthetic (JM Skinz). This is to be worn under a very thin summer-weight v-neck or cardigan, which can be rolled up at the arms but not taken off, since you won't want to meet your new boss in a t-shirt.

    The top layer can be a cotton, cotton blend, or summer-weight wool sportcoat, or a light cardigan. If opting for a cotton jacket, you should be confident that it is of a construction and density that will be generous under wrinkling influences. There are some great blends available in sweaters and cardigans, such as cotton/silk/cashmere, that are thin and breathable, resist wrinkling, and offer insulation against air conditioning or cool winds. If the heat is too much, the jacket or sweater could be donned just before the meeting and taken off if others are in a similar state of undress; but the possibly most comfortable outfit, the t-shirt and sweater combo, would mean that the sweater stays on at all times.

    Finally, a casual outfit can be bumped up a notch with accessories, such as a shiny watch, leather belt, and stylish sunglasses.
