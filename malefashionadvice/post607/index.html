    MFA is typically against all black outfits. What about this guy?

    Meh. It's okay, especially because it fits well and seems to suit his character. The outfit exemplifies some of the difficulties with mixing black items, though.

    Unless the pieces are all made from the same fabric, differences in tone (blue-black vs. brown-black, e.g.) and sheen become obvious. These are too close in shade to appear as differentiated, contrasting items, and instead appear very awkward to some eyes.

    The tie in his outfit, for example stands out as shiny, and therefore loses integrity with the rest of the outfit. Some viewers overlook this issue, but to others it can be cringe-worthy. The one long, monolithic piece standing out in front of everything else can look juvenile -- it's not the way a tie is meant to integrate with an outfit.

    Next, consider the drawbacks inherent in using a single color --any color -- for an entire outfit. Contrast, the foremost element of style contributing to the interest of an outfit, is completely eliminated. The only variation here, as noted above, tends to be cloying.

    Furthermore, there is a heavy-handedness to it. It is extremely contrived, lacking any possible sense that the outfit was casually thrown together. Even if most outfits have some thinking behind them, they don't have to look like every last item was part of a grand scheme: it is integrity within variety, not hammering out a single note with every last move, that is seen as elegance and expertise. This 'artful dishevelment' or sprezzatura is seen as elegance precisely because it conveys a sense of ease and naturalness rather than obsessive self-concern.

    The nature of black will also be too heavy for many. Those without the swarthy complexion may be easily overpowered by the color; and the dour, conservative nature of black may likewise overpower the mood or persona.

    So the last haven of the all-black wearer is in the boldness of the statement. One decides to advertise the fact that, yes, this outfit is indeed quite conceptual and self-conscious, and I'm going all-in with it. Thus the whole idea of black becomes one's cachet. The brooding, intense, mysterious, bad-boy connotations are all bought-into.

    That's fine, but even that is so common and cliché that the outfit threatens to become a costume instead of a skillful communication of one's vision or being. Of all monochromatic looks, all-black is just too easy as a default. The audience isn't sure whether this self-conscious choice came from a place of expertise or because the wearer didn't have the skill or imagination to come up with anything more variegated.

    Basic black works for some guys. For some it represents simplicity, minimalism; for others it represents limitation -- they do it because they can't work skillfully with color or pattern... or are afraid to.

    In the examples, the plain jacket and shirt -- unbuttoned up top, and no tie -- looks better than the whole mish-mosh of black items. And the white shirt with black pieces looks much better yet. Another step up might be a lighter shade of pants or jacket, to provide a more interesting three-way contrast. And further yet, any number of color or pattern combinations could look great on the wearer.
