    Trying something new - shorts and lighter colors. Definitely not what I'm used to. Am I pulling this off?

    I like the idea of lighter and brighter colors, but that particular combination is not best, for a couple of reasons: First; the yellow, and to a lesser degree the white, are really not playing well on your skin tone. I think you might need to steer away from pale colors; but in any case, those are not working well for you.

    Second; there is too little contrast in the outfit, and when you take into account that you're working with all solids, the flat paleness of the outfit is tripled — pale colors, almost no contrast, and no movement or dimension.

    Third; the fact that the shoes also provide little contrast is particularly cloying. Their yellowness is emphasized by the shirt, so rather than showing off the leather as a nice foil for dark chinos or shorts, the effect is very weak. Dark brown leather or navy canvas shoes, for example, would look quite a bit better in that combination.

    The concept is a good one: sunny colors in a simple combination. It just needs to be carried out better.

    MFAers can suggest colors to try; but the proof is in the pudding. You have to actually try them yourself to see how they look on you. There is a big difference between a color that is just nice on its own (like the sunny yellow in your shirt) and a color that makes you look great. The attention shouldn't be on the clothes; the clothes should bring the attention to you, the unique being behind the clothes.

    • Learn your best and worst colors by trying many, many samples near your face. (Check by mirror or photo, near a window that provides good daylight lighting.) Check which colors make you look great — healthy, vibrant, more 'you' — and which colors make you look off — pale, ill, fading, or too ruddy.

    • Be specific about learning your colors. There are so many hues and shades and tints within each color family that generalizations won't help you. "I look good in green" doesn't always apply. Where moss or sage or forest green may work, lime or kelly green may not. The names are not as important as knowing what works and what doesn't. Keep samples of each if you can; and you may even want to bring them when shopping for clothes.

    • Consider contrast as an important factor in creating interest. The difference between parts of an outfit creates an interplay, a back-and-forth, between the pieces. Where without contrast there is a single overall impression that just sits there motionless, with contrast there suddenly arises relationship, which brings the more interesting prospect of tension and movement between the relating elements.

    • When working with solids (and especially the more pale or the more neutral they are), think about adding a pattern somewhere, again for more interest and movement. If you already have solid colors in your outfit, you have a flat, rhythm-free palette on which a pattern can be displayed cleanly and without interference. Conversely, if you're working with a loud or bold pattern (like wide stripes or complex plaid), use solids to tone down the rest of the outfit.

    • If a patterned piece is not part of the combo, consider texture, design features (collar, pockets, ribbing, fluting, epaulets, etc.), and/or accessories in measured doses to add interest.
