    Blazer colors to wear with jeans?

    I'd share your concern about too much blue. Even if the jeans are not exactly the same color as the blazer, I usually find that much dark blue too much. The effect can range from simply conservative and disappointingly boring to noticeably awkward.

    I also really don't like a black blazer with jeans. The colors of jeans that work best with the black -- the lighter fades -- are also the colors of jeans that can easily look ridiculous mixed into sharp, dressy outfits. I've seen it done. I think of Tom Brady, for example, when "blazer and jeans" is mentioned. But to my mind the look is somewhat of a throwback to past decades.

    (Brady's outfit is an interesting one for study. No doubt some will find it the epitome of style. I find it drastically overdone: The blazer and pocket square are far too dressy for the t-shirt and jeans. The self-consciousness of this outfit is very apparent, with the pocket square, scarf, sunglasses, and contrast in levels of formality -- even though the outfit attempts to project effortlessness, it's quite the opposite. The navy blazer plus blue jeans is indeed too much blue, albeit skillfully mitigated by perhaps the one color that could tone them both down -- mid-brown. But then the addition of black bag, black sunglasses, and white pocket square to the colors already in the outfit make me want to gag. I understand that some guys just lean toward the navy-and-black combo. It's a 'thing'. Definitely not my thing, but it has a following. But with brown in the middle... yeah, gag.)

    A blazer is, by definition, a slick piece. No texture and pattern here. The solid field of color in a smooth fabric, with clean lines and a sharp cut bring this jacket to the formal end of the casual range. The qualities expressed by the blazer can be tweaked into conservative yacht-club preppie or sleek night-club cruiser.

    Jeans, on the other hand, are coming from a rough-and-tumble workwear background, and are associated with wrinkling and fading and all things which are opposite to the blazer's associations. The opposition between the pieces is something that is either celebrated or played down when combining them. The difference could be emphasized in an outfit like a black velvet blazer with faded, distressed jeans (Pitt's jeans seem to be a grey-indigo fade). Or the difference could be downplayed, by dressing down with an unstructured cotton khaki blazer; or dressing up with dark wash, fine-denim jeans.

    It's the difference in qualities that can make the combination an alluring prospect, with interesting tension between elements, or a style risk, with potential for looking like a schlep who's trying to be a fashion rebel.

    Of course, when you find your style groove, you may just ride it all the way.
