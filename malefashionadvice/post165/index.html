    How does one make Sambas look good?

    Contrast, and color and pattern matching are definitely issues, but maybe a more primary point is whether the style of the shoe matches your outfit, and your personality, and the situation.

    You've named some of the unique characteristics of your Sambas. The iconic stripes and runner design put them very clearly into a sporty genre rather than a classic old-school sneaker style. The very sharp contrast (shade-wise, as contrasty as you can get), the overall black color, and the brown-black pairing all limit the versatility of the shoe. Black sporty shoes are already more difficult to match; the other features narrow the range of use even more.

    In terms of design style, the solution is to play to or emphasize the sporty aspect. Cuffed jeans with classic dress shirts or workwear, for example, may be an awkward match. But slim-fitting, uncuffed trousers paired with young and sporty pieces like clean-line graphic tees (yes, I said it!), sport shirts, and track jackets may be a better match. Close fit is even more important than usual since you want to emphasize the young, active look.

    What you're going for is not an athletic costume with all sweat pants or basketball shorts, but a slick, sharp outfit that draws on the genre of active wear for its cues. You might also think (and research) Euro style. Other pieces to consider include close-fitting thin pullover sweaters and wool knit polo shirts (l/s also)(30 years ago we might have included turtlenecks), moto jackets, sleek sharp (possibly colorful) belts, Euro-styled shoulder bags or airline bags, sunglasses that express an active feel (e.g., aviators) or that use clean geometric design, and sport watches.

    In terms of color, your easiest matches with pants will be with charcoal or other greys, and very neutral neutrals like taupe and stone. Think twice about using khakis and brown-based neutrals as they may bring too much warmth and softness to an outfit that should instead focus on clean lines and clear color. (Stark reds, yellows, bright and light blues, lavender, periwinkle, teal, violet, burgundy, and clear greens are your best bets for the dual requirement of sporty feel and going well with black.)

    With black shoes, one of your strong looks will be monochrome, with or without bursts of clear color. For a more summery, daytime look with contrast use lighter pants — stone or heather grey; for a more slick look suitable for evening, mix blacks with other dark shades. As an alternative to black-based monochrome outfits, pants with a clear color (like, say, burgundy) may be another option.

    A monochrome look, since it lacks color and possibly contrast, puts more of a premium on texture, pattern, and design features to create interest.

    Black runners with jeans is, to my eye, usually not a good look. If the rest of the outfit sets the sleek, active tone, you may be able to use slim jeans in the outfit. In fact, once you set that tone you may be able to bring in other pieces like collared shirts (under the sleek sweater), contemporary-cut blazers (over it), or even full-on suits (with v-neck t-shirt or sweater).

    The difficulty you're having in putting together outfits with these shoes just goes to show that the starting point in building a wardrobe should not be the trendy purchases around which everything else is supposed to be added; rather, the starting point should be an understanding of yourself and your situation, letting the wardrobe grow organically from there. This brings color, pattern, genre, and all the other elements of style into an integral look that centers on you, not on the objects you've gathered.

    Tailored sweat pants are a trend this year, and with the arrival of the Olympics as a major pop-culture event I predict that 'sporty' will be an emerging movement in fashion.
