    Trying for a new look on a $175 budget.

    That's such a sweet idea. Yet, like some others who have already weighed in, I find alarms going off. I want to encourage you to keep your proper boundaries, because proper boundaries make for good relationship. It sounds like your boyfriend may not be into the idea for his own sake; so I would hope that you don't overextend yourself. You should not be putting yourself out for his sake (or anyone's), but should have a balanced, equal exchange between you. By the same token, if you try to impose your own vision on him or influence his behavior with gifts it may be taken as an intrusion, so balance is necessary in that direction too.

    Also, is it working both ways? Are you and your boyfriend taking care of and being generous to you too?

    If this is a balanced, even-minded gift given without strings attached (in either direction), then I feel better about giving advice. But please keep your integrity. This is most important.

    The principles of balance and integrity also appear in style. Style should have integrity with one's actual life. To my mind, the place to begin in buying a new wardrobe is not a list of basics someone else has put together, but an inquiry into one's own nature and one's own needs. Clothes should serve the wearer, not the other way around. So some points should be clear:

    • What are the lifestyle needs? (What activities and settings are most prominent?)

    • What is the aim? (What does the buyer want to achieve with the wardrobe, in terms of feeling, utility, effect on others, etc.?)

    • What are the best colors and designs? (What colors make the buyer look vibrant and healthy; and what "look" works best on him/her?)

    There is no short answer as to what to buy. If you get a short answer, it will be arbitrary. There are guides in the sidebar at right that will help shape the vision (that, hopefully, the boyfriend is also involved in forming). The most significant points on a small budget:

    • Self-assessment, as in the points above. The purchase(s) should make sense with regard to the buyer's lifestyle and personal qualities.

    • Fit. Whatever you buy, see that it fits well. Most of the time, that will be the difference between looking good and looking schlumpy; and among high school kids especially, it is a decisive difference.

    • First impact. Look with an objective eye at some outfits, as the subject (your boyfriend in this case) is wearing them. What do you notice first? What is the most glaring error or issue? If the first impact is consistent across several outfits — say, wearing bad colors, or all of the shirts are too big, or everything's too different — then you know where to start.

    Note that the most effective first steps may have nothing to do with buying something. Maybe learning will make more of a difference — learning how to combine, learning what colors are good next to the face, learning what others see. And/or maybe getting rid of items that are bad or don't work together will help clear up the whole wardrobe and make it easier to find outfits that do work.

    • Outerwear and footwear. The two items that tend to make the most impact on a wardrobe are outerwear (coats & jackets) and footwear. A jacket is something that can be worn every day while other pieces change. It will also tend to be the thing that people see you wearing in public; so that first-impression point is in effect here. And shoes or boots tend to have a lot of impact on outfits as well. Likewise, they can be worn every day while the rest of the outfit changes. And as the foundation of an outfit, they will tend to set the stage: t-shirt and jeans with ratty running shoes looks slobbish; t-shirt and jeans with clean sneakers or oxfords looks more put-together.

    My suggestion: If your boyfriend is interested, then make it a casual, fun project to learn together. Then spend some time checking out his wardrobe or looking at magazines and getting ideas for outfits that look good. Your gift will be your interest and support and time spent together... and the no-pressure space for him to be himself. Adding your money to the whole deal might add too much pressure. But of course, you're free to decide what you want to do.

    Good luck to both of you.
