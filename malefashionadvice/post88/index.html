    why should dress pants "drape more"?

    Dress pants are typically of a finer fabric; i.e., thinner and of a more refined quality. The twill of chinos tends to be more stiff; it often will have a soft, flannel-type hand (texture to the touch), but doesn't flow so much as it maintains its own shape.

    Chinos will tend to form and hold wrinkles and maintain the shape in which the pant is cut, whereas dress pants will tend to release and move more easily. Because of these properties, a slim cut of chino will look more sleek and smooth, and a looser cut of chino will look more rumpled; but a dress pant can be loose and still maintain a sharp crease and attractive line. (Note on link: Match your shoes and belts, folks!)

    Modern, slim cuts defeat the draping of dress pants somewhat; and in those designs the fluid quality of the fabric is not as critical. In classic designs, when the fabric is very fluid, a cuff is not required; but its effect in weighing down the hem and helping the pants to release and hold their line is used when the fabric is thick and heavy. The weight of cuffs also help pleats to work.

    The physical properties of fabric and design give rise to the rule of thumb for cuffs: pleats and cuffs go together; flat front and slim fit don't take a cuff. This has become both a traditional and national distinction, with American and English classic styles using more volume, pleats, and cuffs, and Continental European styles employing slimmer cuts without cuffs. The exception is occasionally found with heavier fabrics like tweed, where a cuff helps the fabric maintain its line even with a slimmer, flat-front cut (as in the link above); but some traditionalists will regard this as a mark of ignorance.

    It's just another example that there's no one final "attractive aesthetic"; and that countless differences are used to tell the story of an outfit.

    TL; DR: Chinos just don't drape the same way; they bunch.
