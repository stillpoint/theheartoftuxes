    The Pair Of Dress Shoes I Bought Are Really Uncomfortable, Should I Return Them Or Stick It Out?

    OMG, my friend! If you're working at a restaurant you need all the help you can get from your shoes in terms of comfort and support. You think it's uncomfortable now? Just wait after you've run a marathon shift in them.

    There was no mention of your age, your income, or your work in the original post. The key phrases I read were "dress shoes" and "new job"; and that's what I went by.

    Proper dress shoes are lace-ups; and you should consider them for a couple of reasons. First, even if the restaurant where you work would allow you to wear a slip-on, it's not really a dress shoe. If you want to be able to use the shoe in other workplaces or possibly at dressy events in the future, a basic black oxford (lace-up) will be of more use. Second, the laces will give you more control in fitting. You can tighten them snug so they won't slip, you can lace them individually for each foot, and you can loosen them when your feet swell after standing or walking for long periods.

    The shoe you linked to is a loafer. The name is a clue to its purpose (which is somewhat casual), and also a cue as to how some people will view it. At a new job, you don't want to be perceived as 'a loafer', so you may not want to wear loafer shoes to work.

    Visually, the squared-off toe and the split-toe stitching are not considered classy design features. Square toes now seem like a throwback to the '90s, and anyway are jarring to some sensibilities. I don't know yet what your actual job is; but even managers run around a lot in the resto biz. I wouldn't begrudge you a pair of inexpensive (comfortable!) shoes, even if they didn't look spectacular. It's just that you posted here to Male Fashion Advice asking what we think of the shoes, so you should expect some honest feedback on their appearance.

    A round-toe lace-up will be more versatile for you, and will allow you to wear it for work in higher-end establishments if necessary. We would usually recommend a cap-toe design, but if you'll be doing a lot of walking the seam of the cap toe may be an issue with pressure or rubbing over time, so I'd recommend a plain toe.

    You may not be able to get away from a square toe in the $150-and-under range, and that's okay — you're not being photographed on a fashion runway. But do try to find a comfortable plain-toe lace-up if you can.

    None of these shoes is the epitome of style, but they're fine for most restaurant work, all available within your range, and all known for their comfort:

    • Rockport

    • Hush Puppies

    • Ecco

    • Clark's (with the MFA-reviled bicycle toe)

    And you may be able to find an even nicer looking shoe that's comfortable from another manufacturer, like Johnston & Murphy or Florsheim
