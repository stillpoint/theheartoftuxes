    Style or Function?

    Waterproofed boots are only comfortable in a narrow range of cold temperatures, and only necessary in wet conditions. At very cold temperatures there won't be significant benefit (because water is frozen, and the snow is quite dry), and the buildup of condensation within the boot due to lack of breathability can be uncomfortable or worse. And if you're going to be moving vigorously in the boots, breathability is even more of a concern. I wear wool-lined leather trapper boots in the Canadian winter; I treat them with Sno Seal every year, and I've found no need for waterproof boots.

    It's really only in consistently wet, slushy conditions just around freezing that waterproof boots may be superior; and at that, well-treated leather boots will do fine as long as they're not standing in water for more than a few seconds at a time. Check for a gusset at the tongue as well, as that will help keep water from seeping into the boot.

    One area where you may run into trouble is with the sole of the boot. The waterproof model has a nice rugged lug sole; the regular model has a sole that's not only attached by adhesive, but stitched on as well. I might expect water to leach along the stitches. You might want to check reviews, consult with customer service, or do further research to see if that would be an issue. While you're at it, find out how breathable, if at all, the waterproofing is on that model.

    Sno Seal is a very good treatment. It increases the resistance to penetration by water but allows the leather to remain breathable. Avoid silicone-based treatments and mink oil or products with animal oils in them, since both will eventually degrade the leather. Non-silicone Nikwax and Biwell are also good treatments.
