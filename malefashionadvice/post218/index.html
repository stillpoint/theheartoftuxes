    Gait.

    Video feedback would be the best way for you to know what you look like when walking. For a general sense, someone with a good eye for movement can give you a perhaps more vague idea of what may need to be brought into balance.

    Professionals who deal with posture should be able to help you with alignment; and some of those professionals deal with function -- posture in movement. So, while a good chiropractor may be able to say what parts of the structure are out of alignment, others may be more equipped to speak to the issue of gait. Unless I'm mistaken, Feldenkrais, Alexander Technique, and Mitzvah Technique all deal with functional posture. Rolfing/Structural Integration addresses alignment issues that may show up in gait; but I'm not sure if they deal specifically with walking & moving. Similarly, Hatha Yoga and Qi Gong stance practice (Zhan Zhuang) offer teachings and practices for posture and integration, but the way they show up in your walking may not be specifically addressed.

    In general, you want to achieve a posture where...

    • the spine is long

    • the head is centered over the shoulders (ears in line with shoulders)

    • the face is facing forward (the neck has a long gentle curve rather than being compressed because the chin is jutting out or flattened because the chin is drawn in too far)

    • the shoulders are relaxed, dropped away from the ears; and there is relaxed space between the shoulder blades

    • the upper body is centered over the lower body, not leaning forward or backward or to one side

    • the pelvis is upright rather than tilted back (too much of a curve in the lower back) or tilted forward (no curve in the lower back, "tail between the legs" look) -- the tailbone 'sinks' from relaxation, not because it is forced into a configuration

    • the neck/head and lower back/pelvis are free to move easily, without being held in a certain place by tension

    Actors, dancers, and many students of healing arts use a simple phrase to help train posture: "Up and forward." This is a general sense of being lightly pulled up through the top of the head, joined with a general sense of projecting up and out through the chest.

    All of those points of posture should be achieved without using muscular tension to force the alignment. Physical, emotional, and energetic structures will all be involved; so, for example, a certain level of energy is necessary for maintaining an upright and vital posture; and an upright and vital posture will in turn help energy to flow and build. (When you're tired, you will naturally slump; and when you slump you will naturally become more tired.) Likewise, a positive but content and non-acquisitive mood will allow the body to find its natural alignment and function; whereas a good posture will help overcome negative moods and states of mind.

    It's important to note and remember that desperately trying to attain a certain image or feeling will only tend to create a posture of 'desperately trying to get something'. Letting go of patterns of tension may be a long-term, step by step project that deals with issues which have been drilled into you even from childhood. So how far is it worth it to you to take it -- just enough to appear average; or all the way, becoming free of all habits of fear, shame, pride, agitation, and so on?

    Without any outside help, in order to be aware of the body's movement you need to develop an internal sensitivity. You have to have the ability to feel, to perceive the specific places in the body where tension appears and flow is curtailed. One of the best practices I know for this is Zhan Zhuang, which is the stance practice of Qi Gong and many internal martial arts. Standing in one posture is, at first, uncomfortable; but little by little you identify all of those tense and misaligned places, and you develop skill in dissolving the tension and letting your energy flow. Then, when you are walking (or in martial arts, when you are fighting) you can quickly identify and overcome blockages.
