    Cardigan Advice?

    The combination of solid black, solid white, and solid bright red is extremely stark. Not only is it too sharp on the eyes, it is very formal -- about as formal as you can get while having that level of brightness. Add the fact that the cardigan is buttoned to the top, and that the tie is a wide, silky one, and you have a look that is over the top for use with a sweater. If you want to go that formal, choose a suit instead.

    A cardigan can look very sharp, but you need to bring the level of formality down a couple of notches, to 'sharp casual'. Black will not be so versatile; you're stuck with a large field of the ultimate dark shade, and the extreme contrast and intensity it offers in combination with other pieces. So this cardigan will be used for very sharp but casual looks. For example, with just the white shirt, unbuttoned at the top.

    You also may want to loosen up a bit by opening a button or two of the cardigan at both the top and bottom (bottom button should usually be opened anyway).

    A more versatile cardigan would be in an actual color, and if you like perhaps some fine textured pattern that is visible close-up. Colors would allow you to create more outfits in the sharp-casual range, whereas black instantly becomes more formal and serious.

    If you do want to wear a tie with a cardigan, more casual versions tend to be better. Wool ties and knit cottons will go better. Also, narrower, for a more modern and youthful look. It is possible to do a silk tie with a cardigan; but it would have to be in a very lush context -- a high-quality cashmere cardigan, with very elegant color-matching, and with dressy pants and shoes.

    I can't see the fit well. You need a full-body pic with your arms relaxed at your sides. But at first glance it seems okay. Shoulder seam should line up with the bony point of your shoulder, and the sweater should just skim the body closely without billowing anywhere, but not squeeze it.

    Before buying a lot of clothing, you should have a strategy for outfit combinations. Instead of buying individual pieces and hoping they'll go together, it should all work together. But do take your time and go slow with purchases, seeing how each works out for you and combines with others before buying the next one.

    The aim is not to buy clothing that looks great; the aim is to buy clothing that makes you look great. This means that the colors and styles and combining should all reflect and hilight who you really are.

    Find out what colors are best near your face. Check in a mirror with good natural light to see which colors make you look healthy and alive. (Taking photos can also help, as can soliciting the opinions of well-dressed women friends.)

    Next, consider the kinds of clothing and outfits that will be most useful to you. What will you actually be doing most of the time? What situations will you be in the most? Who will you be with the most? It makes sense to buy clothing for the life you actually live, not for some imagined standard set by online blogs and forums.

    Finally, consider who you are and who you want to be. What qualities and energies are natural to you, and which ones do you want to bring out? It may be so, but I don't get the sense that you are as intense as that black-and-white-and-red outfit. If not, do you want a more warm and earthy feel, or a bright and joyful one, or...? Are you really that formal; or would a chunky knit be more your pace? My first impression is that a variety of browns and other neutrals (grey, khaki, olive, taupe) would form a great foundation, which could be augmented with dashes of not-too-intense color (light blues, emerald green, maybe a bit of purple or indigo) in shirts or ties.

    It'll take time to develop your style sense and your skills at combining color, pattern, level of formality, and style. Read the sidebar guides, collect photos of outfits you like, and start analyzing what you see guys wearing in movies, on the street, in magazines -- thinking about what makes it work or not work. (Here's a good exploration of different styles. Just click on the genre on the left and scroll through different looks.)

    And remember, good fit is essential. It can make all the difference between 'schlep' and 'wow'.
