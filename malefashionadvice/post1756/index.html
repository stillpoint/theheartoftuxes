    Help a high school teen pick out a stylish jacket for the winter.

    We need to see what you look like and what your existing style and character are like. Your body shape and size, your skin and hair coloration, and your character all work into the equation. And then there's where you live (a thin jacket for winter??) and the situations and purposes for which you'll want the jacket.

    In general, avoid faux leather, pleather, leatherette, vinyl, polyurethane, etc. The fauxness is apparent. It also doesn't breathe at all, and gets uncomfortable in warm and cold weather. If you can't afford real leather just yet, wool has a lot of benefits: it's durable, warm, weather-resistant, and as a natural fiber it offers more class and depth than many synthetic options.

    When building a wardrobe, first you'll want to get an overview of what you already have that will be useful for combining, and of those questions above -- what style and expression are you going for, what is going to suit you well and show you in the very best light. Fit and color will be very important in helping you look your best. And remember that the point is for you to look your best, not your clothing. Though there may be the urge to buy something that looks flashy or magnificent by itself, most of the time you will be better served if you first consider carefully whether the piece enhances your own face and personality or whether it is stealing attention or subduing your appearance.

    The first, central pieces you buy should be basic, classic, and versatile. You want those core pieces to be very efficient in their ability to combine with each other and make you look good without having to think too much about how to match or when not to wear them.

    Especially if you're on a tight budget, the more care you take in buying classic, versatile pieces, the more usefulness you'll get out of your wardrobe. So besides classic designs, basic neutral colors are usually recommended for your first pieces. It's usually much more cost effective to add color with an inner layer or scarf or hat than to own a brightly colored jacket that is hard to match or won't be worn on many occasions. Once you have a basic core wardrobe, then you can start to venture into more color, pattern, and unique design.

    That's not to say that a classic, neutral jacket has to be boring or colorless. This jacket from Urban Outfitters is a classic design -- the 'Harrington' jacket -- in a neutral brown; but it is a very rich and vibrant brown. If it is a good color for your skin tone, it would be very versatile, combining well with all earth tones, all blues, all greys, and pops of bright color like deep red.

    Of the examples you posted, and not having seen a picture of you, I like the medium grey if it's not in faux leather; and I like the dark military one you posted later in the thread. (Just know that black can be more problematic to match than navy blue, grey, or brown.)
