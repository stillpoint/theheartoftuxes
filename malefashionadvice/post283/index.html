    Crew necks and cardigans.

    It's not the worst fashion crime, but it's not the ideal shape either. "Because I don't have any" is no excuse -- go buy one! Even the shallowest v-neck will be a better shape within the vee of the cardigan.

    But the shape of the collar is not the only issue. If you get a better fit and match hues and shades better, the overall look will improve.

    Fit: The cardigans certainly aren't too small. If anything, they're too big. The first, thin grey one is a bit baggy at the sides; and the shoulders are wide. Some guys like the slouchy look, and others default to a 'classic' cut that has extra width around the body; but neither one presents your form at its best.

    The second, navy cardigan also has some extra material around the waist; and the shoulders are wide, and the sleeves are too long. It's possible that the extra material at the sides is there because the sweater is too long and is bunching up.

    The last picture is dim, so it's hard to see the fit clearly; but at first glance it looks like the best of the bunch. You can see for yourself if there's extra knit material at the sides when you wear the cardigan. At best, it should skim the body closely; or in the case of a stretchy knit, it should cling to the body without needing to stretch at all. This means that if the cardigan is thin, only thin layers should be worn underneath it so that bunched fabric doesn't show through.

    Alteration: None of the fits is what I'd call terrible; but it's an area where you can do better. A good tailor or seamstress can usually take in some fabric from the sides of thin knits as long as they have a side seam. It will leave a flap of fabric inside the cardigan; but the fit will come closer to your body. This can only be done 'somewhat', since if the shoulders are wide or armholes are low, a close-tailored waist will be out of proportion and will leave a billow of fabric around the underarms and upper chest and back.

    Color matching: In terms of color -- or more specifically, shade -- you paint yourself into a corner by wearing dark sweaters with dark pants. The complete outfit then has little contrast, which means visually there is no excitement or interplay, no distinct elements for the eye to move between. If you then add a dark t-shirt, the whole look is both plain (boring) and depressed. If you add a white or bright t-shirt, it jumps out in too much contrast, with just the small knife of brightness in the cardigan's gorge (the vee) and in the slice of shirt exposed at the hem. This also then emphasizes any less-than-ideal collar shape.

    • If you were to wear dark pants and a light sweater, or vice versa, then a range of possibilities opens up with under-layers. A light sweater, for example, can accommodate light colored shirts as well as medium shades. In fact, an underlying layer of all dark (jeans and t-shirt) may have enough unity to look okay under a light sweater. Reverse the principle for light pants and dark sweater.

    • A medium shade of cardigan will also free you to bring more variety in with your shirts. Dark pants, medium cardigan, and light shirt offers three elements that the eye can move between, yet the contrast is not too jarring between any two elements.

    • You can also consciously bring the contrast down for a calmer, more grounded effect. Dark pants, medium sweater, and medium or dark shirt has a bit of contrast, but the blending is smooth and sedate. In this case, your variety should come with hue. That is, while the difference in darkness may not be so great, the actual color should differ: Dark blue jeans, medium brown cardigan, and medium grey or sage green tee, for instance.
