    Is this shirt too busy?

    Almost no shirt is "too busy" if the context is right. So the first question is what are the situations in which you hope to wear the shirt. Next, do you feel it's too loud?

    This example is a fairly common pattern and color combo, so I don't think you're really pushing the envelope here, style-wise. On a separate issue, I'm not sure if it's the best color for your skin tone. It could be good, or it could make a pink skin tone more pink. I'd have to see more images in better light.

    From a strictly visual perspective, the red and blue in the shirt intensify each other; they provide contrast for each other, and as near-complements they bring each other's hue forward more. (The blue jeans also intensify the red in the shirt.) For example, you can imagine that a blue-on-brown check would be a bit more sedate. The red is fairly dark, though, so I wouldn't call it overwhelming.

    The check pattern does have a vibrancy to it. Imagine a solid colored shirt with fine stripes on it — blue on red, or red on blue. This would be a minimal introduction of pattern, and thus rhythm, into a plain field of color. With thin hairline stripes, the contrast introduced is fine and gentle. If the stripes were to become wider, from pinstripes to thick 1" stripes, the contrast would have quite a presence, and the interplay and tension between different colored stripes would be quite bold. (The extreme version of this is color blocking, with just two fields of color next to each other, with a clear boundary between them.)

    When the mid-sized stripe is augmented with stripes in a perpendicular direction, that becomes the check pattern you are wearing. The addition of 90º stripes has two effects: it increases the energy of the pattern — instead of the single rhythm there is now an additional rhythm in another direction — and it fills the field in, making it more homogeneous. So while the pattern's energy is raised, the color contrast is diminished and unified somewhat.

    To my eye, there is too much color and vibrancy of pattern for an untucked shirt. The extra fabric below the waistband makes it too large a field of vibrancy. If you would tuck in the shirt and wear a leather belt that helps to rein the shirt in to the top part of your outfit, that would help.

    This is a very useful principle in working with patterns, or color for that matter. The louder the pattern or color, the more you can limit its size; that is, you can limit the presence of loud influences in the outfit. In this case, you can help your case by tucking the shirt in. You've already rolled up the sleeves, which is another way to limit the field. You could also partially cover it with a sweater or jacket. Sometimes a "louder" pattern is just perfect under an outer layer that would otherwise be too plain.

    And fit also helps mitigate loud influences in this way. The extra billows and wrinkles add not only more visual area but more complexity, which contributes to the loudness. Getting the shirt tailored will make it appear more clean-cut, simplifying the overall sense of it.

    Moving on from the button-up, the exposed undershirt is a no-no. It looks both messy and pent-up. Undershirts, like briefs or boxers, should not be visible. Your better alternatives are to either do without an undershirt, wear a v-neck that doesn't show, or commit to a rugged t-shirt and button-down look where the underlying t-shirt (not undershirt) is exposed by opening another button or three on the button-up. The last option requires a t-shirt that complements the button-up and fits into the overall outfit — not a black one!

    Next, the jeans are far too baggy. Along with their lighter wash, they're starting to look like "dad jeans".

    Taking care of fit and matching, tucking in shirt tails, and removing the undershirt will make the button-up look better.
