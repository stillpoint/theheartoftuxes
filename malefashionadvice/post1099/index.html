    MFA - How do you travel?

    My adoption of modular packing has become a huge relief and joy when traveling. I use lightweight mesh zip-up cubes: large for pants, small for t-shirts/polos & knits, another small for socks & underwear. Dress shirt goes into a shirt folder, with the heavy plastic sheet removed, and only keeping the light plastic sheet, around which the shirt is folded.

    Toiletries go in an ultralight zip bag. Lotions & liquids are decanted into Nalgene or similar bottles, in the smallest amounts necessary; and they all go together in a Ziploc. A couple of extra ziplocs and a sturdy garbage bag are stowed in a side pocket, and are used either for more storage or to protect the contents of the pack from getting wet in torrential downpours or when traveling over water. Electronics go in a lightweight padded cube. I also stuff a reusable nylon shopping bag — one of those that scrunches into a tiny, tight roll — within easy access. Can't tell you how many times that has come in handy.

    Most often, everything I travel with goes into one bag, my Osprey Porter carry-on. (One review here.) I find I can get around more easily with a backpack than with a roller. There are many terrains where rollers don't work or don't work as well; whence the extra weight of handles and casters only adds to your burden. Accommodating roller-bag handles in the design also detracts from available space for packing and means that it will be more difficult to shove the back into overhead bins, under seats, etc.

    (edit: I should mention that the main drawbacks of a backpack are that it presses on the shoulders of whatever jacket you're wearing, and that it can be hot on the back. A well-constructed jacket should rebound from the weight. And as to a sweaty back, I just keep shifting my bag to hand-grip and shoulder strap once in a while to let my back breathe; but it could still be an issue in tropical weather.)

    Inside this one bag, I'll have a grab-and-go bag carrying my essentials, which also serves as a daypack at my destination. (This is a Patagonia ultralight travel duffel, of a design that is no longer in production.) In case I am forced to check or otherwise part with my carry-on, I'll be able to pull out a single bag, with confidence that I'll have what I need to get by in the meantime.

    I usually wear one dress shirt and pack one. If I take the right sweater and polo, I have several options to go with a sport jacket or suit. In fact, one of my favourite travel items is both a sweater and a polo -- a collared merino/cashmere sweater with polo button placket. This piece is incredibly versatile. It can be worn on its own; with a t-shirt, polo, or dress shirt underneath; or under a suit jacket or sport coat. Thin wool knits are also extremely adaptable to weather and temperature conditions, keeping me warm in the cold, breathing well in warmer atmosphere, and wicking moisture when it's damp.

    I usually try to bring only one suit jacket or sport jacket, which I will wear while traveling. If I do pack a jacket, the only two items I'll be concerned about regarding wrinkling will then be the packed jacket and the shirt. The 'fold-inside-itself' method for the jacket has been documented. The shirt is in its folder. To reduce the chance of wrinkling even further, I may fold the pieces around either tissue paper or a cut-open plastic dry cleaner bag. Both are very light, and keep the fabric of the pieces from clinging and forming wrinkles.

    Bringing a travel steamer is another option, though personally I don't. Some hotels will provide you with a steamer or iron, either upon request or as a matter of course. I wouldn't recommend ironing your jackets, though, as it may alter the finish of the fabric; and if any part of the jacket is bonded rather than sewn, ironing could cause that to bubble. I find that if I unpack as soon as possible and hang suit and shirt in the bathroom, whatever small waves may have appeared in their fabric soon relax, and all is good.

    Two other packing methods come to mind that many find to be effective. Rolling pants and shirts separately is also said to reduce wrinkling by keeping items from sliding upon themselves and each other. Many find that the packing becomes more dense with rolling, so you can fit more into the same space. (There are many online articles about the rolling method. Check also the "military roll".)

    The second method is the bundle method: the sport jacket is laid facing down on a bed or table, and other items are criss-crossed on top of it one by one. When all items to be packed are stacked in this fashion, the ends that extend from the pile are folded over the bundle, with the jacket and jacket sleeves coming last. This way, most items will be folded in gentle curves over a soft center instead of 180º over themselves; and the interfolding helps keep the articles from sliding over each other and forming wrinkles. Woe to you, though, if you need to grab an item from the middle of the bundle before you've reached your destination.

    For organization, light weight, ease of access, and speed through security checks, the modular system is by far my favorite.

    I'll also mention a very interesting looking carry-on that is basically a garment bag which rolls into tube form. A suit, shirts, and pants are packed in the garment section; and when it's rolled, the center of the tube accommodates the remaining items. Here's the link.

    Final words: Lighter is better; when in doubt, leave it out.
