    How to wear olive pants

    Almost anything. Olive is considered a neutral color, along with brown, grey, khaki, tan, and indigo. White and black are sort of false neutrals because of their stark contrast.

    Olive pants will work with blue shirts, including oxford, chambray, and denim; all kinds of browns (or generally any other neutral); violets, indigo, purple. You'd have to be more careful with greens, but muted or darker shades would go in a sedate monochromatic look.

    For more striking vibrancy you can go for the complement to green, which is red. A red gingham shirt or pink polo are examples of using the color contrast of a complement but toning it down by using a less intense shade. For more intensity, use dark shades like burgundy or blood red. For in-your-face contrast use bright red.

    Shifting from directly opposite contrast, a happy, bright combination is found in tertiary colours. I've already mentioned the purples, which through their depth are a more conservative but still colourful choice (olive and purple is a classic combination found, for instance, in school colours and rugby uniforms). In combination with dark brown accessories this is a classic go-to outfit full of color. On the other side of the color wheel is orange, which also forms a sort of classic bright and happy combination with greens.

    In multitonal outfits, olive falls within the 'autumn' colours. Picture the colours of an autumn landscape, and you will find the palette that brings out olive's character. Deep orange-brown leathers, adobe and rust reds, layered warm browns in a range of shades, touches of gold, and echoes of moss and sage green form a beautiful, earthy, nature-oriented color scheme. This works best on skin colours with brown, yellow, and olive cast; it will not tend to go well on pink and porcelain skin tones.

    In terms of archetypes you can play with, the foremost is the military ubiquity of 'OD' (olive drab) in uniforms around the world. You can play it up by using camo or military designs like army field jackets, military watches, combat boots, etc. Let it be a casual nod to the archetype rather than a schoolboy dress-up costume. The military style overlaps another whole genre, safari, which also makes good use of olive within a sun-faded earth tone palette. A really lovely combination that avoids being too literal is a safari palette with, say, olive pants and dusty sable brown and khaki layers, but with a turquoise or sky blue shirt or accent (like a scarf): the blue introduces a fantastic cool quality, like an oasis set in a desert landscape.

    In addition to solid colours, olive will provide a fine base for many kinds of patterns. Olive's a neutral, but one with a touch more character and uniqueness (until it trends) than basic blue, brown, and khaki.
