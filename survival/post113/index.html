    Esbit 4 gram tabs as fire starters?

    I don't like esbit as a firestarter because of the strong smell that gets into everything. I still use esbit / hexamine / solid fuel tabs for quick wilderness excursions when I don't want to bother with firemaking at all, and just need enough flame for a single mug of tea or soup.

    Purely as a firestarter, esbit is at a disadvantage. It provides the fuel, but not the tinder. Other firestarters either include fibrous material that can catch a spark (like the cotton fibers in cotton-and-vaseline), or may flame up by virtue of their volatility (like magnesium shavings).

    Esbit will function more like a chunk of wax or a wad of pine pitch: it will keep a flame going once lit, but itself first has to be lit by a match or lighter flame, or in concert with some tinder that can transform a spark to a flame.

    In fact, I'd prefer pine pitch to esbit as a firestarter fuel, as I get the sense that it is more volatile (don't quote me on that), while still giving you many seconds or, with a big chunk, even some minutes worth of flame to work with. Before making a final statement, though, I'd want to test the various fuels myself; and maybe that's something that you should do, since you have the interest to experiment and you're looking for survival knowledge: find out for yourself the properties of various fuels and firestarters by testing them in similar conditions.

    If you're looking for a (literally) solid synthetic firestarter that will give you every possible advantage in survival situations, look instead to some of the firestarters already included with marketed survival kits or sold together with ferrocerium rods and other sparkers.

    • Coghlan's Emergency Firestarter
    • SOL Tinder-Quick
    • Hammaro Tinder Card
    • Wetfire
    • Light My Fire Maya Dust

    The first two above are in form very similar to your cotton and vaseline wads. The Tinder Card is in a flat configuration. With all of these, you have to pull the piece apart to expose the tinder fibers before attempting to light it.

    The Maya Dust is already loose, so all you have to do is gather it into a loose pile, which may seem more convenient... until you have to build a fire in a strong wind. It may also be less convenient to carry than the other formats. The Wetfire is nicely packaged to carry, but has to be shaved into a pile of tinder that will accept a spark, and so may also be problematic in high wind (though it is perhaps more 'sticky' than the Maya Dust).

    I don't recommend the bars of magnesium, or in any case don't like using them myself. Although they can create a white-hot flame (which could be useful for starting fires in winter when many branches are cold or even frozen with soaked-in water), the amount of shavings you need, the amount of effort required, the need to protect your pile of tinder from the wind (making the white-hot eruption more dangerous if your hands or body have to be brought in close), and the wear-and-tear on your knife edge make this more trouble than it's worth. All you need to sour you to the whole concept is one experience of shaving away for several minutes, sparking the pile of shavings, discovering it was not a big enough pile to start your fire, and then having to start the whole laborious process over again. In a survival situation, that waste of time, energy, and resources is unforgivable; you are better served with a method that is quick and easy.

    It may be tempting to try forms like commonly available home fireplace firestarters that have a large match-head attached to wax- or petroleum-impregnated wood fibers; but the fibers in them are not fine enough to serve as tinder by catching a spark — the whole process depends on the ability of the match head to start the flame, and if that gets wet or crumbles off or blows out once lit, you don't get a second chance to start the fire with a sparking implement.

    Another interesting possibility is a windproof torch lighter, which can provide a hot, continuous starting flame under fairly extreme conditions. (All firestarters will be somewhat impacted by cold temperatures; and liquid fuel lighters more so.) While this may be a good front-line option, I'd also want to carry a simple backup like soaked cotton balls, for the simple fact that the more complex the tool the more there is that can go wrong.

    And by the way, although impregnating cotton wool with fuel may give you the advantage of carrying a flame continuously until (hopefully) your kindling has had a chance to ignite, as you soak the pieces with more and more fuel there is a point of diminishing return in its ability to accept a spark. Plain, dry cotton wool that's fluffed up may be quite a good tinder in itself.

    With any firestarter you consider for your survival kit it is essential that you yourself experience what it is like to use it in all kinds of conditions. Once you know the properties of your kit through hands-on experience you will know how much you can rely on it, which is much better than just assuming that you can rely on it.
