    Name top important things to help you survive during major dramatic event

    Clearly, level-headedness, knowledge, and a strong skill set are most important.

    The kinds of gear and kinds of skills that will be most helpful will depend on the situation. In some cases, being able to run quickly will be the most help. In other cases, knowing how to seal a building or room from intrusion of airborne chemicals or biological matter will be the life-saver. Some scenarios will call for blending in or hunkering down, others for bugging out. Your question is too broad for any but the most general response.

    A useful rule of thumb is the so-called Rule of Threes. Note that this is not a hard-and-fast list of priorities, and doesn't hold true in 100% of cases; but it gives some idea of how to organize your efforts:

    • 3 seconds: A stupid move can kill you in three seconds, so in those seconds first STOP, get your wits about you, and assess the situation.

    • 3 minutes: If you don't breathe you will die within three minutes. Access to breathable air is vital. You won't have a chance to apply any of your skills if you can't breathe. The ability to apply CPR and self-rescue techniques can also be life saving.

    • 3 hours: A body exposed to extreme temperatures will (may) collapse and die within three hours. Core temperature must be maintained either with shelter plus warming protocols or, in the case of extreme heat, shelter plus cooling protocols. Proper clothing is most often the difference between death and survival when you're exposed to the elements in cold or even marginally inclement weather.

    • 3 days: You can die in 3 days or less if you don't have access to hydration. A method of gathering or producing and possibly storing clean water is very high on the list of both short- and long-term survival priorities.

    • 3 weeks: Most people in laying-low situations can survive a couple of weeks without food, but certainly not much more than 3 weeks. Low blood sugar level in the brain not only threatens survival directly, it can also lead to inactivity or mistakes that further complicate an already dire situation.

    • 3 months: Other somewhat intangible issues emerge as important when looking at longer-term survival. Mental health and motivation, access to a support network, companionship and teamwork, and other factors can each make an extremely decisive difference to one's prospects.

    In urban and mass-public disaster scenarios, we also have to face the fact that commodities and a standard of exchange will be important. Certain things like money or the tradeable, sought-after items that substitute for money may rise in value far beyond their usual function (e.g., you yourself may not need any more batteries or gasoline in order to survive, but their trade value could be through the roof).

    Knowing how this framework should be applied and which priorities take precedence requires an assessment of your situation.

    • Where do you live?
    • What is the environment like?
    • What risks and benefits are provided by the landscape and climate?
    • What is your support network?
    • What are your resources in terms of long-term shelter, transportation, access to water, access to food, and so on?
    • What is your physical condition?
    • How strong and resilient is your mind?
    • Who depends on you for survival or assistance?
    • What life skills and survival skills do you possess? — the possible list is long, long, long — endless, really:
    • from cooking, sewing, repair skills, mechanical knowledge,
    • to hygiene and self-care, first aid, medical knowledge, practical chemistry,
    • to language, communication, and relationship skills,
    • to bushcraft, shelter building, plant knowledge, wilderness food acquisition,
    • to self-defense and evasion skills, and more.

    In general, practice being capable and taking care of yourself rather than requiring others to take care of you. Learn how to do the things you go to others for help with (fixing your clothing, fixing your car, etc.). And practice generosity, friendship, and the spirit of open exchange to build a very strong network of friends and resources.
