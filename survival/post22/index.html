    Hello. I'm new here, and would really appreciate y'all's input on something.

    Since you're starting out, this is the ideal time to tailor your setup to your own situation. As much as possible, avoid the discomfort and expense of the trial-and-error approach, and do a lot of research before you buy. Of course, there is no substitute for experience, and there are things you will only learn about your setup once you try to use it.

    I agree with suggestions already posted: consult with the salespeople at a good backpacking store, and load the backpack with weight to test its comfort and stability. Try both a common load weight as well as loading the pack with more than you might usually carry.

    But I really can't stress this enough: look into lightweight and ultralight backpacking! Even for big, rugged guys it's a revelation to be free of the discomfort and constriction of a heavy pack. When you consider that an extra ounce or pound of weight will be borne over many thousands of steps, the math is clear. Going light has utterly renewed my enthusiasm for backpacking. The gains in energy and comfort, the greater ability to roam and explore, and the closer relationship to one's gear are benefit enough; but if you add physical issues to the mix, it seems like a no-brainer to consider shedding as much weight as is reasonable â€” which can amount to anywhere from several to dozens of pounds from the load you'd otherwise carry.

    Going light can also help you find a more comfortable backpack, since the hard support structure found in mainstream bags would not be necessary. There are many bags that are a good compromise between traditional support and ultralight construction. Granite Gear, Osprey, and Asolo make good lighter hybrid options. I can attest to the comfort and functionality of the Granite Gear Meridian Vapor. If you don't need lots of pockets on your pack, you can go lighter.

    Many packs are also made with a mesh or other sort of suspension that keeps the load from rubbing against your back. Osprey Atmos packs, Vaude, ZPacks (which offer ultralight options), and Deuter, among others, all offer alternative suspensions. The downside to many of these is that wind, cold, and snow can get in between the pack and the body. This is sometimes addressed by stuffing insulation in the gap (but if you do this, take precautions so as not to lose your fleece along the way!).

    Besides your pack, you should also consider the right -- and, if it suits your requirements, light -- footwear. And also look into trekking poles: using them occupies your hands and means your other gear will have to accommodate that, but they also take a lot of burden off of the skeleton and musculature. The gains are revealed gradually over the course of a long hike.

    If nothing else, do the research and trim the weight of your "big three" â€” the three heaviest items in your kit: the backpack, the sleep system, and the shelter system. The benefit will be multiplied with every step of every trip you take.
