    What would be the best sustenance to keep in a Altoids EDC kit?

    There are a few posts and upvotes for pemmican as a good item for EDC. I think that pemmican and other fatty foods (like a small bottle of olive oil, for instance) are good choices for a longer term strategy. But this goes to show the difference between opinion and experience, and between theory and actuality "in the field".

    Have you ever eaten pemmican? See how you like it; notice what effect it has on your bodymind; check how it is to carry and store it, and how it keeps.

    Fats are a good concentrated food that amass calories in the lightest weight-per-calorie ratio. They can help bump up a daily diet to its necessary caloric levels for long-term exertion and for very cold climates. As such, they factor into an overall preparedness strategy, with the balance tailored to the climate, the activity, and the personal physiology.

    But you should know that fats require glycogen and blood glucose for their digestion. In order to extract those concentrated calories and use them, you have to already have a supply of blood sugar (that is provided by carbohydrates), and even at that, the conversion of fat to useable calories is still slow. In situations where blood sugar is already low, as can be reasonably anticipated in many survival scenarios, eating fat will place a burden on the body and will not provide return: there will not be enough glycogen to fuel conversion, and the glycogen that could otherwise be used for fast-burst activities like running away or preparing a shelter quickly will be depleted. Clearly this is a risky proposition. Just when you should be taking action to save your life you may be collapsing, because you have used pemmican in a way in which it wasn't intended.

    You can see for yourself the effect of eating an all-fat meal. Does it lend itself to a subsequent hour of vigorous, industrious activity, or does it encourage you to lie down for a cat-nap?

    Furthermore, a high-fat diet places more burden on the liver and gallbladder, and the liver in particular is already sensitive to emotional and environmental stress (and is necessary for processing them). Without proper balance, eating high fat diet can undermine your innate "survivability" — your resilience to toxins and stressors.

    If you are going to be ingesting concentrated fats (and proteins for that matter), they should usually be eaten at the end of the day when you have opportunity to rest, when energy can be focused on digestion and assimilation, and when fast energy is not needed for important or live-saving tasks. And for sure, they should be taken in a proper balance with the carbohydrates that support their digestion. Figures vary slightly, but the general best balance is considered to be about 50% carbs, 35% fats, and 15% protein (or 60/30/10). In periods of high exertion or for short bursts, an even (much) higher percentage of carbs and even lower percentage or total elimination of fats is best.

    This is why the best trail mix or gorp should not only be a mix of nuts, but should include lots of carbohydrates — raisins, chocolate chips, m&ms, honey, etc. (And as I have outlined above, even with this balance, the fats included will take time and energy to be digested, and may be a setback in critical situations.)

    For an EDC kit, a balanced fast- and slow-release carbohydrate will address the critical emergencies one may encounter, much better than concentrated fat or protein.

    A little bit of knowledge is a dangerous thing. Keep learning, and don't let ego-based opinions interfere with your open quest for learning. The person with a better chance of survival is the one who has been learning and experiencing and double-checking his knowledge, not the one who has been trying to bolster his self-image as an expert, thus leaving him closed to any further growth.
