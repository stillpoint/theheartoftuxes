    Batoning fail, or why you shouldn't cheap out on your knives. Friend of mine about an hour ago sent me these.

    The issue is not necessarily the 'cheapness' of the knife. Thick logs of hard wood can be brutal on all kinds of knives. The right tool for the job of splitting large pieces of hardwood is an axe or maul.

    Moreover, the type of steel the knife is made of isn't the only factor; the knife design plays into your success or failure, and the technique that you use can make a big difference.

    We can see that the knife pictured has a secondary bevel, creating a thick shoulder that more easily gets stuck in the wood when batoning. The overall thickness of the blade increases friction in the wood, and a broad, sharp shoulder increases it even more, as it digs into the wood being split.

    This is one reason why Skandi grinds and smaller, flatter blade designs are better for the woodcrafting operations of bushcraft and wilderness survival. If the design of your knife is working against you in this way, then for sure, thicker and tougher steel will be needed to keep the knife intact; but then more force will be needed to drive the knife through wood, and you soon reach a point of attrition where the tool just doesn't work for the task.

    For the right size and density of wood, the narrowness of a Skandi grind within a classic bushcrafting knife will usually baton better. But for tough splitting jobs or jobs where many cuts have to be made, it would be more worthwhile to preserve the knife and instead use it to craft a tool for splitting: a wedge.

    Make a few wedges out of wood, and then once the initial crack is started you can drive them into the crack to split the wood. You may need two or more wedges at a time to split unyielding pieces. But the great thing is that you save your blade and its sharp edge. When the wedge wears out, toss it and make another. Split rock can also be used as a wedge.

    A couple of other options for reducing wood:

    • Make a cross-grain (sideways) cut and then hammer the log on another hard object, like a hard log or rock. With each impact, the initial cut spreads along the grain.

    • Depending on what you need the smaller piece for, fire can be used to remove some of the material. Charred wood can easily be removed once it has cooled down.

    • Shave smaller pieces rather than trying to split a large chunk. Follow the demands of the wood: if it resists, make the depth of your split smaller, or move on to an easier piece of wood.

    The knife is so crucial for survival that you should go to great lengths to preserve it and its sharp edge. Know its capabilities, and if you need something done that the knife itself won't do, use the knife to fashion a tool rather than abusing the knife itself.
