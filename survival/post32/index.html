    Many people don't understand the value of those qualities (respect and sincerity) for survival; but from what I see, they are of great importance. I've seen first-hand the difference between someone who has skills and someone who has skills and respect. The difference in the way tools and resources are handled makes all the difference when things get tough. And the difference in group efficiency during hard times is marked, according to whether the group members respect each other and are honest about each other's abilities, or whether the group members are more ego-involved and trying to keep up an act.

    About weapons: where are you located, or what areas are you thinking of entering? I've hiked in Grizzly Bear territory, in the Canadian Rockies, and in mountain lion territory in the Rockies and the California Sierra Mountains, but I've never carried a rifle much less been required to. It's possible that in some areas (Alaska, Yukon, Nunavut) the presence of Grizzlies or Polar Bears may call for a defensive weapon, but I don't have experience with that, so I can't advise you. Where I usually hike, in the northeast, animal attacks are almost never an issue unless there is an issue with the food that is carried or how it is stored, or there is some unawareness about intruding on an animal's territory. Moose tend to be the more significant risk, more so than black bears or coyotes, and the rule is simply to give them good space and don't surprise them.

    There's a whole ton of info out there about food for backpacking, from recipes for luxury meals to ounce-counting packing lists for ultralight hikes and fast-packing. A Google search, a browse in the bookstore's camping section, or a search here on Reddit will turn up a slew of ideas.

    In terms of shelter systems, you should start with your situation and go from there. Too many people buy gear because others say it's good, without knowing how it will or won't address their particular situation. So what areas will you be visiting? What kinds of landscapes will you be dealing with? What climate, and what seasons? What kind of excursions — far afield or close to home; weekend trip or multi-week expedition? How are the bugs in your area? What other gear are you thinking of bringing? (For example, if you are relying on a lot of down gear — down sleeping bag, down parka or sweater, etc. — then keeping it dry will be critical; synthetics will likely be heavier, but let you carry maybe a less enclosed shelter which may itself be lighter.)

    If you're just starting out backpacking, then it may make sense to do some car camping and shorter excursions to test out your gear. Also, hiking with buddies a few times before setting out solo may be helpful, to get familiar with using the gear, packing and arranging your load and backpack setup, setting up camp, navigating, cooking and storing food, and so on. It's a lot to expect, to learn all of the basics on your first trip out; so being close to home base can afford some peace of mind as you learn. Certain packing strategies are possible with a group that are not possible on your own: one person can carry the mess kit and stove, another can carry the tent, etc.

    If you want to let us know the kinds of areas you'll be entering and the kinds of trips you hope to undertake, then I and others here can better advise you on the best shelters for the job. And that will really be more of a list of options than a final word on one best choice. The main thing with any gear is to understand its properties — what does it do well, what does it do poorly? Then you can understand how it may or may not apply to your situation.

    Good luck!
