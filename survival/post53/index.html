    Orienteering in this type of Forest (Pine Barrens?)

    The topographical map will not be useless. It will give you a lot of information, sometimes information you didn't yet know would be useful.

    Several have already mentioned the tree-by-tree method of orienteering in a forest without large landmarks. For each bearing, aim for a distant tree that will remain well visible as you proceed. The farther away the object from which you take your bearing, the more accurate the course of travel; but if you get confused about which tree you're heading toward or if you lose sight of it going down a swale you could go off course.

    You may not have many landmarks in your line of sight, but the Pine Barrens does have rivers, roads, trails, ridges. If these play into the territory you'll be passing through, you may not have to aim directly at a distant object or do tree-by-tree navigation. You can head in the general direction of a feature that you are sure to meet (say, a road) and then travel along that feature until you come to your destination or next jumping-off point.

    With this method, you aim off. That is, you intentionally aim to the left or right of your desired jumping-off point, then head in the opposite direction along the feature until you reach it. So if you aimed off to the right, once you reach the road you turn to the left and follow it. This is an intentional margin for error. If you had aimed directly at a point on the road, upon reaching the road you wouldn't know if errors in navigation would have brought you to the left or right of your destination, so you would have to venture back and forth to find it.

    The extra time it takes to travel the margin of error along the feature is usually made up by not having to take repeated accurate bearings. You can just generally follow the heading established by following the arrow or degree reading on your compass ring determined at the start.

    With straight features, like many roads in the Pine Barrens, you can take an angular reading with your compass and a topo map to come up with a good general heading. If you can triangulate this heading with another feature shown on the map (river, power lines, etc.) so much the better.

    You may also make good use of the Swedish Pole Compass method, which was developed for use in very similar terrain â€” flat, featureless dense brush and forest. It's claimed to be much more accurate for close-quarters navigation than taking compass readings every few yards. I haven't used the method myself yet, but having studied and made use of many of Mors Kochanski's teachings I have reason to be confident about its effectiveness. Of course, one should try it out for oneself under various conditions before making definitive statements about how well it works.

    Using the Swedish Pole Compass, Mors says "you'll be navigating in spectacular fashion."
