    Axe or Machete Question

    EndlessAutumn is right: >You're comparing two completely different tools that are suited to perform two completely different tasks, tasks which are very dependent on the area you plan to be in. An axe is used to chop and process hardwoods and a machete is used to clear out dense undergrowth. Since you'll be staying in a temperate forest environment, an axe is the best choice.

    As with all things, context is vital. What kinds of situations will you be entering? What seasons and climates will you be dealing with? What kind of shelter will be available to you â€” a cabin, a teepee, a hut that you make, or a tent that you carry, or....? In short, how much will your survival hinge on the ability to generate warmth through firewood?

    You will need the conditions that allow you enough comfort to get 8 hours of sleep per night; much less, and you're in a state of attrition, and it's only a matter of time before body and/or mind give out. What will you need to carry, acquire, or make in order to achieve those necessary conditions?

    In a temperate forest you're dealing with a lot of hardwood, and the cutting is tough. Machete is good for jungle conditions, tackling reedy, pithy saplings and brush; it is also useful in general bushwhacking and hacking at wrist-size and smaller soft lumber. But when it gets to the tougher going with hardwoods, you'll be wasting a lot of energy and wearing out your blade edge before you make any significant progress.

    Also, the colder the situation, the more you will depend on an insulated and consistently dependable shelter. Your need for firewood to generate calories of heat will increase by multiples. In deep cold, count on a car-sized pile of wood per day. That is, if you know how to use fire to efficiently heat yourself; if not, you may have to double the amount of cut wood.

    This brings us to the next point: the amount and type of gear you bring should be appropriate to the amount of knowledge, skill, and physical ability you have. Since you are asking the axe vs. machete question, we can gather that you don't have a lot of experience using an axe. That's quite a long learning curve before you can say that you can dependably and safely acquire and make whatever you need each day. Hearing which piece of gear is better according to an open discussion, or even according to a group of experts, is a far cry from yourself knowing intimately the benefits and drawbacks of each item, through actual experience. But that is precisely the type of knowledge you should have before you let your life depend on any survival tool.

    You should have training in felling trees, sectioning them, cutting firewood, splitting and notching for shelters, splitting for kindling, dangers and precautions for all of those operations, and sharpening and maintaining your axe.

    If you're in a moderate-weather situation and hiking long distances with portable shelters, a hatchet or short forest axe will be preferable because of its lighter weight and portability. If you are in a cold-weather situation or otherwise have to do lots of cutting, or if you have the option of staying put for longer periods, a long-handled axe is not only more efficient but safer. A wood handle is much better in the cold. You may want to bring an extra handle and know how to replace it, or learn and practice making your own. You should always have an extra handle available, whether you bring it or make it yourself, because if your axe handle breaks what will you use to make a new one?

    The handle should be straight (neither warped nor twisted), and the grain should ideally line up with the axis of the axe head rather than at an angle to it. Since you'll only be bringing one axe, it should be a compromise between felling and splitting tools; that is, the head should have straight or slightly convex sides rather than either very concave or very convex. The head should be very firm on the handle. Don't use the back of the head (the poll) for hammering, or you may splay the hole and ruin the axe.

    The most useful length for bushcraft will have the handle just fit in your armpit with your hand wrapped around the head. The safest length will have the head bury itself in the ground when swung at arm's length, rather than in your foot (as with a small forest axe) or your leg (as with a hatchet).

    If your survival will depend on the ability to acquire large amounts of wood, you may do well to also bring a sturdy bucksaw and extra blades, as this can get through thick logs at least as quickly and safely as with an axe. The saw is not a substitute for the axe, though. Survival saws and handsaws won't be good enough for high-volume acquisition; it has to be a full-on bucksaw that uses your entire range of arm movement. As with the axe, try it out and prove the amount of work that can be done before you stake your life on it.
