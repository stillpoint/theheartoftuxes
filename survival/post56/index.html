    Still working on my hand drill- smoke, smoke, more smoke, no ember.

    Have you checked your materials — are they the best you can find for bowdrill? The wood of the spindle is especially important, and after that, the board. If you get a polished spindle end and board dimple without getting an ember, that means that your spindle and/or board are too hard.

    You should also check that the spindle end and the depression it goes into have not been contaminated with lubricant — anything from sap to oil from the hands — that would diminish the friction you're trying to achieve.

    One of the most common issues with a stubborn bowdrill setup is the coolness and moisture from the ground drawing away the heat. If your pile of valuable dust gets damp or is in contact with a cold surface it will be very hard to get it going. Sometimes the leaf or other item you place beneath the board to insulate the dust pile from the ground is the very item adding moisture to the dust or drawing heat from it; so this part also needs to be chosen with care.

    In terms of the procedure:

    First of all, I recommend talking to your board and bow, spindle and handhold, and ask them if they will help you make fire. You can also talk to the fire-to-be and ask it to appear so you can learn how to bring warmth and light to the world.

    This step is part of some traditional native training, and is of course left out in much modern training because it's assumed to be ineffective. I had no qualms against this instruction, and carried it out sincerely; and I believe it helped me out tremendously. Perhaps it's a psychological trick, increasing your intimacy with the process and your sensitivity to the feedback of the tools; or perhaps there are other good reasons why it helps.

    Next, I would say be meticulous about your form. An expert bushcrafter can pretty much tell by a person's posture and form in movement whether they will achieve fire or not. Check the posture. See that the tools are the right size, sized to your body. Check that the spindle is upgright and the bow is at a right angle. Make sure the forearm is locked in front of the leg, so pressure is controlled by leaning in rather than through more exhausting and crude muscular force.

    Save your strength for the last part of the process. Gradually add pressure incrementally as you see the signs of a potential coal forming. Start light and pretty fast at first. Once the first wisps of smoke start to rise, you can increase pressure; but you shouldn't be anywhere near full force before that precious dust has built up to reach the end of the spindle.

    It's when the smoke becomes more plentiful that you can kick it into high gear, adding the pressure and speed that heat the spindle end into a coal-igniting object.

    You'll eventually know pretty well when the ember is formed by the type and amount of smoke arising. At that point, it's important to relax. Tension and haste can ruin that newborn ember, so take a moment to breathe, and even maybe say a prayer.

    Rather than breathing on the ember, which transmits moisture from the breath, you can gently waft some air with the hand. It is more the pace of a palm frond being moved by a servant fanning the queen in a sword-and-sandal movie than a bye-bye wave or the flitting of a debutante's fan. If you focus on what's actually happening to the ember rather than what you want to happen, you can see how the smallest air current causes it to glow.

    Good luck!
