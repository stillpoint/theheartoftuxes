    What is a good survival knife?

    Well, we do our best, don't we? We do our best to increase our chances based on what we can realistically predict. And it's the human capacity to predict well that has brought us to the top of the food chain. But you are right if you are saying that we mustn't assume we know what will happen. We do our best, and we may also at the same time hedge our bets. We try to get the best knife for situations that will realistically arise for us, and we may also try to get something versatile enough to be adequate even in unpredictable events.

    When people leap to make suggestions without asking about the situation, they're carrying out a narrow-minded and frankly amateurish approach. It reflects their opinion or what they themselves are comfortable with, not what is best for the person making the inquiry.

    For example, if you're heading out to the wilderness often, with limited gear, then you should know that chances are you'll need a knife that works well fashioning tools and acquiring kindling from wood. That becomes important in a "survival knife" in minimalist, wilderness situations. But if your area of operation is almost exclusively urban, then in "survival situations" you'll likely be getting your resources from man-made sources, and so strength, durability, and perhaps being public-appropriate or easily concealed will be of more concern than a good woodcrafting shape and grind.

    Further... if you are in an earthquake zone you might want a honking big prybar of a knife; if you are in a place where violence is a clear possibility (and if your skills accommodate the choice) you may start to consider defensive aspects of a knife. Living near the equator, suitability for arctic conditions won't be a big concern, and vice versa.

    In terms of gear, the presence or absence of portable shelter and food will change the demands placed on the knife. Without shelter, fire and fuel may become critical factors, so the ability to cut down to dry wood, create effective tinder when necessary (sometimes feather sticks are the only option), and gather large amounts of wood for fuel will (or should) influence your knife choice. Likewise, if you will have to boil water to disinfect it, fire and hence fire-related knife design become top priorities.

    In fact, every part of one's gear is very interdependent: having or not having a certain tool or resource tends to change the appropriate configurations in the rest of your kit. So whether you have access to a bug-out bag, a car bag, a home lay-low kit, full-on backpacking gear, an ultralight or primitive hiking kit, a simple EDC set, or whether you carry/access absolutely no extra gear, certain aspects of a knife will change in priority. That's not to say that it wouldn't be nice to have everything in a knife; but we should all know that that is not possible. There are trade-offs to almost every parameter in knife design — what you gain in heft, you lose in portability and concealment; advances in sharpness may come at the cost of durability or ease of maintenance; a plain edge is best for carving tools, while a serrated edge helps you cut cordage and synthetic materials without the need to sharpen for longer periods; and the examples go on and on.

    Choice of gear, like all things in life, is utterly tied to context. There is no single ultimate choice — to think so would be participating in a little boy's fantasy — there is only what works well according to the situation, and that may be a very specific thing or it may be a wide range of choices. If we are serious about survival, we have to get real about it and ground our choices in our actual life, not vague ideas based on TV dramas or other people's opinions.
