    Which Wetterlings/Gransfors Bruks axes are best for long term survival?

    Wilderness survival, or urban disaster preparedness, or some other scenario?

    Working from home or base camp, long-term shelter, portable shelter, or make your own?

    There are still many questions to be answered, not the least of which is why you think you need an axe and how well you will be able to use it to shape your environment to your needs.

    For survival prep:

    • Hurricanes and storms should be on your radar (no pun intended) in terms of preparedness. Deep cold is not as much of an issue, but stable waterproof shelter, water acquisition, and the ability to bug out quickly should be high priorities. I'd also think in terms of a raft or canoe in case of flooding, with a strategy for escaping the scene rather than hunkering down. In that case, a heavy axe may be more of a burden than a benefit.
    • If you'll be dealing with vine-covered woods and swamps, a hatchet may take on branches of various types and sizes, but a parang or machete will be more comfortable and may be of more use than any kind of axe.
    • In rainy or otherwise wet conditions you will need to get to the center of any wood you find, to expose the dry stuff; a long axe is the efficient solution (a slightly convex head will help with splitting in that case, but won't be as good at felling), and hatchets and long blades won't help a bit. In that scenario, you might look at carrying or making hardwood wedges and bringing a mallet or hammer-capable axe (many axes can be destroyed if you use the poll of the head to hammer with).
    • If you expect to be in the woods more and will require large amounts of wood, a full-length American-style axe will be a better choice since its larger and heavier head can attack hardwood better.
    • In urban situations, I'd lean toward demolition and extraction tools (prybars, crowbars, fire axes) more than wood acquisition (felling axes).

    In northern regions in cold seasons, wood acquisition is crucial, as it provides the heat that keeps you alive.
