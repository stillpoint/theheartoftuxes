    TheHeartofTuxes

    In situations where a signal fire will be either your best bet or helpful to increase your odds of rescue, it would be wise to...

    1. Lay the entire setup ahead of time.

    2. Prepare at least one backup, if not more, ahead of time.

    3. Create it so as to protect the setup from rain or snow, even if it is a clear day when you first set it up.

    4. Carry a portable version for immediate use when you're scouting, foraging, or otherwise separated from your established setups.

    The ideal signal fire will have a fast-lighting, intensely hot combustible base and a very substantial mass of smoke-producing material above it. Proceed on the assumption that you have only one chance to get your message across, but prepared to duplicate your efforts very quickly as well. This means that the smoke-producing material should be very copious, to create massive clouds of smoke for a long time.

    Smoke will take a minute or two to even clear the tops of tall trees, and on windy days will be blown sideways and dissipated to some extent. Speed of lighting, intense fire to quickly produce smoke, and large amount of smoke-producing material are critical factors. This is not a time to go small.

    The other critical issue is that your smoke-producing material should not douse your fire. It is better to suspend that mass over the fire at first rather than just pile it onto a fire that may go out.

    Fast, intense heat is achieved with clumps of dry, dead twigs as can be found on the lower branches of pine and spruce trees. The resinous nature of these softwoods adds to their volatility. The size of twigs should range from matchstick size or smaller to pencil-thick. Entire branches can be folded onto each other to bring the combustible material into mutual involvement. Leave an average of finger-width of space between sticks, with less between smaller twig ends. Folding the ends of the branch over breaks some of the twigs, helping them catch fire more easily. This bundle can be bound with cordage to keep it in ideal configuration for immediate lighting. A hug-size or bigger bundle should be used for established signal fire setups, and one that fits under the crook of one arm is portable enough for impromptu use when moving about.

    One of the great advantages of a pre-bound bundle is that you can lift and move it around in position to accept whatever flame you start it with. Once that first little tongue of flame appears you can lift and turn the bundle to take best advantage of it rather than scrambling to adjust a setup lain on the ground. Another advantage is that you can light it in front of or higher than your face so you don't get smoke and flame in your eyes.

    Add whatever accelerants and extenders you can manage, to guarantee the fastest, hottest, most certain ignition. Natural fluffy tinders along with resinous pitch, insect repellant, rubber, alcohol or other carried fuels and firestarters increase your ability to start the fire at a moment's notice. Most of these can be inserted into the bundle ahead of time, ready to be lit.

    You can make a frame to hold the green material suspended over the fire. Two long poles driven into the ground at an angle and forming a long shallow V configuration form the basis of the frame. Cross-pieces can be added. The green material should be piled up large, and will help form a protective barrier against rain; but you may want to add other cover like a tarp as insurance.

    Since the fire will necessarily be large and fast-burning, take extra care to clear the area around it so as to prevent a forest fire.

    The portable bundle will also burn intensely enough to quickly build a roaring fire that can then accept whatever smoke-producing material you can grab from the surroundings.

    ~

    On another note: Be aware that proper signal mirrors are far, far superior to locker mirrors and other reflective surfaces; and even among true signal mirrors there's a great deal of variation as to how far they can throw a contiguous beam of reflected light. Don't assume; test out your equipment to understand its limitatons.
