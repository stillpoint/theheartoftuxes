    This previous post, with nested links, makes a lot of points about seeking out a teacher.

    1. The first thing is to keep clearing up your life direction. It's not something that happens in one fell swoop, not for most of us anyway; you need to keep revisiting the issue: "What am I on about in life? What is the right use of my time, my life, my effort, my faculties? What are my days for?"

    When your direction is clear, you won't be misled, you won't attach to shiny, stimulating lures that ultimately lead nowhere. When you know deeply, in your bones, in your heart, that your purpose is to wake up and help others, already you will have an affinity for and connection to enlightened lineages and teachers.

    1. Next, you try. You dip your toes in the waters. You take appropriate steps into the unknown. You expose yourself to new possibility.

    It doesn't mean you have to give up your life and follow what someone says; no, not at all! As you progress, as you garner more experience and more information, you become able to make certain choices about where you'd like to be and what kind of environment would be beneficial.

    1. Your steps are taken in good balance with your unfolding experience. When you try a method for a while and find that you become more sane, not less, then you might have a bit of reasonable faith that allows you to commit more deeply. When you see for yourself that the teachers and (senior) disciples in a particular lineage carry themselves with honesty and integrity — that their actions match their words, and that their intent is sincere even in unguarded moments — then you might have a basis of confidence from which to investigate further. When you experience the discomfort of your old egoic patterns being challenged, alongside the warm kindness, truthfulness, and openness to scrutiny that are the hallmarks of good training, then you might consider that a good growing environment.

    2. Valid 'enlightened lineages' have a tradition; they're not pop-up enterprises. They generally have a record of their lineage, and their masters have been verified by other masters. You can ask for lineage documents or an account of the history of the school. These things are confirmable; they go beyond an organization just marketing or trumpeting its own horn. (Some original documents will be protected for obvious reasons, but an account of the lineage transmission should be available in some shape or form.)

    You can also ask on forums like Dharma Wheel, or consult with practitioners that you respect: "Do you know this teacher/school? Is it for real? Anything I should know about it?"

    1. Human nature being what it is, it's rare to find a school completely without controversy. But there should at least be some transparency and willingness to own whatever shadowy material may be part of their history. The vast majority of masters are exceedingly clear, compassionate, and forthright, and have your best interests at heart. If you stay grounded in reality and don't grasp at dreams and quick fixes, you'll be well equipped to sniff out the very rare situations that are not good for you. If you have reason to be concerned at any time, you can easily do a Google search or look names up on cult watch websites.

    The biggest problem usually comes from your own fear, grasping, and speculation; and those find play when you keep yourself isolated. It's like a long-distance relationship: as soon as the parties are not in actual contact, the relationship begins to enter the realm of imagination and second-guessing. Direct face-to-face experience puts so many doubts to rest, one way or another — you discover that you can take another step together, or you discover that it's time to move on. Every real-life experience adds to your wisdom. But when you only fret and imagine, you create many monsters and fairy-tale endings that don't actually have basis in reality. So getting out there and trying is necessary.

    1. Finally, feel free to ask me about a teacher or school you're getting involved with, if you have reason to trust what I might recommend; or ask on Dharma Wheel website.
