    TheHeartofTuxes

    Studying and practicing in isolation causes your own blind spots and habits to be woven into your understanding and practice. Learning with a proper Dharma teacher is necessary. Learning and practicing with others is necessary, so they can reflect back to you the places where you get stuck in your old patterns or don't see clearly.

    Only when the teaching and training have become firmly seated in your conception and action (i.e., in your character) should you consider periods of isolation for the purpose of intensive, focused practice.

    In the Zen teaching of the Ox-herding pictures, after the 'ox' is caught and tamed, one returns to town. Although you might sometimes retreat for the purpose of practice, when you 'get' Dharma you share it with others. It is for everyone; and if we get into the habit of hoarding Dharma for ourselves it only ingrains our own selfishness and blocks attainment.

    We have a very strong motive to stay isolated: it's because then we get to have our own way, hold onto our set opinions, think of ourselves as superior, and never have to make real changes. So practicing together with others is very good in that regard, as it provides a mirror for us, an accountability.

    And then when we start to learn and digest right practice, at that point we can take periods of retreat to practice without distraction; but we always come back to relationship. In a sense, Dharma is relationship.

    >"Spiritual friendship is the whole of the path."

    The big mistake of self-isolated practice -- and it's a mistake that is greatly exacerbated by online culture -- is the notion that one can do it all by oneself and get it all for oneself. The idea "my practice" is a huge, nasty obstacle; it's a practice with egotism woven into its very fabric.

    What can you do?

    • Find a certified teacher in a valid tradition, and study with them regularly

    • Practice with others, not only formally but by working and enjoying together in community. It's necessary to learn not only sitting meditation but everyday relationship: what is correct thought, speech, and action moment to moment, in all kinds of life situations?

    This training should be done in a community with clear Dharmic direction, in which you can learn from, support, and inspire each other with the right motives. (And so it is also necessary, or at least ideal, for the community to be led by someone who can consistently hold the Dharmic view, a view beyond ego, opinion, and speculation.)

    • Embrace the importance of compassion in Dharma. Do compassion-building practice like Metta, Tonglen, or other Bodhicitta-generating practice.

    Make a point of offering acts of kindness and generosity toward others -- don't miss even the smallest opportunity to offer kindness throughout the day.

    • Take care of your life in a balanced way, attending to practical and relational as well as spiritual matters. You won't be able to practice spirituality well if you're scrambling to survive, or if your physical or mental health is in chaos. You won't be able to digest spiritual teaching if your relationships are atrophied: the difference between merely understanding Dharma and embodying it becomes apparent in relationship.

    Avoid thinking of Dharma as one thing and everyday life as another. This, too, you learn through consistent study with a teacher, in a tradition, in community; you learn how to merge Dharma and life.
