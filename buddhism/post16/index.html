    How to be compassionate and at the same time not let people use you (or hurt you emotionally)?

    Compassion does not look like anything in particular.

    That should sink in.... People think of compassion in terms of certain nice activities, and by then trying to be compassionate they limit themselves to those activities, mistrust their own intuition, and distance themselves from the living moment. But the mind of compassion is vastly greater than any plan or preconception.

    Compassion is an aspect of the enlightened mind. (It is one of the Brahmaviharas — the Divine Abidings — in Buddhism that express these aspects of enlightenment.) As such, it is an aspect of your innate, original mind. That is to say; your being is, from the very beginning, perfectly compassionate. You — we — have just learned to mistrust our original being.

    You can experience compassion as your original mind when you are not thinking. At times when you are clear and free of any agenda, compassion is expressed naturally. A simple example is when someone is stumbling, and before you know it you reach out to help them. This happens with the same immediacy with which you reach out for a wall or lamp post to save yourself when you are stumbling.

    So putting down your pre-arranged strategies and your held opinions brings you closer to trust in your original mind of compassion. Before thinking arises, compassion is at work. When a need appears, it responds perfectly. When a need doesn't appear, it doesn't move. In this respect, compassion is like a mirror reflecting the ever-changing situation. When you are cold, you put on a sweater. When someone else is cold, you warm then up.

    But when someone tries to take advantage of you, maybe compassion puts a stop to it. You may disengage from contact, leaving the other party alone in their own attitude until they can wake up to what they are doing. Maybe clear communication is necessary, maybe a series of stronger boundaries... or maybe you accept the behavior without letting it move your mind (bearing the cross, so to speak). The point is that it is not a strategy, but something that springs forth spontaneously in the moment.

    So compassion requires your radical trust in yourself. When you witness this radical trust in someone else it it's quite remarkable, and can be life-changing. Sometimes you may marvel at the strangely creative responses that appear, but at some level there is an awareness that they are precisely appropriate to the situation.

    In practicing — training — compassion, the other side of self-trust is that compassion is never “for me". One practices self-trust with no tinge of gaining idea. So paradoxically, the sooner you put down your ideas about how to get what you think you deserve, the sooner your original mind will function in your defense when necessary. Compassion doesn't have opposites, and doesn't discriminate between mine and yours.

    This aspect is trained automatically in objectless meditation, which is an uncompromising trust; and it is trained overtly in meditations that call up feelings and intentions of benefit toward others, and in the perfect bodhicitta offering-of-merit, where every last iota of benefit you may gain in life is offered up to all others.

    Because ultimately compassion has no self and other; it is all-inclusive. Offering a hand is the universe offering a hand to itself. And sometimes a punch in the face may be the universe maintaining its own integrity.
