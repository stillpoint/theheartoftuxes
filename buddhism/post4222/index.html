    What is buddha's view on suicide? What is "wrong" with doing it?

    You might be interested in Zen Master Philip Kapleau's book The Wheel Of Life And Death, where he tackles death-related issues like suicide, euthanasia, abortion, reincarnation, vegetarianism, and so on.

    This is an excerpt from the book:

    >The ordinary suicide may think he or she is escaping a life of misery, pain, or financial or other difficulties, but life and death are part of an ongoing process, and anyone who understands this will realize that one does not escape one's misery in one life and avoid having to deal with it in another. (See "Karma" and "Rebirth" [in the book]). A friend once told me, "Many times I considered killing myself - particularly in my younger days - but each time the idea came up I thought to myself, 'If I think it's bad now, what will it be like if I kill myself? Then, not only will I still have to face what is unresolved for me now, but I will have the added karmic burden of the suicide to deal with as well - not to mention the guilt arising from my awareness of the pain to others that my self-inflicted death caused...."

    In terms of the Buddha's teachings: the first of the panca-sila, the five lay precepts, is a commitment to refrain from killing. That would include refraining from killing oneself. The Theravadin approach to the precepts is quite strict. The Mahayana and Vajrayana approaches may provide room for applying the precepts differently based on wisdom and in relation to particular situations.

    I have never seen it regarded that killing oneself "pays off" karma, only that it tends to add to and complicate one's karmic burden, and ends the opportunity to clear karma in the current life. I'm afraid that I have no citations to share with you, though.

    Buddhist teaching, including the original teachings of the Buddha, are not couched in a system of good versus bad, but rather in a system of cause and effect. The primary cause is the mind.

    >Mind is the forerunner of all actions. All deeds are led by mind, created by mind. (From The Dhammapada)

    So it is not the mere act of suicide that determines the resulting karmic fruits, but the state of mind and intent behind the act. The intent to escape one's situation is attachment-based, so it will have attachment-based results, contrary to the Buddhist way. There have been examples, though, in both the Pali scriptures and in modern history, of awakened people committing suicide with (arguably) attachment-free intent: to benefit others directly, or to progress on the path (and thus benefit others). But the Buddha, to my knowledge, has never been recorded as condoning suicide.

    Just a note of clarification: the Tipitaka (Sanskrit: Tripitaka) is what many consider to be the complete collection of Theravada Buddhist Sutras (teachings of the Buddha), discourses, and codes of conduct. The Tipitaka doesn't include Mahayana or Vajrayana Buddhist texts.

    Pali is the language of the first Sutras. Later sutras appeared in Sanskrit, followed by texts in other languages.
