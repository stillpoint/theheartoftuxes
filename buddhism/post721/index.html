    Forget enlightenment. I just want to make it through the day.

    How do you get what you want? That's a useful point.

    If you see the stream of cause and effect very clearly you can see that even self-improvement doesn't finish the job, and that chaos and decay always have a foothold. As you suggest, just making it through the day has its difficulties. This is because the entire substance of our lives by its very nature is not satisfactory -- everything is always changing, changing, changing. As much as we try to lift up one part, another part tumbles down.

    If we don't see this clearly, we can fool ourselves into thinking we can 'win' at the game of life. For a while, we can have the advantage. But then flaws become mistakes and mistakes become disaster. Sickness, aging, and death come upon us and can't be stopped. Only one thing is not subject to this process, to this around-around change. So if we see very clearly, then we see that this one not-changing thing is of utmost value. If your happiness depends on something that is always changing, happiness will come and go and will not be complete. Lasting happiness has to be rooted in something that doesn't change. So what is that?

    ~

    When we become aware of a thing of value, the newness of the awareness carries us with inspiration. But very soon it becomes so easy to be distracted. We go down sideroads for years -- whole phases of our lives spent chasing the wrong things, unproductive things, and things that in the end don't address the basic problem, which is that we don't know who we are and we don't know the meaning of life and death. We haven't grasped the one unchanging thing.

    When you are caught up in the sideroads (in the 'ratrace', you might say), considering the big question of life-and-death can seem distant and unreal. But if you do look into the matter you'll find that it's the most practical thing in the world. How can you arrange your priorities if you don't know the point of it all? Not based on a philosophy or dogma or wishful thinking, but actually seeing and knowing the reason for all that you might try. And when sooner or later the reality of loss and death are right up in your face, then guaranteed, you will start to sense the value of looking into what it's all about.

    Self-improvement is not forbidden; it's necessary. If your life is not somewhat in order, there's no way your mind will be clear and sensitive to the truth. Though the aim is to grasp the truth, along the way many things could very well get easier and more clear for you. Or not. But the central point of Buddhism is a completely different matter. It's not about improving on reality; it's about attaining it.
