    What do you call a Buddhist monk?

    Perhaps the best solution is to ask them or one of their sangha members or attendants how to address them.

    In general, you can say Sir and speak respectfully to them. To a nun you could say Madam, but it maybe sounds more awkward.

    The term Venerable can be used for just about any monastic or teacher. "Hello, Venerable, how are you?"

    Really, most monks and nuns and higher beings have no interest in being glorified; and furthermore for the most part they already have a lot of experience in the awkwardness of cultural interface and terms of respect, so they understand very well that sometimes people don't know what to call them. No insult is taken. They are there for your benefit, and don't care about titles.

    There are overlaps of tradition, lineage, and ethnic culture when dealing with terms of address. You need to learn the custom within the particular situation you're visiting. In many cases there is no good English equivalent for the foreign terms of address.

    • South Asian monks are addressed as bhante (bahn-tay) = "venerable one".

    You can also call a monk bikkhu and a nun bikkhuni (beek-koo/beek-koo-nee) = "ordained" / "female ordained", but the respectful address of bhante is more universally appropriate.

    • Thai monks: If in doubt as to whether the teacher is ordained as a monk or nun, you can use the respectful term ajahn (a-jann) = "respected (religious) teacher". The term is equivalent to the Sanskrit acharya.

    Tahn (tawn) = "high monk" can be used before an eminent monk's dharma name as a title.

    • Highly respected Tibetan monks and nuns are addressed as rinpoche (rin-po-chay) = "precious jewel" or "precious one"

    High-level monastics and other enlightened beings may be addressed as "your eminence".

    The Dalai Lama is addressed as "your holiness".

    • Vietnamese monks are called su (soo) = "monk", and nuns are called su co (su kho) = "sister monk" or "miss monk".

    Many monks, but especially head monks and teachers are addressed as thay (tie) = "teacher" or "master", which is the term of respect you can use in any situation. (They will also refer to themselves by this title rather than saying "I" or "me".)

    • Korean monks and nuns are calld sunim (s'-neem or soo-neem) = monk/nun.

    Korean Zen Masters are called soen sa (sohn saw) = "zen master", and when addressed directly soen sa nim (sohn saw neem) = "honored zen master". Very eminent Zen Masters are called dae soen sa nim (day sohn saw neem) = honored great zen master.

    Korean lay teachers are called soen saeng nim (sohn sayng neem) = "teacher", but the term is often used for anyone respected or older, especially older men.

    • Japanese teachers are called sensei = literally "senior" or "born before me" = "teacher".

    Masters and lineage holders are called roshi = "old master", which refers to the spiritual head of a community but is commonly used to mean "zen master" (but is not strictly equivalent, as 'roshi' can have non-religious use as well).

    • Chinese monks and nuns (and lay teachers) are called sifu (shrr-foo) = "master teacher".

    Chinese monastics can also be addressed by adding fa shi (faw shrr) = "dharma master" to the end of their monastic names.

    The term lao shi (laow shrr) = "old teacher" can be used to refer to senior teachers or respected professors.
