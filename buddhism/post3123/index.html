    The unbearable "meh-ness" of sitting

    You're finding it hard to let go because you are human, and you have a lot of holding-on patterns ingrained in you. That's like all of us. It may be that you have a harder time than others, or it may be that you're just one of us. The main thing is to keep practicing letting go, and not to focus on your situation, or on results, or on a desired state.

    Difficulty with letting go is karma; it's the result of the way you've used your mind and intent for many years (and, Buddhists may say, for countless lifetimes). The more you practice letting go, the more you will digest your karma. The more you focus on "I can't let go" or "Why can't I let go?", the more karma you make. So only do it.

    When you do bodybuilding, at first you can only lift a little weight. The more you train, the more you can lift; but you can't lift more without training. There's nothing wrong with not being able to lift a great amount of weight; it just means you have some training ahead of you. The same is true of the power of concentration.

    Here's a suggestion that may help: In our modern world, our energy tends to be stuck up in our heads. In the past we were active and engaged with the natural world; our bodies were in constant motion, our senses were continuously open; so our energy was very grounded — it was down in the body. Nowadays, we relate to the world through thinking, thinking, thinking. We don't actually live in the world, we live in our thinking about it. And we even try to solve the problem of our over-thinking by trying to figure out what we're doing wrong — more thinking!

    So helping the energy sink down into the body can simplify your relationship with life and practice; i.e., it can simplify your relationship with yourself. Bowing/prostration practice is a very effective way of helping the energy come down out of the thinking center and into the being center (zen masters and taoist sifus all point to the lower belly as the basic site of cultivation). Also, any repetitive, strenuous, whole-body activity can help, such as rowing, swimming, running. Free dancing can also be wonderful.

    When doing a full-body practice, it can be easy to just do it automatically and let the mind wander, looking around or thinking about what you're going to get from it, etc. Resist this tendency. Just keep bringing your attention back to the physical movements and sensations. This will train your concentration while helping to settle and simplify your mind.

    When one has not yet developed enough concentration power to bring the mind back consistently to the object of meditation, physical practice is necessary, to ground the mind in the body. That's why physical labor has also been an extremely important part of many meditative traditions.
