    > It is usually a cop-out/excuse for that leader's hypocrisy or misconduct. It's just a deflection that seeks to inhibit critical thought and discussion.

    That may be so for someone else; maybe you've encoutered a lot of people who think that way. In my case it's not true. If you read my posting history you'll see that time and again I recommend and urge proper research and reasoned investigation, in balance with escalating commitment, so a prospective student goes into the student-teacher relationship with eyes open, and doesn't overstep. When evidence is there to warrant greater commitment, then one can go forward with self-responsibility.

    We'll never find complete safety in the world, but we don't have to abandon reason or self-jurisdiction.

    I also consistently recommend clarification of one's correct direction in life. This is the foundation of correct student-teacher relationship, one where the student is empowered because they are clear what they're on about. It is when people are not clear about their direction that they grasp at straws; and it is when people grasp that they can be manipulated. Part of a teacher's role is to expose that grasping; and Trungpa Rinpoche did it in very radical, fiery ways.

    So we may differentiate between the aim of coming out unscathed and the aim of being transformed.

    Buddhist historian Rick Fields said that Trungpa "caused more trouble, and did more good, than anyone I'll ever know."

    I wouldn't argue or begrudge you the right to warn others of abusive teachers. By the same token, I wouldn't begrudge prospective students of wild masters the right to step into tumult for the sake of awakening â€” and many did gain awakening through Trungpa's teaching.

    I don't say 'bad' or 'good'. He was like a fire, that could warm, illuminate, bestow life, burn, overwhelm, destroy.

    Just to be clear, if I were in position to give the recommendation, I might send one in ten thousand students to a teacher like Trungpa; others I would strongly dissuade, as you are doing. As it stands, we can still benefit from the teachings of his that remain, stripped of (some of) the risk.
