    Ultimately, attainment doesn't depend on any external situation; but in practical application, there are many, many ways we help or hinder ourselves.

    For instance, until one has reached advanced levels it is ideal to sit in a clean and mostly quiet space, or a natural setting with pleasant sounds of wind or flowing water and birds. The clothes one wears shouldn't be restrictive but comfortable, and shouldn't be garish or slovenly but plain and dignified. These things affect the mind.

    Since we attend to the senses of vision, hearing, and touch in creating a good meditation space we can also attend to the sense of smell. And as we know from personal experience, fragrances can influence us quite profoundly. They can quickly get through our layers of armoring and dullness, affecting us on primal levels.

    Incenses differ as to their refinement and effect. Some are exceedingly cheap and offensive; but some are almost unbelievably sublime. There are masterpieces of Japanese incense, for instance — recipes that have been passed down through centuries — that have indescribable, almost mystical effect.

    Just consider the kind of gut-level responses you have to certain fragrances: recoiling from rotting garbage or human waste... the freshness of the ocean that you want to take in to every corner of your being... the stuffy oppression of old-lady perfume... the sweet smell of a puppy... the clarity of a cedar forest....

    Think of how you feel when you are hiking in a silent pine or cedar grove. Some of these ingredients are included in high-quality incense. The ingredients have been savored and researched and chosen for the influence they have on the mind and heart. Some factors are calming, some are invigorating (in Traditional Chinese Medical terminology: 'consiousness reviving', as are many resins like camphor and frankincense), some swoon you into a space wider than your small self.

    If we consider how different flavors have healing or balancing properties — sour lemonade for freshness and vigor; bitter greens to regulate bile flow; sweet dates and breads to provide energy; salty miso to condense and consolidate — we can extrapolate to fragrances. It's a matter of direct experience: we can perceive very clearly how instantly and surely our organism reacts to different fragrances. For instance, you may fancy a certain partner, but if you don't like their smell or how they taste when you kiss them, that's going to be an obstacle; and some people don't get past that, it's a deal-breaker.

    All this to say that incense is a way of providing yourself and others with a certain kind of space. Regardless of what else is going on, burning high quality incense can transform the perceived quality of a space and call up spiritual qualities and intentions.

    In addition, the valuable substances in incense are traditionally considered worthy offerings. With the smoke that rises, our offerings and prayers can be transported. It's neither a materialistic nor a superstitious mechanism. I works on the psychology. One is aware of offering something precious to the precious, and as one perceives the fragrance the prayer — the 'message' is received. The mind that perceives is already Buddha. So Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva is 'One Who Hears Universal Sound'; and that same awakened mind is the 'One Who Perceives Universal Fragrance'.

    Fragrance can then be, like any sense object, the gateway for awakening.

    There's a story about a being from another dimension talking to a human and comparing notes about the Buddhas in each realm and how they teach. When the human said that our Buddha teaches with words, the visitor was shocked: "How can teaching be conveyed with something so limited, so unsubtle?!" The visitor shared that the Buddha in his realm taught through the vehicle of fragrances.
