    5 Minutes Of Death A Day

    > I think what made my father's death much harder was in having unfinished emotional business with him

    Metta or Tonglen practices are ways to heal the relationship even without the physical presence or participation of the other person (or people).

    >...might better be done in reverse order of the order done for metta. Start off with indifferent persons, then... gradually move on to people closer and closer to me.

    In Metta practice it's actually recommended not to start with someone too close, because the attraction or other complications and attachments could overshadow the first dawning of compassion. It is sometimes said that an ideal first subject would be a teacher or even a relative stranger who has offered you something without asking anything in return.

    The issue, I think, is the same as the one you bring up: there may be an inherent chance for upset when dealing with close ties.

    To get an idea of how radically one's mind can change with practice, consider the Tibetan practice of Chöd, wherein one sincerely offers up one's very body. Usually we think of compassion as giving good feeling or good action, but it can extend very naturally and genuinely into giving up our claim on the thing we cling to most dearly: our body.

    >I'm an agnostic about rebirth, so I have doubts about how reassuring reflecting on the good fortune of a human birth will help me, but I will give it a try.

    It's not necessary to believe in rebirth in order to appreciate the tremendously fortunate circumstances you have. For example, many humans don't have the leisure to consider these themes at all; they live in poverty or terror or oppression. Some people don't have the freedom to study and discuss spiritual ideas. Many don't have the physical health or mental capacity to even begin to help themselves wake from the dream of life. Sufficient food, sufficient shelter, the basics of life... relationships that leave openness for higher considerations — all of these are extremely fortunate.

    And you may consider the immense value of even contacting the Dharma, much less being able to understand it, embrace it, and practice it. These teachings are not available in some parts of the world. Even a couple of decades ago, 'Dharma' was relatively unknown — you could say the word and people wouldn't know what you're talking about. But we not only have access to it, but can also meet enlightened beings and be guided by them to our own enlightenment.

    All of this is precious beyond any other wealth. If you contemplate just these points, the truth of impermanence, and the endless trouble of Samsara, your mind will turn toward right practice.
