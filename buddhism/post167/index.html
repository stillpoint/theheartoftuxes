    Just realized which form of meditation is most effective at achieving enlightenment

    I have written to OP about his post in private, and have received his/her invitation to post my response here. In addition to my comments below I encouraged OP to delete the post and just return to simple practice, but that was before I saw any other responses posted.

    ~

    What you write here is not realization but thinking and checking: what's good, what's bad, what's better, what's worse.... It is more of the self-idea trying to get you to engage in thinking and distract you from simple openness.

    Building a self-image as an expert or "someone who knows" is very enticing. This is a problem not only for you but for people who could be misled if what you say is not accurate. After a few experiences in meditation, the notion that you know something can arise; but this is just the next phase in self-deception, the force that draws you away from your sincere practice. Having approached Unknowing, the ego reacts with fear and tries to find a way to make a known self-image, and one that makes you feel strong and certain. This too should be dropped.

    >In a similar fashion, if we want to wire into our brain the experience of no-self and detachment from all phenomena, the best thing we can do is put in hours of practicing creating a space between our "self" and phenomena.

    Why make a thing of no-self? If there is no self, then why would we need to set it up against phenomena and put space between them?

    >When that space becomes so large that the self is completely detached from all phenomena, we experience no-self and the cessation of attachment....

    This is just speculation based on your own point of view. The aim is not detachment from phenomena. Avoiding phenomena or trying to create a space from phenomena may actually increase the sense of selfhood that is in opposition to phenomena.

    >The sort of meditation that most directly creates this space between self and phenomena is the fluid, concentrated observation of phenomena, called khanika-samadhi in pali, or shikantaza in the Zen tradition.

    You don't know whether this meditation or some other is the best. Furthermore, shikantaza is not observation of phenomena.

    >Thus, it is not important to do other forms of meditation....

    That's a sweeping statement that brushes aside the many forms of meditation that have led to enlightenment for many people.

    >It also follows from this logic....

    Please refrain from putting forth your speculation as the final word on things. What seems logical to you now may change as you get actual experience. I encourage you to set aside this kind of "expert" talk and return to the way-seeking mind that brought you here.

    I'm on your side, hoping we find the way and grow up together, all of us. If you still want to stand by your post, would you mind me posting my comments on it publicly?

    Best wishes.

    ~

    OP also asked me to add an explanation of what shikantaza is.

    I'm inclined to refrain for two reasons:

    • Shikantaza is not conceived of by explanation, but attained through doing it.

    • There seems to be quite an attachment to concepts in your post and in your responses, OP, which as yet has not been let go of. Building more concepts on top of what is already there doesn't bode well for the actual practice, which instead requires letting go of all concepts.

    My recommendation is also twofold:

    • Just do the chosen meditation practice; just follow the method. A formal, object-focused practice could be beneficial because it eliminates some vagueness about what to do with the mind, and it gives the practitioner quick feedback when the mind wavers. After some months or years of repeated letting-go of trains of thought, the foundation will be laid for shikantaza practice.

    • Find a teacher and practice community, and practice together. Practicing with others, problems with self-image get purified.

    When there is some indication that opinion has been let go of and sincere openness has returned I'll be happy to share what I can about shikantaza practice. Good luck!
