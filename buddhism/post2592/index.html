    Regarding "A Garland of Advice" by Zurmang Gharwang Rinpoche, I have a few general questions on malas.

    Why label it vanity or materialism? Those are your own ideas.

    So the first point is when you are learning something new you should have some humility. If you can only conceive of shallow reasons, that doesn't mean other reasons don't exist. Furthermore, you can't expect it all to be handed to you on a platter, all at once. If that were possible, the teaching would indeed be very shallow. So you need to show the humility and patience to listen openly even if at first you don't understand or agree with everything.

    The author's guidance is rooted in a Tibetan culture where masters are well-known. Their practice, their compassionate acts, their wise teachings are widely known and deeply respected. It's a culture of humility and respect, where even many so-called common people are exceptionally clear and compassionate. So when a teacher gives an instruction in the Tibetan culture it is not doubted and scoffed at as might happen in the west. Naturally, the author you are reading may not explain every last thing to your satisfaction, since he is used to a different kind of teacher-student dynamic. It is up to you to adjust yourself to your teacher.

    Tibetan Tantric Buddhism is also known as Mantrayana, the vehicle of sacred sound. Many masters in this vehicle exhibit extreme control over mind and energy, and many have actual vision of or communication with spiritual dimensions. We can't conceive of their vision or attainment except through our own clumsy imagining. So when they instruct us on the path leading toward their level of attainment, sometimes we just have to accept their instruction based on the evidence of their wisdom and compassion, not based on our own grasp of every detail.

    In Vajrayana/Mantrayana, there are various phases and qualities of practice. Although there are many paths and practices, each practice is a very specific process, very detailed and meticulous. Sometimes you accumulate, sometimes you purify; sometimes the practice is pacifying, sometimes wrathful (to quickly clear away age-old habits, for example). The different materials of a mala in part harmonize with these various phases and qualities of practice. Far from being materialistic, it recognizes that every physical thing has an energy, a connection to the spiritual dimension, and in fact that everything is made of mind or spirit. To confuse practices and bring in energies and processes counter to the intent of a certain method would be to waste your effort, and in some cases to court disaster.

    For a beginner who is doing a simple mantra mindfulness or concentration practice, the material of the mala may not be significant; but when certain commitments, vows, and empowerments are taken on the stakes are raised and more care is required. Besides the issue of using the appropriate tools for a particular practice, it would not serve you well to begin your practice career with a lazy, nonchalant, or arrogant attitude. So when a great teacher gives you instruction on your chosen path you should, within reason, follow it.

    This raises the point that much confusion arises through the attempt to mix paths or to pick and choose whatever you like from various teachers. If you do this it will be difficult to learn any path deeply. You can use any kind of mala for simple practice. Even a pile of stones moved from one spot to another will do. But if you intend to follow the Rinpoche's path you should follow his instruction.
