    What can you tell me about the endless knot symbol?

    That's a very open question! So much could be said.

    I guess to set a context, the prominence of the endless knot/eternal knot in Tibetan symbology may show the Tibetan aptitude for perceiving all-inclusive patterns. Mandala practice and wisdom permeates Tibetan culture; so there's this shared basic recognition, a sort of meta-understanding of the patterned energetic aspect of life. The endless knot symbol is not just suggestive; there can be a more direct acknowledgement of the truth it expresses, which in itself is multi-layered.

    We should not skip over the fact that this is seen as an auspicious symbol. The innate goodness of the totality it represents is respected.

    The symbol of a twined knot is ancient, and may have its origin (at least partially) in a Naga-related symbology. The Nagas are powerful beings said, by those who perceive them, to have a serpent-like form, to possess wisdom connected to the subterranean or unconscious realms, and to have influence over conditions that affect our lives and practice such as weather and health. Some Nagas take the role of Dharma protectors.

    This connects the knot to similar serpentine twines, like the Caduceus of ancient Greek symbolism. There seem to be common threads of meaning between different cultural expressions of this motif: unity in duality (expressed originally with two entwined snakes, and now through the symmetry of the symbol), and the various dualities that may become unified such as unconscious/conscious and masculine and feminine aspects, and thus sexual power and power in general, in addition to continuity/eternity, movement, interconnection, wisdom from the depths (serpent-borne), undulation, and appearance/disappearance.

    The symbol has a linear nature, a segmented nature, and a complete nature:

    • The line may represent individual pathways, how the life story of any being or phenomenon moves up and down, and in and out of view. Sometimes a life or event seems to end, but really it is just that some other manifestation comes into prominence from our perspective; the original stream of causality is still there, in the background. Also, the natural movement of things is up and down; there is never gain without loss, and vice versa. All story-lines are recognized as interwoven, interconnected, and — if you trace them closely — essentially only one line.

    • The segments, the corners and other areas defined by the lines, may represent what we identify as 'things' (as 'dharmas'). We point to something as a person or being, or event, or object, or quality; but really what we point to is but a corner of a completely integral whole. There is no 'this' without all the rest.

    • The whole symbol may represent all manifestation, the whole universe of universes. From this view of the whole, the weaving lines take the character of vibrancy and vibration, insignificant in themselves but enlivening the whole image with an energetic current. Consider from a design standpoint that a line defines; a line and space forms contrast; repeating lines form rhythm and intensify contrast; multidirectional repeating lines form multidimentional rhythm; and interwoven multidirectional repeating lines add profound interrelationship and the sense of levels and appearance and disappearance.

    Knots are certainly deeply rooted in our consciousness as archetypes. They are perhaps given short shrift as tools, but must have been among the first tools created when hominids started to use cordage, and their relationship to utility, connection, binding (and thus unity), and security is understood by us on a deep primal level. For example, one of the most sacred rites preserved in modern culture, the wedding, is known as 'tying the knot' — which is not merely a binding, but an interweaving and interrelating of lives.

    We can perhaps gain some more intuitive understanding of what this symbol points to by considering the nature of knotted and woven things, how they tighten and loosen, and how pulling on one piece affects the others. Maybe explore the nature of a fabric, whether it's tightly woven or loosely knitted, and begin to see all life as fabric — the one substance of mind.

    Knots are also used in some cultures as a way of making records, which in turn conveys the sense of sealing an event in consciousness and in historical and cultural reality. Knots are a way of passing down knowledge in cultures that had/have no written language.

    We might also draw some conceptual (not historical) connections between the eternal knot and the pentagram of Pythagoras, Wicca, and Five Element theory in Chinese medicine and spirituality, as the pentagram too has interwoven and endless lines, as well as the other aspects of segments and complete symbol.
