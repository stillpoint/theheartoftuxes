    You know, don't you, that Ikkyu practiced zazen diligently? There is a very beautiful account of a period when he was in the monastery, and this story may shed some light on our topic. It happened in the course of his monastic career that Ikkyu's brother monks noticed him becoming sullen, perhaps dour, and withdrawn. He wouldn't come to meals, rather retreating to his meditation cushion more often. This behaviour became more pronounced over time, and the other monks noticed Ikkyu growing pale and thin. Their concern for his health and well-being also grew.

    Eventually his fellow monks asked Ikkyu, "What's happening, Ikkyu; are you sick?" And Ikkyu responded: "I fell in love."

    It is not that Ikkyu had met anyone. He just fell in love. If anyone reading this has ever fallen deeply in love, they can relate to his experience. The stomach turns over and there is no desire to eat. The mind is fixed on the loved one, no matter how events are unfolding. The heart, the mind... one's very life is surrendered to the loved one... or to love itself. The only food that has meaning is love. Self-concern seems like something a thousand miles away, or something from a long-past dream. This is like what Ikkyu was going through. He had not fallen in love with someone or something; he had just fallen in love.

    So we may consider better or worse or should or shouldn't from the perspective of someone deeply in love. These things become very trivial, flimsy. There is no time for that; there is just the fact of love, and this love is everything. This is not different from zazen. In my opinion (and it is just an opinion), zazen and "I fell in love" were not two different things for Ikkyu.
