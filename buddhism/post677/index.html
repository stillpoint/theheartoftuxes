    Planning on getting a tattoo, but would it affect any yet-future renunciation?

    Friend, let's be honest: a tattoo is not going to help you be mindful beyond a very crude level. And other objects would serve equally well. Carrying out the will to return to presence is what will develop mindfulness. It's not based on external support.

    For instance, you could have someone reminding you to be present all the time, but not until you start doing it yourself does it becomes your mindfulness. Reminders are okay for beginners who have little command over their monkey-mind, and for that matter you could wear a mala or tie a string around your neck or wrist to serve the same function. But it sounds like you are committed to making mindfulness an ongoing part of your life; so practice is where the emphasis should be, not on external support.

    Therefore there's another reason why you likely want a tattoo, and it has to do with the decorative or ritual aspect of imprinting something on the body. The conceit involved is not conducive to Dharmic view, which is why the practice was discouraged by the Buddha.

    There is, I believe, a primal desire for initiation in our modern society. We want to experience the rites of passage that have been stripped from our culture, and we want to show the marks of having graduated to new phases of life. We want others to see that we have dared something.

    We -- especially young people at a certain phase of life -- want to step into the power implied by a tattoo or other ritual marking; but the mark is not the power itself, it is an empty form without the quest, challenge, and loss or sacrifice required for entry into new power. Sadly, these rites of passage are lacking in our modern world.

    The traditional rituals, grounded in grounded primal cultures, came on the heels of actual attainments where initiates were recognized as having passed a test, or tribe members were ferried into a new phase of life by the tribe. Along with the symbolic wound marking the occasion, the initiates experienced the real loss of their old way of life -- they could not go back (say, to the protection of their mothers, or to their naive view of life). These were not, as a rule, solitary, self-centered events.

    There is another way I recommend to you, one in which you can experience the groundedness of your attainment and forego the self-centered aspect of self-decoration. In many temples, when you take the Buddhist precepts, committing yourself to the Buddhist ethical code and way of life, or upon adopting other levels of views, the occasion is marked by receiving a burn from glowing sticks or cones of incense. This produces a lasting scar.

    In some cases, elders pass through the group of initiates and administer the burns. This, then, is a real initiation, based on a real change of mind and commitment as witnessed by peers and elders. The burn represents the complete purification of karma; and the recipient experiences a moment of that purification within the pure, unmitigated pain.

    Just a suggestion. Outside of religious symbology that might get you arrested or ejected from some temples or countries, it's the conceit of self-decoration that will create the biggest obstacle to future renunciation.
