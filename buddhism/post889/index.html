    People who wear wrist malas.

    Last summer I boarded a streetcar in my city and happened to sit down next to an old Tibetan woman who was reciting a mantra. It was such a happy occasion for me! I was very delighted by the happenstance, and I was very inspired to find someone (else) who just wanted to practice and didn't consider it to be a negative thing to bring into the public realm.

    Our own attitude is what makes overt practice or wearing religious items ostentatious, or not. If I am sincere, why would I bother whether people think of me as sincere?

    I wear a wrist mala quite often, although I don't feel like I need to wear it (as part of an image, for instance). I use it to remind me of my best intentions â€” how I want to move in the world. Although I do mantra practice (among others), I don't often use the mala at the same time.

    There are generally three scenarios where I may use a mala in concert with mantra practice:

    • Just for the enjoyment of it. Kind of like going out back and bouncing a basketball for a while, or hitting a tennis ball against a wall.

    • When using the physical sensation of my fingers on the beads to help me focus better. If sometimes the hubbub of the day looms large, I'll put my mala in a jacket pocket and work with it while walking between errands or while using public transport.

    • To keep track of practice commitments. If I've committed to a certain number of daily mantras I'll use a larger mala with counters on it to keep track of how many I've performed. The counter is a small string of beads you can attach to the mala; and for every round of the mala (108 beads/108 repetitions) you push one counter bead up. So when all 10 counters have been pushed up the string you know you've done 1080 repetitions. Some counters come with a second string of beads, to count the 10's (to 10,800 repetitions).

    Have you seen long malas? Sometimes monks or other practitioners who are doing intensive mantra practice use strings of 1,000 or more beads. These aren't connected in a loop, but form a single string that passes from a basket on one side of the monk to a basket on the other side.

    To answer your question about how I feel about meditation beads being worn by those who don't practice: After all, they're just beads. I don't really mind. I'm interested in fashion and style anyway, so I tend to think I'm pretty open to many different expressions.

    I do however feel bad when I encounter someone who is arrogant about what they're wearing, whatever it happens to be. And I do feel both embarrassment and righteousness coming up when I encounter people wearing things that are insulting to others. So this brings up the issue of context: any object is neither good nor bad, but is only appropriate or inappropriate given the situation.

    For example, keffiyeh scarves (shemaghs) can look great, but wearing one can offend people on either side of the Middle-East conflict. Likewise, there are times when wearing a mala may be either provocative or just not so suitable. There are times when I'll take my mala off, as a matter of interpersonal feng-shui â€” to take care with the little things I do so that I can relate better with others.

    If I were a monk, it would be a different story. A monk's commitment to non-attachment requires him to refrain from concern about his appearance. One uniform, no choice. Sounds kind of liberating, doesn't it?
