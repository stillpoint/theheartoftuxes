    Questions on arousal during shikantaza, fear of annihilation, and koan practice

    > analysis has already caused me a great amount of suffering and I would like nothing more than to break through it, even if for just a moment.

    So put it all down.

    Your true nature is already you; it is not something distant or alien. It's the closest, safest aspect of your life, the you that's always been at the center of everything. So it is not something special. You only make it into something special through your own speculation and imagining. If you put down speculation and imagining, already your true nature emerges.

    To put it another way: True nature is already functioning as this very moment. It's just so plain that people assume "Oh, that can't be it. It must be something more strange or spectacular." In reality, every moment is already enough.

    You write that you never truly understand body and mind like a river and every moment is death. Join the club! This is the club of countless beings all failing to understand the truth of impermanence and insubstantiality. So that is why we study and practice: to clarify the question.

    If you don't understand, that is your correct situation. You are right not to understand. It is not an understandable issue. If someone thinks "I understand", that is a big problem â€” much bigger than not understanding. So you have to become comfortable with Unknowing.

    Unknowing means you let go of understanding, but you don't stop perceiving. Understanding is attachment to thinking; and not understanding is also attachment to thinking. In both cases "I, my, me" is trying to hold something. But Unknowing is just open and spacious. This is the same meaning as "put it all down".

    Since you have always been your true nature, which is perfectly aware and responsive, and perfectly unified with all of life, you have only to let rest the grasping at concepts that cover up your true nature.

    You can rest in your true nature by putting it all down; or you can seek it out through self-inquiry. Two types of path (which overlap somewhat). Either go straight to Unknowing and rest there; or keep trying to find yourself. The second path of self-inquiry has nothing to do with gathering theoretical or conceptual knowledge; it means turning your attention back to where it came from: looking for the 'you' at the center of your momentary experience.

    When you look for your own mind, you soon reach a point where you can't find anything. There is nothing to grasp, and nowhere else to look. This point is still and clear; it is the same as resting in Unknowing. So when you reach that point, rest there. That is true nature. This is the essential koan for you. No fancy words necessary, only look back to where your seeing, hearing, and thinking are coming from. Who is at the center of your experience?

    Don't expect to get a thinking answer. Just rest. Then everything already appears perfectly. A cardinal sings tsew-tsew-tsew. Water is wet. If you don't sleep you get a headache. If you eat too much you get a stomach ache. Life is just clear.

    As to finding a teacher, please keep looking. There are many ways to connect. Monastery is not necessary. Sometimes you can connect my email or online seminar. Sometimes you can travel to meet in person, even if it's a bit far. Sometimes you can find a group in your area that a teacher may visit. Keep trying. I looked for several years before finding my teacher; but it would have been worth it even if I had been searching for many decades.

    Good luck! And put it all down!
