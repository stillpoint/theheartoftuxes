    Our discomfort in seeing others suffer and die may come from ego. It very often does; or at least our experience is complex, and certainly includes heaping portions of ego.

    There are Buddhist meditations that shed light on this point, in many traditions. For example, there are the bodily constituents and processes meditations, in which one exhaustively brings to mind every part that makes up the body, visualizing them graphically: blood, pus, sputum, phlegm, urine, semen, lymph, synovial fluid, bile... muscle, tendon, ligament, bone... brain, liver, spleen... and so on. This develops an equanimity regarding the body and its coming and going.

    Other meditations involve contemplating the processes of dying, or actually going to places where dead bodies are left to decompose, and noting the processes in great detail and with neutral awareness. Besides equanimity, these practices build a compassion that is not based on sentimentality or ego involvement.

    It's really not appropriate for me to defend a certain approach or perspective. The only correct perspective is no perspective. That's what we're aiming at: to hold no view, no opinion. Then truth appears by itself. This is greatness. So it would be best for you, and us, to continue looking into suffering, asking "how can I help?", and refraining from making set opinions on the matter. Gradually the view becomes refined.

    In each phase of the journey of awakening there are things we are blind to. Even, we get big openings and realizations, and develop knowledge and wisdom; but still there are parts we miss. An experienced practitioner should really develop a lot of humility in this regard.

    One example is that in speaking of compassion in these forums, we habitually and unconsciously cling to a view of separate beings. "I" over here "do" some kind of helping for "that fly" over there. And we converse as if suffering is an actual thing that belongs to a being. All of this objectification is false. The whole discussion is based on false projections, a made-up world of separate beings and states and events. This is all ignorance. But at first it is difficult to understand this point, much less to embody that understanding. So we need to proceed step by step, trying practice, letting go of opinion, refraining from making concepts. Wisdom is a completely different way. You don't get to hold some right view. But when you let go of all views, then right view appears, right opinion and concept appear.

    The practice you describe is similar to Karuna and Metta meditations, but be careful about (A) making up stories about people, and (B) inventing your own meditation. I don't say "don't do it", but I say take care.

    The Buddha Way is based on truth, not on idea or feeling. You can consider what you know of people and 'put yourself in their place'. You can contemplate the challenges they face as well as their good qualities. You can speculate and piece together a more vivid sense of the person. But know the part that you are making up, and don't mistake it for the person.

    Next, it is good to find your own way into Dharma, to feel out your own rhythm and connections. But when a person veers off from traditional form and makes up their own practice, it can very easily cut them off from the mirror of Dharma. That is, they lose the chance to have their own mind reflected by the form, because the form they participate in is now based on their own preference, view, opinion, habit, blind spots, and so on — the wisdom of the form has been exchanged for tinkering.

    At some level, you know whether you are being sincere or making something up. Best wishes functioning at that level.
