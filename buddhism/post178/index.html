    >I feel like I've got these things trapped within me and they can't get out.

    That is a thought, not a feeling. Feeling follows the thought. This is an example of your thought turning back around on yourself in a little, cramped loop rather than just moving outward. We call it 'checking': looking back on yourself to check your condition and situation. It is the number one mental sickness among spiritual practitioners. "How am I doing now? Do I have everything? Will I succeed? How is my feeling now? What if I'm not comfortable in the future? How does my condition compare to yesterday? How does my situation compare to my neighbor's... or my rival's...? Did I progress? Am I getting somewhere? Am I getting what I want?" ...and so on, and so on. The endless checking mind that loops back on itself. This is the most problematic kind of object consciousness: making an object of self, and fixating on it.

    So when you check your condition and situation you actually lose your natural and generous outward flow, the movement of life that just moves like a stream following its own course. A stream doesn't pick and choose its preference; it just continues, moment to moment. If something is in the way, it flows around or piles up until it flows over. So your correct creative expression is bigger than your checking, bigger than your idea; it is momentary: how do you express your true nature while reading this message? How do you express your true nature while putting on a shirt or while taking a shit? How do you express your true nature with a stranger on the street corner? It is all your art; your idea of being an artist is not the limit of either your art or your happiness or your responsibility.

    Another note: 'void' and 'sadness' are not wrong. You are an actor, writer, and musician; so already you know this at some level. But your conditioning and the messages you get from society maybe contradict what you know. If, however, you practice complete openhanded Unknowing, which returns you to your center, then sadness is poignant and universal truth, and voidness has a precious and ineffable power.

    If you don't make anything — if you neither grasp nor resist — these facets are already truth. We are born solitary and we die solitary. Our birth and our death are interwoven with the birth and death of all things. There is tremendous sadness to this.

    But also, our original nature is void — completely pure of any mark, any quality — which means it is miraculously functional. It is like a perfect mirror, a pure uncolored light, absolutely clear water, or open space. Our original nature can receive and illuminate anything, without being moved or harmed or marked in any way. If we insist on being a small, separate being, this voidness is terribly threatening. But if we are willing to let go of that illusory role and open our hands, our void nature is greatness, boundlessness, perfect functionality, infinite potential... and the only completely safe and happy home.

    I recommend studying Zen. The practice is taught as moment to moment self-expression — expressing the original void nature in everyday activity. Furthermore, if you train in a school that does koan practice you will find that the space of koan interview is wonderfully, massively creative. It can teach you to trust your center above and beyond any object, and can make your art into a vehicle of awakening rather than a petty pursuit of 'getting' and 'making'. It can teach you how to avoid getting snagged by objects.

    You might be particularly interested in Zen Mountain Monastery, which has frequent programs in awakening arts. (Their founding Zen Master, the late Daido Loori, was a photographer.)

    You might also be interested in modalities that train the creative process, such as 5Rhythms. In my experience (I've done visual art, dance, photography, writing, musical performance, and performance art professionally) 5Rhythms is a wonderful companion to traditional Buddhist practice; but you can't count on it or its teachers to unfailingly provide the pure view or to always point you back to your own center (though they do a good job). For that, you should seek out a confirmed, awakened master in a traditional path.

    As to getting snagged by objects, study the Eight Worldly Winds (also called the Eight Worldly Concerns):

    >Fame and Shame

    >Loss and Gain

    >Pleasure and Pain

    >Praise and Blame

    Learn to identify which of the 'winds' is influencing you at any time, taking you away from your center.
