    Great Faith, Great Effort (or Determination), and Great Doubt are sometimes referred to as the Three Pillars Of Zen, or sometimes as the Three Jewels Of Training. 'Great' means beyond self, beyond ego -- that which expresses or takes you to your original, boundless being.

    Original Mind already has these qualities of Great Faith, Effort, and Doubt, but people sometimes get caught on the words rather than knowing where they point; they may think that 'Great' refers to size or intensity. Yes, there is that aspect -- one does need some more intensity -- but that's not the essential meaning, the original meaning. If 'Great' only means bigness, it's still relative; it's big in relation to something else. Original Mind is not relative. It is is boundless and complete.

    Also, this misunderstanding posits a self that performs the 'big' activity: it's my big effort, my big doubt.

    This is not Zen. Zen is very direct, very pure. It goes straight to Original Mind.

    So whereas good faith means I believe in my Buddha Nature, Great Faith means I go straight there, in a momentary flash. And whereas good effort means I keep taking steps, keep trying sincerely over time, Great Effort is timeless; it is pure momentary activity without a doer (the Wu Wei of Taoism). And whereas good doubt means re-applying a question and looking into things with curiosity free of assumption, Great Doubt is the open, aware space of the question itself, no different from Original Mind, no diffrent from Buddha's enlightened mind.

    This is why the practice is hua t'ou: word-head, not word-tail. Thinking-meaning is the tail of the word, the apparent form. Anything that is still wrapped up in the apparent form -- self, relationship, time, story -- that's all the tail. The head of the word is before-thinking. Before thinking, the questioning, the questioner, and the pure awareness are all one.

    Culturally, in Japan, some streams of practice developed that stressed struggle and buildup of tension, which would later (hopefully) be released in an insight experience. These streams have a bit of attachment to the spectacular nature of what in some respects is a neurotic process. But given the character of the society at the time they developed, these streams were actually a skillful adaptation of practice. The teaching served many who came with a very masculine, effortful mindset; it made use of it, and turned it into a transcendental process.

    Some (not all) streams and teachers of Rinzai are like this. They emphasize struggle. "Heavy on the ginseng", as one Zen Master would say. If the teaching also acknowledges the risk of attaching to a self that's making the effort, it can be a valid path. But it's a bit circuitous, and divorced from the original meaning and approach of hua t'ou.

    Hua t'ou is very, very simple. The question (in many cases distilled from a gong an/koan) generates it, but then you let the question go. Once you have the clear, spacious mind, why go back and make more noise and struggle? To do so would be abandonment of Great Faith. Maybe it's that even some Zen folks can't bear the plainness of Original Mind, and so they trade Faith for method. But true Zen has no method. The Zen institution has many good methods, but the heart of Zen has no method.
