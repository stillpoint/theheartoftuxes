    I don't want to help someone, is that wrong?

    I think all of us have experienced these kinds of complicated situations; and if we haven't yet, we will.

    A few points that come to mind from my experience in similar situations:

    • Most difficulties between relatives are in large part either caused by or worsened by poor communication. There's a tendency to 'write off' the other party, to give up on usual standards of communication because we don't believe it will help.

    But quite to the contrary, these are situations where we may need to take more care — to apply more actual love and meticulous action — and communicate a lot more than our knee-jerk reaction would have us do.

    With your relative, with your wife, with all involved, listen through the noise and perceive the need behind it. And explain your needs with openheartedness. This is different than just accusing and trying to make the other person the bad guy.

    • If you invite someone to listen and communicate together, and they completely refuse, then you have to hold your own boundaries. You have to strongly show others what is okay and not okay from where you stand. 'Strongly' doesn't mean violently. It means you may need to keep holding the boundary again and again, indefinitely.

    As I've heard Dr. Phil say, you teach other people how to treat you.

    • Nonviolent Communication (NVC) provides a good framework for those who don't know how to communicate effectively. That's most of us. You don't necessarily have to follow a script, but the steps give you something to go on.

    • Three-way conflicts seldom work out well. Try to keep the relationship simple, and deal with the issues one-on-one. Husband-wife issues are addressed with your wife. You-and-your-relative issues are addressed with him. As much as you might want to be cheered on for being in the right, it will not help anyone to drag a third person into the argument.

    Having said that, if a third person steps into the argument you may have to invite them to deal with their own issues directly, or to lay down a boundary about it: "I'm willing to work something out that benefits all of us, but I'm not going to do a three-way argument."

    • Maybe thank your wife for her integrity in not taking sides, and help relieve her of the stress of the situation as best you can.

    I personally don't believe that we are obliged to do things 'because of family'. If we're family, then mutual care is the expectation. I hope you find a win-win-win solution.
