    TheHeartofTuxes

    "What's my path" is a common dilemma. But the dilemma is not real, it's just thinking. The Yakkha (in Sanskrit, Yaksha) couldn't disturb the Buddha's mind because the Buddha was not attached to thinking; and the Buddha was not attached to thinking because he had completely fulfilled the qualities he spoke of in this dialog.

    So it's not about Theravada versus Zen, it's about faith and commitment to Dharma, making right effort in the moment, keeping clear mind in the moment.

    All opposites are made by thought. So whenever you have a dilemma or dichotomy, you can know that these are mind-made categories that are competing. When 'mind' dissipates, the competing dissipates.

    Throughout our day, our trickster will arise. Not evil and not obliging, situations will arise and provoke us. If we attach to thinking, the trickster situation — the Yakkha — will disturb us. If we don't attach, the provocation seats us more firmly in our faith and commitment. This is how Yakkhas and other tricksters grow our practice. Whether we conceive of them as external entities or as confluence of conditions (as we ourselves are as beings), they bring us to the crux of our practice: it's not about the polarities of thought; it's about putting it all down in the moment and embodying clarity. This putting down and embodying is refuge in Buddha, letting the great one take care of it.
