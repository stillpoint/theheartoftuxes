    Ego in terms of success

    What are 'you'? What is the self that you would love? This is the central point.

    Yes, in relative terms, self-love is very important. Everything comes from the center, so the center has to be clear before others can benefit. But what is that center?

    You will certainly die. You don't know when you will die. Even before you die, there is an end to everything you try — some things end earlier, some later; but everything ends and likewise you don't necessarily get to know or choose when things end. So all of life is ungraspable, like made of phantoms or dreams.

    Of the time you have before you die, more than a third is spent sleeping. Then you have tasks like washing yourself, grooming, dressing, eating, shopping, repairing things, and taking care of your living space, which take up a considerable amount of time. And work, socializing, entertainment, and recreation take up a considerable amount of time. So very little time is left for actual spiritual work — for looking into this central point of who are you, and how do you find your true life.

    Of the little time left to look into the central point, how much is spent actually looking; and how sincere is the investigation?

    This just highlights what is meant by self-love. What many people mean by self-love is "I try to get what I want" or "I try to do something nice for the body or mind". That is okay. Like you say, nothing evil there. But also nothing spectacularly positive. These kinds of things will disappear at death, and can't help you.

    But if you really love yourself you would look into what this self actually is, and what its fulfillment actually is. You would find out whether your plans and agendas amount to anything.

    Life is precious.

    Maybe you know someone who has a terminal disease. When the prognosis is given — some weeks or months left to live — the priorities in life change very markedly. One may see through the illusion of big plans. A larger question may arise: "What do I do with my remaining time? What is life for?" When the certainty of death starts to dawn in one's awareness, events and experiences become equalized. A cup of tea, the look of a crow sitting on a branch, a walk with a friend may become as precious as or more precious than the greatest dreams of success. The vast game or illusion of all this trying becomes exposed.

    At that time, choosing to play the game can be life-affirming. One may pursue success and do one's best, but it comes from a different motivation. It is no longer an illusion being chased, but an illusion being participated in consciously, like a piece of theater or a dance.

    So in the Seven Points Of Mind Training, there is the aphorism

    >Post-meditation, be a child of illusion

    That is, although you realize that all trying and getting is illusion, you throw yourself into it anyway. You offer yourself to the mystery of life, and allow gain and loss to appear equally. No problem! This is radically different than being swept along in the illusion while believing the illusion is real, like a ghost in your own life.

    So do you realize the illusion? Not as a theory or distant concept, but actually seeing through it and knowing the true nature of your life — do you realize that? The pursuit of this knowing, and the finding it, are real self-love. They are love of the real self, which is not a self at all.

    I've practiced with many very powerful and successful people, some of them Dharma Teachers and Masters. They use their success to help all beings. There is little or no selfishness to it; they could easily give it all up. It just happens to be the way their path has unfolded, so they participate wholeheartedly.

    Know your true self, and know your correct direction — the correct purpose of wealth, power, and other success — which is not 'for me' but 'for others'. This is real self-love.
