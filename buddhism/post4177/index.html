    Very good, and very important questions. I agree with both the content and the urgency of the questions.

    By the same token, we should be careful with what we label 'altruism' as if our understanding of it is ultimate and peerless.

    Just to play devil's advocate, let's realize that Basho was actually an extraordinarily compassionate man, far more heartful, caring, connected, and selfless than the common person. If you read his body of work you will find much evidence of it.

    At some point in his career he also attained enlightenment, casting off the illusion of selfhood altogether (if not yet all of the lingering obscurations associated with it). It is said that his 'ancient pond' poem marks his change of consciousness:

    > ancient pond

    > frog jumps into

    > plop

    The pond, frog, jumping, and water sound all become one.

    I don't recall whether the Narrow Road To The Deep North was written before or after Basho's enlightenment experience. You could easily check. Suffice to say that a liberated person's actions don't conform to any code, but spring from the secret well of wisdom itself. As such, some actions may seem incomprehensible to us.

    For the unenlightened, moral codes and deeply questioned philosophies are necessary, to prevent insincere, false, or aborted altruism. I'm just not sure that we can judge Basho's actions from within an unenlightened frame of reference. (Given the same situation, I imagine that, like you, I would do whatever I could to tend to the child and save it from exposure and starvation.)

    In the end, it is not clear what Basho actually did in this situation. We only hear him talk of what he ought to do.

    We can also note that this example has echoed through history, and that we are now learning from it and looking at compassion with fresh eyes because of it. Compare the 'Nanquan kills the cat' koan in Zen (this quote from the Kwan Um School Of Zen's gates of study uses the Korean spelling of 'Nam Cheon' for Nanquan):

    >Once morning, the monks of the Eastern and Western halls were arguing about a cat. Hearing the loud dispute as he passed, Master Nam Cheon held up the cat in one hand and a knife in the other and shouted, “You! Give me one word and I will save this cat! If you cannot, I will kill it!” No one could answer. Finally, Nam Cheon cut the cat in two.

    >In the evening, when Jo Ju returned from outside, Nam Cheon told him of the incident. Jo Ju took off his shoes, put them on his head, and walked away. Nam Cheon said, “Alas, if you had been there, I could have saved the cat.”

    >1. Nam Cheon said, “Give me one word!” At that time, what can you do?

    >2. Jo Ju put his shoe on his head. What does this mean?

    >3. Why did Nam Choen kill the cat?

    >Commentary: Nam Choen, Jo Ju and all students are already dead. The cat says, “Meow, meow.”

    A further commentary from Zen Master Seung Sahn in the Kwan Um School Of Zen's Compass Of Zen:

    >This is a Great Love and Great Compassion kong-an [koan]. If you have Great Love and Great Compassion, you love and you act with no condition. Unconditional love means not having "my" opinion and "my" condition and "my" situation. Only help other people. So how do you save this cat?

    Here's a wonderful brief talk on the case, by Zen Master Mark Houghton, which illuminates this whole issue.

    We can similarly ask, in your example, how can we save this baby -- really save it from its certain fate of inevitable suffering, eventual death, and endless cycling in rebirth after rebirth?

    It could be that Basho was indeed self-deceiving and cold-hearted. I find it hard to believe, given the depth of his writing. It could be that you have a warmer, kinder compassion. I suppose part of our questioning process should visit the futility of individual, karma-bound life itself. In order for us to take broad compassion to heart we need to understand that merely preserving life is not enough. The whole thrust of becoming -- of returning to Samsara endlessly -- has to be completely uprooted. The countless plodding rebirths have to come to an end.

    The upshot is that we must at all costs avoid building up an armory of opinions and codes of behavior, and rather overthrow our own self-bound ignorance. We ourselves have to become clear and unconditional (what Zen Master Houghton calls 'complete absence of enabling'). We have to save our own abandoned baby.
