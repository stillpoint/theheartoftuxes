    First of all, that's very wonderful that you want to maintain energy and aspiration even in difficult times. This is what separates true practitioners from dabblers. Although it is frustrating, your practice is going in a good direction. If you keep trying, your Dharma will at some point overtake your karma.

    "Even the dullest axe will fell the tree if you keep swinging at it."

    It is also very wonderful that you practice the nine-point meditation. Keep doing it, keep trying sincerely. Just don't check your feeling. Sincerity doesn't have to feel like something in particular.

    Your trying is already sincere. You came here asking this question; so you already have a sincere practice mind. But we humans are caught in the sickness of checking. Instead of just doing it, we check to see how things are going, and we judge how things are going, and then our checking and judging and labeling and scrambling to arrange all create obstacles for our practice.

    One way we 'check' is that we give too much attention and significance to how we're feeling. Instead of just practicing, we expect to feel a certain way. If we don't feel the way we expect, then we decide that something is not right, something had failed. But that's not true. Just this moment is already it; just this moment, as it is, is already a miracle; just this moment is already complete.

    In the moment, when you notice that you are checking your feelings, put it all down and just get active. What is the scene in front of your face? What is your right relationship to it just now? Sometimes sitting meditating; sometimes taking a shit; sometimes getting ready for work; sometimes crying; sometimes helping a neighbor. One Zen master says that practice is "action-style" - it's about doing, not checking and figuring.

    I'd like to recommend physical practice to you. Many great and experienced practitioners know the tremendous value of physical practice. It is something you can throw yourself into even when you are feeling dull, agitated, disconnected, or apathetic. You easily offer energy to physical practice, and you easily derive energy from it. And it very quickly brings you down out of your head, so your energy can reside in its proper home.

    Our problems in practice come from thinking. So many experienced practitioners, including teachers and monks, know that the bulk of practice has to do with maintaining the mind -- getting down out of the head, and into plain, simple, physical, grounded reality. That's why physical practice is highly valued for its effect.

    Next, if you're not already doing so, connect with a teacher and practice group or community. The benefits are immense, immense, immense. Incalculable. And not the least of which are the inspiration, motivation, living example, and direct reflection you receive (and give!) through spiritual relationship.

    We can't do it alone, because -- as you are experiencing -- our karma takes over. So we wisely gather the circumstances that will support us in maintaining practice, not depending on our karma. We begin to commit to more unconditional forms; we change lifestyle structures and schedules; we take vows and commitments; we spend more time with sincere practitioners and great beings (perhaps even living with them for a time); we put ourselves into situations where our coming-and-going mind isn't running the show.

    Participating in unconditional mind, we offer that to the enlightened ones, and all beings, and to ourselves.

    > Ananda approached the Buddha with delight in a recent realization: "Lord, I see now that spiritual friendship is at least half of the spiritual path!"

    > The Buddha replied, "Say it not so, Ananda, say it not so! Spiritual friendship is the whole of the spiritual path!"
