    Single-mindedness is a scary thing.

    >...I found myself having voluntary lapses of single-mindedness and relative mind stillness that I have either never experienced before, or have never noticed.

    "Relative" is a good word to use here. Behind the obvious distracting thoughts are more subtle forms. In a sense, the whole world is a thought.

    >It is a scary thing sometimes, though.

    It is scary only when you are thinking. As you sense the approach of nothingness, boundlessness, uncertainty, you feel the group on yourself slipping away. For the self-creating habit (the ego), this seems like impending doom. But you are actually becoming more yourself; you are giving up the small, illusory self and unveiling the big, real self.

    It's something like taking off in an airplane: when you're just lifting off, still relating to the difference between being on the earth and being in the sky, there can be a queasy anxiety. Once you're up in the air, everything is fine. Likewise, once you stop thinking and enter your spacious mind, everything is fine, everything is just as it is.

    >When we try just to walk, to sit, to stand up, do we just... walk, sit, stand up? With no thoughts, just awareness? If we don't really have anything to think of, what do we do - meditate on the body?

    You're still coming at it from the (false) perspective of a small, separate being who does something. You already are the momentary activity of the universe. The activity arises by itself.

    There is no need to think about what happens when you let go of thought. And there is no need to know whether awareness is going to be there, accompanied by something else or not. When you are in it, there is no separate you to be thinking about the moment, there is just the moment's unfolding.

    You ask what you should do. What do you want to do? What is the purpose of your meditation?

    If you want to embody true nature, which already moves with perfect responsiveness, intelligence, and compassion, then yes, let it all be. Only follow the situation moment to moment. Walk, eat, talk, and think when it's time to walk, eat, talk, or think.

    >It's not like I don't feel allowed to think about anything else, but it's just such an interesting feeling not to have verbal thoughts. It's confusing... I have been making this happen countless times since yesterday.

    >I wonder if someone else here has tried this.

    It's not special. If your intent is to embody your true self, then just continue letting go of distracting thought without becoming self-fascinated (which is another train of distracting thoughts).

    Again, it's only confusing if you're thinking. It's only confusing if you make yourself into a small self that is in opposition to the experience. When you open to Unknowing, all knowledge is present without you having to do something to make it appear.

    In your case, as with most others, following a very clear form of meditation would be helpful. You should know the method you are using and return to it repeatedly. Having a definite method will give you something certain (and beneficial) to return to instead of thinking about your condition. A definite form will train you in letting go.

    Sometimes fascinating, sometimes boring. Don't get stuck in either, only keep going.
