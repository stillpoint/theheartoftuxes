    I have a couple of questions about the mind and thinking.

    All words only point to processes, and those processes are present only by virtue of other processes: everything is interdependent and mutually creating. So words never indicate an actual separate, solid, unchanging thing. This is true of 'mind', as it is of 'brain', 'body', 'self', and anything you could try to point at.

    Usually in Buddhism, different levels or functions of mind are identified. For example, if we're talking about the thinking process, we may say "your mind has to come down". It means that the thinking has to settle before you can perceive clearly.

    But we can also talk about the basis of mind, which is the background awareness that is always simply clear, aware, reflective. It is the awareness at the center of (or behind) your experience -- what you call you (well, you would call it 'me'). This basis of mind is always pure and clear, always present and functioning. It is timeless and eternal.

    Buddha teaches that we have an illusion of selfhood. 'Self' is one of those words that points to nothing real in itself, but rather a process supported by other processes. But because we imagine a separately existing self, we think of mind in terms of something owned 'by me': my mind, my thinking, my perception. If we let go of the false idea of selfhood and just perceive, we can move beyond the mirage of 'inside' and 'outside', and experience directly the unity of mind and phenomena. We can see that everything is made of mind.

    This is a turn-around from the notion that all things are separate objects that exist outside of us. If you do self-inquiry practice, trying to find your own mind, you will literally find the whole universe and all of life there. If you settle the mind through other means, that reality will emerge by itself. Everything you see, hear, taste, touch, smell, or think (which includes envisioning and imagining) comes from mind and is made up of mind. So if you find the basis of mind, you awaken to the basis of everything, the way things really are.

    The difficulty in seeing mind is that (A) the awareness doesn't see itself directly -- it is like an eyeball trying to see itself, it requires a reflection; and (B) there is no 'thing' there in the way we usually understand 'things', as graspable objects. So in the process of finding mind you have to come to terms with ungraspability, Unknowing. You have to be able to let things be, while continuing to look.

    If you continue practice, there will come a time when, on the periphery of completely unified concentration, you become aware of the ever-present awareness or reflecting quality of mind, and you will know that it has been behind all experience. This bright and omnipresent quality is why the Divine is depicted in all religions as omnipresent, eternal light: it is the light of awareness, which is the essential substance of all experience. So the Divine is your most personal and most real you.

    ~

    I can see from your writing that you are attached to a mechanistic, dualistic view. You believe in a world of separate, material objects that actually exist. It is also quite a materialistic view, that the body is real because it is physical. This all comes from your thinking getting in the way of your perceiving. This is the obstacle for you, the ignorance that will have to be purified by practice. (By practice, not by thinking!)

    It's not that you need to adopt a view proposed by someone else. Rather, you need to let go of any held view in order to see for yourself what the reality is. It is the great experiment, and your meditation place is your laboratory.

    The children's song Row Your Boat has it right: life is but a dream. Life is made of mind.
