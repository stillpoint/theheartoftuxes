    Questions about Buddha.

    There are various words in the Pali and Sanskrit of Buddhist teachings that may be translated in English as "mind".

    Alaya, Manas, and Vijnana are three main distinctions of mind. Manas (sometimes called Manovijnana) is the discriminating faculty, the function of mind that judges things. So while your thinking-consciousness may be aware of an event, it is the manas that labels it good or bad, sufficient or insufficient, and so on. It is the part of your mind that creates a world of opposites. So it is said that when manas is subdued, the illusory world of opposites dissipates and one enters Nirvana.

    It may also be said that when all created and conditioned aspects of mind, including vijnana or thought-consciousness, cease to arise, only the pure, awake, reflective quality of mind remains. There is no limit to this mind, because there is no longer any attachment to a sense of self, no sense of separation. Neither subject nor object arises. It is the one universal mind, which includes all things but is not moved by any of them. This may be called Buddha mind.

    All suffering comes from our misunderstanding about who we are. We ourselves suffer from our attachment to self, which is merely a deep-set idea; and we cause all forms of trouble and agony for each other and our fellow beings and world because of our self-cherishing. To understand one's own true mind is to know directly, 100%, that we are all this one mind, and that every object and event is a projection of that mind. This knowledge is the basis of equanimity (you're not thrown about by events because you know they are like a dream), the basis of happiness (you are content in your original nature, and you don't suffer, because you've cast off the attachment that causes suffering), and the basis of true joy (you perceive everything in its purity and unity).

    People think that "mind not arising" means you can no longer think or function â€” that it's like being in a coma, or the same as a rock or piece of driftwood. That's not what is meant here. "Mind not arising" means that illusion doesn't appear, and there is only perfect function. From the outside, from our perspective, it looks like perfect function because we still have a mind that picks out objects and events; but Buddha's mind is far beyond our conception. It is the mind behind our dreaming minds. This can't be reached by explanation.

    Consider space: all beings and events arise within it, but space itself is not moved or harmed or marked by any of it. Your Buddha-mind is somewhat like that.
