    Accepting Death [Serious]

    Well, I have some suggestions for you as a person, not just a Buddhist.

    • First is to express yourself. Make art out of your fear and suffering. Write journal entries or poems, paint or draw about death (and about this death and your relationship with your cat), create an art installation or sculpture, fashion an altar honoring the cycle of death-and-life, and so on. Engage in the subject as it lives in you, letting it emerge sincerely. If you keep it inside it will eat away at you. Expressing yourself also puts you in the time of creator, at the head of your experience, rather than the role of a powerless victim.

    • If you really want to overturn the whole issue, cutting off the deepest roots of fear, then you have to understand completely what it is that dies and what it is that doesn't die. This means understanding what you really are, besides your usual idea, which is just something that has been given to you since you were a child, and which you've acted upon unconsciously since then.

    So you have to wake up to what you really are. The natural meditation for this is self-inquiry. In this meditation, you try to find your true mind -- the central awareness or being (if indeed there is one) for whom every event in life happens. â€œWho am I? Who is feeling this... Who is hearing this... Who is experiencing these thoughts?"

    This is a looking, not a process of making logical arguments or speculations. Let go of any thoughts that appear -- they can't help you. Only seeing that central point directly can help you.

    As you look, you soon come to a point where you can't find anything to hold onto. The mind can't move here and can't move there; there's no object to grasp. Usually we are told that doubt is wrong or inferior, and we feel stupid or uncomfortable in a state of not-knowing; but this kind of not-knowing is very, very precious. When you reach a point where nothing comes up, where all answers are known to be just contrived thoughts, rest in this unknowing.

    Your looking and questioning â€œWho am I?", and the 'don't know' that is revealed are one and the same. Resting in that big open question is the practice of self-inquiry. Soon you can get a glimpse of who you really are, which is not subject to death. That is, you will see the nature of this Unknowing mind.

    • Finally, you can honor your cat's passing, doing a ritual for its safe passage to a new life where it can have a better chance of practicing and awakening. And you can use the experience not to avoid the issue but to come closer to it, to be more familiar and friendly with impermanence.

    In some cultures the issue of death is not hidden, so it doesn't take on such a dramatic charge. Death is seen as a regular part of everyday life. If something is to live, something must die. To think that anything survives is a big illusion. So the more you shed your illusion that you can avoid change, and the illusion that you have ever been able to preserve something in one lasting state, the more friendly you will become with the whole issue, and the less power it will have over you.

    Everything changes. Without change, nothing is alive. Death is absolutely necessary for the slightest thing to stay alive, to keep moving. But there is one thing behind all things, which has not been born, never moves, and does not die. This is who you really are; so if you find that you can be free of life and death.
