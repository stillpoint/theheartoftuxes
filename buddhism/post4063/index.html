    title: "A wandering mind is an unhappy mind. Indeed... 'mind wandering' appears to be the brain’s default mode of operation." - Killingsworth & Gilbert summary: 1.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2014-05-12 22:26:18 some_url:

    It ends up being the same point as Vystril made.

    These studies are important, and I hope more are carried out. The conclusion drawn in the paper also seems to align with Buddhist teachings. I'm just saying the correct view of happiness hasn't been established, and the reason why we should trust people's reports hasn't been established.

    Most people don't know what happiness is. This would likely include both researchers and reporting subjects of the study. If one's notion of happiness means feeling relatively better than some other situation, that's the relative, up and down, gain and lose movement of the Ocean of Suffering. Our shared ignorance is in mistaking unhappiness for happiness, thinking of worthless things as valuable, and treating impermanent things as stable and graspable.

    Right in the first paragraph the researchers reveal their ignorance of the topic — an almost childish ignorance shared by many quite accomplished scientists — in using "brain" and "mind" as interchangeable terms.

    Right on the heels of that, they wrongly equate happiness with emotion; and then they state that the most reliable investigation comes from subjective reporting. If the beginner's experience in meditation tells us anything, it tells us that the general public has little awareness of what really goes on in their minds. Can we trust that the most reported condition in the study, "not mind wandering", is accurate; or might we suspect that the responses are colored by unawareness and wishful thinking?

    In the chart and description, we don't know what kinds of meditating/worshiping were undertaken, and if, based on how the mind is used in them, it's valid for them to be lumped together. We also don't know what is meant by the euphemism "making love" in terms of how the mind is used (is it "love" or is it "intercourse"?).

    It's perhaps telling that one of the Five Hindrances — sex — is perceived by most as an uncommonly happy situation rather than a situation of deep attachment. It is also interesting that sleep/rest is perceived as the most unhappy situation among activities. (For one thing, if they're asleep, how do they know if they're happy or unhappy?)

    The charted difference in perceived happiness between sleep and relaxation is interesting, as is the distance between relaxation/nothing special and meditation. This goes back to the question of what form of meditation is being reported, and why it should differ significantly from relaxation or "nothing special".
