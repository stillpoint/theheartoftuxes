    Spiritual Purge?

    Yes, we have repentance, which is letting go of ignorance and returning to wisdom. It uses the power of regret to turn one strongly back to one's original bright shining nature.

    There are two kinds of repentence.

    One kind of repentance is formal repentance within practice and ceremonies. In cases of harm done to others, there are ceremonies that can be performed to heal the wound and make amends, returning the community to wholeness. In cases of harm done to oneself, personal repentance practice is done.

    Most Buddhist traditions have some kind of daily practice of return. In some schools it is as simple as going for refuge to the Three Jewels; i.e., putting your complete trust in the One Awakened to Truth (which is none other than your own true mind), the Truth itself (which is none other than your own true speech), and the Community of Followers of Truth (which is none other than your own body).

    But in many schools, bows or full prostrations are made by way of surrendering one's old, useless neurotic patterns and offering oneself to the very best in life. The movements are both archetypically meaningful and energetically purifying; they have physical and emotional health benefits; they are expressive of the devotion and surrender that are transformative; and the effort they require builds the will and cleans out negative traits.

    So prostration practice is considered to be a daily repentance ceremony, clearing the way so one can start fresh — a new lease on life. The usual number of 108 prostrations performed is said to represent the 108 defilements of character; so doing one full set sort of cleans the slate for the day (though you still have your ancient karma from countless previous lives to deal with).

    The second kind of repentance is original repentance. This means returning to your original mind, which is already pure and clear. In the flash of a moment, you drop everything — all wanting, seeking, hoping, holding, trying — and return to open awareness. The purity of your original mind shines through, and in that moment all karmic formations are burned up. So one moment of completely dropping everything is one moment of Buddhahood.

    This original repentance practice is expressed in a Zen master's HIT or shout, or in the spontaneous movement to help someone who has fallen or is otherwise in trouble. The flash of a moment is the space in which pure original mind functions.

    So you can try repentance and prostration practice, and you can try the flash of a moment. Just find a moment, and you will have your transformation, your new life.
