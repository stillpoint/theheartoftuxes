    It's not a bad point. But the companion point is also vital: respect, love, care, self-sacrifice. These are possible without getting bent out of shape.

    So your example with Brad Warner is one instance, in special circumstances. That doesn't constitute "Zen priests popping heads off Buddha statues left and right". It was a teaching that had a particular context at a particular time, for a particular audience.

    Most of the time, Zen masters and priests don't pop heads off, and in fact take careful, attentive, meticulous care of Buddha statues and eating bowls and people.

    From one side a statue is just a statue. From the other side everything is Buddha, everything is sacred. Both sides are important.

    But a lot of people, especially a lot of contemporary westerners, and especially a lot of young western men, glom onto the sort of cavalier, dismissive attitude they think they see in radical teachings, and try to copy it. They copy it not because they are trusting their spontaneous wisdom but because it feeds their ego. They like to think that freedom means they can do anything they want, without concern.

    The attachment is revealed when these folks come face to face with a set form and are required to give up their possessions and habits and ideas. Then suddenly they are not so free! Suddenly they stop being up for head-ripping, and cry "how dare you!"

    The destructive impulse is then nothing more than impetuous immaturity, far from the superior consciousness these people think they have. If carelessness and willingness to break things makes you free, the world would already be populated by saints. Any bully or oaf would be a master.

    These kinds of teachings are for the purpose of loosening the grip on material attachment. But if one only knows disdain — if one can't live with respect and compassion — that is also a form of material attachment.

    If you actually spend time with Zen masters (and I've lived and practiced with dozens, over decades) you will know that the vast majority of their activity is very attentive and caring, not dismissive or destructive.

    So Zen Master Dogen, speaking of the ignorance of those attached to ideas that 'nothing matters', said:

    >They know that in the profane there is nothing sacred;
    but they don't know that in the sacred there is nothing profane.

    ~

    >The Buddha on the altar is a statue.

    To believe that and only that, is materialism. It is delusion. The whole point of practice is to drop the thought patterns that trap us in form, and attain that the Buddha on the altar is Buddha... as are the altar, the floor, the sky, and you, and me. Just breaking things doesn't get you past form; and just trampling the things others hold dear doesn't set them (or you) free.
