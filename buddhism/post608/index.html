    TheHeartofTuxes

    The Buddha said, famously, "I teach one thing and one thing only: suffering and the end of suffering." The relative labels 'good' and 'bad', 'positive' and 'negative', 'wholesome' and 'unwholesome' are applied on this basis.

    Positive causes are causes that lead ultimately to liberation, Nirvana, the end of suffering, and the attainment of unconditional happiness (for all beings)... and in the interim to relative freedom from suffering, temporary happiness, and conditions that engender further positive causes.

    Negative causes are causes that block the path to liberation and lead to the many expressions of suffering: regret, animosity, mental/emotional agitation, sickness and death, conditions that block access to positive causes, and lost opportunity to practice Dharma.

    Neutral causes are directionless. They're sometimes referred to as ineffective causes because they don't have ethical substance and don't influence one's movement toward or away from liberation. These causes are mostly automatic and unintentional action, like breathing, walking, sleeping, or accidents like unknowingly stepping on a bug. One common point of contention is whether eating meat of animals someone else has killed is ethically neutral, or just disingenuous.

    It's commonly taught that as one's awareness and commitments deepen, one's ethical responsibility becomes broader and more acute; so eventually, for instance, one may take responsibility for one's mind during sleep, or for the circles of causation that spring from otherwise neutral acts like eating 'clean' meat. A newcomer to Buddhism may make many mistakes without incurring much karmic debt (i.e., the actions are neutral), simply because they are not yet aware of the harm, while experienced practitioners who engage in a high level of samaya (practice commitments) incur proportionally more karmic debt for the same transgressions.

    ~

    Addendum:
    If you're looking for some help in distinguishing between your own positive and negative actions, of course awareness is important: staying undistracted. If you aren't present with your activity you have no chance of applying whatever you've learned.

    Next, you might study the teachings on the Three Poisons or Three Root Kleshas. These are desire, anger, and ignorance. (They are translated in many alternative ways; for instance, craving, aggression, and dullness; or attachment, aversion, and foolishness.)

    These are the habitual and negative ways we relate to the three ways we experience events: pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral. That is, we tend to desire and cling to what we perceive as pleasant; we tend to be averse to and try to destroy what we perceive as unpleasant; and we tend to ignore what we perceive as neutral.

    Any behavior driven by the Three Poisons is inevitably a negative cause leading to suffering. So that is a very good way of becoming aware of your behavior: applying the template of pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral, and determining whether you are reacting with clinging, aversion, or ignorance.

    One very interesting and hopeful point is that if you are avoiding negative activity, automatically you are doing positive activity.
