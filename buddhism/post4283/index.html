    Buddhism and athletics/competition

    Good people are required in every walk of life. If your direction is clear, then you can help people with your action. In my personal opinion, someone who offers his heart completely to his activity, competing with all his might and yet not for personal glory, can be incredibly inspiring for others. A transcendent effort, an effort that goes beyond the ego, is uplifting for all involved.

    I think elite athletes share a certain warrior mentality. Just as a true warrior understands the emptiness of battle and meaninglessness of war but continues to do his best, a great athlete can open his hands to winning and losing, but can continue to give it his all. It will just take diligence, lest cultivating the sport begin to take precedence over cultivating the mind of enlightenment.

    Correct life direction can shine through any activity. If you know the real reason why you are alive, why you engage in any activity, then priorities fall in place. You can play the game, even enjoying winning and being disappointed in losing; but you know that winning and losing are empty. It doesn't mean that you have to become limp. Express yourself through what you love to do.

    When your whole body, mind, and heart align with your direction, that is integrity. We can see integrity in the way a person moves and relates; we can even see it in someone's posture and momentary energy; it shines out to the world. There is nothing wrong with this.

    A Zen Master once visited a public talk given by a scripture teacher. The Zen Master was dressed in rags and had a long beard, so at first others didn't recognize him as the talk proceeded. The lecturer spoke of maintaining virtue: "You must practice well and become like great trees from which temples are built, or like large bowls that can hold many wonderful things. Make friends with good people and you will continue to be good; but if you keep company with bad people you will become bad."

    After this talk, someone recognized the Zen Master (whose name was Kyong Ho), and so the crowd asked him to speak as well. The Master refused, but the crowd pressed him and the scripture teacher also asked him to say some words, so he relented and got up to speak [I'm paraphrasing the speeches here]:

    "Your practice is to be free of desires, and to live only to serve others. Holding on to being good, trying to be like a great tree or a big container will only hinder you. Great trees have great uses; small trees have small uses. Big and small bowls can each be used in their own ways."

    "You must become like a good carpenter. A good carpenter can use any tree, and can shape utensils according to their material. Not one thing is thrown out, not one thing is wasted. If good things come, you can use them; and if bad things come, you also have to know how to use them. Don't attach. Don't try to hold the good and push away the bad. Keep both good and bad friends, don't reject anything. Just free yourselves from concepts."

    ~~~

    You can do anything if you clarify why you do anything. Just free yourself from concepts. (Me too!)
