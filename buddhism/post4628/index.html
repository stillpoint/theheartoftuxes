    How to improve the understanding of the teachings?

    You are already in, already looking into this 'Buddhism'. But you don't have to be in or out; you don't have to worry about that. We are already talking, you are already investigating. That is enough.

    There are many practices in Buddhism, because there are many kinds of mind sicknesses. Not only do we have fear, anger, dullness, lust, grief, etc., but also many different types of these mind states and many ways in which they take hold of our lives. So it is said that there are 84,000 different practices in Buddhism — 84,000 kinds of medicine for all the different kinds of sickness.

    But the central point is that all of these sicknesses have one thing in common: we don't understand who we really are, and because of that we cling to things and experience the resulting suffering. So clearing up this one central point is necessary: who are you, what is the real nature of your mind, your life?

    Both study and practice have important roles in Buddhist training and should be developed together; but sometimes you lean one way because it's most helpful or because you connect with it deeply. Please let me know: do you want to try study style or practice style? Study style is below. If you want practice style, you can seek out a teacher, or I can point you to introductory practice.

    Practice in Buddhism has three categories, called the Three Trainings: Śīla, Prajna, and Dhyana

    • Śīla is the discipline of refraining from harmful action. Stop doing bad things, things that hurt you and others.

    This is not a moralistic category. It doesn't mean that you become 'evil' if you do a certain action. Rather, it points out cause and effect: if you do certain actions, you cause suffering; so stop those actions and you can stop the suffering.

    • Prajñā (pronounced Paññā in the Pali language) means the wisdom to move through life in a beneficial way. At its root, Prajna means the perfect reflecting quality of your original mind; an the way it appears in life is through correct discrimination (between what is helpful and what is not), which you practice by constantly doing things that help you and others to awaken and live free of suffering.

    • Dhyāna (pronounced Jhāna in the Pali language) means the concentration of meditation, the discipline of mind that allows and unfolds the previous two categories. It means returning to that basic clarity and goodness and calmness of your original mind, and expressing it completely, moment to moment. [Sometimes this category is given the title Samādhi, which has a slightly different meaning; but it's the same category, so no need to worry about the different title.]

    Simply contemplating the three categories and starting to consider how you can fulfill them is very wonderful. A proper course of Buddhist training in any of the major traditions (Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana, Zen) will include all three aspects.

    If you like, here is a study guide for you.

    > Please learn these terms and lists, at first so you can repeat them out loud, and then gradually learning more and more about what they mean. Make a schedule for yourself to study one item per week (or longer, as suits you). With items that are a list ("The Three...", "The Four...") you might take a week to look into each part of the list. So in total this study can take about 13 weeks, or about 3 months. That will give you an extremely strong basis for any further study or training.

    > I'm listing these in a progression, as one concept builds on the previous. You can shift them around, but I would say that it's very important to study and practice the Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind sooner rather than later.:

    1. Saṃsāra

    2. Nirvāṇa (or Nibbāna)

    3. The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind From Saṃsāra

    4. The Four Noble Truths

    5. The Three Trainings (Śīla, Prajñā, Dhyāna)

    > *The links offered above are drawn from the various major branches of Buddhism, so you might get a taste for the different styles. You can also look these terms up on Wikipedia, or on Dhamma Wiki or Rigpa Wiki, or the Buddha Net Glossary, or use Google or your library to research them.

    Later, you can study Karma, Dependent Origination, the The Three Marks Of Existence and the Eightfold Path.

    This response to you includes many concepts and categories, so don't try to tackle them all at once. It is a longer-term framework. Just keep it in your pocket and gradually approach each point step by step.
