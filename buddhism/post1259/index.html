    I have fallen off and I need help to get back on

    No wonder there is depression. The father is the teacher of the heart; the mother is the teacher of the body. If the teacher of the heart is closed, fearful, aggressive, it creates a difficulty for the heart in its unfolding. So no wonder there is depression.

    But this is workable. We all have something, some form of suffering. Everyone has pain and difficulty, dark areas and rugged territory. But wherever we are, we can turn in a positive direction. We can make any situation into awakening if we learn from it.

    Starting over again is excellent. Cleaning the slate is wonderful. How tragic if your old life could not be cleared away; how burdensome. So although it may seem horrible from within the situation, a Dharma brother looking on may say to himself "Oh, fantastic! Now what was built up is being brought low, what was sure is now uncertain. You are growing full of possibility. You are coming closer to pure, non-grasping mind."

    At a Zen Center, for instance, people often arrive carrying the scars of very painful situations. They come looking for something beyond their pain. You know, one of the great things about a very bad situation is that it turns us quickly toward Dharma. When things are going our way it's easy for us to be lazy and ignorant. We're comfortable! Why should we care? But when we crash, true priorities come forward. "Dang! I've been focusing on the wrong things! I can't waste my life any longer!"

    So there's a saying that 'garbage' people become monks. When you feel you're not good for anything and your life is crap, if your mind is already attuned to the Dharma your 'garbage' life may turn strongly to sincere practice. Then you can get liberation no matter where you are. If your mind has nothing to hold onto, that is very, very powerful; great leaps can be made with this mind.

    So a 'garbage' person becomes a monk. The saying continues that a 'garbage' monk becomes a Zen monk. LOL! If you're even so useless that you can't be a regular monk, maybe you become a Zen monk.

    I'm not making any suggestion about becoming a monk. I'm letting you know it's widely recognized that having things stripped from your life tends to prime you for deep spiritual practice. It's such a widely recognized dynamic that it has become almost a clich√©.

    A bad situation is a good situation; and a good situation is a bad situation. But you must use this situation properly. You must relate to the moment correctly by letting go of the previous moment, whatever it held. And you must take steps to create sufficient conditions for yourself.

    (Perhaps a list of short-term and long-term aims would help: What needs are urgent, what needs are important but not urgent, and what is the overall direction of your life? Then start another list of action points: What is one thing you can do to improve your situation? What is one step you can take to freedom? What is the first or next miniscule step in your big project of autonomy?)

    If you are only partially lost, then you can be lost. That is, you hold on to something that you don't really have, so you are lost.

    But if you are completely lost, with nothing to hold onto, then you are not lost. There is nothing to hold, nothing to get, nothing to find. You are only where you are just now. I was briefly lost in the wilderness once, and little chickadees and junkos would flit down on the snow around me, so vibrant and optimistic. They helped me realize "Oh, I'm just here." Soon I found my way.
