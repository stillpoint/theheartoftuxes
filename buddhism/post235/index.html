    As your Buddhist friend, I can't speak for others but personally I'm not insulted if you wear a mala or a rosary, Christian or Buddhist. How you keep your mind is the concern.

    If you want to wear it for more than fashion and self-identification you can actually make use of it. Yes, there is prayer in Buddhism, but it's not to an external creator god. There is also devotional practice, if you like that style. And of course there is also the more commonly known meditative style. Which do you like?

    In a very simple way, you can just work the beads in your fingers. One bead symbolizes one awake moment. Feel the bead in your fingers, then move on to the next. Simple moment by simple moment. No other form necessary. This can be considered a re-setting, a returning to zero. It is a very basic and important function, with infinite applications in life.

    If you want more support from form or if you just like more fancy style, you can also do awareness of breathing bead by bead, or mantra recitation, or mantra and visualization. Learn these from a teacher.

    You can also hold the string of beads in your hands, feel their quality, their smoothness or texture, their weight, their temperature, and have a simple moment of presence.

    You can do the same and offer warmhearted compassion to those around you. For instance, in the middle of an argument, hold the beads between your hands, apologize if there is something to apologize for (there may not be), and offer warmth and the invitation to move toward peace together.

    You neither have to show off your beliefs nor justify them. What is the important action in the moment? How do you return to correct relationship, relationship that doesn't attack and defend?

    I hope you can always re-set and return to zero. Holding my mala, I pray this for you, your girlfriend, and all of us.

    There is a famous Zen story about (on the one hand) attaching to religious forms and (on the other hand) attaching to the denial of religious forms:

    > On a cold winter night, a big snow storm hit the city and the temple where Dan Xia served as a Monk got snowed in. Cut off from outside traffic, the coal delivery man could not get to the Zen Monastery. Soon it ran out of heating fuel after a few days and everybody was shivering in the cold. The monks could not even cook their meals.

    > Dan Xia began to remove the wooden Buddha Statues from the display and put them into the fireplace.

    > “What are you doing?” the monks were shocked to see that the holy Buddha Statues were being burnt inside the fire place. “You are burning our holy religious artifacts! You are insulting the Buddha!”

    > “Are these statues alive and do they have any Buddha nature?” asked Master Dan Xia. (One version of the story has Dan Xia saying he's only looking for sarira, the glimmering relics that remain when a saint or accomplished practitioner is cremated.)

    > “Of course not,” replied the monks. “They are made of wood. They cannot have Buddha Nature.”

    > “OK. Then they are just pieces of firewood and therefore can be used as heating fuel,” said Master Dan Xia. “Can you pass me another piece of firewood please? I need some warmth.”

    > The next day, the snow storm had gone and Dan Xia went into town and brought back some replacement Buddha Statues. After putting them on the displays, he began to kneel down and burn incense sticks to them.

    > “Are you worshiping firewood?” ask the monks who are confused for what he was doing.

    > “No. I am treating these statues as holy artifacts and am honouring the Buddha.” replied Dan Xia.
