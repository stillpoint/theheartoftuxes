    Zen, Resentment, and Responsibility

    Trying to act compassionately is not compassion; it's trying to act a certain way.

    Compassion is open and empty and ready. It doesn't pre-decide. So the first step is to put everything down. Put it all down, drop it all. Idea, image, role model, habit, agenda, comparison, judgment, checking, situation, condition, opinion, feeling — put it all down, let it all be. This returning to zero is the necessary first step of compassion.

    So it's not that you hold an idea of what compassion is supposed to look like and then try to look like that. It comes from a different place entirely. You drop everything and put your trust in what's there originally, what is revealed when everything is dropped. This is momentary going-to-Buddha-for-refuge.

    This has no shutting in and no shutting out, no particular approach to hold on to. It is because you start with an idea that your anxiety builds. Compassion is not "nice idea".

    This also points to the likelihood that you don't actually feel kindness yet; you haven't connected to it, but rather hold the idea that you should act in a certain way. So of course your organism is going to rebel and feel resentful: it's being asked to perform in insincere ways.

    Tibetan Buddhism offers some very skillful ways to nurture compassionate feeling and attitude. If you like, you may try some of these practices.

    The Seven Point Cause And Effect Instruction is a step-by-step method for contacting, feeling, and offering great kindness. One recognizes one's dependency and debt to all others, acknowledges their desire for happiness and inability to attain it, admits deeply this shared situation, and commits to saving all from suffering. The essential point is that the compassion is grounded in real seeing and intimate feeling — it's not a vacuous idea, but deeply recognized and felt.

    The Eight Verses Of Thought Transformation is a practice for overturning self-centered patterns. It's quite radical in its approach.

    The practices of Tonglen/exchange of suffering and Brahmavihara, which includes Karuna/compassion and Metta/lovingkindness meditations (and which is common to Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana paths) both develop organic, sincerely felt compassion. Instructions and guided meditations abound on the internet.
