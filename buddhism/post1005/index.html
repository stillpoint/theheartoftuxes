    TheHeartofTuxes

    The question is important.

    We are very attached to things -- possessions, opinions, feelings, things that we think we can hold onto. We habitually grasp and try to hold onto things. This perverts our sense of ourselves; it makes us think of ourselves as separate, grasping-making beings.

    So trying to grasp and hold things creates a mistake about who we are. But it's important not to just avoid or forget about who we are. This point needs to be clarified. Because of our mistake, we do all kinds of bad action in the world, mistreating other people, mistreating animals, mistreating the earth and water and sky.

    Looking into the question without trying to hold onto one particular opinion can turn the mistake around. Usually, small questions try to get something in particular and hold it. But a big question can free the mind. The question can be boundless and spacious, like our true nature. A big question can blow open false and limited structures in the mind.

    When you can just ask... and ask... and ask... questioning, looking, resting in unknowing -- then that is already your true nature. Attentive and spacious. It is an important practice to be okay with this, with the basic mind before it latches on to some object.

    So when you look into questions like "who am I?" and "what is my life for?", don't be in such a hurry to get something. Don't be lazy and miss the chance, but don't try to force insight happen just so you can have something to hold onto. If you continue to ask sincerely, then that will provide space in which truth can appear by itself.

    Next, spend time with enlightened masters, and witness their motivation and activity. Learn from them what it takes to wake up and 'find yourself' -- what kind of attitude, what kind of effort.

    Spend time with sincere practitioners who are very clear on their life direction and practice. Again, being clear on one's direction doesn't mean holding a strong opinion! Many, many, many people have very strong opinion of how things should be. We can just look around and see countless people strongly attached to a belief system, whether it's religion or nationalism or an economic system or a personal creed. Many people are harming each other because of strong opinion. A sincere 'practice person' is quite different. The difference is very refreshing, inspiring, heart-warming.

    Clarity about life is different from just holding an idea. It means you actually see the difference between Samsara (the 'ocean of suffering'; i.e., our endless up-and-down, win-and-lose life that never completely satisfies), and Dharma (the path of truth). It is based on some experience of connection between the way you use your thoughts and actions, and the results that go beyond selfish, clinging, suffering life.

    Notice the rituals that begin practice sessions or daily practices, across many different spiritual traditions. What is the first point, the first intention? Almost across the board, there is a recognition of an intent not only 'for me', but for something greater, something beyond self-centered view. What is that?

    Nothing brings clarity on this point better than awareness of impermanence, awareness of death. A big part of our mistake about who we are is that we live as if we will live forever. Then when death inevitably comes, we see it as a cruel, unfair, shocking surprise rather than something woven into our very nature. When we know that the day is precious, we start to get clear on our purpose. "What am I really on about?"

    There are many good practices that aim at clarifying life direction. One very good practice, performed by millions of monastic and lay practitioners worldwide, is The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind. These are four simple contemplations that help the mind turn away from its deeply habitual dull and selfish patterns (away from Samsara), and toward a noble, awakening life (toward Dharma).
