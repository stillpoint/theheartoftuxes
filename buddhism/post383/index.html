    I think we can all relate to what you're saying; I know I can, and I can feel it deeply. And yet, there are others who live in the same world, in the same conditions, and even encounter far more difficulty and ugliness and evil, but remain joyful, kind, loving, bright, open-minded, adaptable. This is very interesting, no? Very interesting!

    If you read back your comment, you will encounter some 37 references to "I" and "me". This is where suffering arises. Originally, it is not in the world; but "I" and "me" bring it into the world. Because of "I" and "me" and all the things we do for them, all the energy we divert toward their glorification and reign, we create all kinds of untold suffering.

    All sentient beings create this basic problem, but on Earth human beings are the worst at making "I" and expecting all of life to serve that idea. We take from the environment, we take from animals, we take from each other; and this taking continues on all levels — mentally, energetically, emotionally... we even kill each other so that "I" can get what it wants.

    But if you cut off "I", that suffering can be cut off. This doesn't mean suicide, which actually deepens attachment to "I"; it means letting go of false thinking.

    more "I" = more suffering

    less "I" = less suffering

    no "I" - no suffering

    So it is not that you need to be convinced that everything is right; rather, you need to learn the cause-and-effect relationship between false thinking and suffering. This is what Buddhadharma teaches. And it also provides resources to act on that awareness, to let go of or cut off or see through the false I-thought.

    Then you can be happy, and your happiness can extend out to others. You can be a fountain of benefit, your good qualities helping all others in many ways. Instead of living a petty, self-cherishing life you can live a life of great nobility.

    The Buddha's teaching says that as long as we live in the illusory world of appearances, where we think of ourselves as small, separate beings within an overwhelming world of separate objects — as long as we live in that illusion, there will be suffering. No external object or relationship can fulfill us. So living in the common consciousness is always unsatisfactory. (You are right on that point!)

    But... there is the other side, which you are not yet aware of: there is a way out, and a possibility of satisfaction beyond the common consciousness.

    So we sometimes talk about useless suffering, which is just the usual human story of up, down, up, down, around, around, around, and then die... versus useful suffering, which is the practice of the Dharma, which can ultimately lead to complete contentment and fulfillment.

    It is not the nature of the world, but what you do with your mind that makes the difference. If your mind causes suffering, then you live a suffering life. If you cut off the causes of suffering, then you live a complete life.
