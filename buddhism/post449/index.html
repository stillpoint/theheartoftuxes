    I think you're missing some options there. What about: (3) They acted out of great wisdom and compassion, not out of their own desires.

    Not saying this is the case; just pointing out that our own judgments are limited by our shadows: we judge others based on our own blind spots and attachments; we condemn what we condemn within ourselves and overlook what we are blind to in ourselves.

    In any case, I'm not qualified to judge the action of awakened beings. I can only empower others (like prospective students) to see clearly and be responsible for their own decisions.

    There is always the person and the teacher. The two should be distinguished from each other. If you, as a student, attach to authority or grasp at certain states or results, you will be easily manipulated. You should know this about yourself, and seek out safe practice situations, and at the same time develop your ability to say 'no' clearly.

    Some students, with a stronger center, a stronger sense of self, and courageous heart for awakening can undertake situations that quickly bring them to awakening by cutting off all kinds of presumptions and conventions. It is one way among many; but it is not for everyone. Just don't paint all of Buddhism with one brush; the core is the same, but the paths and expressions are as varied as the human mind.

    As sophisticated as we like to get in our conversations, what is the aim? Is the aim to see, or is the aim to render a 'good' vs. 'bad' judgment, to comfort the ego that this wild, hot mess of life-and-practice can be pinned down and controlled? Attached mind is deluded mind, the mind that makes opposites. Seeing means being it: putting it all down and opening the ribcage, mouth, and loins to the great black, wet SEX of Sunyata.
