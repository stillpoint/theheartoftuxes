    How do I start?

    Change your idea of “reasonable". Change “can't" to “can".

    If you can't travel to sittings every week, then try every month. Go once, just to connect and get some face-to-face contact.

    I believe that often it's meeting a clear-minded and devoted person that's the most transformative thing, and not necessarily particular teachings, though the chance to receive teachings from an experienced and authentic teacher can also be invaluable.

    Here's my comment in another recent thread, with further options for folks without a local center.

    ~

    If you are motivated to try, then just start practicing. Find a good, proven method of meditation, and start practicing it.

    You can clean a little corner of your room, establish a focal point that will remind you of your true self and your higher aspirations (it can be an altar complete with candles and incense, or it can be a simple object that you offer with your faithfulness, like an image of Buddha, or an object from nature, or a cup of water, or a single candle), and just dive in to sitting.

    I was quite young when I started practicing, from instructions in a book. The moment I'd awake out of sleep, I'd jump up, hurry to splash water on my face and brush my teeth (mindfully), and then joyfully sit in meditation. Usually this meant I'd be up well before my family got up, while the sky was still dark. I remember the happiness of taking my first steps on this journey, and the quiet, sacred feeling of waking up with the day.

    During my sitting, light would start to spread in the sky, and one by one the birds would wake and start singing -- it was such a beautiful time. I hope you and others can have a similar beautiful and optimistic relationship with practice. Even if practicing alone meant that I had to find my own way through difficulty and misconception, what I got was my own experience, which no one could take away. Just by practicing continually, I discovered a great deal about the mind and meditation, and I developed a good relationship with myself.

    Having said that, I had no clue at the time of how important it was to contact a teacher. It wasn't until my first meetings and my first extended study with a teacher that I realized how many aspects of practice I hadn't been aware of, and how far off the mark my imagined ideals were.

    So both sides are wonderful, and important, and essential; and either one is a good place to start: learn a method and start meditating; and find a teacher.
