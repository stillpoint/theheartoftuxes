    If I start doing Vipassana now, how long will it take me to achieve Nirvana?

    Congratulations on your priorities.

    But beware of 'going too slow by going too fast'. As many practitioners have discovered, and as you will no doubt discover sooner or later, pressing on too forcefully only creates obstacles to practice.

    One of the main reasons for this is that by striving so overzealously you strengthen a duality between you and Nirvana — a duality that originally does not exist. If you strive too hard, you build the lie that Nirvana is somewhere out there, distant from you. But Buddha Himself, upon his own enlightenment, said "all things have this nature of awakening".

    Nirvana is already your own innate nature. It is not somewhere else. This is an important point.

    This important point and many others comprise the waystations along the path of practice. You do not yet even know that some of these points exist, much less how to approach them. That is why it is paramount for you to practice with a good teacher, someone who has traveled the path and visited the waystations, and who can keep you traveling in the right direction at the right pace.

    Joining a sangha of practitioners — not necessarily monks — is very helpful. Depending on your own capacity and on the style of study and practice you take up, it may very well be most effective to become a monk. But that doesn't necessarily mean that you are ready to be a monk, nor that you have the right understanding and mindset to be a monk.

    In any case, there are many, many enlightened people throughout history who have been householders, regular working people, and even prostitutes. Some enlightened people have been great scholars, and others have have been nearly illiterate or have learned a bare minimum.

    The outside form is important for beginners, but the outside form is not the essential part. The essential part is seeing your own nature directly. Any of the major traditions of Buddhism can bring you there, if you take the steps yourself.

    Wanting to know 'how long?' is the clinging activity of the ego. It is the part of you that is not yet ready to let go, and wants assurances. If you really want to know how long it will take, I can tell you that it will take no time at all. When you find this 'no time', then you will already have Nirvana. But if you still have time and concern about time, you will have to arrange your life to gather the conditions for moksha. That means, first and foremost, find a good teacher who can reliably teach from beyond ego; and then offer yourself to study without concern for time.

    Don't go too slow; that is to say, you should make a sincere effort without slacking off. But don't build a lie and make obstacles by checking the time, comparing progress, and rushing.

    "You can't make sprouts into crops by pulling them out of the ground." No one ever made an onion grow faster by pulling on its above-ground parts. A certain amount of trust in what happens below ground, within the darkness of the earth, is necessary. Learn the skill of letting-be and trusting Nirvana to express itself. It is much more expert than you.

    Finally, don't assume you'll be aware of your own progress or even your own awakening. If you think your progress is attached to a certain feeling or a certain experience, that is again building a lie. The truth is that you really don't yet know what constitutes 'progress'. So certain things have to be learned first; and certain things have to be trained and developed first; and your letting-be has to be cultivated and perfected. For this reason, your relaxation is very important. The heroic clamor for progress among beginners is often a subtle strategy of laziness: they don't really want to be patient and persevering, they want a quick, easy, and dramatic result. Instead, you should cultivate the attitude that you will continue your moment-to-moment practice for a hundred thousand lifetimes if necessary.

    Good luck, and let me know if I can help further.
