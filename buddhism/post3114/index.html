    Involuntary meditation.

    From the looks of your username I suspect you may already have an idea why you're experiencing these events. What crazy/stupid/smart thing have you been getting into?

    Yes, finding a good teacher is a good idea.

    I can't really offer any advice without knowing more about your context and history. In my experience, these kinds of involuntary unbalanced states are almost always associated with a history of drug use or other addictive/mind-altering behavior like sex addiction, gaming addiction, food, work, exercise addictions. The strongly attached and self-manipulating mindset tends to translate to a strongly attached (obsessive) and self-manipulating approach to spiritual practice.

    It's also possible that mental illness or unbalance pre-exists, outside of addictive or mind-altering behavior. There may be a chemical or psychological instability behind your experience.

    If this sounds familiar, the focal point for you may not be meditation at all; it may be looking into the wounds and habits that drive you, with the help of an expert counselor or psychotherapist, and/or checking your brain chemistry, with the help of a doctor or psychiatrist.

    Edit: I just read your other comments in the thread.

    Meditation shouldn't be considered a cure-all. If you are prone to mental imbalance, many forms of meditation will bring you too close to your thinking mind. So before you have a chance to practice stabilizing, you are already close to the thing that destabilizes you. A good teacher may not assign sitting meditation to someone in your situation.

    Movement meditation might be far more helpful. You might at least try it out to see if it's a good fit. In practices like prostrations, Yoga's sun salutations, or Taiji forms — to name a few — you are at a safer, more neutral distance from the tumultuous thinking mind. Furthermore, the engagement of the body is naturally grounding, helping you come back to a physical simplicity rather than go flying off into mental or energetic fugues. And even better, the movements themselves promote a smooth flow of breath, blood, energy, and emotion.

    If you find a teacher that you trust, you should disclose your full situation to them. Hopefully they will also say whether they are qualified to guide you; but just in case they are not so forthcoming you should be aware of the difference between spiritual practice and psychological healing. There may be some overlap between the two, but they don't necessarily have the same aims and timetables. A spiritual teacher may or may not be able to oversee your psychological well-being; some teachers are also psychotherapists, for instance, while others may be fairly naive in this regard.

    In the meantime, your job is to help yourself return to simplicity and groundedness. Stay in touch with real, physical objects and events. Wash the dishes, go for a walk, have a swim, play with a dog. Holding a weighty and/or textured object can help you stay in touch. For example, handling a rock, or prayer beads, or a flannel scarf can be a way of returning to the simple, real moment. Also sometimes listening to the sounds outside might help bring you back to a more settled state.

    I'd caution against pushing the mind to concentrate or doing mentally demanding meditations like following the breath, counting, or vipassana. The main question is: "is this helping me be more stable, or is it moving me toward instability?" You might be able to see it for yourself; or you might need some objective feedback.

    Best wishes!
