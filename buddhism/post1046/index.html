    From my previous post on this question:

    >...they can be used simply as a reminder of practice when worn, or the beads can actually be worked.

    >When performing practices like mantra (or in some cases chanting out loud), the beads can provide an extra measure of grounding. The tactile feedback can help the practitioner stay present, which may be helpful for a couple of reasons: first, the fast repetitive nature of some mantras may encourage drifting, as the repetitions start to be held in the background while the thinking wanders; second, the involvement of the body through the sense of touch brings in another dimension of practice. In Vajrayana, body, speech, and mind are developed together but also distinctly; so in mantra practice the accompanying visualization trains the mind, the vocalized or internalized sound of the mantra trains the voice/energy, and the posture or activity of the body trains the body.

    >In times of tiredness, crisis, or illness the mind may be difficult to tame, and the working of the mantra in the fingers can provide a very useful support. Even in delirium the mala can be worked, helping maintain the thread of compassionate intent and the application of attention.

    >The mala itself is a teaching about time and mind. Time and mind are not separate. (This is reflected in the East Asian word nen or nyan, meaning something like mind-force, mind-moment, or present-mind.) Each bead represents a moment in stillness. The connecting string represents the moment in its continuity. As you make the effort to focus only on the present bead and present moment, the timeless continuity of mind emerges. You might even say that timeless mind runs through the center of all moments.

    >You don't have to even have a separate practice in order to use the mala. Just being present bead by bead for a few seconds or for one revolution of the mala is a very wonderful way of bringing simplicity to life. Regardless of what difficulty you're facing, you can be simple for a few moments. That is the epitome of self-liberation: just being simple, just now.

    >In some traditions the mala is used as a counter for mantra, chanting, visualization practice, or prostrations. When a vow has been made to complete a certain number of repetitions, or when a commitment is set between teacher and student, an easy way of counting large numbers is necessary, and one that doesn't get in the way of the sometimes very complex meditations involved. In the 'preliminary' practices of Vajrayana, where the aspirant purifies her/himself and gathers the supportive merit required for higher practices, hundreds of thousands, if not millions of repetitions of the practices are performed.

    >The mala can be used as a counter without looking. In conjunction with other attached counters many thousands of repetitions can be easily counted. For each revolution of the mala, one counter can be pushed up a side string (of ten small beads). Once all ten beads have been pushed up on the counter, a bead on a separate counter string can be pushed up (indicating that a 'thousand' repetitions has been reached). There is also a small clasp or charm that can be moved along between the main beads, one step for each ten thousand repetitions. When the charm reaches the end of the mala, it shows that '100,000' repetitions have been performed (actually 108,000).

    >Korean Buddhism (uniquely, as far as I know) has a thousand-bead straight mala of large beads that is worked from one basket to another, to count special prostration practice. Imagine doing so many prostrations in one session that you use a thousand-bead mala to count them!
