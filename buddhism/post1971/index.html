    Question about the nature of self

    The first thing that comes to mind is the question "What makes you think you are not experiencing emptiness?"

    The thing is that every experience is the experience of emptiness. Just experiencing something deeply, without adding concepts to it, will be the experience of emptiness. So, for example, whatever you are hearing right now, where is that sound? Inside of you or outside of you? If you say inside, then how can the sound be created? If you say outside, then how can you perceive it? The place of the sound is mind; find that place and you find the boundless, empty self.

    If you hold an idea of self -- "I want to experience emptiness" -- then you can't attain emptiness. Making "I want" creates a separation that is not originally there. But if you put it all down, awake but not seeking anything, then the true nature of things appears by itself. Another way of expressing this is that you can't know it, you can only be it. There will not be a separate entity experiencing totality or boundlessness, only totality itself. This is not something arcane or pie-in-the-sky; it is very simple and very common. Just that our thinking mind tells us that our present-moment, regular experience can't be it. Why not? If this is the true nature of things, why should the experience be any different than what is happening right now?

    So, that is the no-path way; there is no way to get there, because it is already here. Holding a big question is a way of practicing this as well. "What is this?" creates a space of un-knowing, of non-conceptual awareness. If you then let go of the question, you can rest in that. If instead you keep regenerating the question, that also brings in the element of concentration (as below).

    You can also try the way of concentration, if that appeals to you. For this, you have to focus your attention on one object consistently, and let other experiences and speculations drop off. Repeated returning to an object of concentration, as during a retreat, calms the mind to the point where emptiness is experienced. You don't know that you experience emptiness until you come out of it. When you're in it, there's only emptiness. Coming out of it, you realize, "Oh, there was no separation just now".

    Also, some people are more inclined to analysis; so there's also the analytical path. Considering in detail the compound, co-created nature of things can be a mindfulness practice that leads to the experience of emptiness. Emptiness just means that every thing reaches everywhere in terms of influence and co-creation. Nothing has stable, impermeable boundaries that set it apart from everything else. So with every object, every person, every event, to maintain the awareness of co-creation -- or, we can say, to maintain awareness of the movement of things -- is to come closer to knowing boundlessness/emptiness.

    For example, to consider that this computer is not a permanent, solitary thing, but a confluence of minerals, formed into shapes, designed through communal knowledge, marketed and transported by thousands of beings, and channeling electricity flowing from distant sources and supported through the participation of countless events and beings... this kind of vision of objects and events approaches emptiness.

    Since the object we have the most attachment to is the body, the most effective analysis will usually be body-based. Hence, the Buddhist contemplations of the compound nature of the body (marrow, bone, sinew, flesh, fluids, organs, skin and hair, etc.), the contemplation of impermanence of the body (visualizing decay and decomposition), and immediate looking-into of impermanence and insubstantiality (vipassana meditation).
