    Is Buddhism a religion? - why we need ethics!

    Maybe it's just how I use the words. Simply put, the "ism" is a concept. The Buddhadharma is a living reality beyond concepts, but able to use them.

    Furthermore, attaching to the concept promotes prapanca (division and proliferation of concepts), encouraging debates about religion versus philosophy. The living reality is just something to participate in if you have the discernment to see its value.

    I'm certainly not as erudite as you, though I have some experience studying the sutras; but I also consider what was intended by this wise, compassionate, enlightened being, and I believe it's an approach that would not bind followers to a form just for the sake of the form. There's no self-glorification in it for the Buddha or for those who follow his teaching (though we may try). On the other hand, we do often see ego investment in "being Buddhist".

    What passes for Buddhism these days is broader than just Buddhadharma. The "ism" of Buddhism is maybe a useful categorization so that we can talk about what is really a whole constellation of scriptural records, historical events, cultural trappings, absorbed folk religions, old and new practices and techniques, philosophical insights and interpretations, and enlightened lineages (Buddha's own mind, alive and well). In terms of Buddhadharma, some of this is beside the point (that point being, shall we say, "waking up"?).

    If the /r/Buddhism subreddit were strictly focused on clarifying Buddhadharma, for instance, so many posts and comments would not apply. But we do encounter cultural references, philosophical tangents, speculative excursions, angry arguments, and so on.

    From what I've seen, the Buddha didn't urge people to treat his teaching as a religion, nor as a philosophy. He said, in effect, "look: if you do such-and-such, you will get this result; if you do so-and-so, you will get that result." He adapted the religious forms of the day so his followers could practice and express the teachings in their lives, but in another era and another situation other forms would have been used. The bottom line was that He urged people to do what leads to happiness and fulfillment.

    One of the best known examples of shedding the "ism" in the sutras is the following, from the Kalama Sutta:

    >Now, Kalamas, don’t go by reports, by legends, by traditions, by scripture, by logical conjecture, by inference, by analogies, by agreement through pondering views, by probability, or by the thought, ‘This contemplative is our teacher.’ When you know for yourselves that, ‘These qualities are skillful; these qualities are blameless; these qualities are praised by the wise; these qualities, when adopted & carried out, lead to welfare & to happiness’ — then you should enter & remain in them.

    Then we have the equally famous example of the raft from the Alagaddupama Sutta:

    >I have taught the Dhamma compared to a raft, for the purpose of crossing over, not for the purpose of holding onto. Understanding the Dhamma as taught compared to a raft, you should let go even of Dhammas, to say nothing of non-Dhammas.

    People only familiar with this excerpt should also know that this just states one side of the matter, and that the Alagaddupama Sutta also teaches about using discernment to grasp the Dharmas, cleaving to form. It points to the middle way, neither attaching to forms for their own sake nor abandoning forms just for the sake of formlessness. Rather, we use form and non-form for our liberation.

    I understand if some people want to make that approach into an "ism", but I don't care about it. I don't think that it's necessary in order to attain and actualize the Buddha's teachings. We just use the term so we can converse.

    So I don't believe in Buddhism, and yet I practice Buddhism. Go figure.

    If you think I'm mistaken, please chime in. I always enjoy your posts.
