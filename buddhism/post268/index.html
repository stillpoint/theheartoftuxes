    We all have some kind of hindrance. But the option of giving up and giving in to negativity isn't going to help.

    When we act with compassion, generosity, and integrity, we develop the karmic merit that gains better conditions in the future, including in future lives.

    Acting with these positive intentions also calms and focuses the mind in the present and near future. People often think that meditation is the only gateway to development, but that's a bit of a manipulative mindset, as if we're going to force our mind into submission. Actually, there are several interdependent fields of practice, not only sitting meditation. Without ethical behavior, for instance, no form of meditation can save you; and on the other hand, devotion to ethical behavior will bring greater peace of mind and confidence. Turning karma around is a very broad, very long-term project.

    Environment, relationships, and lifestyle all have very significant influence on the mind. If you can make these conditions supportive of clarity and nobility, they will go a long way toward bringing the mind under control. Some great meditation masters even say that eating can alone account for at least half of spiritual effort; that is, correct eating can bring the mental, physical, and ethical dimensions into alignment, while bad eating makes just as many harmful and disruptive influences.

    Bring in frequent contact with great beings -- teachers and master practitioners -- also subdues and clarifies mental and emotional faculties. Not only the teaching and example, but also the continuous practice of great beings benefits you directly. Hang out with sincere practitioners and masters, and it will be like a fragrance that gradually permeates your clothing.

    Physical practice is also highly recommended for people who are too agitated or distracted to do sitting meditation. I know many monks for whom physical practice is a major part of their lives. Prostrations are highly regarded (I've written about prostrations in several previous posts), or you can try Yoga, Taiji, Qigong, or conscious movement/dance practice like authentic movement and many other modalities.

    High-level practitioners don't tend to talk about enlightenment much. Their focus is on being clear, moment to moment. This is the momentary embodiment of practice, rather than ruminating, checking, doubting, fearing, being prideful, and so on. Only do it! Don't make a big idea about enlightenment, because it will become a big hindrance. If you have your right life direction and commit to it no matter what, enlightenment will come in its own time. Even for someone very committed and having gathered many good conditions for awakening practice, enlightenment may take dozens or hundreds of lifetimes. But it's better than being endlessly lost and suffering.

    But most important is your relationship to the moment. How do you embody the will to be clear and compassionate across hundreds of lifetimes -- in this moment, in this situation? Don't let mind wander to doubt and speculation. Your job is always facing you. What is in front of your face? Where are your feet standing? That is the arena of your enlightening practice. Find your right relationship with the real-life moment.

    Avalokitesvara is the great being who embodies compassion above all. Samantabhadra is the Bodhisattva of social activity and great vow. Ksitigarbha fearlessly looks into death, and ferries beings from state to state. These great beings have developed their practice to exceptional, transcendent degree, each in their own way. Meditation is not the only game in town. Though it's important to try some kind of grounding practice, there are many ways to unfold enlightenment, and each being finds her or his or its path uniquely.
