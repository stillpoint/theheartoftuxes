    Seeking feedback from experienced meditators

    I understand the concern, but like I said in my post, there may not be a 'perfect way' for you. Just like there may not be one 'soul mate' destined for you. There are just a bunch of people, and maybe you make one your soul mate through your choices and actions.

    Testing out a recipe of combined practices... meh. It's kind of re-inventing the wheel, no? I mean, there's little harm in trying out various groups and methods to see what you connect with; but don't harbor the illusion that you can figure it out better than enlightened beings and hundreds of generations of practitioners have.

    I attended a talk and empowerment by a very wonderful, brilliant, vibrant teacher Jetsun Khandro Rinpoche. She addressed the question of why we western students seem to try and try but have much difficulty breaking through — why we keep falling back into our habits. Khandro Rinpoche noted that while we are indeed sincere, we are also quite self-centered; and this is the core obstacle — or rather, this is the base cause of all obstacles.

    She talked about how people listen to her talks and nod as if they are in full agreement. They hear her guidance about the steps to take, what their proper attitude should be, how to carry out the practice; they nod in agreement. They hear her talk about unity, about cause and effect, about emptiness; they nod as if they understand. But then after the teaching there is a line-up of people wanting to talk to her because they think they have issues that apply to them alone! Khandro Rinpoche laughed at this, and we laughed with her. "Yes, your talk was wonderful, but I have this issue...."

    So everyone thinks their own situation is special, like the cause and effect of practice doesn't apply to them in this case. This is self-centered thinking.

    All major traditions of Buddhism have produced enlightened beings. All major traditions of Buddhism have produced many wonderful, noble, integral humans. All major traditions have training that is more or less complete in itself. Mixing is not necessary if you follow the whole instruction.

    You can choose what you like. You can combine practices. Then just do it.

    If you find that choosing what you like takes you away from consistent, deep practice, then you have to let it go. If you find that combining practices confuses the instruction, then you have to let it go. Those are a couple of the risks.

    Our mind habit is manipulative. It always wants a hand in the game, always wants to be hanging on to the remote control, ready to change the channel or tweak the settings. This is just the mind habit; it's not practice, and it's not necessary for your well-being. So when you feel the anxiety about whether a course of action will be right or wrong for you, recognize that as your self-centered and manipulative mind habit.

    Just doing it will help you. You don't need to tweak it. Develop your faith by trying.

    If you need a place to start, approach a teacher that you respect and ask them how to start and whether it would be good to combine practices. Then they can say what would be the benefit and what would be the risk with regard to their particular field.

    I guess what I'm saying is that while your question may feel crucial, it's actually not so necessary for you to create an ideal blend of practices. Do the actual practice, and let go of the figuring-out part. Never mind "what if", focus on what's in front of your nose right now. That's my advice, in case you find it reasonable enough to trust it.
