    How do I pierce the fog in my mind?

    That's great that you're identifying the need and following up. Don't stop. I've met many wonderful teachers, but it took me 10 or 15 years of searching before I found the deepest and fully trusting connection with my master. Even a lifetime of searching would have been worth it.

    Where do you live? Maybe I or another Buddhitor can make a recommendation.

    • You don't always have to be close to your teacher. Travelling to another town once a month or a couple of times a year to spend time with your teacher can still be every bit as transformative. That's how it used to be done in the old days: people would travel for weeks or months, would have one meeting with a teacher, and would return home to digest that one meeting, sometimes for years before the next meeting.

    • If you can't hook up with a residential center or temple you may still find a local sitting group. Look for postings at health food stores, libraries, alternative caf√©s, etc., or create a post yourself. Network; talk with people, ask if someone knows a friend of a friend who sits.

    • If you can't find a Buddhist group you may still find meditation practice and good teaching at other venues like some yoga centers, community centers, or (admittedly more rarely) churches.

    • Even a low-class teacher can be a high-class teacher for you if you stay sincere and open. You don't have to swallow anything, and you don't have to reject anything. Only show up sincerely.

    • If there's nothing out there yet, you can start a group yourself. Don't try to teach anything; just choose a practice and set up a schedule. Perhaps do a reading (consistently from just one tradition) at the end of sitting. Tea or snack is fine, but end soon after sitting so that chatter and group politics don't take over. Keep it very simple and don't try to cater to everyone's preferences, or there will likely be confusion; just say "this is what I'm doing, and I'd be happy if you want to join me".

    Once you have 5 people supportive of each other and committed to waking up, you have yourself a sangha.

    • Some teachers offer webinars or teachings over the internet. (Certain teachings, though, require live face-to-face contact.)

    • You may help yourself by arranging part of your living space as a visual reminder and physical connection to your spiritual aspiration. It can be a whole room or it can be part of a shelf ‚Äî as big or small as is appropriate to the situation. This is a space that you keep clean and spacious, and where you place images or objects that help you connect to your own bright, clean, spacious mind.

    Lighting a candle can remind you to keep your brightness and warmth alive. Refreshing a bowl of water daily can be a way of offering your freshness and renewal to what is most precious in life. And you can leave a space open or place an object representing the teacher ‚Äî the teacher you have not yet found but are coming ever closer to, and the inner teacher that you already have.

    Aside from that issue, have you chosen a good, clear method? Is it clear to you, according to the method, how you should use your mind and body moment to moment? And do you follow the method very precisely? This is important, and by itself has the power to raise your practice to new levels.
