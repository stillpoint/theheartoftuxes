    Why is there so much, how should I put it, "magic" references in the Buddhism that I am coming across?

    What is 'mystical'? What is 'magical'? What does 'power' refer to?

    Much of what you're talking about amounts to misunderstanding of (or not yet understanding) the origin and meaning of many ritual and cultural forms involved in Buddhism. Instead of assuming that your narrow view is the only valid one, perhaps you can be willing to learn a bit more about the forms on their own terms.

    In Buddhism, everything in one way or another points to true nature and liberation from suffering. All images, chants, ritual objects, postures, and the like are aimed at liberation. That is the first point: whatever you encounter has this one direction: wake up out of suffering.

    But the way the forms develop comes from both the enlightened minds of the masters and their local cultures. Buddhism is medicine for our human mind sicknesses, so of course it will unfold in harmony with the sentiment, etiquette, and aesthetic that people find accessible; it will adapt to local culture.

    So you have to know what the central point is, and what is peripheral. This will come over time, and with the help of a teacher. Just try not to be like the clich√© of the ugly American who, understanding nothing but his own way, deems everything else worthless.

    Images of Buddha may be representative of many things; among them:

    • A real person like you and me, who through extraordinary courage and perseverance woke up out of life's dream

    • Your own true mind, your own enlightened nature, which has always been there and need only be uncovered

    • A universal quality -- the awakened aspect of all things

    • The personification of that universal quality, couched in a form we may connect to devotionally

    • An aspect of practice or object of meditation tailored to certain personalities, helping to speed their progress in overcoming particular neuroses and digesting them as wisdom

    • A connection with esoteric or protective properties, enhancing the place of practice

    Anyone who has even dipped their toes into meditation can attest to the fact that the mind is a wild, ungraspable, and mysterious field. And on the contrary, anyone who thinks of themselves as just a body walking around, and who thinks reality is limited to what they see and understand, very clearly hasn't made the slightest progress in meditation and insight. One's experience of magic and mysticism exists on that basis.

    Mysticism means unity with reality. This is a very simple issue; but many millions of people still can't achieve it. Reality is not apprehended through thought or language. Your idea can never be big enough to grasp it; your belief can never be fluid enough to move with it. The only reason mysticism seems strange to someone is because of what that person clings to. Otherwise, it is very plain, simple, everyday life. It's just that you don't know it through your thinking-understanding.

    As one develops concentration, mental and emotional stability, insight into the way things really are, and subtle understanding and connection with the movement of mind, one also gains a self-mastery and influence on the world that can seem grand or unreachable to the common person, trapped in their narrow-mindedness. Magic, then, becomes just a label people apply to something beyond their conception. Everything moves, everything transforms; but we don't know how; so the name for that is 'magic'.

    Finally, 'power' just means not being moved by the things that usually move people. In Buddhism, there is the teaching of the Eight Worldly Winds (pleasure and pain, praise and blame, fame and shame, loss and gain). When one completely understands one's true nature, the worldly winds no longer have sway; one is free. That is power.

    But don't imagine that real power is 'possessed' by a 'self'. Once real power is attained, the illusion of selfhood has already been seen through. So power is not transmitted from one being to another -- not this kind of power.

    Enlightened beings are everywhere. Upon enlightenment, although they may retain their everyday form they also merge with the infinite. So quite literally Buddha is in the Buddha image, and in the mundane objects that populate your life, and in you. It's completely up to you whether you open to this universal awakening power. But its existence and transmission are indeed mysterious and beyond the conception of common people.

    So yes there is much in Buddhism that is strange or ungraspable, and rightly so. If that were not the case, it wouldn't be a very deep enterprise, would it?
