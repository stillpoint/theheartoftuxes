    You are training to be a nurse, so please tell me: is this reactivity a short-term, acute condition; or a long-term chronic condition? Did it come about just recently, due to some fortuitous condition; or is it the result of conditions that have been at work for a long time: upbringing, social conditioning, emotional wounding, deep-rooted ignorance?

    Your answer will reveal the kind of medicine required. A short-term, acute condition can be dispelled quickly with one or two courses of medicine. A long-term, chronic situation may require ongoing treatment, and treatment on several fronts.

    I think, clearly, our propensity to be tossed about by the world (in this case, by the suffering of others) is a long-term, deep-seated condition. So ongoing and multi-faceted remedy is required.

    Get proper training. Compassion in the world is a big project, which directly faces and eventually transforms deep psychological patterns. Good training offers wholistic benefits: proper guidance, support structure (group, setting, schedule, conceptual teaching, example, feedback, lifestyle training) and an ongoing practice develop compassion and personal stability/equanimity together, in balance.

    Right knowledge is necessary. Understanding karma — that we are all authors of our own situation — is important. Understanding samsara — the truth of our shared situation, insufficient, unsatisfactory, impermanant — is important. Understanding anatta — the unreality of selfhood — is important. All of these understandings contribute both to equanimity and to compassion.

    And along with understanding (or, we can say 'wisdom', which is understanding that functions correctly in the world), right mental training is necessary, so as not to be carried away by understanding, not to be kidnapped by thought, but to have a strong, stable, unmoving center.

    The first of the so-called 'perfections', or paramitas, which are the qualities that go beyond limited self-serving mindset and bring one to enlightenment, is dana paramita or openhandedness. This has a very profound meaning, which is revealed throughout the spiritual career of a Buddhist. For an entire lifetime, one may plumb the depths of its meaning and clarify its expression: to be openhanded, to be generous in both giving and receiving, to be free of grasping and manipulating.

    Dana is one of the key qualities you will express as a nurse. Help what you can. Be openhanded in that way. Don't assume there is only one way to help. Don't assume you have to only 'wear the nurse hat' in order to help. Yes, nursing knowledge and nursing activity helps. Also, smiling may help; or sometimes being stern. Practical help or kind words may help. Walking mindfully or waiting mindfully may help. Listening may help. Cutting off suffering trains of thought and speech may help. Resting and taking care of yourself may help. There are countless ways.

    But the openhandedness also means that you let the world turn as it does. You open your mind and heart to reality. Suffering can't be avoided. Illness and death will come to everyone. Usually when people have trouble with that truth it is because they avoid it in themselves. It is not only (or even mostly) the patient they are concerned for: they are concerned for their own impending suffering, their own impending death. They have not practiced deeply enough being with the truth of impermanence, being with it openhandedly. Bearing witness without turning away. Bearing witness to the fear, the horror, the vulnerability... and to the tremendous meaning it bestows on each living day, each living moment.

    We don't have to allow suffering to be the only word, the final word. There is liberation, now and in the future. There is the will to be fully present, fully alive, regardless of the fluctuations of life.

    Knowledge, mental training, and then the application of these in compassionate action fall under the categories of the three subjects of Buddhist training: Prajna is wisdom — not only knowledge, but correct, appropriate knowledge springing from original emptiness (Unknowing). Dhyana is meditation, the right mental training. And Sila is ethical discipline, which means the application of Prajna and Dhyana in relationship — acting compassionately in the world. These three categories must be trained together in order to attain freedom while still remaining engaged, with care and kindness.
