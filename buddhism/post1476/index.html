    My wish for someone's well-being, and my seeing what is required to achieve it, are completely separate issues from wanting them to be a certain way so that I can feel better.

    Ongoing, moment-to-moment spiritual practice keeps the mind smooth and clear. Our usual mind is very sticky: the slightest thing clings to it and affects it. But keeping a practice of mindfulness (mindfulness of breath, or walking, or mindful physical activity, or mindfulness of ethical activity, or mantra, or visualization, or any of countless moment-to-moment practices) keeps the mind smooth. You can perceive suffering, but you don't latch onto it and make stories about it and react to it.

    Likewise, you can perceive suffering and not run away. In many cases our bad feeling and inner drama is a strategy to avoid actually being responsible for what we can and should change (which often has to do with our own bad karma, our own wrong intentions and actions).

    I think we'd find that a lot of the fear and grief we generate when thinking about others' suffering really is rooted in fear and grief about our own situation. We don't really care that much about others. But when compassion is cultivated and made broad and all-inclusive, we don't shy away from great sorrow. We just don't make it personal.

    Then my suffering or my loved one's suffering is just a gateway into universal suffering — the situation we all share, all beings everywhere. Knowing the particular, our hearts can open to the universal. And this immediately leads to our redoubled efforts to wake up out of Samsara, and to our specific response to the momentary situation.

    Universal suffering is a truth we awaken to. The present-moment, specific situation facing us is a truth we awaken to. But making thoughts like "Oh, why is this happening?" or "How terrible!" is something we ourselves add on top of the situation; it is not truth, it is the agitated activity of thought. Practice helps us let that go, moment to moment, so we can be effective.

    Address the practical issues you can address, and by the same token don't think that maintaining a radiant field of love or joy or clear, non-judgmental presence is not practical or not helpful! It is never a bad idea, and in many cases it is absolutely the most valuable thing you can do for another suffering being. If you keep your strong root in the face of your loved one's pain and difficulty, it helps them experience it as truth rather than as 'problem' or 'tragedy'.

    I also want to bring awareness to the issue of co-dependency. When we have a strong sense of self-worth we are less likely to be thrown around by what happens to others close to us. Co-dependency means that we take on material that is not our own in relationships.

    > Attention and energy focus on the family member who is ill or addicted. The co-dependent person typically sacrifices his or her needs to take care of a person who is sick. When co-dependents place other people’s health, welfare and safety before their own, they can lose contact with their own needs, desires, and sense of self.

    – From Mental Health America: Codependency

    > Broadly speaking, in dysfunctional helping relationships, one person’s help supports (enables) the other’s underachievement, irresponsibility, immaturity, addiction, procrastination, or poor mental or physical health.

    – From Are you in a codependent relationship?

    The aim is not to take on someone else's turmoil, but to empower them (and ourselves) to face the truth of living-and-dying.
