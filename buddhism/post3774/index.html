    Selfishness and death, old age...

    That's great that you're considering life and death. But you're going too fast with your thinking, making assumptions and jumping to incorrect conclusions. So please allow down and be more meticulous with your analysis.

    We should be aware of our capacity to fool ourselves with thinking. We should be aware of the capacity of excitement about ideas to carry us away. We should be aware of the deep-seated fear of uncertainty, and the deep-seated urge to cover up that uncertainty by clinging to ideas.

    Maybe there are some matters that can afford speculation and lazy or hurried thinking, but this is not one of them. In matters of life-and-death and matters of practice, meticulous analysis is important. If you are going to follow a path of analysis, the analysis had to be clear and certain; and some things may take many years our even lifetimes to clarify.

    The serious student calls on experienced and wise guides to help clarify the analysis, to help find the places where thinking had gone off. So it's good that you ask for feedback here; although there is no guarantee that the people who respond in this forum will respond with clarity and wisdom. So it is really best if you find an awakened master or at least a very capable teacher to train you in the proper method of analysis.

    Death is not liberation. Merely dying does not cast off the karma one has made through one's life and previous lives.

    Death does not end suffering; it merely ends the temporary association with this particular body; but with or without a new body, one's mind-stream retains the clinging patterns that block wisdom and cause suffering.

    So there very much is a point to thinking â€œWhat have I done with my life?" Without that questioning, how will you develop the will to look into your ignorance, your self-centered habits, and the ways you hurt yourself and others. Awareness of the reality and finality of death is the great motivator for us; and when we naively presume that everything will be fine, or vaguely imagine that we have a lot of time left, there is little impetus to change our ways and to be serious and meticulous about our thoughts and actions.

    If a person realizes the essentially empty nature of form, there need be no sadness about death and the transformation of form. But even though many of us understand it in our heads, we haven't digested it and made it a reality in our relationships and everyday activity. Even small things we still cling to and take personally and mourn; how much more so when it's a deeply cherished relationship?

    So I wouldn't begrudge someone their sadness and mourning. Instead, let's have the courage to open our hearts and join them in whatever vulnerability they're experiencing. If we ourselves are clear and stable, we can afford to open to the suffering of others, not just brush it off with the wave of a hand, saying "you shouldn't think that way."
