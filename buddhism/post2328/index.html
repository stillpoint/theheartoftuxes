    TheHeartofTuxes

    Bodhisattvas are embodiments of awakening and blessing.

    Bodhi' comes from the same root as the word Buddha. It means 'awake', and refers to the pure, absolute wisdom mind that the Buddha attained, the mind that is the essential nature of all beings.

    Sattva literally means 'being', both in the sense of a separate embodied existence and in the sense of existence itself. It also carries the meanings of goodness and truth.

    So Bodhisattva is the pure awakened mind that comes into being (and is also good and true).

    We hear or read about these beings and see their images, so it very easily happens that we conceive of them according to our own understanding of what constitutes a 'being'. In particular, we may think of them as basically people, perhaps with some good qualities. This is an extremely limited view.

    Buddhist teaching says there are realms of activity that can't be perceived by the common person. A Bodhisattva's activity goes far, far beyond our limited perception and concept. That is why we look for other words to speak of their activity, none of which suffice.

    Bodhisattva is not only an external, person-like being. It is also ultimately an aspect of your own awakened mind. In the case of Avakokite≈õvara, it is your innate, perfect, infinite compassion. The mind that has been attained by a Bodhisattva is also your own innate, as-yet-unrevealed nature.

    Bodhisattva manifests where there is need, and according to their vow of beneficial activity. The great master Khandro Rinpoche says that Bodhisattvas may appear as bridges, music, and other unacknowledged blessings in our lives. They also appear as blessing completely unseen by us (or at least, to those of us who have not yet attained exceptional perception of the spiritual dimension of Sambhogakaya and the pure dimension of Dharmakaya).

    Bodhisattvas may take gentle forms or extremely wrathful forms, or all kinds of forms in between (e.g., stern or semi-wrathful). These too respond to ignorance and suffering in their manifold forms. For example, if you make a mistake that causes suffering, Avalokitesvara may appear in nurturing form as the benefit that arises through atonement and good spiritual practice. But if you keep ignoring your responsibility and keep doing bad action, Avalokitesvara or some other compassionate Bodhisattva may appear in more and more fierce forms, as your pain and loss and disaster, to shake you out of your harmful delusion.

    When you participate in practice vows and close relationship with a teacher and lineage, other beings may come into play: protectors of the practice and lineage, who help you when you are sincere and bring repercussions when you break your vows or threaten the lineage.

    There are many dimensions of beings, awakened and unawakened, common and powerful. Some powerful beings could be called 'gods', but more in the sense of Michael Jordan playing basketball against five-year-olds -- it doesn't make him necessarily an object of worship, it just means his powers seem to be vast and inconceivable. So there is a distinction between merely powerful 'god-like' beings and beings who are both powerful and fully enlightened, who bring forth unimaginable benefit in infinite ways (hence the many hands of some depictions of Avalokitesvara).
