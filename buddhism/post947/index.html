    > Could you please tell me what the use of this compassion is? Do you intend it to "manifest" somehow in the world in a practical manner?

    These are very good questions.

    Of course we have the motivation to act with compassion, even recognizing that we are flawed and will sometimes fail. (And so we also offer ourselves compassion.)

    But in the active moment, the truly compassionate mind doesn't have 'self' and 'other'. If there is an opinion or agenda brought in, the mind is not in harmony with the moment. It is something imposed upon the moment rather than harmonization.

    The name Avalokiteshvara means "the one who hears universal sound". This is the bare, clearly perceiving mind. In some depictions, we see Avalokiteshvara with a thousand hands, each with an eye in the palm. This means that the compassionate ways of responding are infinite, but in each of the ways, perception and action are inseparable. This points to how that mind is used.

    First, put it all down. Go straight to emptiness, not holding any concept or agenda. Then trust that mind in action. It is completely natural, uncontrived activity. For example, when a friend says "hi", you respond effortlessly; and when a stranger asks your name, there's no second thought about it.

    But does one trust the uncontrived mind? Or does one try to be 'good' or 'correct', or just mindlessly follow past habits and ingrained preferences? 'Good' changes from culture to culture, family to family, and even from one phase of life to another. Trying to fit into an image or set of rules sometimes works out nicely, but strictly speaking it is not compassion. We have seen entire cultures decimated by a nation or faction imposing their idea of goodness. Colonization, slavery, and even large-scale torture have been performed in the name of goodness. Within our personal relationships and even within our own minds, we continue to carry out this kind of totalitarian reign, with concept and habitual view controlling the agenda.

    It is difficult for people to grasp uncontrived action without training. It is a spontaneous, creative process. Perhaps artists can understand it better. A poet or dancer may recognize that open, receptive moment through which spontaneous action arises. It is not merely acting without thinking; it is appropriate, it reflects the situation. When the mind is clear like a mirror, the situation is reflected in it.

    Someone puts their hand out, you respond to shake it. The traffic light turns red, you stop. You spill a glass of milk, you clean it up. Someone falls, you help them. If it is performed from the uncontrived mind, this is all compassionate activity. Like the shining of the sun.

    > There is a lot of mutual hatred, violence and anxiety in my country right now. People seem to be willing to die and kill each other for their ideas. Wouldn't helping them mean that we have to argue against these ideas?

    Where does the hatred, violence, and anxiety come from? It comes from ignorance about the empty nature of self. We don't understand our true self. This creates original separation, from which all divisive activities arise. They process is called prapanca in Sanskrit: "proliferation". Original separation continues dividing and becomes a world of division.

    The cure is the original, non-divided mind (called bodhicitta or "mind of enlightenment"). This mind is clear like a mirror.

    Sometimes the clear mirror of mind may respond appropriately as teaching words. Yes, sometimes that is necessary. But in general people don't believe deeply in words; they believe deeply in faith-mind and action. When you witness the actions of a selfless hero, for instance, that moves you and transforms you much more than mere words. That kind of selfless action may open you to subsequent words.

    Sometimes offering space, silence, and open listening is far more necessary than words. Sometimes offering your own sincere vulnerability is far more important than acting as an expert or authority. Very often, people who are distraught with sorrow and vengeance will only mistrust or twist any words you offer, and argument will drive them to further violence. You have to find a way into their hearts first; you have to unify with them, giving up your own way in order to act together with them.

    My thoughts turn to some of the most influential beings, who chose to heal through compassion: Buddha, Ashoka, Jesus... in more recent times, Gandhi, Thich Nhat Hanh, Martin Luther King Jr., Maha Ghoshananda. Their words were powerful because of their actions and the way they kept their minds moment to moment.

    It is said that the activity of a Bodhisattva is like how you adjust a pillow in your sleep. Simple, uncontrived.
