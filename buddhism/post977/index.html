    Thoughts about 'The Middle Way'

    First of all, the quote is a broad paraphrase; it's Alan Watts' speech, not the Buddha's. But it offers one take on the Middle Way. Secondly, you should know that Watts has his own slant on things; he's a lovely speaker, but sometimes misses deeper levels of insight available in wisdom lineages.

    The point here is that externals can't save you. You can't arrange your liberation by having or not having the 'right' things. Your liberation comes through your non-clinging relationship to any situation and condition that may appear.

    (Actually, when non-clinging is complete, there is no more relationship. There is no longer an assumed distinction between subject and object, between you and your possessions, experiences, and other beings.)

    Monk style life is correct for monks. Homeowner style life is correct for homeowners. There are many ways, but the Great Way means to be non-attached to any way.

    Watts was very helpful in offering a perspective that embraces our human tendencies. The western Judeo-Christian mind may have begun to embrace Buddhism, but it did it through the filter of its own religious perspective, in terms of sin and punishment. Even though westerners learned about karma, emotionally they still connected to it through deeply ingrained feelings of shame. Watts offered a balancing view, a compassionate view that embraced our desires.

    But though these human faults are to be embraced and met with compassion, they are still faults in terms of our need for awakening. Craving anything, whether it's less of something or more of something, is a problem. We just need to have wisdom about how we approach that craving. It won't do to just try to destroy it outright. That just tends to drive it into the unconscious.

    Whatever you imagine about what you will or will not be able to do in the future is idle speculation. This moment, what is your correct relationship to your situation? Buddhism is extremely practical. It focuses on cause and effect: how can you avoid the causes of suffering for yourself and others; and how can you create the causes of liberation and happiness for yourself and others?

    Many possessions are not necessary, and only get in the way. We create a lot of suffering in our world because of the objects we crave. Factory farming and animal abuse, environmental devastation, child exploitation, colonial war, and the soulless vacuity of consumer society are a few of the ways we cause suffering with our craving for more.

    But depending on your situation, shunning possessions may also create a block to practice (for example if you become too busy trying to survive or maintain your health, and have little time or energy to devote to practice). 'Doing without' can also become an ego activity, full of self-congratulation and a sense of spiritual superiority. Another example is that dogmatic parents may sometimes deprive their children of basic needs because of their own attachment to austerity.

    Some possessions are good for one person and not for another. Who knows what kind of beauty and inspiration come into one's life in relation to a particular object? It may vary.

    It's not about the outside, though outside things may either hinder or support practice until the independent mind becomes strong. When, through spiritual practice, the mind becomes strongly seated in unconditionality, outside things will no longer have the ability to sway you. Then you can use every situation for the benefit of others: rich and poor situations can both be used wisely.

    Check Chogyam Trungpa's book Cutting Through Spiritual Materialism.
