    Quick (maybe) question on meditation

    I don't think we really disagree much, other than using words in a different way. Your use of the term meditation is somewhat broader than mine.

    It's good that you point out the aim of non-attachment from thinking rather than obliteration of it. If you were familiar with my other posts over the years you would know that I am a firm and eager advocate of that point. It's also good that you point out that beginners can do good and pure practice. I'll hold to a more pure definition of meditation, though.

    What you describe is very necessary for practitioners along the way, but there comes a point where samadhi is required, which arrives as either unity with the object in object-related meditation (and then opens into clear, still mind), or as the leap to effortlessly still mind in objectless meditation.

    >This is the foundation of practice whether you've practiced for 1 day or 60 years.

    That may be the approach in some traditions or courses of training, but not in all. And to say it is the "foundation" is also perhaps misleading. Letting thoughts appear and then returning to the object is a common form or method. The foundation is the root of mind. So what is that? It is not reached or expressed by any technique. No strategy can find it, no words can grasp it, no thought can attain it. In it, there are "no eyes, no ears, no nose, no tongue, no body, no mind...."

    Only that point is true meditation. The rest are practices that take you to the door. Although they are wonderful and beneficial, if you stay there you can't be liberated. The reason the object-related form may still be an expression of awakening for a 60-year practitioner, a master, is that somewhere along the line he or she has attained the foundation and is now embodying it in the form. If he/she only ever maintained a relationship to an object of meditation, he/she would forever be stuck in the world of objects, participating in the illusion of a separate meditator "doing" practice.

    The next step is necessary.

    Sorry if you take umbrage at me excluding some practices from the category "pure meditation". I did mention a couple of times that the term meditation is also used as an umbrella term referring to a wide range of practices. If you want to use the term in that way, that's fine; but then what words do you choose in order to talk about the embodiment of original mind, which is pure and clear like a diamond, or like open space?

    I'm glad you spoke up, though, to fill in points not mentioned.

    Edit: It occurs to me that you might be interested in my response to a query about Metta practice. You might find that you and I are not far off in our views.
