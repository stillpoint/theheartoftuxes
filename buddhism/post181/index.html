    Cause and effect are clear. This is the bottom line. Cause and effect are clear. Your mind, your intent, your clinging, your habit, your courage, your avoidance... whatever result you get comes from the cause you make.

    You are speaking from purely speculative position. At this point it is all just theoretical for you, because you don't know why retreat is valuable, and you don't know how retreat unfolds. So you only have opinion. It would be most proper to just usher you toward further experience in training and practice, but in case empty words help you or others I will explain a bit:

    In isolation, your own whim and preference becomes king. It is very common for inexperienced meditators to imagine they can do it all themselves, but what actually happens is that their own patterns and views just become ingrained. Of course! Because they always have things their favorite way. No new, unknown, transformative experience enters.

    But when thrust into an uncompromising structure, the cavalier attitude is soon exposed. When one's favorite is not longer the lead agenda, but rather awakening is, all the struggles and lamentations of ego come rushing forth. This is part of the value of a clear and devoted practice setting, and part of the value of practicing with others: one's attachments get brought to light, so one can finally start to actually be free of them.

    For clear practice structure, a teacher is required, and retreat leader overseeing the quality and practical requirements of the practice, and administrators taking care of the venue, registration, accommodations, food, heating, health issues, etc. All of these efforts are aimed at providing the retreatant with space and time and focus, so that every moment can be devoted to the most precious pursuit.

    On your own, you need to attend to cooking and cleaning and other everyday practicalities rather than focusing on meditation and spiritual practices. On your own, your mind tends to wander, unclear about aspects of practice, or it tends to stagnate, satisfied to remain at its current level of effort and consciousness. The structure of retreat and the teaching provide the container and focus that anyone but the most advanced practitioner fails to maintain on their own. (But then, the most advanced practitioners are the ones who know they need retreat, and participate with the greatest joy and consistency.)

    In our self-centered, miserly, indulgent society it is perhaps easy to imagine that teachers and teaching venues should be there to serve us, and that we should be free to come and go, consuming at will. But the reality is that the people and institutions also require support. Food, electricity, rent, taxes — practice centers are not immune to expense. And if they are to be free to offer the precious Dharma to many others, the teachers require our input, our support.

    People often withhold funds, complaining about the supposed greed of teachers and administrators at practice centers; but really the issue is the person's greed — our tight-fisted, money-grubbing mind. Even at that, many centers still offer student discounts, work-study exchange, scholarships, and other recourse for those too poor or lazy or cheap to pay their own way.

    As to the benefit, until one is pushed to do hard practice, complacency tends to be the rule. It's very easy to talk about merging practice with everyday life or being mindful 24-7; but it is very, very clear who actually transforms their mind and life. Practitioners who are passionate about waking up commit themselves wholeheartedly. They rush to practice; they find joy in supporting their teachers, offering some relatively tiny compensation in exchange for the immense compassion they receive; they take every opportunity to challenge themselves to go further, go deeper.

    Those who talk a good game but don't follow up with actual effort get results to match — the results of their good talk and poor effort.

    You don't yet have experience with retreat; so you don't know why $500 or $3,000 might be required or might be worth it. You don't know where the money goes, what's it's used for, and what benefit you and others get.

    You don't yet have the heart that wants to offer all it can to a teacher and community; your heart is so far only concerned with expense vs personal gain.

    You don't yet know that money is empty — just another form of energy, just another mental projection; you are as yet clinging to temporary and unreal forms while spurning true treasures.

    Hopefully you will be able to let go of clinging and opinionation and joyfully embrace strong practice.
