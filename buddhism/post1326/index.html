    >Forgive me, but I have to ask. You say:

    >> One has to expose oneself to good teaching, not just arbitrary or random ideas.

    >And then:

    >> People think that a concept can be right or wrong.

    >Is this not circular logic?

    No it's not circular knowledge.
    Do you understand why?

    >I'm always very confused when people are encouraged not to discuss topics.

    Confusion comes from attachment to concept. If you don't hold your opinion, then you can see the point, and you can understand the difference between good and bad teaching.

    >There are even examples in the sutras where groups of monks were able to find Nirvana through discussion.

    Yes, monks. Monks who had studied and practiced with the Buddha, and who had devoted their every moment of every day to clear practice. The Buddha also taught very often about non-attachment to concept.

    Do you think the monks had good teaching available to them? Do you think they were encouraged to speculate on unnecessary things and indulge in making opinions?

    >I am not so foolish as to believe this will happen on Reddit

    So you do see the point. You just want to hold on to the opportunity to chat.

    >1. If the discussion may help someone along their path, why discourage it?

    If the discussion furthered the Dharma, why would I discourage it?

    >2. If this subreddit is not for discussion, what is it for exactly?

    Yes, excellent question.
    Important question.

    Are you assuming the existence of this forum is the most important thing? It is a very good point to consider. "Does my engagement in discussion here further the Dharma?" If one answers too quickly, there may be some deep attachment there.

    Everyone wants attainment; but only a rare few are willing to give up their favorite attachments, such as spouting opinions. If I get to spout my opinion, then I get to think of myself as someone who knows; and that is the all-consuming egocentric pursuit, regardless of whether it's covered with a spiritual paint job. The survival of the ego is the motive behind the scenes for most of us. "Pay no attention to the man behind the curtain!" Reddit and other online forums are opinion factories. It goes against good practice. You already know that many discussions here are fruitless. You already know that many redditors circle around exchanging talk without getting to the point.

    There are persistent illusions here: that one can talk one's way to knowledge, that conceptual knowledge itself is enough, and that agreement (with online strangers no less) constitutes knowledge. The mutual contract to take refuge in concepts rather than in the Three Jewels is dominant.

    I do see clarity and devotion on these forums, but it is the exception, not the rule. When there is clear, Dharmic communication, I note it and support it. When the talk is fruitless, I point out out.

    The tragedy is that newcomers who don't know better believe, and are encouraged to believe, that 'Dharma by consensus' is a thing. They don't know what good teaching is; and they don't seek it out; and they collude with each other to keep things that way, unaware that the 'armchair practitioner' approach -- seeking and making ideas, without ever getting wet with real Dharma -- is ego-driven, control-driven, contrary to the path. The medium is the message, as Marshall McLuhan taught. The way in which we investigate Dharma here, in large part, obscures it.
