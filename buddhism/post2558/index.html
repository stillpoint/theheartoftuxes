    Probably a silly question but...

    >I am very worried I will not be able to really enjoy it and live in it as I want to - which was the whole point of scheduling this vacation in the first place. At the same time, I am worried that if I do truly allow myself to enjoy it, I will be all the more disappointed to return to my stressful life.

    This is very, very familiar. One worries about opening the floodgates and then realizing how much there is to release, and how little opportunity, before one has to get back in control and tend to the demands of the "other" life.

    There is also a wish to let relaxation and its healthy repercussions and realizations unfold in their own way, which could easily happen over weeks and months rather than days.

    Let me assure you: any letting-go, any movement to clarity and peace of mind, is worthwhile. Within the first minutes of being disengaged from schedule and breathing the new air, you may find yourself in the joy or grief that needs to flow through you. Even within the first seconds, you may be deeply grateful and realize that of course even these seconds were worth it.

    I encourage you to not have an agenda for your rest time. Don't force it along, but listen. One thing you can do is to be conscious of what you hope to accomplish — or at least start — during your vacation time. Open rest? Self-care? Getting clear on something? If you are honest with yourself you know what you need. It is good to detach from routines, and especially from the things we commonly turn to in avoidance of listening to painful truths.

    As to how much stress to take on, there is no formula. Each according to his capacity. Each according to what she is responsible for. If you make a mess, clean it up and then refrain from making a mess in the future.

    Too much stress saps life. Too little stress discourages life. Like sprouts finding their way through the earth, life likes a challenge. It is said that the cold winter makes the blossoms. If you have a clear life direction, then all your trouble becomes wisdom and compassion. If you don't have a clear life direction, then trouble is just trouble without any good purpose.

    Buddhism means saving all beings from suffering. The first one is you. You can't save anyone else if you throw yourself away. You can't offer anyone else peace or clarity if you yourself don't have it. So how much do you value your peace of mind? Is there something more valuable?

    If you're going through hell, then keep going — keep walking, keep moving on. Buddhists and other people who practice become good at dropping something gripped and moving on to the next thing. This is your momentary salvation. In the broader picture, you may have to look at your life and ask how it is serving you and how it is holding you back. No new life arrives without a death; no new chance appears without something else moving out of the way.
