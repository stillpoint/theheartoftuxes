    TheHeartofTuxes

    If you were in a horrible situation, would you continue to make it worse or would you work on making it better? This is the functional point.

    Likewise, if you have an exceptionally good situation, would you squander it and turn it to garbage, or would you work on extending and improving it?

    Suffering terribly in a human lifetime can still be regarded as very fortunate, based on the singular fact that in a human life it is possible to turn toward the Dharma, and thus toward the prospect of completely doing away with the otherwise endless cycles of suffering. Great pain and difficulty can be used as fodder for awakening. And in fact it often turns out for us that we only start to wake up and make sincere effort once we hit rock-bottom, once we are humbled and realize there is nothing to hold onto.

    So living a human life of misery could still be seen as having some good karmic seeds. You may have done horribly negative deeds to bring about the suffering, but you also have done some kind of tremendously positive deeds to attain a human birth and have a chance at planting Dharmic seeds.

    The twist of tragedy happens when the misery blinds one to the possibility of positive action, or makes life so difficult that no time, leisure, or resources are available for Dharmic activity. This kind of situation is said to occur when one has slandered the Dharma in past lives, or created obstacles to others' practice, or severely corrupted one's samaya (one's practice contract). That kind of action creates a karmic block to Dharma, so that even with the tremendous good fortune of a human birth one's contact with true teaching and Dharmic activity is thwarted at every turn. It is as if you were given a precious treasure and willfully disowned it. Okay, then because you disowned it, it doesn't come back to you. The only recourse may be to do positive, compassionate activity as much as possible, building up karmic merit until suffering sufficiently exhausts the negative accumulations.

    Zen offers a bit of a different approach: only stay clear, not depending on the external situation. Lovely or horrific, pleasurable or painful — either way is no problem. When you have wonderful karma, your wonderful karma is medicine. When you have dreadful karma, your dreadful karma is medicine. When you live with the devas, you keep clear mind and help them all wake up. When you life with hell beings and suffering ghosts, you keep clear mind and lead them out of suffering. Any situation is no problem. Don't check, don't judge.

    Similarly, Dzogchen offers the view that all manifestations are originally pure. Heaven and hell (and heavenly and hellish situations) are all projections of basic mind, which is essentially Buddha-nature. Lovely experience is lovely enlightening being. Fierce, fiery experience is fierce, fiery enlightening being. Again, no problem. Stay relaxed and clear; that is all.
