    >This leads me to say that much of what is called Zen these days is not Zen at all but an interpretation of Zen based upon a misreading of Zen literature.

    I couldn't agree more.

    >It is rather ironic that modern Zen teachers hardly discuss the importance of seeing the Buddha nature or kensho.

    It may be ironic in some cases; but in other cases it may have implications more profound than the author imagines.

    >Some of these teachers teach the path of Dogen Zenji who taught “The essence of the Buddha-Dharma is never seeing the nature”!

    To think that Dogen's teaching is a negation of true nature is — ironically — a misreading of his literature. It's hard to overstate the arrogance of someone who cautions against misinterpretation and then throws one of the greatest Zen Masters of all time under the bus based on a misunderstood statment.

    Although we may use 'seeing one's true nature' as an upaya (skillful means), a concept contrived to point one in a certain direction in practice, the idea that true nature is a thing that exists is false, as is the notion that one can 'see' it as a subject seeing an object. The 'true nature' is... what? Unknowing, boundless, ungraspable, empty of own-being. It is utterly dark in our ability to distinguish it; it is utterly brilliant in its illumination of everything.

    There is no 'getting' enlightenment, and no "I" that gets it. When realization is achieved, what is realized is formless and ungraspable.

    Although from one side, realization depends on practice; from another side, practice depends on realization. Dogen's teachings on the unity of practice and realization are far deeper than many readers realize; it's not a departure from realizing true nature, but a very devoted and stringent application of it: it is realized from moment to moment, from the very first aspiration, rather than merely at the end of an otherwise deficient process. If critical readers would undergo the actual training and practice they could unfold those teachings in their lives rather than rail against them in their discursive thought.

    Furthermore, to conceive of oneself as a being not privy to true nature is just as false. The idea of an external true nature and the idea of a path to it or a being who follow the path to it — all of those are mistaken. True nature, the path, the realization, and you are never separate; and none of those things (true nature, path, realization, self) are things that can be identified in form.

    To condemn Dogen for this teaching is, very ironically, indicative of the very ignorance and clinging to false concepts that the article asks others to rise above. It's a matter of someone condemning what he/she doesn't understand.

    This is yet another example of 'armchair philosophizing'. The mistake may have been prevented with a better embrace of context: realizing the era and audience for which the teaching was presented (an era in which zen had in some cases devolved into striving for external states; and in this particular example Dogen is addressing the case of a philosopher wrongly using the phrase 'seeing true nature' to equate Buddhism, Taoism, and Confucianism). The mistake almost surely would have been prevented with a more real grounding in practice: embodying the non-separation of realization and momentary practice.

    This is not an issue of Rinzai versus Soto. It is an issue of poor realization and opinion versus deep realization and nonattachment to concept.

    >Here lies the paradox of enlightenment. On the one hand, when one practices the way of awakening, there is already enlightenment moment after moment. On the other hand, one has to endeavor long and hard to achieve a breakthrough. Dogen says, “There are those who continue realizing beyond realization.” Thus, enlightenment unfolds itself, but the unfolding is fully grasped by one’s body and mind only when one has a breakthrough. In other words, unfolded enlightenment is initially subconscious awakening, which is spontaneously merged with conscious awakening at the moment of breakthrough.

    >The koan studies of the Linji-Rinzai line are an excellent method for working consciously toward breakthrough. By contrast, Dogen’s training method was to keep students from striving toward breakthrough. Although he fully understood the value of breakthroughs and used breakthrough stories of his ancestors for teaching, he himself emphasized “just sitting,” with complete nonattachment to the goal of attainment. But isn’t freedom from attachment an essential element for achieving breakthroughs?

    > – Fundamentals Of Dogen's Thoughts, by Kazuaki Tanahashi
