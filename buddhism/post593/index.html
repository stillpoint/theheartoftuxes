    Mu is the famous teaching uttered by Zen Master Zhaozhou in the koan case "Does A Dog Have Buddha Nature?"

    In Zen koan practice, Mu is beyond concept. That is the point — or one of them: to bring the student beyond concept, to direct insight into reality.

    If you are interested in koan practice, you need a teacher to at the very least introduce you to the proper approach and monitor your understanding and application of it. After you have demonstrated that you can apply your beyond-concept mind to practice within the specific frame of a teaching situation (koan), then if your original motivation is very strong and selfless it may be possible to proceed independently for some time, as was occasionally done in the days of ancient teachings.

    If you want to get to the heart of Mu, you need to let go of trying to figure it out. Getting opinions from others is not only of no help, but an obstacle that takes you further from the essential point. Generating opinions on your own likewise is of no help, and an obstacle.

    This kind of approach — looking to have Mu explained — is rightly considered cheating, not because it goes against some stiff-collared rule book, but because by going about it this way you cheat yourself and others of the opportunity to actually find out this most important point.

    Only finding Mu in the well of your own Unknowing wisdom mind, your beyond-concept mind, will bring you wisdom that is your own. You have to have your own direct experience. Anything else is not your wisdom, but merely the spittle that comes from flapping mouths.

    In order to practice correctly with Mu, then, two major requirements or conditions have to be fulfilled: you have to have a clear, expert teacher; and you have to have the right motivation regarding the absolute necessity of waking up and very firm faith in the method. Without either of these, you'll just be messing around making problems for yourself and others, you won't be practicing Zen.

    ~

    From Soen (Zen) Master Jinje on questioning with the central point of a koan:

    >If you can keep this question then this is called "keeping the hwadu [the question beyond concept]" or "pursuing the hwadu" and this method of practice is called Ganhwa Seon [Zen inquiry]. So this type of practice is not simply meditation and, without this question it is difficult to determine whether you are practicing Seon [Zen] or just feeding your delusions. If you think you know or think you can find out what the masters Zhaozhou or Yunmen meant, then you are also simply feeding your delusions. The value of the hwadu is that it cannot be answered by thinking, it must produce this question. This sharp question that cannot be forgotten, if you can keep this question all throughout the day you will eventually fall into "Hwadu Samadhi" where thoughts, discriminations, even your own body is forgotten about. Once you are in this Samadhi you may hear a noise or see something and then the hwadu will break open, then you can know what the masters Zhaozhou or Yunmen meant.

    > A seemingly simple exchange of words with a master, like the above, can be a hwadu in itself. Otherwise you can recieve a formal hwadu from a master and pursue your study with that.

    > But hwadu practice isn't something you can start after reading a book, you must meet with a clear-eyed master or else practicing with a hwadu will become nothing more than any other type of meditation practice.

    > It is important that you receive a hwadu from a clear-eyed master for two reasons:

    > 1) While practicing with a hwadu may seem to be simple without instruction it is very difficult to continue your practice correctly.

    > 2) During Seon practice, if you have an experience that leads you to believe, "I know!" you must have a clear-eyed master whom you have faith in, to examine your practice. If he says "that is not so" then you must be able to put down your opinions and resume your practice with hawdu.

    ~

    And on working with the Mu koan specifically: >...the intention of the “mu” character hwadu does not lay within the “mu” character, it lays within Zhaozhou who said “mu.” Therefore, don’t investigate “mu,” you should be delving into the intention of the one who said “mu.”
