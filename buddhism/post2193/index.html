    >I don't really know how to live my life now

    This is appropriate. Wisdom doesn't mean knowing what is going to happen. Stay momentary, and keep your correct, unknowing relationship with the situation as it changes.

    >I feel like my whole life was a lie, I felt sickness in my stomach.

    The thinking and the feeling are two different things. We say "I feel..." when we should be saying "I had the thought that...."

    Sickness in the stomach indicates readiness (need) to give something up -- to throw it up, to stop swallowing it.

    >My meditation this morning was absolutely out of this world

    Your meditation was as it was. That was already in the past, but you made the thought "out of this world".

    Many, many strange, awesome, and terrible things will appear in the course of practice. Don't attach or make anything of them; only take the next step clearly. When the sitting is over, stretch your legs and stand up. That is all.

    So let things be what they are, let them speak for themselves. It's not that you aren't having certain experiences; it's that you then make ideas about them, hardening them into concept (which can never contain the experience) rather than letting them be what they are and letting them move and change in their own natural ways.

    >I'm not sure if there's a specific step that I should take now, do things in correct order.

    Previously, you weren't concerned with 'proper order', you were only doing one thing then the next, without checking. If you don't make anything in your mind, that is already 'proper order'.

    So this highlights the value of an ongoing practice that can integrate the experiences of meditation, rather than glorifying or focusing solely on (past) meditative experience. How does the meditative experience function well in life?

    In the overview of your meditative career, yes a correct foundation must be established, upon which all else is built.

    Going away a little bit is not bad; too much talk, from many different perspectives, can muddy the waters. By the same token, avoiding all interaction invites egotism and misses out on teaching. If we only avoid, then our ability to deal with the world at large atrophies.

    So retreat must have a clear direction. It should be purposeful rather than reactive. If it is time to let an experience unfold in silence, good, take retreat. But if you are avoiding something, it has to be looked into and rectified, or else the meditative experience will go for naught -- it will not be able to unfold in everyday life, and you will never be free, always needing to find special cloistered circumstances. That's not the right function of your insight experience.

    The ideas 'my practice', 'my peace', and 'my insight' are not correct! If you are free, you can go anywhere and face anything, only for the sake of others. If you are free, you don't need to hide or arrange things to get your own comfortable situation, because you are comfortable anywhere. If you are not free, then proper teaching and training are necessary.

    You are not alone. Many, many people have gone through phases like this. If you visit a practice community, almost everyone there will be able to relate; and many will be able to tell you how they are dealing with it or how they came through the other end of it.

    Because insight experience is beyond thought, and because our thinking habit doesn't end with a single meditative experience, confusion arises. The teacher and community are there to help you find the path through confusion, and retain the important essence of what is attained in practice. Everyone is going through it together.

    If I may, let me recommend keeping your mind simple. Before all this talk and before your experience, you were just going along, not making a big deal of any particular part of life. This allowed you to have the experience. If you had been making a 'thing' of it, it never would have arisen.

    So keep your mind simple, and don't make a big deal about anything. Follow what is in front of your face at any moment. When it's time to eat, eat. When it's time to do work, do work. When there's nothing in particular facing you, rest. Your feet and hands will tell you what to do, moment to moment; you don't have to make ideas.

    This will allow insight to function naturally. You don't do it, okay? You follow the plain moment, and it unfolds through you.

    If the mind starts racing, have some cool water and go for an enjoyable walk or bike ride. Nothing special is necessary.
