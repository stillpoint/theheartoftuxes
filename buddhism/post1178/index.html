    I am unforgiving to others

    Anger is the energy of justice. It is the energy that holds boundaries (correct or incorrect ones). So there is a right function for anger.

    If you used to have an 'oh well' attitude and let people walk over you, then at some point your anger came up. It sounds like you ignored or suppressed it in the past; and now you are dealing with backlog and confusion about whether your anger belongs to your immediate situations or whether it belongs to the past.

    The way to balance isn't by holding one particular stance. You can't just take a strategy like "I'll never give in to anger" or "I'll always express my anger by standing my ground". Balance has to be dynamic. It has to be alive and responsive in the moment.

    So the first thing is that awareness is a requirement. If you are not aware, not present in the moment, then you can't respond to the moment. You'll be responding to the moment when it's already past, blowing up in anger at something that happened ten minutes (or ten days) after the actual thing you were originally angry at â€” the thing for which an appropriate boundary was necessary.

    So awareness is necessary. Not that you will be able to keep perfect awareness; but that's your aim, to stay aware.

    It doesn't mean you wait for your chance to pounce. It means returning to openhanded awareness again and again and again. Maintain a neutral awareness that doesn't hinge on any particular event; then see what arises in you. The bodymind has its own wisdom. It doesn't come from thinking or pre-planning. The bodymind already responds quite well if you don't force your opinion on it. Maybe an event will trigger anger, and then you can respond with a kind but clear boundary ("no, I don't want to do that" or "that's not right; please don't do that again"), or with a direct communication ("I just want to let you know that what you said made me feel crappy. And it's important to me to tell you this because I value our friendship.")

    Sometimes the boundary required is the one within you. Sometimes your anger tells you that you need to let something go, cut something off, or be more stringent in your own thoughts or behaviors.

    And sometimes, surprisingly, it won't be anger that arises at all, or it will be mixed with something more significant, like sadness or love. If you assume that each transgression has to be met angrily, you'll miss the times when transgressions are to be met with extreme kindness, or with shared vulnerability and disappointment, and so on.

    So awareness is necessary.

    This also entails the willingness to feel. If you don't have the courage to feel, you can't be aware of your feelings and thus the forces that drive you to respond in certain ways. Away from others, just practicing feeling â€” whatever the feeling may be in the moment â€” can greatly increase your functionality (your "emotional I.Q."). You can not only do better out in the world, but you can be more comfortable in your own skin when the emotions are felt, acknowledged, and allowed to flow. Then you can apply this practice when face to face with a difficult situation: feeling what you're feeling, so you're not controlled from the shadows of your mind.

    Sometimes you won't be aware, and the opportunity will be missed, but anger (or whatever difficulty) will still linger. Therefore the next requirement is compassion. Have compassion for both yourself and others, because we're all in the same boat. We're all dealing with ignorance and sloppy boundaries and awkward communication and dysfunctional patterns and unclear relationship and emotional backlog, and so on. All of us deal with that; so let's keep that broad view and have some compassionate understanding for our shared situation.

    Be aware that there are many options for expression. The immature person knows only shutting down or acting out â€” in terms of anger, either suppressing it into self-destructive depression or frustration, or raging out of control. The mature person has access to countless variations of expression: letting out an exasperated grunt, making a clear vocal statement, creating an angry piece of art, going for a run, writing a letter explaining what's going on, asking for understanding, taking a physical posture communicating their feeling, and so on. Even in the speech and body language you use there are infinite possible variations.

    And finally, just being openly aware of the difficult feeling, without trying to manipulate it in any way, allows it to self-liberate. Eventually it will dissolve, without making a problem for you and without creating new patterns that have to be dealt with in the future. This is possible, although it may also be necessary to act on the situation that brought up the difficulty in the first place.
