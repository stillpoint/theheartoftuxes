    TheHeartofTuxes

    It's not a bad thing to have to make an effort. Usually we think that it's not an ideal situation if it takes time and effort and patience; but from another perspective this is a very beautiful aspect of practice: we are finding the path and unfolding the path simultaneously. As we seek and try and wait, we are expressing ourselves and standing up for what is important in life. We are deepening our embodiment of noble, enlightened qualities like devotion, perseverance, and vigor. We are deepening our inner connection with Dharma.

    Making effort and undergoing difficulty for the sake of Dharma is extremely auspicious -- very good karma! So we should keep a faithful mind, a mind that can't be defeated. "My karma may not be open yet in this regard, but if I keep trying it will open." In the big picture of countless lifetimes disconnected from the path of Dharma, trying consistently in this lifetime is of immense significance. It can turn around eons of useless suffering.

    I'd urge you and anyone to look further afield if you can't find opportunities near you. I looked for a teacher for over a decade, traveling around North America, visiting various communities. Although I did have wonderful teachers closer to home, I made a profound connection to a teacher and community that was a 12-hour drive away. And it was far beyond 'worth it'! One of the most precious, blessed things ever in my life: to find my wonderful teacher, and confidently forge ahead in practice with him.

    (I want to mention that I've also met many great female teachers, beings of great attainment. There can be a whole other dimension to studying with a woman, which is significant for many students.)

    Regarding Shambhala, it is an authentic tradition, not just a modern invention. It is conceived as a spiritual practice that conveys much of the wisdom of Tibetan spirituality without much of the Buddhist framework -- a sort of Vajrayana for laypeople.

    Shambhala was transmitted to the West by Chogyam Trungpa Rinpoche, a great, if controversial, master. I've only had brief encounters with the tradition. My sense is that one's experience of particular communities will depend somewhat on the character and attainment of the teacher and students.

    In general, in case you're concerned about a school or center being 'lower level', it's not necessarily a bad thing. There's a place for all kinds of forms and levels, for the simple reason that they speak to where some people are starting from or connecting most deeply. Thus a particular form can become a very good support for them at that juncture, a good fit.

    When you visit a center, you might do a Google search about their lineage and the teachers involved, to see if there are any issues to be aware of. Controversy doesn't necessarily mean you shouldn't benefit from the teaching (after all, we will all make mistakes and encounter controversy in our lives -- the point is how well we move on from it); it's just good to proceed with awareness.

    As you try out the practice at a new center, notice whether the community members, especially the advanced students and teachers, are compassionate, reasonable, good-natured. Do their actions match their speech -- is the teaching lived, rather than merely spoken?

    Also important is to avoid being one-sided in your participation. So many new students arrive with a sense of entitlement: "what are you going to do for me?" They seek their own perfect fantasy situation, without even yet knowing what real practice entails. A taking-only attitude is not only negative because of the selfishness being expressed; it blocks one's progress along the path.

    We are meant to participate, to express our own nature and let our own energy flow. Practice starts from the center and moves outward. Of course there are many things that need to be taken in, and good listening and patience are vital. But our practice is our life; and we must be active participants in it.

    So you have your own Dharma to offer. Know this from the very beginning. It doesn't mean you strut around like an expert; it means you show up and give whatever wisdom, compassion, and effort the unfolding scene demands. So for instance, even a new student can help a teacher grow and improve; even a new student can help uplift a community and maintain a high standard of practice. This is every bit as important as feeling out a community to see if it suits you.

    We need each other.
