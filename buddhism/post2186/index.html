    This kind of thing is developed through practice, not so much through getting more ideas. I recommend retreats and frequent group or residential practice. That way you can get the proper teaching and support for integrating practice and life.

    In one sense it's simple: stay aware. In another sense, you have to learn and practice how that works in all the various situations in life. And most students also need to witness others doing it, to stoke their aspiration, to see that it can be done, and to soak in the subtle how.

    Don't worry about the future — how things will go, how you'll handle it, what will come of it, etc. Take care of now. If you take care of now, the future will go well.

    In the meantime for you, I can recommend a practice that might help you start to bridge the gap. 'Feet on the ground' is a simple practice that can be applied in countless life situations. One of its benefits for you is that it is so flexible, so adaptable; so it can be a more focused and formal practice in your quiet and open times, and it can be a background anchor — literally a grounding influence — when you are more active, or when things are complicated, or casual, or stressful.

    In this practice you maintain some awareness of your contact with the ground. When you are standing, you feel through your feet what it is like to stand on the ground moment to moment. When you are walking, you feel your feet stepping, lifting, stepping, lifting. When you are sitting, you feel your feet on the floor, your butt and legs on the seat, and perhaps your forearms on an armrest. When you are lying down, you feel your body's 'footprint' on its place of support.

    No other special technique or state is necessary, just maintaining some background awareness of your physical foundation, your support, the place-where-you-are.

    When you are watching sports with friends or involved in a heady discussion, you can maintain some bit of underlying feeling of feet-on-the-ground. It is so simple and physical that it is easy to return to and keep in practically all activities. It doesn't get in the way or compete; it just gives a sense that the whole experience or activity has a root, a place. And in the meantime it keeps you present and aware.

    One Zen Master said:
    >"Zen mind is everyday mind."

    Your question is essentially the same question that initiated one of the famous ancient encounters between student and Zen Master:

    Before he was a Zen Master, Zhaozhou was a student of Zen Master Nanquan. He had the same question you have: "What is the Way?" How do I do it, how do I live the Way?

    >So one day Zhaozhou asked his teacher,
    >What is the Way? [What is the true way?]

    >Zen Master Nanquan replied:
    >Everyday mind is the Way.

    >Z: So should I try to follow it or not? [Should I do something in particular to accord with the Way?]

    >N: If you try to follow it, you lose it.

    >Z: Then how do I know if it's the Way or not?

    >N: It is not a matter of knowing or not knowing. Knowing is delusion. Not knowing is ignorance. When you attain the true way beyond concept, it is clear and open like space.

    >Upon hearing this, Zhaozhou's mind opened and he attained enlightenment. Later, he became a great Zen Master himself.

    This is a beautiful teaching, with many points to be deepened and clarified over the years. But to attain this teaching — to attain 'everyday mind' — requires correct learning and practice. The teacher helps you distinguish between everyday Way-mind and inattention or laziness or over-striving, for example.

    I hope you find a good teacher and embody the mind that is clear and open like space.

    If you like, try feet-on-the-ground. You can experiment with the various situations in which you can practice presence. Then you can see how wide practice can be, not rejecting any part of life. It is a very human and humanistic discovery: "Oh, everything is practice!"
