    TheHeartofTuxes

    One of the big burdens that teachers carry in the West is that many of the people they serve don't yet understand their part of the symbiosis, aren't yet aware of what constitutes correct relationship, and haven't yet attained enough in practice to truly grasp the value of a teacher.

    In traditional settings, there are usually elders and experienced students who convey this information to others â€” the 'behind the scenes' of production, the demands people place upon the teacher that make her/him less available for others, the work and money required to run the group, the kinds of practical help needed, and so on. Someone other than the teacher could teach the community how to support a teacher.

    But in many Western settings, the teacher has to be the one communicating all this. It puts them in the tricky position of having to speak up for themselves just so they can continue offering the Dharma, and then being judged harshly for speaking up. Really they shouldn't be in that position in the first place, and they wouldn't be if our priorities were straight.

    Instead of caring for the most precious influence in our life as if it were precious, we want it all done according to our old samsaric agenda: "A teacher should be only giving 100% of the time, never asking for anything... but I, on the other hand, will show up when it suits me."
