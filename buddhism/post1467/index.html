    I'm not that knowledgeable about iconography; I'm sure someone can say better than I can what's going on there. /u/HipsterMonk, what can you add; has your teacher talked about the iconography here?

    It's hard to see precisely, but from the 'calling the Earth to witness' mudra it seems the main Buddha statue is Shakyamuni. Also perhaps the smaller one. The side altars usually have Avalokitesvara and Ksitigarbha Bodhisattvas (Great Compassion 'Hearer Of The World's Cries' and Great Transit Between States 'Earth Store'). Both may wear the five-pointed lotus hat of a Bodhisattva, though Ksitigarbha is sometimes depicted as an itinerant monk with bald head and monk robes.

    The taenghwa (altar mural) behind the main altar at closer inspection seems to be of the retinue at the Buddha's Vulture Peak sermon, so associated with Shakyamuni. Usually there are Dharma Protectors in front, Bodhisattvas in the middle, and Arhats at the top/back. The arhats will have the shaved heads and robes of a Bikkhu (monk). I could be wrong, but I seem to recall hearing that Ananda's hair was rendered as green, as was Ksitigarbha's in his depiction as a monk, so that may be Ananda or Ksitigarbha (Korean: Jijang Bosal) in the top left corner of the main mural; but don't quote me on that.

    The Vulture Peak sermon became the Lotus Sutra. The first chapter of the Lotus Sutra lists the attendees; so you can read that part and compare with the mural.

    The other mural seems to be of Dharma protectors and beneficent spirits. There's a traditional mural in many Korean temples depicting celestial spirits, essentially the beings and energies associated with major stars. This hearkens back to the shamanic roots of Buddhism in Korea, and shows how closely shamanism and Buddhism are tied even today.

    In the front of that mural there is a bald bearded man with a staff. If we could look more closely we'd probably find him holding a gourd or peach. This is the 'immortal' of longevity, who usually appears also with the immortals of prosperity and influence, all three of whom are also star spirits or star-related gods.

    It is an interesting coincidence that another thread mentions the Buddha winning over a Yaksha (Pali: Yakkha), because these beings are often depicted in altar murals. A forest spirit with a tiger is often found, for instance (which I believe is another form of the same Immortal of Longevity who is otherwise depicted with the long beard, staff, and gourd). It points to the fact that good relationship with the environment, with the land and all its inhabitants, and with the cosmos, is woven into Buddhism. We realize that we can't and don't survive and thrive in isolation, but that we are fed and kept alive and prosperous by countless other entities. Our practice and its venues are not merely dropped on the environment without regard, but negative influences are driven out or won over, and positive influences are harnessed.

    In the case of powerful, long-lived beings that most of us don't perceive, once they are given the gift of real insight into Dharma they are liberated from powerful, long-lived suffering, and often they take on powerful, long-lived vows of protection. There are stories of the founders of temples casting out or winning over forest or mountain or lake spirits, Nagas, and other powerful beings in order to establish the practice place. So our practice at temples is supported and protected in many unseen ways, from the old lay-women and worker monks who keep the temple going behind the scenes, to the powerful beings who keep negative influences from entering us once we have the courage to open and surrender (but perhaps don't yet possess the overwhelming stability of a Buddha or Bodhisattva).

    When we see or think of a retinue or congregation gathering around the Buddha, although artwork may vary in the number of attendees it displays we are to understand that all enlightening beings are there. It is all a lineage of, and a participation in, the one universal Buddha mind. All various expressions and conduits of one boundless bright purity.
