    What do you do between breaths in a breathing meditation?

    There are many variations. The main aim is simply to be present with reality. The best course of action is to hook up with a good teacher and follow the method he or she gives you.

    Here are a few of the possible variations:

    • Feel the breath at the nostrils
    • Feel the breath at the belly
    • Follow the breath through its channel and into the belly
    • Count both in-breaths and out-breaths
    • Count in-breaths while you gently watch the process of breathing
    • Count in-breaths, focusing on the number until the next breath starts
    • Count in-breaths and then rest in the empty space between counts
    • ...and others

    In actuality, there is no "between breaths", so if you want to be meticulous you can maintain an even attention, without trying to identify a beginning or end. That means being aware of all parts of the breath cycle equally, including the turnings between in and out. These turnings are interesting; there is an inherent silence to them, physically and mentally.

    For counting styles, though there is no need to grasp or distinguish between any particular part of the breath, one can take the opportunity to let go and rest deeply in the silent turnings. This silence carries over through the next breath counting, with the possibility to deepen the letting go and resting with every breath.

    For concentration-on-nostrils styles, the aim is generally to maintain unbroken concentration, whether you feel something or not. The attention and sensitivity are trained, so you may gradually become more aware of even delicate flows of air, and of the qualities present when the flow of air stops. There will still be many sensations and non-sensations to attend to, including a background buzzing of nerve endings or just of bubbling energy, a sense of position and either tension or relaxation of the nostrils, and a shifting sense of numbness or nothingness. All are grist for the mill: your unbroken attention welcomes it all, without discriminating.

    Eventually you may reach a point where "I am concentrating" is let go of, and the difference between concentrating mind and blank mind disappears. There is just breathing. Beyond this, the attention may attend simply with itself — just the fact of silent attending comes to the forefront. When this becomes effortless it is a radically different kind of concentration (called samadhi). A good teacher can guide you through the stages.
