    You come here with this struggle. What shall we do with you?

    ~

    You are mistaken that a 'swift boot' will end suffering. If it were that easy, we should all just die and then be reborn without suffering. But our mindstream continues moment to moment, through life and through death. It is the mindstream that is the repository of our suffering karma â€” the imprints of all the good and bad things we've thought, said, and done. Mere death doesn't end all that. As we know from science, energy doesn't end, it just changes form.

    Many of these questions have compassion on the face, but self-interest under the surface. The motivating force is not to relieve the suffering of the fly, but to relieve the viewer's suffering as they identify with the fly. The viewer is disturbed by the thought of pain and death, and wishes to end the scene. This is the same dynamic as happens in the sometimes sappy, doting ways people express sorrow when a human dies: they are not so much caring for the deceased or their surviving loved ones as they are fretting about their own relationship with death. "Oh, this is horrible!"

    The problem you perceive is made by your thinking. For you it is complex struggle, but for the fly it is very simple. The fly doesn't have your complicated thinking; its experience is very direct. It doesn't project into the future and consider how much longer this struggle will continue. It doesn't even make the idea of struggle as opposed to any other state. We sometimes 'harmfully over-protect' innocent beings because we experience their situation through our complicated thinking. But they don't experience it that way. They are not complicated. Animals and young children are mostly living in the present moment; they don't have many of the problems we do because they do not make them with their thinking.

    Compassion means first you look, first you bear witness. See the situation deeply. All beings are involved in this struggle. If you're a 'being', thinking of yourself as a separately existing entity, then you are subject to the same situation. One time powerful spirit-being, another time human, another time fly, another time hell-being, another time toad, and so on.... Universe upon universe of similarly struggling beings. This can't be ended through killing.

    Only waking up will do it.

    Furthermore, if we look at the karmic benefit to the fly, killing it may take away its opportunity to expunge its negative karma. It is in this form and situation because of previous causes. When those causes ripened and found their condition for emerging, they appeared as this particular suffering. Once they have emerged, that particular ripening is finished; but if they are not given the chance to emerge, they will still have to emerge in the future. In the meantime they may link with other causes and create further negative states.

    If you kill the fly, you do not know that you have saved it. You may doom it to rebirth in a lower, more painful realm because of the karma it is carrying. Instead, we should generally allow all beings to work through and purify their karma.

    From the perspective of your personal evolution, you should not tinker and manipulate. Interference with the process of others is ego-based. Those thoughts and actions don't align with Dharma. Where would it lead? How many beings can you 'save' by killing? This kind of interference is very low-level. For your own progress you ought to think in terms of saving all beings by waking up from the dream of life. As long as you yourself are stuck and struggling, your help is limited. But when you awaken and develop boundless compassion, there is no measure of how you can help. Really even one step toward awakening is immeasurably greater than saving a single life.
