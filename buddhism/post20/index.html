    TheHeartofTuxes

    Maybe others can elucidate it better than I can.

    Upaya is the concept of working with the conditional to express and attain the unconditional. On one side, we have the Absolute: Emptiness, divinity, pure Wisdom, Dharmakaya. On the other side, we have our compassionate and effective interaction with objects and beings and situations.

    Although from an Absolute perspective we recognize that beings, objects, and situations are not real, we still respond to them. This is very beautiful. It is one of the most beautiful and laudable aspects of Buddhadharma. Even if suffering beings are not real, we still respond to their suffering and help them. And we help them in the ways they need to be helped. It is not dogmatic. It doesn't not attach to Emptiness, but uses Emptiness to empower specific, useful action.

    This is complete teaching. Attainment of the Absolute and expression of upaya are not separate. Wisdom and Compassion are not separate.

    Many people who have a first taste or two of Emptiness become so thrilled by it that they get stuck there. After all, Emptiness is — finally! — one's own true nature. But Emptiness is only part-way. Unfortunately, without good teaching and correct direction from the beginning, a great many people become attached to Emptiness and see it as the final destination. Therefore they attain Wisdom, but their wisdom can't function. They can't use it to relate to others and help them. They only spout Emptiness. Some Zen Masters refer to this as a special circle of hell. Once you understand this dynamic, you can see it at work in many of the people who post here and on r/Zen: very confident, because they indeed have attained some direct experience, but unable to function in normal reality, helping others.

    As an example of upaya, the Buddha always adjusted his teaching to suit his audience. If he was teaching long-time disciples with very refined perception, he would present a certain teaching. But his first teachings to people who had never heard them before would take on a quite different quality. The teachings had the same essence and direction, the same Absolute basis, but their particular function was quite different according to the situation.

    In some paths, such as Vajrayana, the unification of Wisdom and Compassion is woven into the practice formulation. Both aspects are very consciously cultivated, so as not to lose either part. And it is recognized that originally they are not two different aspects — they are the same awakened mind.

    In paths that aim at provoking a breakthrough experience of Emptiness/Wisdom, it is extremely important to have the basis of compassionate direction. From the beginning, the motivation must be very clear: it's for the sake of all beings, not just 'for me'. Unfortunately, it's often the case that people take these teachings as mere words or mere rituals rather than taking them to heart and transforming their motivation. Then when they do get a breakthrough experience, it doesn't function well and they get stuck. For the uninitiated, it may seem that they got something very special, because they are bright and clear and confident. But the attainment is not yet complete, as they have not yet digested upaya.

    This is the reason why comes first. "Why do practice?" The why must always communicate with the how. If you have correct direction, then even if the method is mistaken you can still find your way. But if you don't have correct direction, then even very good method falls off the path. So in my experience, all traditional paths have some practice for reconnecting with correct direction at the beginning of each day — taking refuge, Four Great Vows, remembering the precepts or the Perfections, Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind or other awareness of death, and so on.
