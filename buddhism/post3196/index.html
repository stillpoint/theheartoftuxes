    >> People should come to their own conclusions about what is and isn't true

    >That is dangerous thinking, particularly with Zen, where it's very easy to fall into incorrect conclusions.

    This is such an important point and it often gets swept under the rug in western culture and especially in the Reddit demographic which, let's face it, has a decidedly brash, iconoclastic young male leaning, and which is strongly conditioned by the anonymity and disconnection of online virtual 'reality'.

    Who is actually devoted to the process of dismantling their self-centeredness? (Rather than just talking a good game.)

    Already shrouded by profound attachment to our own personal views and opinions, we are further encouraged by online exchanges to deal with life through words and concepts, to respect popularity rather than depth, and to snatch up convenient snippets rather than devote time and effort to a rigorous process of clarification.

    In one's own head it seems reasonable to advocate for free thinking; but this fails to recognize the vast garbage-making machine we've created in our minds -- the extremely ingrained delusion within which we habitually and unconsciously operate.

    So while free thinking and critical examination are absolutely vital, they are not sufficient. Wise, transcendent guidance is necessary.

    It is wise to seek guidance from the wise. It is wise to have a more reliable mirror for our mind than our very own preferences and tendencies.

    >I think it is a better idea to make your own observations, but then also discuss them with other people to test whether the observations are complete.

    Yes. Not just other people, but wise, insightful, experienced, compassionate, attentive other people -- people who have a good footing in reality, and care to help us find ours.

    Because we can have a lot of clever conversation with a lot of clever people who nonetheless are clueless about the central point of life and how to address it. These conversations can continue endlessly, and we can mistakenly convince ourselves that merely being interested in new ideas constitutes spiritual progress (or even growth of character).

    >I think if everyone in the conversation is respectful and understands that everyone is working on the path, then you can actually have a good conversation about things, and everyone can benefit.

    Amen.
