    Meditation question/fear

    Meditation in Buddhism has a particular role that might not be the case in other traditions or non-traditional settings. In Buddhism, meditation is just one part of a complete life path, with every component aimed at liberation from suffering.

    You meditate to develop the stability of mind that is not pulled here and there by common influences (pleasure, pain, loss, gain, praise, blame, shame, fame); and thus your action can become tremendously beneficial for yourself and others.

    You meditate to achieve the stillness from which wisdom emerges, and the focus with which insight is obtained.

    With good action, wisdom, and insight you have the building blocks for a noble and free life, unchained from grasping and agitation.

    This mental training, which includes concentration and mindfulness, is supposed to be accompanied by proper development of your knowledge and your general approach to life, good intention and action and daily activity, and appropriate speech. All of these are aimed at overcoming your original mistake about what you really are. When you overcome this mistake and understand what you really are, then you can be free of the things that now cause you suffering, and you will be equipped to help all others get free as well.

    ~

    Should you be trying to attain detachment? No. Detachment is not a state or feeling that you get to, so you should not be trying to get to any imagined state or feeling.

    The correct meaning of detachment is that you let go of the things that move your mind. Just opening your hands is detachment. When someone flatters you and you lose your balance and just completely devour their words and cling to them, that means those words have the power to move your mind. Likewise, when someone insults you or talks down to you and you immediately feel ashamed or angry, that also means that your mind has moved. But if you keep your mind stable, you can see through the illusions that otherwise control you: you don't have to cling to the flattery or react to the insult; you don't have to keep making the same mistakes in life.

    If you have a stable, not-moving mind, it also allows you to show true compassion to others. You can feel emotions deeply without being blinded by them. You don't need to offer false pity, because you can offer clear presence. Sometimes another person requires tenderness, and sometimes another person requires a strong boundary or a kick in the ass: compassion responds appropriately because the mind doesn't cling to only one idea of goodness, and isn't moved by worldly winds.

    In many meditation instructions for beginners a structure is offered: you count or follow breath, or you say a mantra, or keep returning to some other object of meditation. That structure helps you to practice correct detachment. The aim isn't to get to any particular state or feeling, but just to keep letting go of whatever arises.

    You let go and return to the method. A thought comes up... you let it go and return to the method. A distraction takes you away for a minute or for half an hour... you let it go and return to the method. You fail... you let it go and return. You succeed... you let it go and return. And so on and so on. Gradually your mind becomes stable. The usual worldly influences can't throw it out of balance like before. And as a result, your perception is more clear; you live more in reality, less in your thinking and imagining. Eventually, you wake up to the true nature of life.

    That is Buddhist style.
