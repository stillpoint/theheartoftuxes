    Where is Buddah?

    > trying to force a narrative on the indescribable.

    That's a very nice turn of the phrase; a good way of putting it.

    Cosmic self has self, which is illusory. But if it didn't have form it would be limited to the absolute, with no chance of relationship, evolution, movement. So what is omnipresent, real, and not limited to form or emptiness?

    What do you think: can this be reached by talking and thinking?

    "Where is Buddha?" is a very good question, if it has a direction. But these kinds of questions have a way of devolving into tortuous intellectual fancies, mired in their own concepts. The traditional way of practicing with this question is to allow it to stay big, and not to try to fill it in with thinking.

    We human beings (indeed, all sentient beings) are all threatened by boundlessness. The idea of Buddha doesn't threaten us, because at a certain level we realize the idea is limited, controllable, graspable, containable. It fits very well with our egocentric view. It doesn't threaten our identity.

    But the experience of Buddha is another matter altogether. The first taste we get of our boundless mind, our boundless nature, sets the tone. It tells the tale of our relationship with our Buddha Nature. How do we digest that experience: do we let it be boundless, or do we try to ensconce it in concept?

    This is the basic difference between immature and mature in terms of spiritual practice â€” not just in Buddhism, but in any mystical or religious path. Do we have the courage and wisdom to let the boundless be boundless, or do we try to corral it and pin it down in concept? The two motives are very different.

    If I say "your mind is Buddha", I make several mistakes: I make the mistake of you and your, I make the mistake of mind, I make the mistake of Buddha, and I make the mistake of is. These are all utter mistakes! But if I make these mistakes I make them to point toward the thing that is not a mistake. So I make the mistake for your sake.

    Your mind is Buddha. If you find your mind, you find Buddha. If you find Buddha, you find your mind.

    So how will you find Buddha? Where can you look?

    Do you understand that thinking can't help you â€” that the very nature of thought, of concept, is limited? Or do you merely suspect it's so? You'd better find out!

    If you know that you won't find mind or Buddha through thinking, then where can you look?
