    Yes! That's why we practice. And that's why it is a practice. It doesn't come automatically to us.

    But it is natural and innate. Unconditional love is the state of our original mind. It's just covered up with layers of reaction and patterned views and behavior based on a false notion of self. When we see the unreality of those patterns and that underlying false belief, we can attain our true self, and unconditional love can function.

    We also practice acting with love even if we haven't yet grasped its origin. Using our ability to reason, we may decide to follow an enlightened path even before we have realized enlightenment. Step by step, we come closer to our perfectly functioning original nature.

    A good teaching tradition can help you connect with, build, and attain true love. You're right, we don't feel it much of the time. But when we work on it, real love and compassion do grow.

    You can see that love is already within you, already your innate nature. For instance, you came here asking about love, and you also expressed your desire to learn unconditional love. If you didn't already have it, you wouldn't even be thinking of it.

    When you see an animal suffering, your heart aches for it. There is an undeniable response. That is your innate compassion. When you watch a heartbreaking movie, you feel for the characters and you feel through them. This is your capacity to love. The more you notice your innate love, the more you can see the ways you betray it, trade it in, or cover it up.

    Correct meditation is an expression of unconditional love. You sit and offer your presence to all things, without judgment, without grasping or avoiding. It is the same open, non-judging presence you offer anyone -- a close one, a person in need, the stranger facing you at any particular moment, a dog, a bird, the scene at large....

    Love radiates, compassion responds. They are two aspects of the same mind. The development of deep, unconditional compassion -- the desire and intent to help all beings -- is said to be absolutely essential for enlightenment. There is no liberation through thinking-understanding alone; the heart has to be transformed. This compassionate heart or mind of enlightenment is referred to as Bodhicitta (literally, “awakening mind"), and it is at the heart of many teachings, and is an essential part of all paths to enlightenment.

    There are definite steps offered for the development of Bodhicitta. The Vajrayana Tibetan Buddhist path in particular has many direct teachings on that subject. There are also meditations you can practice to develop love and compassion: Metta (lovingkindness), Karuna (compassion), and Tonglen (exchanging suffering) meditations are probably the most well-known among them.
