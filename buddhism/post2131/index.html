    A) You are not enlightened, so no need to bring that fantasy into the discussion. Let's talk about your situation rather than speculating about unreal futures.

    B) It doesn't sound like you actually have a teacher and clear practice; so that might be the logical place to start.

    Don't imagine that inventing your own practice based on sundry readings is the same as studying Buddhadharma in depth. If you go to a practice place you will find practice people and can make relationships based on Dharma.

    You can also then witness how others move in the world and relate based on Dharma rather than ignorance. Having role models -- living examples -- is very important, very inspiring and transformative.

    C) Yes, sometimes it's more lonely when you start cutting off useless and negative parts of your life. Many, many people relate based on ignorance and negativity. The period of loneliness is an appropriate purification, a time when you feel and commit to what is truly valuable to you.

    You may go through several of these phases, depending on how clearly you embrace the positive. If you dither, there will be more back and forth, more cycles of clinging to the negative, experiencing pain, and then letting go of it.

    Later, if you find correct practice and get out of your head, it will usually open into a broad and gregarious warmth with everyone. For the very rare person whose path continues in isolation the loneliness nevertheless goes away -- they are too involved in brilliant, radiant practice to dwell on "I, my, me".

    >They mess it up.

    This is wrong thinking.

    If your practice is not strong (we say "if your center is not strong"), then you need to regulate your environment and relationships to protect the delicate sprout of practice. It doesn't mean that others are the problem, that others are the enemy. They are just further reflections of your own ignorance.

    It is the ignorance that is the enemy. We are all united in our suffering and our susceptibility to ignorance. So instead of seeing others as a problem you ought to see them as struggling beings, just as yourself. It doesn't mean you can't create firm boundaries to protect your integrity, but you do it for the sake of awakening and kindness, not to preserve your little island paradise.

    Correct practice means you wish great contentment for everyone.

    When you train with a teacher and a clear practice, your center will become stronger and stronger. Then you will not be swayed by outer circumstances. You will be able to be with people in their own situations, without losing peace and clarity, even for a moment. Or if you do lose your center you will be able to return very quickly. Your mind's light will shine to others and guide them out of negativity. That is the purpose of the Dharma.
