    Everyone's path unfolds differently, so there are different steps for each person.

    Starting with the basic concepts common to all Buddhist traditions is not a bad idea; and there is hardly a better source for this than the book *What The Buddha Taught* by Walpola Rahula.

    Trying some meditation — 5 or 10 minutes every day — can also be very influential on one's life. One can try from a book (written by a respected teacher in a clear tradition — Philip Kapleau, Pema Chodron, and Culadasa are examples of teachers who have written works that can help one begin a meditation practice) or from a beginner's meditation course offered at a local practice center.

    Beginning to clarify life direction is also absolutely essential and foundational to everything that follows. The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind is a very excellent daily practice that can be done before daily meditation. Whether one chooses this form (practiced by millions of people) or whether one more flowingly considers impermanence, death, and life purpose, this kind of contemplation is very important to revisit time and again. Realization of impermanence, death, and our shared suffering situation is what led the Buddha on his path of awakening, and it is what leads countless others on the path.

    And regardless of what other steps one takes, I highly, highly recommend finding a teacher and Sangha sooner rather than later. As soon as possible, start to let the influence into your life, of people who have their hearts set on truth and compassion, and of people who have gone far along the path and have attained the fruit of practice. Even a few meetings once in a while can be transformative; there are usually events open to the public or available by donation, which will allow one to dip one's toe in the water.
