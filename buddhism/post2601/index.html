    Would this be adequate practice?

    You can try. You'll get some experience from that.

    But study-and-practice is a path, not something you get all at once just by hearing a definitive statement. So even if you heard the perfect explanation of the eightfold path, properly explaining all of its implications could take weeks or months... and digesting that material — getting it past your own conditioning, opinions, reactions, habits, and attachments, and owning it as your own embodied wisdom — takes years and lifetimes of sincere study.

    Even if you stumble upon very high-class teaching, how will you know that it is high-class teaching? What experience do you have beyond common sense and common opinion, by which you distinguish between something that doesn't make sense because it's wrong and something that doesn't make sense because you aren't there yet in your view?

    Furthermore, online forums, blogs, even many books often include incomplete, low-level, or simply wrong opinions and instructions. Reddit really isn't the best place to come for clarification. There are some sincere, experienced practitioners, and there are some folks who have just read a lot and hold strong opinions; and then there are those who really just speculate and pose. But even the good advice comes from differing perspectives, various traditions and schools and cultures, various abilities to convey the knowledge. These conversations can step on each other's toes: what would be good advice within its own tradition may contradict or obfuscate good advice from another. The same words may be used differently, different methods may be confused as the same, and so on.

    So just for the sake of the clarity and simplicity you seek, studying with one teacher and tradition for a while is important. Beyond that, the living example of enlightenment or dedicated practice is valuable beyond measure. To see clear mind actually at work in everyday life is an indescribably valuable experience; and when you see it at work in all kinds of situations, day after day after day, you grow not only in understanding but in faith — the intelligent, evidence-based faith that can carry you beyond obstacles you would not be able to overcome on your own.

    We can certainly start the conversation about the Eightfold Path and complete training and other questions you may have; but don't think that getting a response here will in any way mean that you have received the teaching. You have to understand it through your own experimentation and experience — through your own practice. Only then can it be considered your own knowledge or wisdom (as opposed to mere theory that doesn't have any transformative effect on your life).

    >It is not a flower that you pick; it is a ladder you must climb

    So, yes, try on your own. Do your best, expressing your sincerity at every turn.

    But that is not the last word. As soon as possible, go to the well where the water is drawn — go to the living source of Dharma, a realized master or experienced and qualified teacher.

    ~

    It sounds almost like you are asking to just be given a practice, without having to understand the wherefore and the why of it. That's possible in a very faithful or devotional relationship with a teacher. It's not common in western society, because we are so analytical here; but it's one of the possible streams of training. It does, though, depend on your surrender to a guide. You would need to find a master in whom to place your complete trust.

    But the more... intelligent path, shall we say?... is to gradually deepen your understanding of why the practice is the way it is; and that means making the effort to understand some of the philosophy. Even Zen requires correct conception. Erudition isn't necessary, but right understanding is.
