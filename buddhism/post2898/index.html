    How does one know if one is a "poser"?

    There are three different questions in your post.

    • The title asks "How does one know if one is a poser?"

    • The question at the end of your post asks "How do I let go of this worry (about being a poser)?"

    • And your comment about latching on and wondering asks "How do I dispel doubt?"

    So the first thing is to please consider what is the real question, and what is the real issue for you.

    1) One doesn't always know if one is a poser. At some level, one knows; but sometimes we stay dull and clueless about ourselves, and various events and situations in our lives may create an unconsciousness about certain aspects of our character. For one reason or another, the organism one has identified with has decided that it's in its best interest not to know.

    So if you really want to know, reflection is necessary. That means both reflecting on your own character honestly, and receiving reflection from others. Working closely with others will bring feedback automatically: you will not always agree, your ways will not always align; so those points of conflict will show you where you are holding on to your patterns.

    But if you want more complete information, and sooner, you should go to the people in your life and ask them to speak honestly about how they experience you. If you try this, know the difference between someone's experience of you and their analytic opinion, and ask them to stick to the former. People love to make opinions; and those opinions often say more about the opinion-maker themselves than the object of their opinion.

    2) You let go of worry by letting go and returning to sincerity in the present moment. As many times as you wander off, let go and return. You cannot create sincerity from the outside, by trying to look or act a certain way. The practice comes from the inside and radiates outward. So just be sincere in the moment.

    3) There are a few points regarding the dispelling of doubt.

    First; know the danger of any opinion, and drop it. Opinion is none of our business in Buddhist practice. We are concerned with Dharma — reality, which doesn't depend on thinking-understanding. Just as sincerity must move from the inside, outward, certainty begins at the center of the being when you let go of holding. Anything you try to hold is a phantom, a shadow; by letting go you allow reality to be what it is, and move as it will. That is called "going for refuge in the Buddha (the awakened one), the Dharma (truth), and the Sangha (participation in truth)."

    Next; there are two aspects of faith: conditioned and unconditioned. Conditioned faith grows through practice and experience. When you use your rational faculties to see what is reasonable and what is not, and when you use your clear senses to experience how things actually move, and when you undertake the practice and obtain its results, then you yourself will know what is true about practice and what is not.

    Before that, you don't know. And this is a very important point for all practitioners, to admit when they don't know! Admitting that we don't know makes space for knowing. Without that space, knowing — although it is already there — does not emerge for us; we deal in bullshit. Unknowing is actually an expression of your unconditional aspect of faith. It is that same "going for refuge". We see that the old things we tried to take refuge in — possessions, opinions, pleasure, accumulation — are empty and futile, and we take refuge in what is stable and fulfilling. We let go of grasping at outside things, and we rely on something greater to guide us. There is a particular letting-go, trusting, unknowing quality to this. It's like taking the Nestea plunge into all of reality.

    The unconditioned aspect of faith is always, and has always been, present in you. It is there because what the Buddha and his teaching point to is what you really are. So the aspect of you that is what you really are is already perfect faith, and nothing can cut or mar or shake that aspect. When you practice, you practice as that pure aspect, that perfect faith. You don't practice as the outsider. At least, that is the correct approach, and if you find your thinking or attitude differs you need to drop it.
