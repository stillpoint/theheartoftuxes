    See Hoy/Mu Deung Sunim himself admitted his arrogance at that time in his relationship with the Zen Master. No need for either me or you to be attached to that descriptor. No need for us to speculate on what the phrase "go fuck yourself" means, either. You think it's a challenge to authority. Maybe. Or maybe you'll find a different insight when these opinions are cast aside. That is the Zen way, after all.

    But you miss what I mean by 'arrogance'. There's no problem with taking a shot. Like the Great One said (I'm talking Wayne Gretzky, not some Zen Master): "You miss 100% of the shots you don't take." The willingness to put yourself on the line and trust your inner truth, however faulty you may be in expressing it, is highly valued in Zen. But the attitude of just throwing things out there and 'damn the consequences' is still immature and irresponsible; and Zen training is nothing if not responsible. We take the entire universe on and don it as a robe, in devotion, paying attention not only to our small circle, but also how our actions affect our relationships and our world.

    So while the gumption to blurt something out or jump beyond social conventions may be laudable, the recklessness is not. It is still the arrogance of youth.

    But a Zen Master can take anything and make it into gold. Sometimes absorbing it all, sometimes reflecting clearly, sometimes piercing with ferocity. It is all from the spontaneous wisdom, not from any set way of relating.

    Letters like these between Zen Master and student don't conform to the regular standards of communication, and shouldn't be judged by what we find 'right' or familiar. If there is no authority, that includes our own opinion.

    Actually, there is authority; but that's a whole other topic.

    Thank you for sharing your point of view.
