    I echo the responses here that highlight how petty, mercenary, and irrelevant much of r/zen has become. As a Zen practitioner, I completely avoid the forum.

    Regarding your question: the central point of Zen -- Original Mind, Primary Point, Prajnaparamita, or whatever you want to name it -- is not dependent on anything. So the heart of Zen is beyond all religions; and pure Zen can be practiced by adherents of most religions.

    But the institution, teachings, courses of training, and cultures surrounding and supporting the practice of the central point were, and are, originally Buddhist. So while you can say that Zen is beyond Buddhism, the way Zen is realized in the world is largely Buddhist, and its roots are most definitely Buddhist.

    The first transmission of Zen is said to have occurred when Buddha stood before a large congregation who were expecting his usual wonderful teaching speech; but instead, Buddha only held up a flower. Among the entire congregation, only Mahakaśyapa understood, with a smile; so Buddha proclaimed "to Mahakaśyapa I transmit my Dharma!"

    This is an example of Zen's direct mind-to-mind transmission that occurs beyond words, beyond rituals, beyond rules, beyond all religious structures. So this most vital point is not 'Buddhist'... but as we see, it all happened within a Buddhist context and was made possible by Buddha's awakening, his ripening training of Mahakaśyapa, and Mahakaśyapa's own awakening through training.

    So the point is not completely black-and-white; but those who utterly deny any Buddhist context in Zen tend, in my experience, to be attached to a narrow and elitist view. Unsurprisingly, perhaps, they also tend to be attached to emptiness (another way of describing the central point), and don't realize that Zen doesn't stop there; the emptiness needs to find its correct compassionate function, which Buddhism (and correct Zen) so eminently trains.

    ~

    Edit:

    The view of those Zen practitioners (and even teachers) who only attain emptiness is called 'Tathagata Zen': the person attains the essential substance of the Tathagata (Buddha), but doesn't understand its correct function. This might characterize most who insist that Zen has nothing to do with Buddhism.

    There is also Theoretical Zen, which we see online most often -- people who have some insight and can talk well, but who haven't realized that wisdom in their own lives. Tathagata Zen is like the graduation from Theoretical Zen, when the person actually attains emptiness, and the freedom from concept.

    And finally, there is the complete attainment of Patriarchal Zen (sometimes called Ancestral Zen), which is the full realization of truth just as it is -- free of concept, because the essential point of Tathagata Zen has been attained, but also perfectly able to function appropriately in any situation: lemon is sour, sugar is sweet; in the morning you brush your teeth and do morning practice; and if someone is suffering you help them -- everything is 'just like this'.
