    Questions about compassion

    Compassion means no separation. It is not a strategy. It is your original mind, which is not separate from anything.

    So coming back to neutral in your mind, coming back to 'zero', is compassion. From this open, uncontrived awareness you can spontaneously respond to each moment as-is.

    Therefore, you don't step outside the situation and check how you're doing. And thus you may not know whether you're being compassionate or not. Checking and knowing doesn't matter; that's outside of correct practice. What matters is your momentary, wholehearted sincerity.

    Put it all down, let it all go, and return to zero. Then participate in the moment wholeheartedly. That is the practice. There is no rulebook. There is no yardstick. You don't know what the next moment will require; you just enter it nakedly, fully trusting your original mind to deal with it.

    You don't need to be anything in particular. You don't have to take abuse and you don't have to lash out defensively. Sometimes holding a clear boundary very strongly is the most compassionate thing you can do for others who are trying to manipulate you. You do it to save them from their own bad action, to show them a way with more dignity and call them to their own higher nature.

    And when others are too dull or pig-headed to even recognize the invitation to dignity or the wisdom of clear boundary, then you may have to communicate with language that they understand. But it comes from loving; in your heart the intent is for their benefit.

    These are questions that have to be clarified by your activity, not by someone else's words. You have to put into practice the courage to show up openhandedly. This is necessary. You have to do it before you can understand what it is.

    There is a phrase that appears several times in the I Ching: "See the Great Man." In Dzogchen Buddhism they talk about "Pure Perception". We clarify our perception, letting go of all ideas and strategies, so that we can see the greatness, the perfection in every being and every situation.

    Likewise, the Dhammapada talks about how it's mistaken to think "he beset me, he harmed me, he abused me". Even as we may need to restrain someone's violence we can still see the best in them and hold that view until they themselves can own it — even if it takes years and lifetimes. But you have to do it before you understand what it means. Carrying around a grab-bag of opinions about compassion has nothing to do with true compassion.

    The Bodhisattva of Compassion is spoken of in the Heart Sutra. The view there is interpenetrating emptiness and form, and all identifiable aspects of life are cut off: no eyes, no ears... no body, no mind... no color, no sound... no perception, no consciousness... no attainment, nothing to attain. So this is the practice of the Bodhisattva of Compassion: in a moment, put it all down. Boom! Zero. Then from that place, trust your activity.
