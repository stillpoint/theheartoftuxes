    Not only monks! Many of us! Me too!

    Realize that for the experienced practitioner the struggle to attain tends to settle down, and constant pure practice comes to the forefront. Effort doesn't necessarily decrease (it may increase); but a different sense of time appears, as well as a different, more relaxed sense of selfhood.

    This is a tortuous subject because of the different ways the terms are used (and translated).

    Some Theravada schools might say that enlightenment is an attainment of wisdom, and Nirvana is the non-state of non-becoming that lies beyond that insight or wisdom. Others may equate the two terms and use them interchangeably. And some Mahayana, Zen, and Vajrayana schools may say that Enlightenment is beyond Nirvana! It may be seen as an attainment of both Nirvana and (what was previously) Samsara -- both the eternal unconditioned reality and the universal co-created reality. In practical effect, one attainment often includes or triggers the other, but not necessarily so.

    There are also different depths of enlightenment. One may have glimpsed the truth of no self-nature; another may have seen that emptiness is the true nature of everything; and yet another may embody that insight in their every activity, in a broader way than mere insight.

    Yes, many many people and other beings have attained Nirvana, and enlightenment. What's unique about the Buddha's enlightenment is that not only did he get perfect insight into the true nature of everything, he also completely eliminated all conditioning. All faults, all habits, even the slightest subtle tendency were purified. Making karma and lingering results of karma were both completely extinguished. And the Buddha's wisdom and compassion can and does effortlessly manifest in all realms -- Dharmakaya, Sambhogakaya, and Nirmanakaya. This is a very different, much more exceptional attainment than 'merely' awakening from the dream of life.

    Don't think that these things are not attainable. Depending on your capacity, effort, and conditions, you may get Nirvana within only fifty lifetimes... or even next life ...or even this life. For instance, some Vajrayana paths claim that if you complete their practices you will certainly get enlightenment within this lifetime. (Which raises the question of whether you have the capacity to complete the practices, or the karmic merit to even encounter them.)

    But in any case, it's much, much better to be going in the right direction than the wrong one. This is true at any stage of the path. And each moment itself is complete. Although there is a long-term aspect of cultivation of they path, there is also an immediate aspect of integrity. It is noble and good to act in accordance of the Dharma in this moment, even regardless of future effect.

    And finally, this moment is already Nirvana. That's a higher-level teaching that recognizes that the content of Buddha's enlightenment is the fundamental truth, even before it has been fully realized by others. Although we make effort and cultivate benefit and progress from lesser to greater attainments, essentially we are already there at the ultimate. The thought that you can't attain it is one kind of hindrance. The thought that you haven't already attained it is another kind of hindrance.

    (And, for the sake of those lazybones who deduce that they therefore don't have to do any practice: the thought that you have already attained it is yet another kind of hindrance.)
