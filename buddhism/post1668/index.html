    Dealing with distractions and emotions during meditation practice

    If there is reason to trust your teacher, then trust your teacher. Follow your teacher's instruction to the utmost. Right relationship with your teacher is important; it helps you have right relationship with your practice, freeing you from doubting and checking, doubting and checking.

    If you're not so sure about your teacher, ask for clarification. You can say that you've read this thing here but the teacher is saying that thing there, and you can ask how to understand the difference. It's important that you keep good communication with your teacher and don't undercut their efforts. Every system has its own method and its own way of presenting the method. If the overall direction is good and if through research and personal experience you find the teacher trustworthy, put down other opinions and follow the teacher.

    In addition, if the teacher is just learning how to teach you can help them by being a good student. That doesn't mean you have to swallow everything they give you without question; but it does mean listening well, following to the best of your ability, and clarifying doubts when they arise rather than dividing your mind.

    Both instructions are correct within their contexts. Your teacher's guidance is true of deeper concentration. Any movement away from the object means you're not with the object. But although that is true it can promote an unhealthy way of thinking, as you've discovered. Trying to push away other objects just means you're engaging with them more, not less.

    If there's any "trick" to meditation, it's being immediate and gentle in returning. When the mind wanders off, engaged with other objects, rather than wrestling with it let the returning be instant and gentle. It's a very light move, as if it were insignificant; don't charge up the situation into a dire struggle.

    When you spill a bit of soup on the stove, you grab a towel or sponge and clean it. No big deal, no drama, no philosophizing. There's absolutely no reason to make a problem around it; you just wipe, and then move on. It's the same in meditation. When you notice you've gone off, at that moment just return. There's no need to judge what else may be going on. There's no need to check how much you've returned. There's no need to think of the situation as good or bad, or think of yourself as doing well or poorly. Your job is to return to the method.

    Over time you will see for yourself what is the truth of how the mind works. Even so, your job will be to return to the method. So might as well do it without adding extra drama.

    Just now you can try something: whatever direction your right hand is facing, flip it over. Left hand too; just flip it over. There â€” very easy, very instant, no big deal. Your returning in meditation should have that quality. You only need to keep applying it. We have lots of "going away" karma, lots of distracted habit; so distraction will come up 10,000 times. We return the same way, 10,001 times.

    The returning is very momentary. Your whole practice is momentary, okay? If you find yourself thinking of how you've been doing over the course of the sitting, or over the duration of the last minute, or over the past few weeks, that's being outside of meditation. Meditation is just about now. Just now is where you change your life. Away, or return? That is the crucial difference in your true life which is only ever just now.

    Easily returning will help your concentration grow, and it will help your numbness soften. You will not be fighting yourself. You will be returning to a state where everything can be as it is. So don't push something away, only instantly and gently return.

    Good luck.
