    I don't want to help someone, is that wrong?

    Buddhism isn't based on ideas of right and wrong (like the notion of sin in some religions) but rather on cause and effect. It invites you to see the results of what you put into motion with your thoughts and actions.

    So there is really nothing 'wrong', ever. Everything is just movement. You are free. It's just that there are inescapable results to your intentions and actions.

    The concept of right action or wrong action is used as a short-form way of explaining causes that either lead to awakening and happiness or to ignorance and suffering. This is a bit like telling a child that a certain action is wrong, but when they reach an age where they can understand better, we explain in greater detail why. The action itself isn't wrong, it just has certain consequences.

    As to the situation you write about, the central question is "What is help?" Help doesn't always mean doing whatever someone else wants. Sometimes help is quite the opposite: setting boundaries that support integrity.

    Sometimes you need to protect yourself and others, so you set a clear boundary or you limit what you offer with conditions: "I am happy to help you, but I expect you to respect me and respect my time; and I feel I can only spare an hour."

    But if catering to your relative's desires is debasing â€” if it means they take on more superiority and control and you are expected to be subservient to them, that doesn't help you and it doesn't help them. It only teaches them that they can continue being selfish. In this case, it is actually quite a bit more compassionate for you to set a boundary and say a kind but firm "no".

    If the relative gets up in arms about it, you can invite them to hear your reasons. "Would you like to hear why I don't want to do this for you?" This gives them a choice that can raise their awareness: either they refuse, and experience themselves refusing to listen to you, or they open themselves to hear your side of things.

    You can also calmly, firmly require a certain amount of respect while you are sharing your experience of the relationship and your reasons for refusing to go along with their wishes. "I'm willing to discuss the matter, but not if you yell or insult me." Clearly the other party may not have a lot of skill in communicating and listening, so you also need to be understanding and compassionate in this regard; but you don't have to allow them to insult or demean you. If they do, you can step back from engagement until (and if) they eventually come around.

    If you actually hold care for others in your heart, you can do or say just about anything. There's a time for softness and a time for firmness; compassion can be either one at various times.
