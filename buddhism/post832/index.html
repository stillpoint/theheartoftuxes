    >Even though Zazen is at the heart of Soto Zen, Soto Masters have never discarded the use of koans outside the practice of Zazen.     I don't know what Hakuin encountered that made him so sour on Soto. It's often the case, for example, that students or break-off sects may misrepresent or fail to convey the true depth of their teacher's attainment and teaching; so it's quite possible that Hakuin encountered misunderstanding (perhaps coupled with defensiveness or bravado) and took to be representative of the whole school. Or that he was actually just criticizing a subset of the Soto school or certain teachers. Or maybe he just didn't get it.

    Teachers may also sometimes be very set on their way, for the simple fact that it is what has brought them very real, undeniable results. And enlightenment has to do with seeing the nature of self; it doesn't always mean one's character has been perfected and all blind spots have been eradicated, or that cultural conditioning has been overcome. Anyone who has helped administer a Zen school (or almost any institution) knows, all kinds of social and political forces come to bear on the policies and communications that leaders put forth.

    We also know how issues get dumbed-down over time in the public eye, and that far more complex and reasonable motives are often behind the simplistic headlines. We know from records, for example, that Hakuin also held Dogen in high esteem. I'd dare say that Hakuin might have railed just as strongly against lax or misguided factions in his own Rinzai lineage. But narrow-minded interpreters and our own laziness are to blame for the overly simplified and often altogether false dichotomies passed down (like the similarly superficial analysis of Theravada versus Mahayana). In real life, most masters get along just dandy.

    On the other hand, in my experience, there are some who are proud of their opinions, and who gather writings and sayings to support them in their view. These are the people who, year after year, tend to remain crusty and haughty. They don't seem to progress either in insight or in character.
