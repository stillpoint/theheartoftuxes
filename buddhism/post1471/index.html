    Who or what are you angry at?

    I'm not saying you have no right to be angry. The truth is that you're angry. I'm not even sure that I'd put the adjective 'irrational' in front of the word 'anger'; it's possible that your anger is completely reasonable. I'm asking if you have a sense of where it is directed.

    Often we jump to anger because we're skipping over another more appropriate emotion that we're not so skilled with, or that we habitually avoid. Sadness, for instance. Fear. Grief. Regret.

    These emotions cause us to feel our vulnerability, as opposed to anger, which makes us feel strong. Often we avoid anything that will bring up a sense of tenderness; and this is especially true of men. This kind of habit makes us emotionally inept, emotionally illiterate. When a painful, charged event gets under our skin, under our ribs, and into our heart, we may immediately and unconsciously push it away. But the energy is still there; it still needs to be expressed. So we may unconsciously turn to anger as a way of boosting ourselves and covering up what's really going on.

    But actually the vulnerability is a true and necessary first step. You have to be with the softness of your heart and with the knowledge of impermanence first, before turning to action. Being in your heart is what allows your anger to act with wisdom and compassion â€” as justice rather than as violence.

    All humans have a responsibility to live truly. It is not just for a special few; it is up to all of us to be true to what we are and what moves through us. Men especially need to courageously enter their hearts and feel the full truth of what's there. The modern dysfunctional idea of a man is someone who is indeed irrationally violent and opinionated; but deeper tribal and communal ways support the model of a man who has the courage to feel everything, to face what is real rather than put on a mask of strength while being inwardly unconscious and out of control.

    Another point here is that while of course we grieve the death of our loved ones (and pets, because of their innocence and faithfulness, can really get into our hearts), actually a lot of our reactivity around death has to do with our own fear and clinging. Because we ourselves haven't come to terms with the reality of death, someone else's death triggers us. Again, it is a deep and often unconscious reaction; but essentially, we are not so sad and angry about the other's death â€” we are sad, angry, and fearful of our own death. This often prompts seemingly irrational behavior around a time of loss.

    (Sorry to use the word 'loss', because death is only a loss if you make it so.)

    When anger is taking over, it's neither good to ignore it nor to go into it. Some people, who have a very healthy relationship with their emotions, can go into negative emotions and process them; but many of us need to keep a healthy distance. If we only go into the negativity, without some external support or perspective like a strong spiritual practice, or a professional therapist or other guide, the negativity will often take over.

    I recommend expressing yourself, but then moving on. You can create some piece of art; you can go running or do martial arts; you can pour out your heart in a journal; you can dance it out or do theatrical therapy; but you should do it by way of letting the anger be expressed and then moving on. Don't do it as a way of going deeper into the anger, with repeating thoughts of self-justification, and so on.
