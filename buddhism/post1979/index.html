    What to wear

    You don't have to be formal (tie and dress pants), but you should be respectful in your clothing. Nice jeans are usually okay; chinos or other pants comfortable for sitting may be even better. Avoid ratty, torn jeans, dirty clothing, revealing clothing, clothing with loud and overly-casual patterns or graphics, and clothing with lots of words on them. Avoid wearing shorts or exposing too much chest. You won't get turned away for any of these at most temples, but it's nice to take the extra care.

    If it's cool enough, a plain collared shirt. If it's hot, a polo or short-sleeve collared shirt. Again, T-shirt is not necessarily a problem, but it might be nice to show a bit more respect.

    Although most temples have some chairs available, plan to sit on the floor if necessary; so do wear pants okay for sitting cross-legged. Most temples also serve tea and a snack before/during pujas, but I usually bring a small bottle of water as well.

    Tibetan temples tend to be quite a bit more casual than most Sunday church services; but on special days people dress up, either in nice western clothing or in wonderful Tibetan traditional costumes.

    If you want to be meticulously respectful, wear a prayer shawl for all Dharma activities. It doesn't have to be wine red color like the monks' shawls, just something to cover the shoulders. This is the kind of think that's not required, especially for newcomers and westerners, but if you go to pujas frequently it's a good practice of respect.

    Also, you may want to pick up a khata or two â€” a traditional silk scarf. There are many occasions where a silk scarf is offered. You place it across your hands and offer it to the honored lama, and he/she will place it over your head as a blessing. If you don't have a scarf and people are lining up to offer them, look around you and you may find someone handing them out. (Either someone from the temple or someone with 'grandmother's mind' who carries extra for just such an occasion.)

    Experienced people often can produce khatas seemingly out of thin air â€” they're up the sleeve, they're in the shirt, they're in the bag.... You can keep it folded up until you start to line up, and then unfold it across your palms. Offer it with the open side facing the lama (you can remember which way around by thinking about offering openness rather than cutting with the folded crease.)

    The same goes for envelopes, in case you want to offer dana (donation). Always good to have an envelope or two around. The money goes in the envelope, and the envelope is held with the thumbs on top of the silk scarf as it's offered. Any time you want to offer something like a gift, or bring an object for the lama to bless, you hand it to them on top of a khata.
