    How does one feel empathy for those who suffer and at the same time refrain from feeling sad and depressed?

    Most people don't understand compassion; they only have ideas about compassion.

    This holds true even here on the Buddhism subreddit: most of the folks who post are only writing about their idea, and don't understand what compassion actually is.

    Compassion only looks like rushing to help once it has begun responding to a situation. So people confuse that outer form of doing something for compassion. But before it has responded, compassion is just ready, without a speck of an idea about how to act, what to take in or reject, what resources to use... nothing, no idea. It is silent, open, boundless, and aware.

    So there is no gain or loss, no place for harm to enter. It's that old saying about Shaolin monks that we heard in the TV series Kung Fu: the buffalo finds no place where it's horn can pierce, the tiger find no place where it's claws can tear.

    True compassion is radiant. One might say it is radiance, the radiance of open, unchoosing awareness. It doesn't seek to take in or hold anything for oneself, but rather is ready to move out from the center with complete confidence and integrity. In some respects, compassion is like a mirror; it only reflects the situation, without holding or making a single idea about it.

    But unlike a mirror, compassion then responds, spontaneously and beyond doubt. It is the responding that we usually identify as compassionate activity, but the open unchoosing space preceding it is the essential part.

    The bottom line is that real compassion doesn't get stuck anywhere. It experiences no clinging, no loss. What you're talking about is all involved with the thinking and the ego characters: if one is invested in thinking of oneself in a certain way (as a nice person doing certain kinds of nice actions), and if one is invested in making ideas about the situation (too bad, poor you), that is what gets stuck, and that is what causes burnout.

    Trying to be compassionate is already a problem, already a departure from the pure openness of real compassion.

    It is said that the Bodhisattva never for a moment falls into the delusion that sentient beings exist, but also never for a moment fails to respond compassionately to sentient beings.
