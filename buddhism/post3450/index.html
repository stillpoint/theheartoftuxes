    How comfortable someone else is doesn't reflect your situation. Some are comfortable, some are not.

    If you are doing a practice with a group, that is already fortunate. The first point is to recognize how precious group practice and spiritual companionship is.

    There are many strategies for overcoming self-consciousness. One very good way, and perhaps the common and basic way, is to throw yourself into the activity. Take the energy of fretting and thinking and checking and comparing, and channel it all into chanting (or whatever the practice of the moment involves). When you become one with your activity, the external checking mind disappears. No more problem.

    You say chanting "would" feel awkward. So this suggests you haven't even tried it yet. You are deciding ahead of time what your experience is going to be. Again, that is your thinking mind controlling you, keeping you enslaved to whatever thought patterns have been programmed into you. If you want to be free, you have to do practice. You can't get free of your thinking mind if you avoid practice; you have to choose one or the other.

    You also use the phrase "meditating with an audience"; but is this the case? If you are practicing with others, that is not an 'audience'. Everyone is in the same situation, making sincere effort toward awakening. Don't be so prideful that you imagine yourself to be the focus of the group. You are not the performer, nor are others the audience. You are all practicing together.

    In group practice you may actually find that the group helps you let go. Alone, you may have your favorite insulated experience, but you are not necessarily practicing letting go. But in the group, everyone's effort merges, like streams into a river, and you may find yourself propelled into action — with growing confidence and abandon — because you are in a group.

    Realize that this is not about singing voice. It's not about being pretty; it's about waking up to truth. Express yourself completely. Don't embody 'not belonging'. It is up to you to stand up for yourself. Perceive your voice and merge with it. Let your voice help you be present, momentary. As you pour your energy into voicing and perceiving, you progressively let go of the problematic thinking mind, and you become (A) clear and (B) free.

    In every center I've practiced at (and there have been many), there have always been people out of tune or out of sync. It doesn't matter. In some traditions, this is even built in to the practice. For example, the folk Korean style is totally chaotic, with everyone doing their own tune at their own pace, like a droning beehive. Whatever the style, the main thing is exerting yourself for the sake of awakening.

    So it doesn't really matter if the outside situation is something familiar or favored. Throwing yourself into it with abandon will save you all kinds of suffering. Whatever's happening, just participate. If you can do that, you're free.

    But if we're talking about having non-practice-people around, that's different. Until you are quite centered and humble it's best to meditate in private. For one thing, don't make a show. For another, don't expose yourself to unnecessary distraction. This is one of the many reasons it is good to connect with a teacher and a practice place, so some of the time you can have those supports and some of the time you can train on your own in private.

    Then, when your ability to stay present and not get carried away by thought is strong (we call it 'having a strong center'), you can be anywhere without being distracted. When your center is strong from long-term practice, you won't be affected by thoughts of what others think, whether positive or negative, praising or blaming; you will only keep your mind open and momentary.
