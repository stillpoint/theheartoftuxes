    I've become attached to getting rid of this problem

    Thinking badly of yourself is completely and utterly useless for spiritual progress. What is required is knowing your mistake deeply, and then working to change it. Anything else just builds complex patterns around the issue that you will have to deal with later.

    Even if you make a mistake, you shouldn't think poorly of yourself. Instead, be a good friend to yourself and remind yourself of your good qualities and noble wishes.

    So what is the mistake? Not sex or attraction, but rather illusion and attachment are the issues. We are human, with human responses. It is what our mind does with these responses that makes problems, placing illusions over our real experience and distancing us from each other as real people.

    One angle that may help you is that it is easier and more natural to do what you are motivated to do than it is to stop doing what you are attached to. That means you can focus more on doing good deeds and participating in good activities, and you will thus naturally crowd out the negative behavior because your time and energy will be enjoyably occupied.

    Participating in positive activity, you will change your mind about yourself; you will think of yourself as more worthy of noble activity. You will also create the conditions for better practice. The Tibetan Buddhists call this "gaining merit": you do the things that clear the way for awakening.

    Yes, turning away from negative actions is important; but it doesn't have to be the center of your life. In fact, the more you make it a big issue in your thinking, the more energy you give it. This can be a subtle psychological ploy: you build up tension around the issue so that you can later release it, convincing yourself that the tension was so overwhelming that you had no choice. Then self-blame comes in as an adjunct to the ploy: you make a deal with yourself to feel bad, in payment, rather than actually working to change the behavior.

    So focusing on the positive is one step. Another is to arrange a supportive lifestyle and environment.

    • Balanced diet and regular sleep schedule help to calm your energy. Erratic or heavy or overstimulating diet and poor sleep or too much sleep create "fire" energy, which is very difficult to control. Balance in these energies promote cooler "water" energy, which actually helps you make conscious and positive choices.

    • Clean, tidy living space, orderly schedule, and elimination of trivial or negative stimuli support the calm, centered mind. Be aware of the physical triggers for negative behavior (computer/TV/meaningless objects) , and gradually eliminate them from your living space. This goes for your activity outside of the home: keep to noble, uplifting places as much as possible.

    • Be aware of how acquaintances affect your mind and mood, and choose accordingly.

    • Be aware of how your activities may contribute to despondency or a sense of meaninglessness that causes you to seek something to make you feel more alive (lustful images, for example); or how your activities may incite frustration or tension. Seek out activities that help you feel vibrant (say, physical activity outdoors) and meaningful (say, creating art, helping friends, or doing charity work) within yourself.

    The Buddha recommended body and death visualizations as cures for lust. You can Google for the outlines, or reply here. In general, being aware of the true make-up of the body (all the fluids, tissues, bones, veins, fat, etc.) and of the process of aging from conception through death helps to dispel the illusions that lust is based on. Shantideva's chapter on Meditation in Bodhisattvacaryavatara also speaks to this issue.
