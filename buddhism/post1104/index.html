    TheHeartofTuxes

    The phrases are, in part, a framework to guide the general direction of the practice. If you know the practice — a big 'if' — you can just do it without the phrases. Or you can seek out translations and interpretations that better suit your bent of nature. There are many, many doorways. So if you do away with the words, this is still a wonderful practice; but know that it is not the practice as it has been passed down. (Actually, I don't know when the phrases became part of the practice, whether it was earlier or later, but the practice has been passed down to you in this way so it's your responsibility to look after it with care.)

    Words and sounds have a power. We know some of this power in our daily life through poetry and music, affirmations and incantations, hurtful words and loving words. To eliminate the speaking part of a practice may seem like no big deal; but that may be because we don't yet realize the significance of it. This is why recommendations like "do what you feel like" may not only fall short in personal practice, but may quickly erode the Dharma and make it unavailable to others.

    Voice has a power. Sound reaches many realms that other media can't reach. For instance, when you're sleeping and dreaming, a sound from the waking world can enter your dream and wake you up. And even if you live far from a temple and are not taking part in the temple practice, the huge temple bell and drum may reach you, bringing you into the moment and moving your heart toward the Dharma.

    Similarly, the speaking part of practices carries some of the Metta and transmits it — to your deep mind, to other beings seen and unseen, and even vibrationally to the environment. When practice develops, your voice may become an instrument of Dharma, soothing, clarifying, blessing others.

    The "May I..." is not a question. It is a seal. It is similar to Captain Picard saying "Make it so, Number One!" He says it, then something happens — the Enterprise goes to warp speed, or whatever. It establishes that your practice is not just theoretical, but is expected to actually manifest. It voices the actual unending intent of your enlightened nature.

    All the Brahmaviharas, of which Metta is part, are aspects of enlightened mind. When we say that we 'generate' them, it's only apparently so, from our standpoint. But actually our enlightened nature never stops saying "May it be so... May all beings be well... May this enlightenment come to fruition... May we all wake up together...."

    You can definitely adapt the practice to what naturally moves through you. Self-trust is an important part of practice. At some point, the calligraphy student has to start making his own brushstrokes by himself. But that presumes that there is a 'student' — one who has studied, who has opened fully to the teaching, and whom the teaching has entered. So first it's very important to learn well and completely, and not be quick to do away with parts of a method.

    In some traditions, it's not considered okay to change the method at all. In other traditions, it's considered okay to change the method once you've attained enlightenment and passed a course of training. And in other traditions, you receive guidance and then not much is said about how you apply it. There is a proper cause-and-effect relationship to each of these ways, and it's vital to know which way you are choosing, and why; because of course everyone wants the third way, but actually that is the most difficult and most disciplined of all.

    ~

    Edit: I just wanted to add that 'May I...' is really only a first step. 'I will...' is also absolutely necessary. It isn't enough to just intend to make it so; the action of making it so is essential, and is the one thing that can empower the first part, the 'May I...', and make it vital and true.
