    What to wear while sitting?

    You'll want clothing that allows you to take the meditation posture easily and without self-consciousness. If sitting with others, care should be taken not to wear clothing that is too revealing or visually distracting. Solid colors, and muted or calm ones, should be worn — black, grey, and earth tones rather than bright colors or vibrant patterns. Avoid having words or images on your meditation clothes.

    There are reasons to change into clean, simple, subdued, and loose-fitting clothing that go beyond merely comfortable and respectful, although those are also considerations. Whether you consider it a personal feng shui, or whether you consider it an embodiment of your true nature, when you wear clean clothing you reflect and support your original purity; when you wear simple clothing, you influence your mind with that simplicity and evoke the simplicity of your being. Conversely, when you wear clothing that is dirty or shoddy or associated with lounging and sleeping, you reflect on yourself in those ways; it becomes more likely that your mind turns in those directions rather than toward its sublime or rigorous qualities.

    Taking care of your clothing can, like all kinds of maintenance activities, merge with taking care of your practice. It becomes a way of honoring and expressing your Buddha nature. Of course, if you have taken the precepts or have been ordained, this reaches the pinnacle of devotion in the care and honoring of the kasaya/kesa/jiasha, which is the sign and physical form of your great Bodhisattva vow. In some Vajrayana practices, you envision yourself adorned as a Bodhisattva, and you may even actually wear the crown and robes to deeply establish your faith that you are that — that you are the great Bodhisattva being, not the petty, suffering, mundane story you have previously been identifying with.

    There are psychological effects of clothing, and there are effects on energetic levels that may be considered subtle by some; but if you have some awareness and sensitivity toward these things it is easy enough to perceive the effects yourself. See how your mind and energy are when you don't wash all day, versus when you have just come out of a sweatlodge, or on the tail end of a sauna and shower. Notice your quality of mind and mood when you are around people who are grimy, smelly, or lazy. Some people are very attached to their gross physical nature, and refuse to keep clean. They have almost an animal quality to them. Others are very attached to purity, and are unwilling to participate with people in many everyday settings. (On the Wheel of Life and Death, these people may occupy the deva region — beings lost in heavenly pleasures and unable to muster the will toward awakening.) The Buddhist way is the middle path, finding the balance between purity on one hand and compassionate action together on the other hand. In meditation, however, we are looking into the pure basis of mind; so it is best to keep as clean, simple, and dignified as possible. This extends to your environment as well. Regularly refreshing and cleaning your altar and sitting space can be very helpful for your engagement with meditation, with respect to the body, the heart, and the mind (posture, mood/aspiration, and concentration).

    It is not necessary to have a special uniform or religious or ethnic costume for sitting, but pajamas and a ratty sweatshirt are not conducive of the subtlety and nobility one seeks to embody in meditation practice. Some will point out that meditation and awakening don't depend on any outer form, and that is true — you can and should keep up your practice regardless of the outer situation. But it is also true that we are subject to cause and effect, and that until we reach a very high degree of stability we are quite influenced by our surroundings and the company we keep.

    For sittings at home I usually wear either the uniform of my school, which is traditional monk's clothing, or plain loose black pants similar to those worn for martial arts, and a clean (usually white) t-shirt or collared shirt.

    On some occasions I will meditate in pajamas, but those are impromptu sittings, often on my bed, taken in addition to the more formal sessions on the mat by my altar. On these occasions, it is usually the case that I suddenly want to sit right then and there, so whatever I am wearing at the time becomes the de facto uniform. If I have any sense that I will be sitting soon, I always want to at least wash my face and hands and wear fresh clothes.
