    In the initial stages of training toward enlightenment a certain amount of imitation is performed. You adopt rules of conduct and submit to contrived situations in order to de-program the many negative habits and views that cloud your (our) innate brilliance.

    But the rules and contrived situations are only upaya, only 'skillful means'; they are not the main point. The main point is to contact that innate brilliance, which is your own enlightened nature. Contact it, recognize it (because many contact it without knowing it, and many don't contact it but think that they do), and learn to trust it.

    Then that is what you rely on, not some rule book. The difference between an enlightened master and a regular deluded person is remarkable. If you've ever been around enlightened beings you will be familiar with the presence they radiate, and also their easy and solid confidence. The confidence is not what we usually think of as confidence, because it doesn't hinge on a 'self'. It is not a house of cards, it is not pumped-up; the confidence is a function of their reliance on original enlightened mind. It is the absolute ground; nothing can shake it, because it is the basis of everything.

    They let go and un-know, and let that vast, spacious awareness take care of everything.

    The great Taoist sage Chuang Tzu talked about this kind of confidence:
    > Chaos and doubt --
    that is what the Sage steers by.
    He does not use things
    but relegates all to the constant.
    This is what it means
    to use clarity.

    The great Tang dynasty Zen Master Ta Hui taught:
    > If you want to study this path, you must understand right where you are. As soon as you rely on the slightest knowledge, you miss the scene right where you're standing. When you've completely comprehended the scene right where you are, then all kinds of knowledge -- all without exception -- are things right where you are.

    The luminary Tibetan master Tilopa taught:
    >Don’t recall.
    Don’t imagine.
    Don’t think.
    Don’t examine.
    Don’t control.
    Rest.

    These masters and many more point to the kind of effort necessary to unfold enlightenment. Essentially, they point you back to you, back to your own original spacious mind. It is this one central point that all true practice relies upon. Gathering rules and knowledges may sometimes be part of the path; but just as often they are big obstacles, and in any case are beside the one central point.

    I hope you always return to the one central point, whether you are using rules or not. I hope you rely on your own Unknowing, and let it shine to all beings in all situations.
