    Quick (maybe) question on meditation

    That's just the wording that some people use, but it's not really a precise use of the term meditation. You're right, meditation means keeping a mind that is clear and aware without (the mind) moving. No thinking, no going toward or away from anything. Only reflecting the moment as it is.

    But because of our common habit of living in the world of thoughts and believing in it, it's very difficult for many people to go straight to true meditation. That's why we may use an object to help us. Instead of keeping a clear mind, the meditator takes hold off an object and keeps returning to it. When thoughts come and distract, the meditator disengaged from the thoughts and returns to the object. So the object provides a rallying point or anchor, and also helps the meditator identify when he's been distracted. Eventually, attachment to thinking settles, the mind becomes clear, and one can then practice pure meditation if one chooses.

    This kind of object-related practice is more properly called concentration practice, but most people will include it under the broad term "meditation". Even if it's not strictly correct, the word meditation is used to talk about all kinds of methods that engage with the mind. These methods don't all have the same effect. In fact, some methods are quite opposite to others in aim and effect.

    Within the concentration-style practices there are many possible objects, including sense objects like sounds or colored discs, visualizations, breathing, analytical methods, and others. Sitting and thinking around a particular theme is not even necessarily such a good concentration practice, because it encourages more thinking and doesn't distinguish between returning to the object and distraction: any train of thought could be followed. There's some form of discipline to staying on-topic with thinking, but it can't lead to liberation.

    These kinds of thinking sessions are sometimes called contemplation. People will still speak of it as “meditation", but it's quite far from true meditation practice.

    People will even say “I'm going to go meditate on that" when they mean to think about it. That's the opposite of meditation. If they were really meditating on a subject, they would bring the subject to mind once and then completely drop it without a single thought more. Sitting in clarity, the clear mind digests the topic much better than thinking possibly could; most people just don't trust that.

    Watching the breath at the nostrils is a classic concentration-style practice. It is sometimes taught as part of a broader course of training that includes insight-promoting practice as well as character-purifying behaviors and lifestyle guidelines. The breath is commonly used because it is considered to be an object appropriate for almost all types of mind (almost all bents of nature), and it is an object that can be used in practices that lead to liberation from all self-delusion and suffering. Many other objects are useful, but can only take you part way along the path. (Which could mean many years of practice before you find the need to move on to another practice in order to progress.)

    Your first notion was correct: meditation means keeping a clear, unmoving mind.
