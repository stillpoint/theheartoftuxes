    PLEASE help me understand the charlatan, Tsem Tulku Rinpoche...

    I'll preface this by saying I am not a student of Tsem Tulku Rinpoche's, have not made any purchases from his institution's website, and have no connection beyond seeing some of his online videos.

    So from this relatively oblivious perch, here are a few possible explanations to chew on:

    • It's common for a person who, through personal power and lots of sincere effort, has built a teaching institution or practice place to become surrounded by people who peddle a cheaper version of his/her teaching. I've seen it with Swami Satchidananda and others: the teachers themselves may be enlightened and very sincere, but the structure built up around them can be tacky, if not questionable. (Not that I'm saying the Rinpoche's school or center is questionable, but if it were this could be one of the reasons.)

    • Some Vajrayana practices, and several of those Tsem Tulku Rinpoche teaches, emphasize generous offerings. As you let go of your selfish gaining idea, gratitude and generosity naturally arise which may be expressed as offerings to your original enlightened mind in the form of lineage teachers, guiding deities, and home images and altars. The offering of riches, both material and envisioned, builds the merit that allows for progress along the path. Some practices are not possible without a great deal of accumulated merit, so practitioners may take up a practice of making large or repeated offerings. (It is up to the practitioner to decide what constitutes a large offering; I've never heard Tsem Tulku Rinpoche name a particular amount, instead always recommending one offer the best "within one's means".)

    • It may at first seem ridiculous to associate spiritual things with expensive prices, but consider that this value is essentially empty. Money has the same value as anything: zero. It is only because of our collective thinking that it has the value it does. Consider further that if the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha are to actually hold the central place in our lives, we should perhaps offer what we value most to them. So we don't just give lip service to honoring the Buddha, we give the best to the best by using precious materials and well-crafted items, and by caring for them with great devotion. Some paths are paths of moderation and humility; this path is a path of full, lush life and humility.

    • Also — in Vajrayana more than some other paths — the teacher, as the most immediate and living gateway to Buddha Mind, is considered to be supremely precious to us. Visualization and devotional practice, which are both valid paths to enlightenment, can be supported by having an image of the teaching in view, in a place of honor. The teacher's essential mind is the same as the Buddha's essential mind. Whatever faults the teacher may be working through, we empower them to empower us by believing in their enlightened nature. It goes along with offering the best to the best: we train in seeing this world as a Buddha realm, and seeing our teacher as the Enlightened One. This is not making up stories, but rather seeing life on a deeper, more real plane.

    • Some monk bowls actually do cost a lot. $300 or more for a begging bowl or oryoki set is not uncommon.

    We regular people are quick to judge and quick to denounce. We may be right sometimes, but in any case we are quick to judge. It is part of our ignorance. This form of arrogance and doubt has to be eradicated when following a path of surrender. Reasonable investigation is important; habitual, misinformed suspicion is to be purged. So it's good that you ask for explanation. If I haven't provided good enough explanation, don't assume that none exists. Even if Tsem Tulku Rinpoche has serious flaws, he is still far more thoroughly trained, accomplished, wise, and actively compassionate than I and — I'd venture — you.

    My own teachers have passed on this piece of guidance to me, and I'd like to pass it on to you, as it is close to my heart: If your teacher has a fault, how can you help them? Don't complain! How can you help?

    If you're particularly curious or nonplussed, why not ask the Rinpoche himself, or the administrator of his online store? Don't beat around the bush; find out!
