    One of the most famous 'live words' Zen Masters was Zen Master Yun Men (Jap. Ummon), who was known for his one-word teachings. But though he used words, his words were not usual words, and not used in the usual way. If a person has not been trained in Zen, it will do little good and may do much harm (to the potential for clarity and awakening) to read, guess, and develop opinions about these teachings.

    Most good teachers and experienced practitioners will, through their reticence, save you from yourself. They will refuse to share teachings you are not ready for, and will not provide a springboard for guessing, dabbling, or fake practice. It is very honorable how consistent and caring many Dharma practitioners are in this regard.

    If you want to learn 'live words', get proper Zen training, or proper training in another path of enlightenment. For those who, despite strong and sincere effort to find a teacher, are prey to circumstances that keep them isolated, I recommend self-inquiry practice in the interim. I.e., I recommend doing the practice of trying to find one's own mind. That is "withdrawing [the attention] inward and searching for the ground upon which you stand".

    "Who am I?"

    "Where is the 'me', the one who experiences everything?"

    Try, try, try to find it, without giving rise to any idea and without trying to hold or avoid anything. The trying, without a single concept about it, is 'live words'. When you are in the thick of the questioning, it is ungraspable and without personal motive.
