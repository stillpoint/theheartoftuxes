    27 Sources of Negative Behaviour (Shantideva)

    We see life through the filter of our self-obsession. Unbeknownst to us, this self that we are constantly, habitually trying to feed and elevate is an empty illusion. All of our personal suffering, all of the suffering we cause those around us, and all of the shared suffering of sentient beings comes from this illusion, this misunderstanding about what we really are.

    Therefore Shantideva gives guidance on completely overcoming the illusion and the profoundly deep habitual attachments that go with it — the negative forces that control us. Even the fear and revulsion you experienced at the suggestion of letting go of cherished things points to the control this illusion has over your/our life.

    But don't think that this is denial or self-hatred. A greater, kinder, more stable delight is revealed; but a delight that doesn't depend on having your favorite of everything. Our human history is really sub-human: a mass of animals all struggling to get what they want, rather than living up to more noble potentials of mutual support, harmony with nature, and love for all beings.

    I think of the mind that appears at birthday parties when the cake is served: The clamor that arises "Oooh, cake!" The anxiety that arises, "Let me in there... I hope I get a good piece... I hope there's some left for me... I hope I get a big piece... I hope we can go back for seconds..." You can see the grasping mind arise in the room, just as it appears en masse in the world: "I... I... I..., mine... mine... mine...." And then the irony and tragedy is that the 'cake' never satisfies. It is never as good as you hope. It may be too sweet or a weird taste or undercooked. And even if it is the most delicious cake you've had, soon you eat too much and it is no longer satisfying. In fact, if you continue eating, that same wonderful cake becomes a source of torture.

    So this is our life of attachment. There is no satisfaction in getting our favorite, but we devote our lives — our thought and our energy and our effort — to it nonetheless. Shantideva points out the futility of it, and leads us to perfect freedom.

    But Shantideva's guidelines are very pure. This is high-level practice. If you judge it from the perspective of a low-level practitioner, or from someone outside of practice altogether, you will not be able to accept it.

    Beginners do beginner style. Advanced do advanced style. Transcendent beings do transcendent style. If you see through the illusion of your life, you will be motivated to try. Then you can learn about turning your mind from attachment and toward liberation. If you practice sincerely you will experience the far greater benefit of shared delight — love that is not conditioned by only having your favorite things and people. You will experience greater bliss of stillness that is not dependent on things that come and go. You will have a joy that persists regardless of how things go in external life. This is possible. You can witness it in advanced practitioners and you can experience it for yourself.
