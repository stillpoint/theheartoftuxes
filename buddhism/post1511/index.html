    TheHeartofTuxes

    Wisdom is the pure mind, your innate, original, primordial mind. Compassion is its spontaneous and unhindered expression in relationship.

    If you want to approach it from a pure practice standpoint, you need to get rooted in your original mind. That means, first of all, identifying it. For this you may need a teacher to point it out to you. But if you do correct openhanded (non-grasping, non-making) sitting meditation it will no doubt emerge in you anyway.

    Next, you need to revisit original mind many times, to become rooted in it. Then you begin to trust your original mind, beyond the thoughts and knee-jerk reactions that now control you. You establish stability because you depend on the one thing that does not depend on anything â€” the one thing that is not conditioned, that is not born and doesn't die, that doesn't change according to outer situations.

    Eventually you become effortlessly rooted in original mind, so it is no longer a matter of effortfully returning there. That is mastery.

    But this is pure practice style. It is effective, but it is not necessarily most effective for you at this time. You may need some support in becoming conscious of your patterns in relationship. This may take the form of a relationship counselor or therapist, or it may take the form of a consciousness-building practice like psychodrama, movement practice (5Rhythms, Open Floor, Movement Medicine), or any of a great number of dialog, journalling, or art therapy methods. These help you both to see the pattern and to embody new possibilities. Physical movement is especially important for learning new ways of being. As Jung noted, it is only when a lesson is experienced in the body that it can really be digested and owned. (For instance, in the movement practices noted you get to practice saying and embodying the energy of your clear 'yes' and 'no' in partnership, within a safe environment; so you can see where you need to grow, and you can try out ways to be loud and ways to be clear without being loud.)

    Besides these kinds of specific practices, training in relationship is very transformative. Doing group practice gives you the chance to (A) see strong integrity demonstrated, and absorb that energy, and (B) learn from situations where you have to hold up your end of things, within the context of compassionate but stringent training. Face-to-face training with a teacher, such as in Zen, also leads you to your own deep resource and knowledge of appropriate relationship, and helps you learn to trust it.

    This is not something that gets overthrown all at once. You have to have the momentary awareness that can actually change your way of relating as the scene is unfolding. Case by case you respond from integrity rather than from an idea or set plan. Some people make the mistake of taking on a harsh, unyielding persona in order to compensate for the doormat pattern; but it only alienates others, and ultimately crumbles under pressure. The integral response is momentary, spontaneous, and alive. It happens because you trust your authentic being, not because some tactic has been gripped as the only possible response to every situation.
