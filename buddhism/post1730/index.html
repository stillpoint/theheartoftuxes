    I am struggling with following the Dhamma, and my relationships with people.

    You are becoming more aware of your mind habit. That is beneficial. Now you just have to practice letting go. It's not something that happens overnight.

    You are also discovering that the mind is not easily settled in the midst of a tumultuous situation. You have to practice before these situations arise, to develop the stability to deal with them. When you add moment-to-moment mindfulness throughout the day to your sitting meditation practice, you will develop the ability to avoid many tumultuous and harmful situations altogether.

    >I meditated that night, hoping to clear my mind of any anger and grudges....

    Setting out to force something out of your mind or to get and cling to a certain state of mind is the wrong attitude to take into meditation. Meditation doesn't move toward or away from anything; it is an expression of the mind which remains stable among all realities: anything can appear â€” no problem!

    If you try to force something out of your mind, at that instant you already make an enemy of your mind. So you must not seek any result from meditation; only do it. Only do it. Soon, gradually, the waves will settle, you'll start shedding some attachments, and the mind will calm down. But you don't force the mind to calm down; you only pay attention to the method, not the result or the condition of your mind.

    So don't check your situation or condition. You write that you find yourself upset at things, angry and holding grudges, and stuck in that cycle. So you need to let go of the cycle of attaching and manipulating. You need to practice letting your mind be, as-is. Angry thought, no problem; only follow the method of meditation. Grudge thought, no problem; only follow the method of meditation. Angry thought about the angry thoughts, no problem; only follow the method of meditation.

    If you try to control your mind you will make a war in it. Your job is to be open, stable, and compassionate with your mind. This amounts to letting it do whatever it does, and bringing yourself back to the object of your meditation.

    Do you have a very clear method of meditation? Are you very clear on what you are supposed to be doing with your mind, moment to moment? This is very important. The more clear you are about the form the more help you will have in seeing your tendencies and letting go of them. If the method is not clear, then your blind spots creep in and take over.

    >I am not upset right now, but if I think about it too much, I am.

    That is correct!! You've hit the nail on the head!! Your true mind is not upset. Your thinking generates emotions that are difficult and uncomfortable â€” especially your thinking about yourself. So when thinking about yourself arises, cut it off. In meditation, return to the object of meditation. In daily life, return to the situation your hands or feet are touching and follow it.

    Edit: I want to mention that for people with intense thinking or emotional activity, sometimes even just sitting down to meditate is too difficult at first. If that is the case with you, physical meditation can help tremendously. Doing dozens (and later, hundreds) of full prostrations helps to process the turmoil very quickly. It brings the mind down out of the chaotic head space, and prepares you for deeper meditation. Besides bowing, other full-body vigorous rhythmic exercises can be very good. Running or fast walking or swimming, for example. And free dance is also very wonderful, and can be a safe place where you can express and release a lot of emotional energy so that you can settle down to proper meditation.

    A change in approach and some meticulous care with the method of meditation may be all you need. If after trying my suggestions you still find yourself stuck in cycles, write back and we can look into it. Meditation is not a cure-all; sometimes you may need another course of action.
