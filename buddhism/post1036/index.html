    Often we ask questions when we really want to make a statement. Is there something you want to say? It's okay to let your own wisdom on the matter be heard, by others and by yourself.

    Despite the fact that you can get endless answers from outer sources, don't think that you can actually get any answer from outside sources. They can only point you in a certain direction; they can't resolve the issue for you. So maybe from the beginning, from now, it's best not to make a habit of relying on outside answers.

    If you don't attach to the responses you receive, then the responses can help.

    If someone is taking advantage of you, in what way is it compassionate to let them continue? Who does that help?

    Everyone has to grow up, together. Enabling or encouraging attachment isn't compassion. Avoiding the difficult but right action isn't compassion. Making "don't rock the boat" the main priority isn't compassion.

    But also, there is no rule book. For the sake of training, guidelines exist. But ultimately, there is no guideline. Compassion emerges from the clear heart beyond (or before) selfishness.

    So being compassionate is not a matter of having a list of behaviors that you consult. How exhausting! How limiting! No, being compassionate means allowing your clear heart, your original clear mind, to function. That requires (A) that you can identify it and return to it, and (B) that you can consistently rely on it and get out of its way, not imposing your concept, opinion, preference, or habit on top of it.

    Therefore authentic training and practice are necessary. Training that points you to your own mind, helps you gain faith in it, and shows you how to let it function in everyday life; and practice in actually entrusting yourself to that original mind. This is the inner meaning of 'going for refuge' in Buddhism â€” putting your life in the hands of Buddha, the Buddha-mind, rather than trying to arrange things to gain advantage in Samsara.

    You already know the answers to your questions; but you seek affirmation. You already know that compassion must include everyone, including yourself. In fact, if you are not included, how will it even emerge? If you yourself are not peace and love and clarity, how will peace and love and clarity move through you?

    So it's not so simple as just having a belief and trying to follow it. You have to have the faith to let go of belief, and to trust that your own clear heart functions appropriately and kindly.

    >How kind can you actually be?

    Completely letting go of 'you'. Like branches and wind together as one dance. Like sky and cloud together as one dance.

    We tend to interpret kindness or compassion from our limited, ignorant perspective. So we tend to think of the criteria for kindness as being Samsaric benefits: to feel nice, to prolong the body's life, to get some money or fame or status, and so on. But in Buddhism, kindness is always rooted in awakening, which is the path to freedom from all suffering. Whatever specific kindness we may offer ourselves or others, it must have this ultimate direction of awakening. Otherwise it's just useless effort, just a withdrawal from the karmic bank account.

    Remembering that, we can 'do the hard thing' and hold boundaries that we might otherwise give up on because maintaining them goes against our own habit and attachment. When we cringe because someone else is having a tantrum, or pouting, or wailing, or acting with passive-aggressive behavior, or even when they are threatening our very lives, we can nevertheless hold the boundary because it furthers their awakening.
