    Is complete awakening the same as being one with the supreme eternal transcendent Om that is the source of all reality?

    "Being one with" always strikes me as an unfortunate phrase, in that it assumes a duality — a person, and a reality that they merge with. If you really get that since the beginningless beginning there was never any separation, then the idea of being one with or becoming one with misses the mark somewhat. Only our thinking keeps us separate from that absolute reality, but that separation is an illusion; it is not a real separation. So awakening is the dawning of realization about the illusory nature of our ideas of separation. Upon this kind of awakening, many people will laugh: "Oh, it was right here all the time! It has always been just this!"

    Attaining the Absolute, though, is only part of the way there. It can be seen as an awakening, but is not yet complete awakening. Being one with Om, or attaining the Absolute, means realizing one's true nature or true substance. But that does not yet include function. Complete awakening includes both Absolute and Relative, is not limited to either, and can move freely between both or leave both behind.

    There was a study done some decades ago where the brain waves of various meditators were monitored for their reactions to a repetitive stimulus. (If anyone knows about the study, please help me with the citation.) The stimulus was a clicking sound at regular intervals. Interestingly, the brainwave responses of Hindu yogis showed a propensity to gradually decrease until the clicking sound barely registered a reaction, presumably indicating the absorptive nature of their meditation. That is, the Hindu meditators were aiming at merging with the Absolute, which involved a disengagement from the (illusory) sensory world.

    The brainwave reactions of Zen monks, on the other hand, continued to register; and the more experienced the meditator the less the reaction would diminish. This presumably indicated the Zennists' aim of staying present with the moment, always refreshing their open attention.

    These kinds of interpretations easily fall into generalizations that are not accurate or useful. Although there is some truth to how the approaches differ, when you experience the traditions and practitioners directly you see that it's seldom cut and dry, black and white. Someone devoted to an absorptive practice (I usually say "concentration-style meditation") may retreat from certain aspects of life during meditation but be very engaged at other times. Or their retreat may indicate a vocation; that is, it may just be their job or function to hold the purity of oceanic mind — to embody the Om — for some time, or for some lifetimes. And even practitioners that practice with open awareness (as opposed to concentration-style) may take comfort in settling their minds through concentration, or may use absorptive practices for very beneficial purposes.

    In short, even though it is important to know that various paths and their techniques, aims, and even results may differ, it is also the case that many of those paths and techniques can end up at the same destination; and even if they don't, there can be a good purpose to each nonetheless.

    A hopeful practitioner should know how to distinguish between various paths and techniques, and most of all should be clear on what they are looking for. What's the motive; what are you really on about?

    In support of clarity about the various styles, I'd recommend studying Zen Master Linji's (Jap.: Rinzai) teaching of the four relationships between subject and object (called the Four Ways of Host and Guest or the Four Levels of Host and Guest); and Zen Master Dongshan's (Jap. Tozan) interpretation of the Five Ranks, or five modes of relationship between absolute and relative, emptiness and form. These have been expressed in a more recent and somewhat well-known teaching tool offered by Zen Master Seung Sahn: the Circle of Zen.

    ~

    Donshan's Five Ranks:

    • Form in emptiness
    • Emptiness in form
    • Coming from within the empty
    • Going within both empty and form
    • Arriving within both together
