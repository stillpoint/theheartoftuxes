    Buddhist teaching distinguishes between tanha (craving, grasping at), which is the cause of suffering, and chanda (desire) which is more of a neutral motive force, and can turn toward positive or negative ends.

    In fact, chanda/desire is seen as the root of energy in practice: the fortitude and vibrancy that makes awakening possible, and that expresses compassion and benefit throughout life rather than letting them fritter away as merely nice ideas. So in effect what you're saying is correct: desire is the basis of great achievement.

    The mistake is in clinging to the concepts "my desire" and "my achievement". Humans don't understand their true self; they don't know what they actually are. So although you are right in theory, until you clear up what you really are -- who is the supposed maker and owner of desires, actions, and results -- you are wrong in actuality.

    Originally, neither chanda nor tanha exist. Desire is not real. In order to understand and attain that point, right teaching, training, and practice are necessary.

    >Through his own example and his personal presence and power, we felt this great sense of chanda in practice. I don’t know how many people are familiar with this word, but it’s a vital word to understand. Western presentations of Buddhist teachings have often led to the misconception that because suffering arises out of desire, you shouldn’t desire anything. In fact, the Buddha spoke of two kinds of desire: desire that arises from ignorance and delusion, which is called tanha, craving, and desire that arises from wisdom and intelligence, which is called kusala-chanda, or dhamma-chanda, or most simply chanda. Chanda has a range of meanings, but in this case I’m using it to mean wise and intelligent desire and motivation, which the Buddha stressed as being absolutely fundamental to any progress on the eightfold path.

    > ~ Ajahn Jayasaro, Just Do It, Lion's Roar magazine (from an article titled Skilful Desires in other sources)
