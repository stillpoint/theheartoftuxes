    From what you write, it sounds to me that this is an issue of right timing and right balance. In particular, I get the sense that you need more space, more ease, more warmth and kindness and love.

    Ngondro arose as part of a spiritual technology and culture from a place where these qualities are very strongly woven into life. Space, ease, and loving kindness may even be regarded as tacit foundations. Certainly, Bodhicitta is consistently voiced as the very foundation of all attainments; but in a more subtle way, I think many westerners don't get the same background or basis for practice as Indo-Tibetans do. We tend toward a much, much more intellectual approach; but in actuality, it would in many cases further our cause to establish a more warm and easy approach.

    That's not to say it's not serious or devoted. But the balm of emotional warmth can be panacea for a great many problems that arise for westerners, and especially westerners who deal with trauma in addition to taking on new cultural forms and world views and lifestyle changes for the sake of practice.

    For most others, I'd say that the main key to motivation is digestion of the Four Thoughts -- to encounter them heartfully, not just in a rote manner. But for you and others who burn out from trying too hard, although awareness of death may certainly be an important part of the picture, I think balance of effort is a more predominant dynamic. When the string is wound too tightly, it breaks.

    This also relates to what Shunryu Suzuki called "the gaining idea". When we have a gaining idea in practice, he said, we will encounter disappointment and dejection.

    I want to tell you that it's okay -- that you're okay. Buddha's insight was that we already have it; we are already complete. I want to invite you into relaxation -- to let go of the belief that you have to make something happen. I want to invite you into enjoyment of the moments of your life.

    Maybe after giving yourself a sufficient period of space and enjoyment, things will arrange themselves and you can once again actually enjoy Ngondro, just because it is lovely activity, lovely to be doing with your body, speech, and mind. Maybe after sufficient space and ease you will be able to return to your teacher heartfully and help him/her teach you: "Teacher, in all sincerity, I struggled with your guidance. Here's the problem I encountered, and here's why your instruction didn't work for me.... What can we do to improve this situation?"

    (Sometimes you need to help your teacher teach you, and give them the feedback that helps them become a better teacher for others. Sometimes you need to let go of opinion and listen more deeply.)

    Best wishes. Please remember the Sangha is there for you. And even if individuals can't be there for you perfectly, if they were more perfect at it, they would be.

    Together in warm, soft, comforting compassion.

    Regarding working with injury: some lineages allow dispensation for physical limitation like injury. Consult with your teacher, as you may be able to do modified prostrations or even visualized ones.

    Often people get into a sort of competitive or spiritually materialistic mindset with repetitions. It may be helpful to go much slower, rather than push. Just do this one mantra or prostration, and do it with ease, and do it enjoyably, and be alive with it. Don't fuss with goals to achieve hundreds of thousands, or goals to achieve enlightenment. Don't make ideas of enlightenment, because you can't conceive of it!

    When you're walking in a cathedral forest, you bask in the surroundings and ramble over the landscape; you don't check how many steps closer to the end you are.

    If you choose to do Ngondro because you decide that it is a lovely expression of love, purity, generosity, etc. in its own right, you may decide that you will continue Ngondro for the rest of your life and in future lives. In that case, you can free yourself of the burden of having to count repetitions and check 'progress' as if it were a numerical matter.
