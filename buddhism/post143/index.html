    TheHeartofTuxes

    This is an image of Kuan Yin (Quan Yin in Pinyin Chinese), the Bodhisattva of Compassion in her feminine form, commonly found in China and East Asia. Indian/Tibetan/Nepali representations are usually male, named Avalokitesvara in Sanskrit and Chenresig in Tibetan.

    Kuan Yin's full name is Kuan Shih Yin, which means 'perceive world sound'. It is often translated as 'The One Who Hears The Cries Of The World'; that is, she hears and responds to all the suffering and calls for help. The name also refers to the mind that perceives universal sound, your own pure perceiving mind that doesn't make distinctions. This, too, is the source of compassion.

    Kuan Yin is perhaps the most common and beloved Buddhist deity in East Asia. The sense of motherly kindness, along with the quality of responding to all kinds of suffering without fail makes for an intimate connection.

    If you want to know more about Kuan Yin in Asian folk culture you might like the somewhat autobiographical book Searching For Guan Yin (Companions For The Journey).

    The repetition of Kuan Yin's name is a very prevalent practice in East Asia. "Kuan Shih Yin Pusa" in Chinese, "Kwan Se Um Bosal" in Korean, "Kan Ze On Bosatsu" in Japanese are extremely popular mantras.

    In Indo-Tibetan cultures, the mantra associated with Avalokitesvara is more commonly practiced. This is the very famous "Om Mani Padme Hum".

    A longer recitation practice is the Great Compassion Dharani, which includes many names and qualities of Kuan Yin. It is a longer mantra that is most often chanted aloud in formal practice, but can also be recited silently throughout the day.

    All of these mantras can be used in several ways: in one-pointed concentration on the vocalization and sound; tracing the vocalization and sound back to the source of mind; as a devotional connection to Kuan Yin, receiving her blessings; as a vehicle of benefit for others; or as a background anchor to continuous mindfulness.

    Other practices include generating the visualized presence of Avalokitesvara and unifying with it (a practice that requires initiation by a master/lama), and finding the 'mind that perceives universal sound' by tracing sound to its source (called the 'Kuan Yin method').

    But as you've discovered, one can also view an image of Kuan Yin and very simply bask in its good and boundless qualities of beauty, luminosity, peace, compassion, integrity, presence, etc. With practice, one can then call on these qualities without an external image, and eventually completely unify with the qualities, essentially gaining the same mind and heart as Kuan Yin.

    Since you have such a moving connection with the image, perhaps you have some karma with Kuan Yin, having done practice related to the Bodhisattva in past lives.

    Edit: On further observation I noticed the mudra (hand position), which has an interesting feature. Kuan Yin is often depicted in variations of this raised hand mudra, which symbolizes both bestowing and circulating or communicating. This is appropriate for a figure that is so interactive with us (suffering sentient beings that we are).

    The standing rather than seated posture is somewhat active, ready to respond; but the stance is very peaceful, indicating that the response comes from a serene source. The hand bestowing the blessing is often (perhaps usually) shown with the thumb touching the middle finger, symbolizing compassion. Completely appropriate for Kuan Yin, the Bodhisattva Of Compassion. In this case, however, the thumb touches the ring finger, symbolizing good fortune. It is not that compassion is any less present, but this image emphasizes the aspect of compassion that pro-actively as well as responsively bestows all the things we consider to be of benefit: the basic supports of life, the items addressing practical needs, and the qualities and mind states associated with happiness and fulfillment.

    The other hand, not shown, will often be holding a fruit of compassion or a vase pouring the nectar of that fruit or a sprig of willow sprinkling the nectar. Other possible attributes include a lotus, a rosary, or a fish basket. (Avalokitesvara has many possible attributes, especially in his multi-armed forms.)
