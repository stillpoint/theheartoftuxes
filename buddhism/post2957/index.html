    Questions from a confused teenage Buddhist

    Personally, I can't stand to not be real. It makes me sick. If I try to force my emotions to be something they are not, it makes me sick physically and mentally, and it adds a layer of frustration and self-hatred to the emotions.

    But what I don't have to do is add an ongoing 'story' to emotions. "Oh, poor me. I'm so victimized. Life is against me. ....I hate so-and-so... they deserve to be punished! I'm going to teach them a lesson. Next time I will say this and this and this...."

    I want to be real, and I need to be real. But my inner dialog is not real. This is where people get confused. They think that their thoughts about a situation are the situation itself. So they magnify and tinker with and prolong emotions instead of just feeling them, expressing them directly, and moving on.

    It has been found in studies that emotions last for up to about 90 seconds. That's it! Sad, angry, fearful, whatever â€” only a maximum of a minute and a half. Everything else that seems to be drawn out is actually you revisiting the emotion with your thinking. So this is the difference to understand.

    Yes, if it's time to cry, just cry. How radical! To just be sincerely whatever you are!

    And that's not to say that all sadness requires crying. There are all kinds of intensities and flavors. But any emotion can bring you to your heart, and to connection with others. If it's an angry sense of injustice, it can remind you of the injustice that everyone (even villains) experience and participate in. Rather than separating you in righteous isolation, it can bring a resolve to have courage, to protect others weaker than yourself, and to be impeccable in your own conduct.

    All emotions have a true side, and a 'near enemy'. The near enemy is often mistaken for the true or positive emotion, but is actually negative. The reason it becomes an enemy is that it is based on ego, on self-centeredness. You can find teachings on near-enemies in discourses on the Brahmaviharas ('Divine Abidings' or 'Four Immeasurables'). For example, the far-enemy of love is hatred, but the near-enemy, the thing that looks very similar, is seeking connection for yourself rather than offering generously to others.

    So for instance, when sincere sadness turns into self-pity, it has taken on a self-centered story. When sincere anger at injustice turns into an aggressive, self-righteous indignation, it means it has become more about your own story than about healing the injustice. When plain fear, which can inform you about danger, turns into paranoia, it shows that an inner victim's dialog has taken over. When love moves into a sappy clinging, it is no longer moving out and offering openly to others, but rather trying to gain something for yourself.

    The practice of dealing with emotions is "don't touch". Don't try to do something in particular with them. Just be sincere. Therefore there is no need to have a plan about what you will do if sadness or whatever feeling arises. Just keep a just-now mind.

    When you keep your just-now mind, you know exactly what to do when you are hungry. When you are hungry, you find or make something to eat. And when you are cold, you put on a sweater or get moving. Your just-now mind will know when it's time to be active and when it's time to rest; and it will know when it's time to be firm and when it's time to be soft. Just be sincere in the moment. It is very, very simple; you just have to do it. This is essentially the Buddhist path, all the way to enlightenment. Just don't put anything on top of the simple truth of the moment.

    If you feel the need for more specific healing, expression is very good. Writing, singing, creating art, acting, dancing, and even expressing yourself through activity like sport can be healing.
