    I have a question about Buddhism and love

    Being meticulous about language can help a great deal, not only in understanding these points but in getting free of trouble in your life. The word love is one of the most meaningless words we know, because it is kept intentionally vague by people. A thousand people may have a thousand different interpretations of the meaning of "love", and in general those interpretations are merely comfortable rather than incisive, self-serving rather than complete.

    So if we're going to talk about love, let's be sure we mean the same thing, and let's mean something meaningful. Love is not "for me". The emotion and drama and fantasy and desire we associate with the popular notion of love have nothing to do with real love; they are all part of clinging and grasping — trying to get something for oneself.

    Love is radiant, moving from the inside, out. It doesn't seek to take something from the outside, because in love there is no outside. There is completion, fullness, abundance. In practical terms, love always has enough, so it always offers the best to others.

    Sex is no problem. The motivation to connect, to unify, or to surrender in sex comes from the deeper motivation to awaken — to connect with, merge with, surrender to our original totality of being. It is the desire part that makes us small and broken. When we make desire, we imagine ourselves as something separate and lacking. But that is not true; it is our common perception of things, but it is not true.

    To try to eliminate or shut down desire is just making further division, trying to cast off part of the totality. Buddhism travels in the other direction, against the stream of dividing and grasping. It brings us to our unshakeable center, the perfectly still ground that embraces all events and phenomena.

    Since this unshakeable center is not our common experience, we find it difficult to conceive that this is actually our original and natural being. All we can imagine is that we are small, weak entities facing overwhelming turmoil in the world outside and in our own minds and emotions. But we are not small; we are the whole thing. We are not a wave being thrashed about; we are the ocean.

    Both Buddhism and love bring us to this view. In fact, I would say that real love is more fully and naturally the Dharma than a "Buddhism" that can be studied.

    Understanding love comes from being love. Love can't be understood by talking about it or figuring it out. It is not an amassing of concepts but a surrendering of the heart and mind. It is just another facet of the awakened self, having much in common with humility, patience, generosity, clarity — all of these involve a surrender to reality.

    The real wisdom and beauty emerge when you no longer need to pin it down and figure it out. You radiate within a great mystery. This is Dhyana. In meditation, in practice, you build a greater and greater comfort with the mystery, a greater and greater openness to openness. In that, the puzzle of desire and the wellspring of life and the overflow of kindness all dance together freely although inscrutably, and this may continue for the rest of your life — you may never "get over" or understand desire. How wonderful!

    Doing is understanding; not this crap here.
