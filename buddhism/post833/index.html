    My writing on that point comes from a variety of sources: long experience in spiritual communities, discussions with other students and teachers, experiences teaching others, and maybe most telling, my own experience being that person.

    I was very religious when I was younger; but incongruously, when hanging out with a friend we'd often criticize people, and pose with airs of superiority and aloofness. It was an attempt to pump ourselves up and to hide our extreme distress at being in the world and not knowing how or who to be.

    At the time, I didn't yet have good models of integrity. I hadn't yet met people who had taken vows of correct speech, for instance, who take care not to gossip or not to criticize without doing something to help the situation. Fortunately, I got sick of my own self-deception and stupidity. Later, training with masters and living in community helped weed out some of that ego (though I'm sure many would agree there's still much weeding to be done).

    It is very good to talk about this, to bring our characters and masks to consciousness. Admitting is very good practice. It is part of Shadow Work, in which we start to reclaim the material we've previously tried to hide, disown, or avoid.

    Sometimes you can just note it to the person you're with, when one of your ego characters comes up: "There I go again, trying to look all spiritual." The characters don't control you if you can admit them.

    One skillful technique of admitting is to use the example of another to work through our own karma.

    For instance, when we see an example of stupidity or low-class behavior and we jump to ridicule or condemn it, the speed with which we jump tends to indicate how much we hope to distance ourselves from it. We want that stupidity to be 'over there' in that other person, not in us. So we use our thoughts and words, and sometimes our actions, to pin the negative quality on someone else.

    But if we are mindful enough to catch that fast jump to ridicule or condemnation, we can stop the process of distancing. We can say instead, "Oh, look; there's that part of me." We can take the example of the other person, and live through it, taking back ownership of that part of ourselves. "Yes, I'm stupid... unreasonable... opinionated..." â€” whatever we see out there that we habitually try to avoid admitting.

    It's "there but for the Grace of God go I". And if we do it sincerely, we not only take back our shadow and gain more control over our reactions, we also purify our karma. We can purify our shame through the shame of another. We can purify our anger or fear through another's example. Each time, we become more capable of compassion. Since we own up to more of our flawed humanity, we can be present with it in others and in the world.

    I recommend looking into Shadow Work and Spiritual Bypassing. And, coincidentally, I also recommend reading Chogyam Trungpa's Cutting Through Spiritual Materialism.
