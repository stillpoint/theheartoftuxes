    These phrases are more about (A) returning to the present moment, and (B) maintaining a positive intent; so language doesn't matter. Use whatever language is most comfortable for you.

    In fact, when using a phrase to help you return to presence, even the words don't matter, as long as they don't encourage concepts to be generated in your mind, whether consciously or unconsciously. For example, repeating something nasty or trivial would not be helpful if you attach to the words; but one Zen Master said "Even using the mantra 'Coca Cola, Coca Cola' will work if you believe in it."

    All true meditation has in common one quality: it brings you back to the present moment. Whatever method you use, this is the basic aim — to return to reality, to get out of the thought-made world and into the real world of this moment. Very simple! We usually make it much more complicated than it really is.

    In some meditation traditions, however, mantras have another function; and that (C) is to convey aspects of awakening (some may call it 'divine energy'). The syllables themselves are connected with an enlightening being or some aspect of enlightened mind. So in these cases, being precise with the syllables is more important. If you are imprecise you can still get tremendous benefit through (A) returning to presence and (B) keeping broad, compassionate intent — and you may still attain enlightenment with the method if you are sincere — but you just won't get the benefit if (C) the expression of the energetic or sound aspect of enlightened mind. Mantras originally in Sanskrit, or transliterated from Sanskrit to a close equivalent in other languages are examples of this kind of mantra. Om Mani Padme Hum is perhaps the most famous. It has not only an outer meaning, but an inner meaning and also an effect through the sound-energy.

    OP asked about practices for healing fear, guilt, and sadness. I responded with what came to mind. Really, any sincere practice will be healing. Opening the hands and letting things be is healing. That is the (A) function of mantra, and also of so many other practices: one lets go of the things that limit and separate and agitate, and returns to the plain moment. Mantra helps calm the thinking mind that limits, separates, and agitates. That's where the name 'mantra' comes from: it means 'mind protection". Because you have something to always return to, the mind doesn't follow harmful pathways; it doesn't keep clinging to negative things, it doesn't keep shutting off parts of reality. This happens not because the mantra has some kind of magic, but because you keep applying it; you keep coming back, coming back, coming back.

    If you want to learn more, I suggest you find a capable, living teacher in a traditional path. All Buddhist traditions can lead to enlightenment. Choose one and begin to learn it deeply with a real live human teacher.
