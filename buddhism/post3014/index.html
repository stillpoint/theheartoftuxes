    I want to brighten up the world, but still have five small questions.

    I don't care to go into the debate either. We have to go a little into the issue to understand what is meant by positive and negative in Buddhism.

    I've been a vegetarian, and I've been a carnivore. I can be many things — many forms, many roles. But do I care? That's the main point.

    Do you care that you hurt another being? ...That you pull it away from its family and community, away from its home... that you cause it distress... that you cause it pain... that you end its life?

    Are you on the end of the hook — your heart, your life equal to the being you're killing? Or do you keep yourself separate?

    Many people who kill by hunting or fishing or factory farming or home butchery numb themselves. They live in denial of their actions.

    But one could be very justified in pointing out that vast populations are far more removed from the reality of where their food comes from, and the suffering that is caused by its production. Some hunters, for instance, are much more aware of their relationship with the food that sustains them, because they take it with their own hands. As a society, we are in tremendous denial when it comes to our relationship to the earth. And great suffering is not only caused by the meat industry; the way grains and produce are cultivated, exhausting the earth, spreading poisons, decimating genetic diversity, putting great burdens on farmers, polluting the air through transport — for the vast majority of us, suffering is implicit in our eating.

    On the other hand, there are people who are very conscious of their connection to their food. Whether it is brought to them by others, or whether they take it themselves in a more simple relationship through hunting, fishing, small farming, or wildcrafting, there are those who honor their food and realize with each meal that their life proceeds on the basis of countless other lives.

    So before we look into positive and negative actions and repercussions as if we were balancing an account book, the primary issue is whether we have actual consciousness and care for others, for other living beings. "Thank you for giving me your life. You died so that I could live. May I use this life well."

    Having said all that, in common Buddhist usage, 'positive' and 'negative' refer to either the cause or relief of suffering, or to activity that leads to enlightenment or creates obstacles to it. The two are tied together: suffering and awakening. If you awaken, you end suffering. If you cease to cause suffering, you can awaken.

    Conversely, if you cause suffering, you will bear the results. Your consciousness will be shaped by the act. Your capacity for progress will be diminished. Obstacles will appear. You will have to deal with suffering yourself instead of being at leisure to practice the Dharma.

    It is not that a moral code is imposed, saying that you are bad and must be punished. You can choose! In fact, sometimes you may need to do bad action in order to help someone. But you will still bear the result of your bad action, even if the result is lessened by the situation and by your compassion.

    In Buddhist teaching, there are categories of negative actions that can be purified, the results steered toward positivity; that is, the effort and difficulty of practice can in some ways expunge the karma and change the results. And there are categories of negative actions whose results absolutely cannot be averted. The latter are known as the heinous crimes. (These include killing parents, killing an arhat, with ill will causing a Buddha to bleed, and causing a rift in the Sangha.)

    There are many teachings on killing. It's not a moralistic thing; it's an issue of clarity. At a fundamental level, we kill almost every moment of every day, because we are not yet awakened to our true life. We think of ourselves as our bodies, and so we 'kill' ourselves and others with our ignorance. Ultimately, this ignorance is what allows us to harm others without a care. We don't understand that we are one.
