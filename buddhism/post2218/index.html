    > I am fascinated by the assumption that everyone will automatically have the same associations to a particular sensory experience

    Did I give the impression that I assumed that? Sorry if that's the case; I certainly didn't mean to say that, and I don't think I say that in what I wrote.

    I mean that each sensory experience has a particular, true, quality. You and I may react differently to the taste of a lemon, but nevertheless the lemon has a sour flavor. And the chemical makeup of that sour flavor has particular effects on the chemistry of the body and the experience of the mind. What your particular conditioning does after that first truth hits you is indeterminate.

    Or if your sense organs or brain are not functioning properly your perception may be altered. But the essential quality is still there. The sky is blue. Water is wet. The bell has its bell sound.

    If you've been raped around a campfire or bitten by a fragrant puppy, then we can talk about what your conditioning does to cloud your perception. We are physiologically and genetically predisposed to certain responses, but conditioning does influence us, you are right. That is why we may adapt wisely (like attending a no-fragrances place of practice) rather than holding narrow opinions.

    As to shit: it is indeed a miraculous mysterious expression of anatta, but it's also still shit; and if you have trouble perceiving that, it means that your perception is not functioning in conventional reality. This is one kind of sickness that appears in spiritual practice.

    So you can like what you like; and you can have difficulty with some forms; but let's not disown the fact of our own opinions and conditioning. Or, saying it in the positive way, let's own the fact that we simply don't like something, and let's not project judgments as if they have a real existence, as if something out there is actually 'bad' or 'inferior' or 'wrong' just because we don't like it, or 'good', 'superior', or 'right' just because we like it.

    Skillful means are applied case by case. Sometimes the skillful means will apply to many people â€” billions of people throughout centuries may benefit. Sometimes the application is quite narrow, and only suits a few, or just the one person at whom the means are directed.

    Incense, or any beneficial form, is not the essential thing. Using a mat or chair is a ritual to let go of. Sitting and meditating is a ritual to let go of. Eating and breathing are rituals to let go of. But we choose what to use to help us along, or... just because we love it.

    So while your capping statement is true from one perspective, from another we can also say "Sorry it doesn't work for you, but nevertheless it is a skillful means and your judgment is to be let go of."
