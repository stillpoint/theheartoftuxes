    TheHeartofTuxes

    What do you think 'giving up' your mother means? Where do you think your 'actual journey' is?

    You can be a monk and still love your mother. But whether you are a monk or not, you should be clear that Dharma is central, and important beyond all worldly relationships.

    If you are not able to give up your mother at this point, that is what the training is about. Don't try to jump ahead to a place you're not at yet. With good teaching and good practice you will understand and digest the emptiness of all beings and manifestations. You will gradually attain the basis of Buddha's teaching that nothing whatsoever is worthy of holding on to.

    Waiting for someone to die before committing to practice is self-deception. But by the same token there is much territory to be covered before 'leaving home':

    • Do you have a teacher?

    • Have you cultivated a close and trusting relationship with them?

    • Do you understand practice very clearly, beyond doubt?

    • Have you established a rock-solid practice? Have you done numerous retreats?

    • Have you offered yourself in service to the teacher and community?

    This is all preparation for monkhood. You don't dash into it blindly, with wishful thinking. While you are taking care of these and making progress on the path, you can take care of your mother and you can talk about your wishes. Whether she frees you or you free yourself, you will have to deeply understand the utter aloneness and self-responsibility of every being in the universe. We will all die, for sure. We are all authors of our fate, for sure. This is the bottom line, whether you become a monk or not. This has to be realized.

    By committing clearly to the most important thing you will also liberate your mother. Taking care of her body is a blessed and honorable duty, but it can't save her. You can only truly help your mother, or anyone, if you wake up from the dream of life.

    So where are you actually at? How is your relationship to your teacher, and how is your practice? Deal with the real situation; don't speculate about future possibilities.
