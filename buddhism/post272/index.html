    From a previous post:

    If you're interested in Zen, I recommend finding a living teacher as soon as possible. Zen is "the teaching beyond the scriptures, pointing directly to mind". It is beyond the scriptures, so you can't get it through reading and understanding. But some reading might help you get that (that it's beyond words and concepts).

    ...

    • A very good book that sets out the basic framework of Zen teaching is Getting The Buddha Mind by Zen Master Sheng Yen. It's a series of talks given during a retreat, by one of the great masters of our time.

    • Three Pillars Of Zen by Phillip Kapleau has its issues, but it is well organized and accessible, and is one of the few books on Zen that actually includes guidance on sitting along with inspirational stories. I wouldn't rely on it as a practice manual, but it might serve as an introduction, especially to Japanese style Zen.

    • I haven't read it myself, but I've heard that The Eight Gates Of Zen by John Daido Loori, Roshi is a helpful and complete manual for newcomers, covering many issues one first encounters in practice. I met the late Loori Roshi, and can recommend him as a clear and complete enlightened teacher.

    • No book is better at conveying the spirit of Zen than the classic Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind by Shunryu Suzuki, Roshi. This is one of those books that will keep teaching you even after a hundred readings. (I've read it dozens of times myself.)

    • A very direct, clear teaching is presented in Dropping Ashes On The Buddha by Zen Master Seung Sahn. This book tends to be difficult for those who aren't actually trained in Zen practice, and includes teachings that are quite advanced, but it is one of the purest expressions of Zen I've encountered in contemporary written form. [For an excellent survey of Zen through it's integration of the major vehicles of Buddhist teaching, see Seung Sahn's Compass Of Zen.]

    I recommend avoiding (at least in this context) the very popular The Way Of Zen by Alan Watts, as his writing is quite a bit more interpretive, colored by his own personal philosophy, and comes from the perspective of someone who had contacted Zen when it was still young in the West, and who himself did not have profound training or realization. It's high on inspiration, but misses some important points.

    Also avoid Katsuki Sekida's Zen Training, which teaches a very controlling approach that has been criticized by many teachers and experienced practitioners.

    ~

    These books are all from different styles and lineages. Trying to piece together Zen by reading them all could be a recipe for disaster, as they get at it in quite different ways. Much better to start with just one and let it sink in. And much, much better to actually meet and study with a living teacher.

    Just be mindful that the ego craves thought, while your free mind loves simplicity and Unknowing. When you find yourself craving knowledge, it's time to start practicing by letting go of concepts. Then wisdom — your own innate wisdom — will take its proper place ahead of mere conceptual formations.

    Also remember that you can access many teachers without immediately committing to a course of study with them. You can get an experience of a clear person, you can get a feel for practice, and you can receive some good teaching beyond what you might uncover on your own — all for the little effort it takes to attend a public talk or sitting.

    ~~

    On second glance, I can also recommend Zen Master Robert Aitken's clear and deep writings, such as the excellent Taking The Path Of Zen. And for a perhaps more accessible introduction to Soto Zen than Shunryu Suzuki's writings, consult his Dharma heir Jakusho Kwong, Roshi: No Beginning, No End: The Intimate Heart Of Zen.

    I'll also add that if the reader can reconcile herself or himself to openness (i.e., not demanding to immediately have things defined), the clear and profound teaching of source writings from ancient masters such as Hui Neng, Huang Po, Hui Hai are wonderful when read while training in a proper school. The context of actual practice experience, based on correct teaching that always points to the non-conceptualizing mind, is so essential.
