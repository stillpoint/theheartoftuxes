    Meditation Postures

    You can use supporting cushions or folded or rolled towels or blankets under the knees if it helps to attain a stable and comfortable base. If you do so, try to keep it symmetrical, otherwise imbalances that are already there will get increased. So for instance, place an equal support under the other knee even if you don't need it as much.

    Sitting may hurt after a while; that is usually no problem (and quite a commonly shared experience) if it's not severe enough to disturb your meditation. So in part it depends on your level of concentrative ability. But if there is already pain as soon as you sit down, it means there's already too much burden being placed on the body. This kind of pain leads to damage. You have to be self-responsible and find another way, unless you don't care to use your body in the future.

    Burmese puts the front leg pretty far forward. If you raise the cushion or block you're sitting on so the knees can touch the mat, the hips then get more strained. If the hips are tight, this pressure gets transferred to the knees. So there are sort of diminishing returns at some points in these postures.

    You should try a few sitting sessions devoted solely to figuring out posture and how to support and care for your body. Don't try to figure it out while doing your meditation, or you'll develop a very bad habit of checking your situation when you should be meditating. Devote the time to finding a good solution.

    Try the different postures. Have a lot of firm cushions, folded blankets, kapok bolsters, and rolled towels around so you can use as much support as you need to achieve a stable and comfortable posture. What would you need to do to at least start from an utterly comfortable arrangement? Give yourself that permission and care.

    Also try the chair. Don't be prideful. Don't let prejudice get in the way. Just try.

    There is no one perfect way. Really, no setup will be perfect. You are always changing. Your situation is always changing. Inner and outer conditions are always relating in new ways. So learn to feel rather than trying to fit an image. If you learn to feel how the body is most natural, you will be able to change with change.

    If you think seiza might work well, maybe try a T-shaped cushion setup, with a thick firm cushion on edge and flat firm cushion across the top of it like a bench seat. If it feels comfortable for your joints but unstable, figure out what sort and size of cushion (or bench) would fix that.
