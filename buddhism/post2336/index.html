    There is often unclarity around the word 'ego'. It is given some quite different meanings in different usages.

    There's the psychological ego, as originally described by Freud. This is a theoretical part of the individual mind or psyche, a description of the activity that tries to render the Id's desirous and instinctual impulses in rational ways to benefit the organism. (Psychologists please chime in if I have this wrong!)

    There's the 'spiritual' ego, an idea that arose a century ago with the early advent of Eastern spiritual paths into Western culture. The master Sri Paramahansa Yogananda in particular couched Yogic teaching in Western scientific and psychological terms. The word 'ego' was drastically simplified, and taken to mean the general notion of selfhood â€” the idea that we are small, separate beings. This was called the 'I-thought' by Sri Ramana Maharshi. This use of 'ego' was picked up by Zen writers and teachers early in the last century, and by the Beat Zennists in the '50s.

    And there's the notion of 'ego' as the broad group of selfish character traits, especially pride. This is often what people mean when they say someone 'has a lot of ego'. It refers to one's negative or destructive emotions or thought patterns (roughly corresponding in Buddhism to the 3 Poisons and the 5 Kleshas or Afflictions). At the foundation, these traits are based on the notion of a separate self; but there are skillful ways of dealing with them before one reaches the final overthrow of the illusion of separate selfhood.

    In Buddhism, there are five major negative selfish traits (5 Kleshas/Afflictions, also sometimes called the 5 Poisons):

    • Ignorance (Delusion)
    • Attachment (Desire)
    • Aversion (Aggression, Anger)
    • Pride
    • Envy

    The Buddhist path in all traditions is the cure for these afflictions; but they are said to have specific cures. One knows these cures very intuitively; the difference in one's life comes down to actually applying them:

    • The cure for Ignorance is Insight and Wisdom â€” drop delusive thought and return to where you are

    • The cure for Attachment is Renunciation and Generosity â€” know that everything is impermanent, and open your hands and heart

    • The cure for Aversion/Anger is Acceptance and Lovingkindness â€” realize that we are all suffering, and offer your love

    • The cure for Pride is Humility â€” perceive interdependence, seeing that you depend on all others for your existence, and that even a moment of life is a tremendous gift

    • The cure for Envy is Shared Joy â€” know that you can't gain from someone else's loss, and that you are uplifted through joy for someone else's gain

    If you take a moment when a Klesha begins to arise, you can apply the cure. Mindfulness practice slows down the reactivity of the mind habit so you have a chance to apply your own wisdom from situation to situation: for example, when you see pride arising, you can know it as pride, and let it go.

    Also, the insight and stability that arise from most Buddhist meditation practices cure the 5 Kleshas.

    The Brahmavihara practices (Compassion, Lovingkindness, Equanimity, Shared Joy) also cure the 5 Kleshas.
