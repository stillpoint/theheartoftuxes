    Questions on arousal during shikantaza, fear of annihilation, and koan practice

    A) You need a teacher.

    B) What, if anything, has continued from the time you started writing this post to the present moment? In this duration, are you the same person? In this duration, is there annihilation or not? In what sense is there annihilation; and in what sense is there not?

    C) You need a teacher.

    If you are not clear on the method of koan practice, you may waste effort and even travel down some wrong roads by trying it. So you should learn it very, very well before you try it on your own. Having said that, in the old days sometimes people would travel a long time just to meet a master once; then they would be given a koan or have an experience, and go away for years to digest it on their own. They were left to their own devices. It helped a lot that people were more simple and perhaps more sincere or faithful back then; we have altogether too much understanding and analyzing these days, and that can make a big, big problem for us. Trying to figure out a koan can create all kinds of delusions; and without a teacher you may get more and more entrenched in those delusions without knowing it.

    Koan practice doesn't require a monastic setting; it requires persistent return to Unknowing. You have to have faith in Unknowing; you have to know that nothing you hold on to is it.

    There is not just one koan method; various styles exist. Much of Rinzai koan practice is very driven and concentrated. The encouragement may be to build a big ball of doubt until it sort of explodes under the pressure. This is one way.

    Another way, and in my view a much more pure way, is to simply generate the question once and then let it go, sitting in Unknowing. The question will work at its own level, without need for your mental involvement; and the Unknowing will provide the space for it to unfold.

    At its most basic, koan practice is about being sincere. If you know — if you recognize the knowing reflection deep within you — then you learn to trust that completely. If you don't know, then you learn to trust your not-knowing completely and let it be what it is, thus providing the necessary space from which your wisdom unfolds.

    There are yet other ways.

    How you work on koan in daily practice depends on your style of practice and on your character or bent of nature. Discursive curiosity is a waste of time and effort; seeking entertainment is just another way the mind wanders away from its proper task. But if a true interest arises, a real embodied curiosity, that can indicate where the energy is flowing in your life. For instance, some famous masters already had a natural koan practice despite the kinds of practices they were instructed to follow. Someone might have a profound question about death, for instance, and that might become their life-long practice, to look into that question. It has a lot of energy for them, and they can look into it energetically and sincerely because of that.

    So is it that you are just curious to try koan practice, or is there actually a question that is pressing, vibrating within you?
