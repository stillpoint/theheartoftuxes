    Oh, that's a completely different issue!

    There's an alternative besides either high reactivity or depressed self-censorship. Your issue (as you state it) isn't emotional sincerity; it's knee-jerk reactivity -- acting from unconscious habit rather than clarity.

    There are many strategies for addressing reactivity. Shadow work is very good and important for any adult, and even more so for a spiritual seeker.

    You might also look into Eckhart Tolle's teaching about the pain body and how it triggers reactivity.

    And certainly practicing mindfulness will help you avoid knee-jerk reactions; but I hear that you are not clear on what would then take the place of that reactivity. That's why proper training is important. This is not something that a person can take of themselves for the most part.

    A traditional course of spiritual training will reveal how to marry mindfulness with everyday life situations. There are different approaches, some more buttoned-down and renunciation-based, others more inclusive of all kinds of emotion.

    Zen koan training, for instance, is in large part a training in trusting your immediate wisdom rather than the figuring and manipulating mind. Unfortunately, some people (both outside of and inside of Zen institutions) misconceive the practice as a self-controlling project. This is a danger in almost any meditative path: using meditation to manipulate or hem in the mind rather than finding connection to the faculty of wisdom that can be trusted.

    Another traditional practice, Tantric Vajrayana, works with all emotions to reveal their wisdom aspect (rather than suppressing or avoiding certain 'negative' emotions).

    But I do highly recommend the movement practices I noted in my first response. Get the body flowing, and the mind and heart will follow. If you practice sincerely and consistently, gradually you will develop a broader a and more nuanced vocabulary of expression, and more conscious freedom as opposed to either reactivity or suppression.

    Psychotherapy, in its many forms including vocal dialog, art therapy, psychodrama, etc., is another contemporary option for developing greater emotional ease and flow.
