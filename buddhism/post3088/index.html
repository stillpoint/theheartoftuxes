    Why is there so much mysticism in modern Buddhism?

    Some people see what others don't. Those who don't see that realm may call it 'mysticism', but to those who see it's just another form of the manifest world.

    For example, if you have the faculty of vision you can see a whole realm that someone who is blind can't see. You can see light and dark, magnificence and ugliness... you can see facial expressions and microscopic images and wafting, shimmering light of Aurora Borealis. You might try to explain all this to a blind person, and you might try to establish other proofs of the existence of these things, but because of your ability to perceive you will have the benefit of direct perception.

    Thus it is with the view of devas and Bodhisattvas and pure realms. Some can actually perceive these things, because their perception has been refined, purified of the attachments and other obscurations that prevent us from seeing them.

    In any case, you don't necessarily have to believe in these beings and realms. You can still make use of Dharma. If it is useful to you, you can take them as allegorical truths â€” truths of how our minds work: within our minds there are heavens and hells, there are petty and inferior aspects and boundless enlightened aspects, there is appearance and there is what is beyond appearance. You can honor your own enlightened nature.

    Buddhists don't worship devas. Devas are like us, just in very fortunate, cushy circumstances. Maybe you have a friend who is very wealthy or skilled, and that friend can offer you some kind of assistance from time to time, but in reality they have their own problems to deal with, their own suffering. It is the same with devas.

    A Bodhisattva is different. Bodhisattvas are profoundly wise, compassionate, enlightened beings. When we bow to a Bodhisattva we offer our ignorance, attachment, and negative habit to the enlightened aspect of the world and of ourselves. This is very important, and in fact central: what we really are in essence â€” our innate mind â€” is the same as the Bodhisattva's mind. We are not only honoring the Bodhisattva as a symbol; we are honoring our own true mind, our own essentially awakened nature, and we are making a commitment to give up that which gets in the way of it. This is the meaning of taking refuge in the Truth and making offerings to enlightened beings.

    When you get through a problem in life, you then gain an ability associated with that problem. It is like you often see on Oprah's shows: someone who has survived a very difficult situation then appears in front of millions of people because they are motivated to help them with that kind of situation. Their experience has given them a certain kind of expertise.

    This is a crude example of the Bodhisattva mind: the Bodhisattva is a person like you and me, but who has been liberated. Whatever their particular suffering has been in life, they have moved beyond it to awakening and liberation. So they naturally develop a compassion and commitment toward others: "Having gained wisdom, I offer you wisdom." "Having overcome lust, I offer you purity." "Having overcome suffering, I offer you compassion."

    Of course, all Bodhisattvas have tremendous wisdom and purity and compassion to offer, but the way these qualities are expressed comes out in a particular way for each being.

    Similarly, you have these qualities in you. Wisdom, purity, compassion, dedication, resourcefulness, vow... and so on and so on. All these enlightened qualities are already yours. If you wish, you can honor them and have a closer relationship to them, either symbolically or through their manifestation in great enlightened beings.

    But originally, all beings, including you and all animals, devas, and Bodhisattvas, don't exist. All beings are appearance, and come from mind. The world of appearance comes from mind. This is the foundational knowledge upon which all practices and attainments are built.
