    TheHeartofTuxes

    I use one (a few, actually) for several purposes.

    In the Vajrayana tradition, the three 'roots' are trained together: body, speech, and mind. So when you practice there's usually an action, a mantra or recitation, and a visualization or other mind practice. If you're not using bell and drum or doing prostrations or some other physical action, working a mala can fulfill the 'body' aspect of the practice.

    Next, I use a long mala with counters on it to count the repetitions of various practices. Sometimes you just want to know when you've achieved 21 or 27 or 108 repetitions of certain mantras and other practices; but if you make a commitment to practice a certain number of repetitions (as in Ngondro practice, where repetitions can number into the hundreds of thousands, or millions) a mala can facilitate the counting. Some people use mechanical or electronic counters instead.

    I also use a mala quite often when I'm sick or exhausted, as a support for presence. Having the simple tactile feedback can help you stay in the moment or with your specific practice at times when you might otherwise drift off or find it a struggle to maintain. I'll also often use a mala with or without mantra when I'm watching a movie, riding on public transit, going for a walk, or generally doing activities that occupy part of my mind. It's a way of staying connected without forcing the issue.

    A mala can also be useful when you're distraught or caught in a chaotic situation. The very simple act of sensing one bead at a time can help your mind become simple. Simpe mind is clear mind.

    Finally, some folks wear their mala to remind them of their practice. When they see or feel it, they repeat an inner vow or return to a mindfulness method. A master can also bless a mala, imparting helpful qualities that stay with you when you wear it. Some masters are particularly adept at infusing objects with blessings; though the more you use a practice object with clarity and love in your heart, the more you infuse it with blessings.

    But as I said above, also a mala is not necessary; and if it gets in the way, you can give it to someone else who might do well with it. The main thing is your mind: how do you keep your mind from moment to moment?

    If you're getting rid of Dharma objects, don't throw them out. It's very inauspicious to treat sacred objects as garbage. The traditional way of disposing of sacred images and objects is to burn them in a fire. Sometimes it's also permitted to leave an object in a natural setting, like in a hollow spot or crook of a tree somewhere in a wild setting (not in a public park, for instance).
