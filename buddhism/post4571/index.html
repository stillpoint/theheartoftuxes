    So, I have some questions about the three poisons

    Thank you for the time and care you took with your very detailed reply. You do seem very engaged with the topic; and -- not that I'm the arbiter -- if open questioning is your motive, that's wonderful. I am also skeptical of dogmas, and hope to confirm any views with actual experience.

    I have no argument with much of what you're writing here; and I respect how vigorously you present it.

    > I guess understanding when a sense of self appears would entail a knowledge of exactly what the sense of self is meant to be.

    I'm still looking into that myself. I only find readings and discussions useful if I take to heart the limitation of word and thought. Like you, I trust what I actually experience, so I'm looking into where a sense of self arises from moment to moment. That is, I try to experience 'who', if anything, is hearing, seeing, feeling, thinking, etc.

    Even when reason and experience both confirm the unity of self and experience (or self and world), some sense of a 'me' has persisted; so I continue to look into it. Yes, much of it seems to be patterns of thought; but even when the thinking mind is extremely calm there seem to be other, subtle, constructs. I suppose I would also speak of these as a type of thinking, although they are not word-based.

    Why don't we let each other know what we find?

    > Right, but the core of 'getting out' is deconstructing the sense of self, isn't it? Buddhism then rejects the last part of my story: that the sense of self is not attached to craving and aversion?

    Getting out of suffering may involve deconstructing the sense of self; and it may also involve resting and merging with it. I suppose the deconstruction does in any case happen in a way; but I think it may prove useful for you to keep in mind that it is not necessarily by pursuing ideas that this happens. It's a possible way (viz. some of the analytical methods of Nagarjuna and Tibetan Buddhism); but for many -- most, I'd say -- it's a way fraught with self-defeating effort. Right Thought is one of the spokes of the eightfold path; it's a necessary jumping-off point, but it has to be followed up by discipline and meditative wisdom. (This is a relationship that is observable. You can see for yourself how your ethical behavior affects your mind and heart; and you can see for yourself that correct meditation can penetrate where ideas can't.) When reason is followed up by discipline and meditation, it becomes clear that right thought is letting go of thought, and right view is no view.

    It's truly amazing how deftly the 'self' and its associated thought patterns co-opt even the effort to be free of those patterns. The ego is all too happy to make an intellectual exercise out of it, and take the spotlight away from emptiness. That may be behind the Buddha's famous refusal to discuss certain questions and his warning that certain inquiries are like a net that traps you.

    > Sure, all of those things manifest from our desires, from our senses of self. But so too do our desires for love and intimacy, passion and compassion, and the rest.

    Our desires for love... and the rest may stem from a sense of self; but the love itself may not. Compassion becomes honed through integration of the whole range of human motives, the lofty and the low; but the original mind of compassion is imminent and essential -- everything has it, and it doesn't stem from anywhere.
