    On the other hand, you're greedy enough to withhold your support and be all in a huff about it. So without knowing more about the situation, it's unclear whether the greed is your own projection or whether something untoward is going on.

    In most cases where people bristle about the way things are done, communication is vital. If you talk openly and ask questions sincerely it will be much more helpful than seeking folks to join you in outrage and blame.

    Have you asked why they charge what they do?

    Some temples and practice centers have sliding scale, student discounts, assessment on the basis of financial situation, or work-study options. Some places offer a range of learning and practice opportunities, some of which are open to the public and some of which are accessible only for a fee. If a center can afford to offer free programs, that usually means it has a rich benefactor.

    If the community were generous and wise, the teachers and temple structures and staff would be well taken care of; but in the West this is rare, and people here have a lot to learn about their proper part of the teacher-student relationship. (And if the community were generous and wise, they maybe wouldn't need the teaching so much!)

    Is there actually greed on the part of the temple staff and teachers; or does the necessity for charging a fee originate with the stingy, lazy, privileged attitude of the community? I don't know; but there's more than one side to the story.

    Different approaches to sustaining a temple appear according to the tradition, the culture, the community, the economy, and various other factors. There is not only one way; and to insist it has to be your way may be narrow-minded.

    If we turn around your complaint we might instead ask what you are willing to do for the sake of teaching, for the sake of Dharma. Will you give up going to a few movies? Will you set aside a few hours worth of wages (or get a job in the first place)? Will you be patient and save until you can attend? Will you approach the temple with kindness and openheartedness rather than judgment? Will you not only pay the fee but also offer a personal donation, for the well-being of those leading the practice?

    If we look at it from this perspective, demanding a fee has a function beyond just supporting the temple; it has a teaching function, requiring people to step up and offer something of value for that which is valuable. It may even require some hopeful students to take some steps toward self-mastery before attending a course. It also serves the function of teaching a more proper relationship, where the student takes the first steps, rather than sitting back and expecting everything to be handed on a platter.

    In traditional Buddhist home cultures, there is often quite a remarkably greater sincerity and effort on the behalf of the adherents. They are willing to give very generously of their money, time, talents, and physical effort. It is often very clearly understood that a teacher is a revered vehicle of Dharma, and should never have to ask for the things she/he needs to live and teach. In the West, on the other hand, people are often blissfully unaware of how much is actually being given by the teacher. The way teachers are treated here, like they're just part of the crowd and should be fending for themselves, could often be considered shameful in the East. And yet they show up out of compassion and patience, for our sakes.

    Instead of saying "yeah, I want to learn without spending a lot", maybe consider what it would be like to say "I actually want to devote all I can not only to my own learning, but to supporting the Dharma and helping spread it to others". It's quite a different perspective.
