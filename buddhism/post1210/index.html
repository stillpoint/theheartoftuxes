    Why do some Buddhist monks sleep in a lotus position?

    Your original nature — your true heart, if you want to call it that — is completely awake, responsive, and energetic. As you progress in practice, your true heart is revealed more and more, and its aspiration is also naturally revealed. So people who have done sincere practice for a long time often have aspiration, a spiritual drive, that seems unusual or unreachable for many of us. For them, though, it is quite natural; it has come through a gradual process of unfolding.

    The same aspiration that brings you here to ask a question may at some point flower into a fierce motivation to awaken. You may realize that your time in this life is very limited, and you may want to devote every possible moment and effort to awakening. You may not necessarily lament time "lost" to sleep, but you may no longer want to assume that your practice has to stop when night comes.

    So it has happened that many practitioners adopt various kinds of practices to maximize their practice around sleep. There are various methods of practicing within sleep and dreams, and there are various forms that reject sleep altogether. When the mind is in deep concentration, it is very still. This is deeply restful in a way, and can reduce the need for common levels of sleeping (although certain aspects of mind and body may still experience the effects of lack of sleep).

    In addition, when the mind is ungrasped it is not pulled around by life. So there is less agitation and wounding that needs to be processed by healing dreams.

    These aspirations have become traditional forms in some schools of meditation. What originally came from one person's sincere motivation was witnessed by others, who were then inspired to try the same. In some cases, the form has overtaken the motivation: although the monk or lay practitioner gives a sincere effort, they may be following tradition more than expressing something that moves through them naturally. This is of course an issue with all traditional forms; the form may show a good way in general, but may or may not be the best way for someone in particular.

    In any case, the one form of sitting in lotus position during "sleep time" itself has different practices associated with it. For some it is a way of establishing and continuing a certain energetic pattern within the bodymind. For others, it is merely the best way to keep (awake) practicing despite the desire to sleep, since it offers the most stable upright posture.

    As someone who has done his share of long retreats and late night practice, I can tell you that you can often think you are practicing when in fact part of the mind has shut down and started to dream. People often mistake a calm thoughtless state for practice when in fact it may be a dull, numb form of thinking or dreaming. That the experience lacks distinguishing landmarks doesn't mean it's not sleeping.

    By the same token, I've seen monks who seldom or never lie down to sleep, and the jury is out on what actually happens when their heads nod as they sit in lotus posture. None of our business! How are you keeping your mind right now? That is the essential point here. Moment to moment, what is your relationship to this real life?
