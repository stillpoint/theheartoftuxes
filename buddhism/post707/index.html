    About "devotion" and forcing yourself to endure practice

    >From someone who experienced severe panic attacks...

    Perhaps a more sophisticated and to-the-point teaching is to practice according to your ability. You've presented one side of the issue, and admittedly from the perspective of panic. Now, what's the other side?

    The other side is that relative physical and mental health are not the final word, not the final aim... and that we cling tremendously to our status quo... and that an effort beyond our common state and beyond our comfort zone is required. This doesn't have to mean self-harm, but it often does mean facing fears and attachments and passing beyond them.

    Instead of saying "don't do what you don't feel like doing", which can be taken as a blanket permission to indulge in any petty, ignoble behavior, it might be more skillful to discriminate between needs and wants, or between issues of health and issues of the ego's attachment.

    The ego is insidious and sly and desperate. Self-concern and pride can arise even among experienced practitioners. Left to our own devices we almost invariably follow our own preference, our own ignorance.

    That is why, in our wiser moments, we choose an external structure to surrender to. (The surrender is necessary.) We choose a setting, a schedule, and a course of training that help us maintain activity beyond the ego, beyond our old ingrained habits and preferences. If this structure didn't sometimes feel like something is being imposed on us, then it's not really getting at the ego.

    Simply put, when we're are required to go beyond the illusion of self, that illusion rebels and fights for its survival. But that reaction, that discomfort of the ego, is a good sign, a sign that we are indeed moving toward a greater freedom.
