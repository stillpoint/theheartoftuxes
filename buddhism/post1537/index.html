    > But if I have them present in my thoughts I can't mediate either.

    This is an interesting and significant point.

    Often we think there is something more valuable to do with our time and energy — and in particular, with our mind — than let go and participate fully in meditation. This shows a fundamental lack of trust not only in meditation but in the world's unfolding, in life. We believe we have to intervene with our concerns and best ideas.

    But in Buddhism, we don't go for refuge to our concerns and ideas; we go for refuge to the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha. It is not our conditioned and reactive mind that heals the world and relieves suffering, it is our Buddha, our awakened and compassionate nature. Therefore we practice returning to our awakened and compassionate nature, which exists before concerns and plans appear. We go to the before-thinking mind for refuge.

    Meditation must proceed without doubt. That is what the structure of the method is for: to show us when we're going off, when we're doubting the Great One and replacing it with our own concept and strategy. At that moment, the form of meditation shows us that we've wandered. We are no longer with the mantra or koan or breath counting or open space (or whatever the form of the method is), we are pursuing our own idea. The moment we notice that, it is our job to take refuge in the right thing — pure unknowing, ungrasping awareness.

    It is very, very tempting to follow our emotional reactions, especially when we think of them as noble or altruistic. But the real nobility is unwavering peace and awakening. This is a very important point for meditators, because we will often encounter things we think of as so important, even urgent, during meditation. We will start to figure out relationship problems or how to fix some big problem at work. We might have spiritual visions or wonder if another kind of practice wouldn't enlighten us faster or better. Insight means realizing that all manifestations are empty. Even the seemingly most important thoughts are just thoughts. They arise from a confluence of causes, and dissipate from the disassociation of causes, like clouds that come and go. The important point is to keep the unmoving center, the spacious, aware, before-thinking mind. This is actually going to Buddha for refuge. It's placing trust in the right thing.

    We know from reading and study that anatta (non-self) is a keystone teaching in Buddhadharma. No being or thing is a being or thing unto itself; all come from interdependence. That means everyone and every thing and every event is empty of separate selfhood. We apply our compassion in that context, in the context of wisdom, not in the context of delusion. That allows us to wield compassion grounded in ultimate reality, without desperation and reactivity.

    It is said that the Bodhisattva never for a moment falls into the delusion that sentient beings exist. But also, the Bodhisattva never for a moment ceases to respond to the suffering of all sentient beings.

    Both sides are necessary. If you try to respond compassionately but are stuck in a world of beings, that is delusion. If you recognize emptiness but fail to respond to beings, that is also delusion. (It is a famous stumbling block for many meditators: they get a taste of emptiness, which is truth; but they abandon correct relationship and can't function compassionately. This is called 'attachment to emptiness' or 'knowing One but not knowing Two'.)

    Don't attach to feeling. Don't put feeling or thinking about feeling in the driver's seat. The driver's seat belongs to wisdom: the before-thinking mind. Let your wisdom-mind, your Buddha, do it. Then you can do all kinds of compassionate activity; but it will be grounded in reality, not chasing after ghosts.

    The most compassionate and effective people I know are masters. These great beings never depart from their before-thinking wisdom, not even for a moment. As a result, they are fountains of benefit every moment of the day, in every activity. Teaching, they save the world. Working on community projects, they save the world. Responding spontaneously to newcomers, they save the world. Taking a shit, they save the world.
