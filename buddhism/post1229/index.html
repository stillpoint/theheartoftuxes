    Xu Yun was a great Zen Master. Very good source material.

    It's important to know the center of practice, which is Unknowing. This is very open. It is like when you are completely taken aback by something for a moment: your mouth opens but you can't speak; your mind can't move in any direction, it can't find anything to grasp. Usually we think of this as wrong, and to be avoided or overcome, and so we seek an 'answer' -- something to have in hand. Our society, our education, tells us we have to acquire knowledge; empty space is seen as an enemy. But actually, this Unknowing is the source of wisdom. This is where we rest in meditation.

    So, while we do eagerly and energetically seek out "who am I?" or "what am I?" or "where am I?", we shouldn't expect to find a thing or get an answer.

    Actually, the asking/Unknowing and the true nature that is seeks are the same, not separate. The question of the hwadu and the place where the question points are the same.

    Asking "who hears" and "who thinks" are essentially the same question. The issue is not to fixate on the object but to seek the subject. The object is the sound or thought. The subject is the 'one' who hears or thinks. So the hwadu is for turning your awareness back to the source of awareness. Don't get caught up in words.

    This, of course, is problematic from a logical standpoint because it's like an eyeball trying to see itself. Impossible! So in practicing hwadu, we soon reach a point where we can go no further: there's nothing to gain hold of. When you reach that that point, let it be. Rest there. It may last a second or so; or it may remain for a minute. No need to regenerate thought when you have before-thought.

    "You can't know it, you can only be it."

    When the mind becomes active again, creating the illusion of separate subject and object, you can call on the hwadu to point you back to Unknowing.

    The timing differs according to instructions. Some teachers may say you only use the question of the hwadu once in a while; others might have you constantly repeating it, getting into a kind of rhythm; or you might use it in concert with your breath: (in-breath) "who am Iiiiii...." (out-breath) "don't knowwwwwwww...." Maybe you can find your own pacing. But choose one and stick with it for a while instead of always changing. It's not that you can't change, but if you allow yourself to do it frequently it invites the checking, doubting mind to take over, when instead you should be proceeding with confidence.

    Coincidence is part of the practice. Not only is great Unknowing doubt necessary, but also great Knowing faith: the faith in your own innate awakened nature and the fact that it is most real, most 'you'.

    Confidently maintain Unknowing. Open, unmoving, ungrasping. Look for self, but don't expect to have something to hold onto or do with your mind. This is the word-head, the mind before thinking arises. In some traditions, it is called Dharmakaya, or Original Mind, or Primordial Mind.

    One final note: a lot of the difficulty or interruption in hwadu comes from restlessness. The agitated, unfocused mind finds things to do, problems to make where there originally are none. So many practitioners start sittings with some calming, focusing, concentration practice for the first few minutes. Perhaps using the breath: the in-breath to consolidate and arrive in presence; the out-breath to let go and relax into emptiness. Also, as above, since will use these quantities of breath constantly in with hwadu. This is also why some training paths start by first clarifying life direction (great vow), which forms the basis for everything else, then building the concentration, before moving on to hwadu (though these trainings can often overlap as well).

    And then again, if a practitioner is confident with hwadu and using it continuously through the day, the simplicity of just regenerating questioning once in a while to cut off thinking may suffice.

    Furthermore, physical practice is a tremendous support and aid to hwadu. And in fact all the traditional devotional practices, rituals, vows, physical practices like bowing, walking, and working, and other broad practice guidelines all support the hwadu.
