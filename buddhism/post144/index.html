    You learn this better through in-person training and relationship than through shotgun-splatter advice on an online forum. Each tradition has its own structure of teachings and practices, which make sense with each other and within the full context of the training. Without the context, random suggestions — though well-intentioned and beneficial in themselves — could prove to be confusing and ungrounded.

    Your comment may betray a self-fixated mentality, which a lot of meditators unfortunately fall into. Rather than being gradually transformed through contact with something that can take them beyond themselves — transcendent teaching, an awakened teacher, a clear community, a proven and established course of training — the meditation becomes co-opted by the same old 'I, my, me' mindset. So take care that you are not using practice to try to manipulate and control your mind. That's not the kind of 'control' we look for in practice. We control by letting be.

    In other words, there's a danger in always checking your condition and situation to see what your mind is like and how you're doing, and then trying to make it all a certain way. Emptiness mind is non-checking. Only doing. The drama of the self is cut off.

    >A wide pasture for the horse.

    When a horse is always tied, it will naturally look for opportunities to roam; but when it is provided with a wide pasture, it will naturally settle down, content to sometimes frolic and sometimes rest, but all within its home pasture.

    So the general practice is to maintain the breadth and openness of emptiness, moment to moment throughout the day. As your mind is non-attaching in this mode, there is no incorrect relationship — if you can trust what is reflected in your mind. Sometimes peace and calm will be reflected; but sometimes very clear or even fierce action may be reflected, as the appropriate response to the situation facing you. It doesn't come from thinking; it doesn't come from pre-arranging or strategizing; it emerges in the moment, from the emptiness.

    This, then, is the training: to maintain that empty point, and to trust what appears. Who can do that, based on their own individual effort? If someone can, that's wonderful; more power to them! But most of us need training, i.e., external support, in order to carry this out. We need to have the emptiness mind pointed out so we can identify it. We need support in ongoing practice so we can develop our contact with it. We need both support and testing in order to trust it more and more. And we need the context of guidance and example and face-to-face communication to understand how it is applied in life situations.

    ~

    As I said above, each tradition will have its own suggested methods. You may find some good tips among other comments here. Sometimes these are also offered in online blogs and e-mag articles ("How to deal with anger", etc.). If you can take one method and make good use of it, good for you. But in my experience the people who actually have changed their lives significantly have committed to a course of training with a good teacher and community.
