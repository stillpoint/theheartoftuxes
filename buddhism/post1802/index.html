    TheHeartofTuxes

    Approaches to eating vary, from rules that govern much of waking life, to specific mindfulness practices, to a single all-pervading practice of non-attachment/clear mind.

    The aim in all of them is to go beyond preoccupation; there are just different ways to do that.

    Most people are very attached to form, and don't even realize the degree of their attachment. It is unconscious attachment. Because of their deeply attached mind, they can only imagine spiritual life in terms of dependence on form. Some crave structure, some reject it; but it's all attachment. Since you are so attached to the realm of form (in this case, to food) you think preoccupation depends on the outer form — on the availability of food. But if you realize that mind is primary you will know that outer form doesn't matter: little food, much food, no food — no outer situation has the power to move the unattached mind.

    For the monastic, every aspect of the day is a kind of training. There's no 'off time'. Sitting or walking in meditation is only one small part of the training. How to wake up, how to wash, how to prepare, how to work, how to eat, how to rest, how to communicate, how to learn, how to worship, how to teach, and how to sleep are all part of the training, whether it's overt and rule-based or whether it's subsumed in one all-embracing practice. The mindfulness of how to live, and why, are brought to life in every action. That is the practice of the monk's life.

    And there are many food-related issues beyond mind training. There are many broader ethical and practical reasons for being mindful of eating. Both over-indulgence and asceticism dull the mind. Most of us are habituated to indulgence, so we can't imagine what an efficient diet looks like. But each tradition has pared its relationship between spiritual practices and eating down to a science. The relationship between diet and mental state is known very well, in great detail. For those who practice in a very sedate and solitary manner, one meal a day suffices. And if the practitioner doesn't engage his or her picking-and-choosing mind in the process (instead, receiving food by alms-giving), so much the better. For those who do much 'karma yoga' — beneficial activity— and who work for and with many others, more food is necessary to fuel those efforts. Each tradition has its own style of eating, which harmonizes with its aims and methods.

    We also offer compassion through our eating, being mindful that we are borrowing life, minimizing our impact on the environment, avoiding killing and causing suffering to animals, avoiding taking advantage of farmers and factory workers, being frugal with temple or community resources, and ultimately respecting every animate and inanimate object as Buddha. Even in traditions that allow the consumption of meat (because of the climatic and geographic realities, and sometimes because of the tradition's non-discriminating practice), practitioners still act with compassion, taking care to minimize their consumption and make the best use of it.

    In a sense, all of the spiritual path is about eating. Everything 'eats' everything else; we are all interconnected, with nothing and no-one standing separate. So the great master Maha Ghosananda said that knowing how to eat is the key to spirituality.
