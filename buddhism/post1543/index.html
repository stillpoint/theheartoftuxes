    Every quality has a positive side and a negative side. When expressed through self-centered mindset, a quality appears as hindrance and harm; but when expressed through a mind free of self-clinging, the same quality appears as wisdom and benefit.

    Self-cherishing stubbornness may seem intractable; but that very same character trait can become great spiritual determination, stability, faithfulness, and unfailing protection of others. It's not the quality itself that is the issue, it's how the central mind is wielded.

    I think you are right that compassion and appreciation would be tremendously helpful. This is true in all cases; and I don't think we're in danger of overstating it. But if your friend wants to change, she needs to invite change herself. That's the first point. She needs to take steps toward Dharma, to invite teaching, invite blessing, invite beneficial change in some way. Each person will have their own gateway; we don't know what hers will be, but she needs to come to the gate herself, to call out or invite or take a step in some way.

    The second point is that although you can be a supportive friend, you are not the guide of her life; you are the guide of your life. Ultimately, if you want to help guide her mind or anyone's mind, you have to be clear. So your own practice is very important. It is paramount. When you are practicing consistently and sincerely, your mind's light will shine without you having to do anything in particular. Your light will shine, and if your friend is ready she will see it and it will help her. We don't yet know how.

    I feel we should acknowledge that there are some character types that get the short end of the stick. Nicey-nice, sweet, gentle, accomodating types are often considered preferable, and treated that way. Never mind that there may also be a dark side to this trait — its lack of clarity, its smarmy, wishy-washy nature, its absence of passion — we find it more comfortable, so we support it.

    But when someone more rigorous or challenging appears, our own ego takes offense. Really they may have a very good influence on us, bringing attention to our own weak boundaries for instance; but because they are not comfortable for us, we will usually treat them as somehow flawed or inferior, and we may even make up stories about the supposed problems they have. And if we're the person with that uncomfortable trait, we may believe what others communicate about it. We may think of ourselves as flawed. The truth in interpersonal situations is that things are seldom all one way; there is almost always positive and negative together, and that is our shared human situation.

    People with fierce energy often tend to be ostracized, but for a practitioner's mind that energy is a benefit. We should actually embrace those with strong and cutting qualities, so that our lingering mess might finally get shaken or cut off.

    The question behind it all is what is your life for? Until that is clear, all of these issues will just go around and around. Although Buddhadharma says that our job is to awaken and to save all beings from suffering, until that becomes very real and embodied in us we're just reacting to life rather than moving strongly from our own center. Perhaps if your stubborn (or is she?) friend finds her correct direction in life, the stubbornness will manifest as unstoppable drive toward liberation for all.

    If you practice well and keep your own mind clear and stable, then you can help your friend. It's not really for us to judge whether someone's traits are good or bad; it's for us to make benefit of all of it.

    For your friend, I might just say "If you truly want peace, you could have it in this moment. You already know what you need to let go of to achieve peace in this moment. Whether you will make that trade or hold on is another question."
