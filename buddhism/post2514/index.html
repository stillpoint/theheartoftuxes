    Proper Speech

    If you try to imagine being good and then try to make yourself into that image, you will only trample your natural and appropriate responses. Rather than trying to bring something into the situation that you've already figured out or decided upon, practice showing up open-handed. Keep showing up for the new moment without knowing what is going to happen. Your motivation to show up will help bring out your innate goodness, and your presence will help keep things from getting out of hand. Even if you do make a mistake, you can soon make it correct: apologize, speak with sincerity, and bring the situation to its correct balance. Presence will help you accomplish this.

    People who look to develop the spiritual side of life very often put too much emphasis on appearing spiritual or trying to "be good". This handcuffs them. Because of the ego characters (self-images) they develop, they are often unable to relate to others with vulnerability or sincerity. It is extremely important to be able to apologize quickly or do whatever appropriate action is necessary to correct one's mistakes. We are not yet Buddhas, so we are not yet perfect in our view and behavior. We are human, with human nobility and human pettiness. It is very important to be able to say "Whoa, sorry -- I didn't mean that." A simple, sincere comment can defuse an entire drama before it starts. Take an example from guys on the basketball court: when a mature player makes a mistake, he quickly calls out "my bad!" When it is said quickly and sincerely, it already includes compassion for oneself. "Well, I'm not perfect here, but I'm trying!"

    A good mindfulness technique — a way to keep returning to presence — is to feel your feet on the ground. Your body is your best grounding influence. When you are asleep and your mind is disconnected from your body, your mind floats here and there; but when you are aware of your body, you can be conscious and in command of your responses. So moment to moment, feel your feet on the ground. Feel the sensations when walking; feel the weight of your body on your feet when standing; feel the contact of your feet on the floor and your butt on the seat when sitting; and feel the contact of your body on the bed or sofa when lying down. This can become a sort of background anchor. At first it may take more effort, but soon you can devote, say, 20% of your awareness to the anchoring effect of the feet, and then you will be very present for whatever situation you are in, and you can trust your responses.

    Keep in mind that this is a practice, so it will require your motivation and repeated effort. If it doesn't work perfectly, you must let your expectation go. Have a flexible mind. It doesn't help at all to blame the method or to blame yourself. If you make a mistake, just correct it and move on without blaming the practice, without blaming yourself, and without blaming the situation.

    Daily or several-times-daily reconnection with intent will help. You can state your intention to use proper speech for the sake of all others. This wakes up your motivation, keeps it active, and helps you to be more aware of your actions.

    Meditation and spiritual training with others will help you to become more natural and trusting of your own innate mind and its activity. You can learn how to depend on Unknowing (the momentary, reflective quality of your true mind) rather than on conditioning, habit, opinion, concept, prejudice, or strategizing.

    A specific way of purifying the speech is practicing mantra and chanting. A mantra such as the famous om mani padme hum or a dharani — a longer mantra — like the Great Compassion Dharani can be practiced formally and informally, for set durations or numbers of repetitions, or continuously throughout the day (and, silently, even into sleep).

    A mantra specifically for purifying what goes in and out of the mouth is suri suri maha suri susuri sabaha. Repeating that throughout the day, with the intent to help all beings through your speech, will help you.

    Finally, silence is golden; and actions speak louder than words.
