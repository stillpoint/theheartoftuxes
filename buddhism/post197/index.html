    New to Buddhism, struggling to not "want"

    You know how to let go. It's a matter of doing it just once.

    That doesn't mean what we usually think it means. Usually we think of getting rid of something we don't want; so "letting go once" seems to mean that we find the linchpin for everything and get rid of wanting in one fell swoop. But that's not what letting go once means.

    Letting go once means: be momentary. Just now, you know how to drop something. That it may come back in the future doesn't matter. What matters is your relationship to the moment. Just now, drop it.

    Let's assume that wanting will come back in the future. It may even come back in the next split-second. That's none of your business. The future, in this sense, is none of your business.

    So this makes it simple and easy. You don't have to figure out the secret mechanism of your wanting and destroy it all like Luke Skywalker destroying the Death Star. You don't have to unload a past lifetime of wanting. You don't have to prevent wanting that may extend into the future. You don't have to deal with a month's worth, or a day's worth, or a half-minute's worth. You only have to deal with a moment's worth.

    Only keep your correct relationship with the moment, and your life will go well. Little by little things will get clarified by themselves.

    Next, if you drop everything in a moment and return to equanimity and openhandedness, your way will appear by itself. When you are wanting some imagined possibility you are not dealing with reality. But when you are open, life will still be moving in you. Your interests, curiosity, aspirations, yearnings are the voices of your life. They are expressions of where your life is moving. There's no need to ignore or squash them. Beyond giving you good information about yourself, they in fact are already moving you somewhere.

    So you already understand that attaching to an image of the future is suffering. But you can acknowledge the things that light up your life; it is okay to have needs and aspirations, and to address them. If you look into it with this in mind, you may determine, for example, that it's not necessarily about that particular beach but maybe that being close to nature is important to you, and that it's not necessarily about that particular residence and surroundings (brewery) but that maybe you need a certain balance of space for work and introspection, and social contact for nourishment. I'm sure you're clever enough to drill down and get to the needs, values, and inner movements behind the wanting.

    Maybe at some point you discover that it is about one particular situation. When you have dropped everything in a moment — when you have returned to equanimity and open-handedness and have seen the situation clearly — it's possible that you will identify something as very valuable, something connected to the central issue in your life. If that is the case, then you will know what you have to do: make every effort to attain it. I don't think this is one of those cases (and I don't think you think it is).

    If you put everything down in a moment, the movements within you and outside of you will express themselves clearly.

    Feeling your inner movements helps you become aware of the goals and values that are important to you. Then you can let go of specific outcomes, because you have guiding principles to follow as the situation changes.

    All of these guiding principles have one central principle. Keep that one principle in its proper place, at the center of your life, and things will go well.

    So two main points for dealing with wanting:

    • Keep the central thing central.

    • Stay momentary.

    You can trust your innate wisdom and abilities to take care of things as they arise.
