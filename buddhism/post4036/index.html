    Can anyone explain the Mahayana understanding of emptiness in Buddhism?

    If you look out your window you will see the best explanation of emptiness. If you fall in love you will experience the best explanation of emptiness. If you try to find the one who sees and the one who falls in love, emptiness will emerge by itself.

    But seeking it through explanation is inherently problematic, because the very nature of explanation presumes that there is some fixed, identifiable thing to be conveyed.

    Things are only things because you think they are things. We agree amongst ourselves that there is a world of things, so we go along as if this were true: “that's a tree, that's a road, that's a house, that's Robert, that's the sky...." But everything we can point to its made of and conditioned by other things, and is in a constantly changing relationship with them -- molecules are exchanged, forms shift, perceptions move. Only our belief in the constancy of our words and thoughts -- our labels for things -- causes us to live in a world of defined forms.

    So things are aswabhava: they have no self-being; they are empty of themselves. There is no world of things, except what you make with your thinking.

    Next, what is the substance of this non-thing world, what is this life made of? Every phenomenon, every being and event in your life, appears in your own mind, as your own mind. If you no longer make an assumption of a world of things, you can perceive this clearly. In fact, this is meditation: clear perceiving. Sound does not appear “out there", vision does not appear “out there", and so on. The place and material of all of these is mind.

    But this mind itself doesn't have a form. It is open, boundless, ungraspable, and unmarked by the things that appear in it. It is also perfectly clear, luminous, and aware. So it is called “empty". But perhaps a more useful translation of sunyata would be “boundless". The description of it, though, can't help you. Only attaining boundless mind can help you understand, overcome suffering, and save all beings. So we do the practice of ungrasping the empty world and letting the mind return to itself, to its original bright, boundless nature.
