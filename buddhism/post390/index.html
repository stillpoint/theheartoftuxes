    How can suffering truly end and existence be impermanent? (and other questions?)

    Today there is a big blizzard in the northeast. So I make a big snowball and throw it at your head by surprise. Have you had that experience? You turn around and POW. At that moment, there is no suffering, because there is no you, no world outside of you, no condition you're in. There is only POW.

    So non-suffering practice is, in a sense, letting the moment hit you deeply so that you lose the illusion of a separate, suffering self. You have already likely dropped it momentarily many times — when laughing uproariously, when first smelling an unexpected fragrance, when surprised by a barking dog, when momentarily disappearing in someone's eyes — but you also likely do not have control of that faculty; you can't yet make it last long.

    Your mind makes the world. People think that the world is out there and their mind is inside of it; but the world is in your mind and comes from your mind. This includes all beings. When you're falling in love, the world is beautiful; but when you've been abused by your boss, the world is horrible. Same world! It changes because of your mind. It is not enough to just think that there is no self. To say there is no self or there is a self are both incorrect. But when you have seen through what "self" really is, then you see the nature of the whole world clearly and you understand what it is that suffers. When you no longer populate the world with illusory beings, then you have made the whole world free.

    It is said that the Bodhisattva never for a moment labors under the illusion that suffering beings exist; and yet the Bodhisattva never for a moment fails to respond to suffering beings. There is a movement in your heart that responds with appropriateness and care, without thinking of separate beings. When, without thinking, you are moved to help someone who has fallen, that is your Bodhisattva heart in action. It also has that quality of POW — no self and other, and also no failure to respond. So how can you reveal that, let that emerge completely?

    These questions can't be resolved by thinking-understanding. You actually have to see and attain for yourself. The nature of thought is to limit and pin down meaning; but the truth is limitless and flowing, and can't be grasped by thought. Doing is the real understanding.

    The precepts both reflect and approach the enlightened mind. In the enlightened mind, there is no intoxication, no separation, no lack of awareness, no confusion. So "no intoxicants" is a precept that reflects that aspect of enlightenment. And for the person aspiring to embody the Dharma, vowing not to participate in unclarity, unawareness, and confusion is part of the path toward embodying the Dharma completely.

    I think you want to understand before you proceed. That is usually not possible. And anyway, if you only proceed once you understand there will be no opportunity to activate your trust in yourself. You will be stuck clinging to the things you always have clung to, chief among which are your opinions. Do you know the danger of having any opinion? You can't gather the correct opinion. Truth is revealed by itself when you let go of your opinions; as long as you hold them, you block the Truth from being revealed. So don't try to gather more evidence to show that you should proceed. You only need to trust yourself.
