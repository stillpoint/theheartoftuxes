    >Before my realization or awakening I used to....

    Is there some sort of virus going around? It seems everyone and his brother is having realization experiences.

    What you're describing is not equanimity, it's arrogance. In your post there are 38 instances of "I", "me", "my", or "myself". Whatever you call your 'realization' has numbed and isolated you rather than bringing you into greater engagement. It's not oneness, it's attachment to an idea of oneness.

    You are not superior to others because you hold a wide view. Wide is not better; wide is just wide. Sometimes wide is appropriate, and sometimes narrow is appropriate. Sometimes you need to see the way over the mountains, and sometimes you need to sign on the dotted line, or add a quarter teaspoon of salt to the soup, or report your child's temperature to the doctor.

    Things don't become meaningless for a realized being. To the contrary; previously meaningless things are valued as worthy of complete attention. You may be going through a fairly common phase in practice, a malaise that can arise when you begin to understand conceptually the empty nature of all things, but have not yet attained the experience of emptiness. The two are of course connected, but very different. The experience of emptiness is not vacuous or bereft; it is very bright and clear, and when you attain it you are energized because you have had a real taste of your true nature rather than just an image of it up in your head.

    As I say, you may be going through this phase, or there may be other explanations for your lack of satisfaction. Many people who try meditation are psychologically, emotionally, or socially wounded; and that creates and shapes the projections of mind that they mistake for spiritual attainment. It's not that the meditation is useless, but that the incomplete or skewed ego still runs the show. (I say this with the full admission that I am one of those people who set out on the path carrying many wounds that only came to light much later, but which shaped the way I experienced the path.)

    The psychological ego has to be healed; it has to be well formed before it can serve as the proper interface between self and spiritual experience. In the old days, this re-formation happened through grounded training: the trainee was put in very simple but effortful work or practice situations, and his/her twisted patterns would gradually get straightened out. Accomplishment by accomplishment, the interface between self and practice would get purified; the mind that clutches at fantasy ideals would settle down, and confidence in simple, grounded reality would increase.

    These days, we also have psychotherapeutic process and self-help modalities to heal these wounds, the most significant of which tend to happen in the formative years of childhood and youth.

    You don't manage this issue just by getting hold of a good quote. There's no tool you can buy to quickly 'fix the machine'. It takes time and ongoing effort to face the wounding you incurred in your life, and the sense of vulnerability behind it, and the neurotic armoring or avoiding patterns that arise because of it.

    In short, you have to go into the feelings you habitually avoid so that you can be alive in your daily activity. The meaning appears when you reclaim yourself. Trying to fix it through thinking only continues the habitual deflection, the pattern of going up into your head, into concepts and theories, into grand visions and overblown ego characters, rather than feeling the hard truth in your gut.
