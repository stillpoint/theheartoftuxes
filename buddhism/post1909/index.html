    Question about friendship.

    Communicate.

    Listen, ask questions, express yourself sincerely and openly, and keep trying. Realize that you may not achieve perfect understanding right away, so give each other some leeway with the knowledge that you're trying to come to clarity together and that it may take a few tries.

    If you have gratitude in your heart the communication will go better. Even before you have a chance to speak, just considering the things you're grateful for in the relationship can not only ease your mind but can help things resolve for your friend. The two of you have a connection; you have karma together; so how can you use that karma to help each other?

    But if the karma you have together is coming to an end — if the causes that brought you together and kept you together are now shifting and moving you apart — how can you use that to help each other?

    Can you have compassion for your friend? If so, you can be around him. But you can keep your clear boundary. You can forgive your friend for mistakes, but you don't have to put up with the mistakes continuing. You can say that it's not okay for him to burden you. If you are aware in the present moment as it's happening, you can speak up and let your friend know how his action and demeanor are affecting you. If he can't receive what you're saying you can still keep the boundary by saying it needs to stop, whether he understands or not.

    But if you find the limit of your compassion or your ability to hold the boundary of appropriate behavior, or if your friend doesn't care enough to work on this, you may have to remove yourself from the toxic situation so that your own peace of mind isn't threatened. If you are strong and clear, you can bear a lot; if you are easily influenced, you need to protect yourself.

    One question that comes to mind is why your friend's negativity is aimed at you. Is there cause for resentment? Have you done something to hurt your friend? Do jealousy or shame have a role in the relationship? These might be among the first things you ask your friend.

    Sometimes people seem like they're acting cruelly toward you, but it's really a reaction to your own mistreatment of them or some other way you yourself create the bad situation. Other times, a friend or loved one may aim their negativity at you, and it's because you're the one they feel safe with. This happens often with children and parents, or between partners: someone is having a crappy day, but they won't express it to others because they don't feel safe being that open; but when they come home to the one they know will keep caring for them, they let out all that frustration and anger.

    Really, most of these problems come down to misunderstanding or remaining ignorant of what's really going on for the other person. If you sincerely try to communicate, at least you'll know you gave it a good effort.

    You might be interested in Non-Violent Communication (NVC). In its basic form, NVC is a simple structure that helps you to hear each other. It's based on the belief that if we really understand what's going on for someone emotionally we are naturally moved to respond with compassion. Here's another link to an NVC resource sheet.
