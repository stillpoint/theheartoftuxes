    How can I cultivate and grow seeds of Love and Compassion inside me

    First, you can keep asking that question: "How can I grow the seeds of Compassion?"

    Atisha's classical Six Contemplations to develop Relative Bodhicitta is a practice of recalling great gratitude and subsequent kindness for all beings.

    You can practice point by point, but in a less formal way you can simply keep remembering that we're all in the same boat, all going through the same ups and downs, all subject to the same major pains of dissatisfaction, sickness, and death. The follow-up is to put yourself in others' place, considering their vulnerability and suffering. When a friend is going through a hard time, you can see yourself in their place, relating to the hard times that you've gone through or actually envisioning yourself "in their shoes". Even with animals, you can open to the difficulties they encounter daily. If you continue this practice it may open into a broad view of the struggle of all beings together, and the inevitability of decay and death. This in turn generates a broad compassion.

    The essence of this approach is the same as Tonglen practice in Vajrayana Buddhism, which is a more formal and precise way of "exchanging oneself" compassionately. There are resources online, and you can also read about Tonglen in many authors' work (like Pema Chodron's), as it is a common and foundational practice. Basically, you breathe in the hot, dark, gunky pain and suffering of the other, and you breathe out cool, bright, clean goodness — the very best of what you have — peace, composure, ease, wakefulness, and whatever their situation requires. Rather than fearing you won't have enough to spare, in doing this practice you realize that you have access to a bottomless well of compassion, and the more you use it the more it flows.

    Brahmavihara practice also develops compassion. There are many instructions for this practice online and in books as well. One by one, the enlightened qualities of equanimity, loving kindness, compassion, and mutual joy are radiated out to the world in ever-expanding circles until they are realized as the boundless nature of your own original mind.

    In the most simple, basic practice, keeping openhanded presence is the expression of compassion. The original mind is already perfect compassion. If you don't trade it for something cheap, original mind already reflects perfectly from moment to moment. Just keeping awareness without adding or subtracting anything is the embodiment of compassion. The practice, then, is to maintain that moment to moment, and to trust its movements. Zen practice and koan training are very applicable in this regard, as is the "choiceless awareness" practice in Theravada Buddhism.
