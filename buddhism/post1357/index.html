    Please take care about putting words into my mouth. ("You weren't inclined to answer.") That may be the case, or there may have been a different kind of inclination or answer. My responses have a function; they're not just about answering in a robotic way, on a purely conceptual level. To do so would be to deny the truth you're extolling here.

    So sometimes I decline to offer more words so that the truth beyond words can be honored. Sometimes I exhibit reticence in order to invite things to speak for themselves. Sometimes brevity or silence becomes an open question, an unknowing that itself expresses the nature of God.

    I personally have no problem using the name God for the bright, living truth excpressed in Buddhism. I understand that neither Buddha nor God is or has a set being. But since it is a matter of great attachment and assumption for so many, I pick my spots.

    The litmus test is this: who made God? If you can answer this, we can talk about God.

    As the beautiful heart poet Ryokan wrote,

    > My poems are not poems.

    > If you know my poems are not poems,

    > then we can talk of poetry.

    ~

    I agree with you wholeheartedly and vehemently, that rigid conceptualization prevents so many of us from understanding Buddhadharma, much less seeing the profound wisdom of other religions. Concept, tribalism/chauvinism, and ego attachment are all mutually supportive structures.

    In this sense, Joseph Campbell is right when he says that religion is the structure we use to ward off religious experience.

    I really couldn't agree more with your statement, and the optimistic direction behind it.

    One recalls Master Meister Eckhart's affirmation,

    > If I had a God I could understand, I would no longer consider him God.

    This awareness seems to be reflected across religions, for instance in the fact that Judaism was so careful to render even the name of the Nameless as only whispered vowels , and why the very first lines of the Tao Te Ching read "the Way that be spoken of is not the eternal Way."
