    Seeking feedback from experienced meditators

    The procedures are traditionally there to ensure that the practice session is complete. We bring our inherent ignorance with us when we enter practice; the procedure is a way to get beyond that more quickly. For example, the approach of many meditators is â€œwhat is this practice going to do for me?" That's an inherently small-minded approach that makes progress very slow indeed, and can undo much of one's effort. Many traditional practice ceremonies will automatically bring in a broad view (for the sake of all beings everywhere) and more holistic practice (including the physical, emotional, and visionary aspects of being, not only the mental).

    Going for refuge, bringing up the mind of compassion, remembering your vows or commitments, dedicating your practice in a broad-minded and generous way, creating the ideal intention and space for practice, doing the meditation, and closing the session without sinking right back into self-centered thought are some of the functions that might come of a traditional practice session. For the newcomer, and even for some experiences meditators, that view is not yet internalized: they don't yet see all the different aspects of a conditionless, beyond-self approach to practice. So the ritual procedure takes care of that until the view is digested and made one's own. Of course, you still have to participate and do your best from your side to let go of small-minded self-clinging.

    Having said that, an adaptive, more spontaneous approach seems to work for some people. Not as many as want to take up that style, though. For the most part, meditators who look for more leeway and variety are just avoiding clear, disciplined practice. But for a few, who already have a very clear direction and -- let's say -- a mature, relatively ego-free view, a spontaneous and creative approach may work well.

    But in the meditation itself, form is essential. It is essential that you work with a good form, one that can ferry you to liberation or otherwise produce the results you seek, and that you follow it meticulously. The clarity of the form is what reflects your mind to you. If you shift styles when difficulties come up, for example, you don't get the chance to deeply experience the difficulties and your reactions to them. So you miss the chance to see your karma and change it.

    Likewise, some things just require time and consistency; so for a whole other reason, if you keep changing you may never drill down to the depth of one practice. It depends on you, your aims, and the practices you're working with, but that's another concern.
