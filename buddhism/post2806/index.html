    I don't believe in spirits, deities, or karma, is there any actual application of atheistic Buddhism?

    Dukkha implies a much more fundamental aspect of life than the word "suffering". There will be many times when we feel just great, or are having a peak experience or undergoing joy or falling in love or any number of things that we wouldn't call "suffering"; but if we are experiencing them as a separate being having an experience in time, the nature of that is impermanent, insubstantial, compound (made up of ever-shifting parts rather than something in and of itself), and unsatisfactory.

    I'm not an expert on this. The right guy to ask about the correct translation of the term Dukkha might be u/michael dorfman.

    You ask if the term "suffering" is meaningful only from or relative to the perspective of someone who has suffering. I think this is possible, but we should be aware that someone who moves on does not necessarily lose the ability to understand and sympathize with what he has moved on from.

    We should also note that our conception of Buddha does not at all attain the reality of Buddha. Our idea is not nearly the reality. There are many instances in the sutras where the Buddha chides his disciples about thinking that they know who or what He is, or about identifying him with his physical form. If we understand that Buddha is not simply a being existing along a historical timeline, we may then very well ask "What is Buddha?" This is a very famous question, and one which has been asked repeatedly throughout the ages. What is the awakened mind? In what sense is that my own mind, my own self?

    Yes, from the relative perspective we may see a "person" progress in realization and apparently then become an awakened person. From the awakened perspective, there is only awakeness. But although Buddhas reside in vast, inscrutable, unshakeable wisdom, they are not limited to either absolute or relative realms. They can move freely between perspectives, and in fact are beyond perspective since a Buddha encompasses everything, everywhere. This is difficult for us to conceive of.

    It is said that a Bodhisattva never for a moment maintains the delusion that there are beings (that suffer), but that a Bodhisattva also never for a moment fails to respond to the suffering of beings. This is original goodness, which is beyond our conception of good and bad, suffering and not suffering.

    We mustn't think that enlightenment or Nirvana or becoming a Buddha is a departure from this world into a different world or state. Nirvana is not separate from what is here in front of your face.

    To avoid evil and do good is indeed Buddhist teaching; but keep in mind that the Dhammapada speaks to a certain audience, an audience for whom evil and good are significant distinctions. It's like when you teach a child the difference between good and bad behavior: later in life, when wisdom has grown, he may realize that "good" and "bad" are just shorthand for something that doesn't really have a clear-cut distinction. At some point, the notions of good and bad may be discarded, or at least moved on from, and awareness of cause and effect may take precedence. At a more advanced phase still, one may realize the unreality of "beings" for which cause and effect would take hold; so emptiness becomes the overriding issue.

    The Buddha taught many different styles and levels, adjusting to what his audience needed to hear and were capable of digesting. This was called upaya, commonly translated as skillful means. Different language and action are used depending on the mind of the student. It doesn't make any of the teachings less true or less valid, it just goes to show the all-embracing vastness and flexibility of awakening.
