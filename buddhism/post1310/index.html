    I don't think I can judge OP's standards or motivations; so I can't say 'should' or 'shouldn't' on this point.

    Have you ever watched a daytime soap opera? I remember seeing them as a kid when my grandmother would watch. Then, some 15 years later I happened to catch the same soap opera again, and the same stuff was going on! The same conflicts and dramas.

    I believe it was Ram Dass (here talking about Information Addiction) who spoke of returning to society after years of intensive spiritual training, only to find the same old stories unfolding in the newspapers — the same old soap operas.

    Social engagement is indeed part of our responsibility; but if we have the idea that we are actually going to eliminate conflict from the world, that can spill into delusion and egotism. The first place to bear witness is in ourselves. The whole world unfolds from mind.

    On that account, you might consider why this is such a pressing issue for you in particular. Why does the feeling of righteousness arise in you in regard to this particular issue? Could it have to do with the things you impose or avoid in your own psyche?

    The Buddha recognized that there are all kinds of ways of benefiting beings. There are great beings who govern societies and there are great beings who do solitary practice in mountain caves. There are social workers, poets, marine biologists, and Medicine Buddhas, all with their own expression of Dharma. There are many ways; but what is it in you that causes you to insist on only one way — "you must be engaged in this way"?

    Here, Zen Master, artist, writer, and peace activist Thich Nhat Hanh speaks on the topic of engagement:

    >One day in New York City I met a Buddhist scholar and I told her about my practice of mindfulness in the vegetable garden. I enjoy growing lettuce, tomatoes, and other vegetables and I like to spend time gardening every day.

    >She said, "You shouldn't spend your time growing vegetables. You should spend more time writing poems. Your poems are so beautiful. Everyone can grow lettuce, but not everyone can write poems like you do."

    >I told her, "If I don't grow lettuce, I can't write poems."

    >When I'm taking care of the lettuce or watering my garden I don't think of poetry or writing. I focus my mind entirely on taking care of the lettuce, watering the vegetables and so on. I enjoy every moment and I do it in the mode of "non-thinking." It's very helpful to stop the thinking. Your art is conceived in the depths of your consciousness while you're not thinking about it. The moment when you express it is only a moment of birth, the moment you deliver the baby. For me, there must be moments when you allow the child inside you to grow, so you can do your best and your masterpiece can contain insight, understanding, and compassion.
