    Thoughts regarding mixing schools

    Zen and mantra practice are in no way incompatible. There's just a Zen style of doing it, and other styles. But mantra is often actually recommended, in Korean Zen especially, as one of your daily practices along with chanting, bowing, and sitting. These practices together train body, speech, and mind.

    Furthermore, all schools of Zen Buddhism employ chants and mantras in their ceremonies.

    The Zen style of doing mantra practice is to just do it. Only completely do mantra. This has a slight difference from some styles that try to achieve a certain state, or alternately allow the mind to wander while mantra goes on in the background. In this practice, no particular state is pursued and in fact no thought is given to anything other than just doing the mantra.

    This also means that no idea is applied that 'mantra' means only the words or the beads moving. You only do the mantra, and let the mantra and the scene of the moment merge. It's not something you try to do with your mind; you just don't block the broader moment from being there. There is no thought that you should only perceive the words of the mantra, and nothing else; and there's no thought that you should be perceiving other things besides the words of the mantra. The mind is not kept in any fixed posture, you only do mantra completely with your whole body, mind, and heart.

    Another way of saying this is that Unknowing is the space in which mantra practice happens. The essential practice is always Unknowing, and these forms of sitting, walking, mantra, chanting, bowing, and so on all happen within that one essential practice.

    Another way to do mantra in Zen style, if you are working with self-inquiry, is to reach a fairly consistent attention with the mantra -- a level of concentration where you are not wandering off in distraction frequently -- and then gently turn the mind to look back at its source: “Where is this coming from; who or what is perceiving the mantra?" It is not that you expect an answer, but neither does lack of expectation diminish your looking. The big question behind the practice -- “Who?", “What?" -- is that same Unknowing space in which all things appear; you rest there, as that big open question or space. The form of the mantra helps keep you anchored, present.

    But to tell the truth, there are many ways of using mantra that can be beneficial. If you are returning to presence and your attitude is not grasping, it's hard to see how that could be harmful. If you are studying within a tradition you should take up the issue with a teacher in your school. Most advanced students or teachers should be able to help you out with the form of mantra practiced in your school; it's not necessary to talk with the master about it. If there is a head Dharma teacher in charge of the Zen hall, she/he would be the ideal person to talk with.
