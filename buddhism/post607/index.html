    Yes, that's a good comparison. Christianity has so much wisdom, and often presented with great conciseness and clarity, especially in the Gospels and the Gnostic Gospels.

    You still seem to be seeking a checklist, a guidebook for behavior. It's not like that. The way is wide. Keep your center correct and things will unfold correctly.

    In the way it plays out, Buddhists (or at least those who self-identify as Buddhists) have fallen on both sides of the tax issue, with some conforming to the law and some breaking it.

    Unjust laws should be challenged. But let's not think that we can actually fix the world! As long as we have ignorance, we have Samsara, the endless cycle of insufficiency, flaw, harm, and suffering.

    There is no way to perfect life by manipulating outer forms. Form itself is unsatisfactory, impermanent, and empty. So anything made by man will be inherently flawed. That's what we live with. We do our best, we challenge it, we try to make it better, more just, more conducive of happiness; but that's not where our salvation lies.

    Taking another page out of Christianity's book, perhaps it boils down to Saint Augustine's statement: "Love, and do what you will."

    Are you thinking and acting from a clear center; are you thinking and acting out of love for all beings, a love that emanates from your original mind? If so, do what you will; there is no rule book. But if your love is not yet clear and consistent, the rule book is used in the interim: the Four Noble Truths; the Three Marks Of Existence; the Noble Eightfold Path; the Five Lay Precepts and further precepts of sadhakas, monastics, and Bodhisattvas; and the Six or Ten Paramitas ("Perfections"). This is where study with an achieved master, in a traditional lineage, comes in.

    Here's a story that might help.

    When I first started studying regularly with a master and practicing in the temple, I was maybe a bit like you. I was very concerned with social justice, ecology, human rights, animal rights. This is something very wonderful about a lot of the young people who take up Buddhist practice: they are very sincerely concerned and ready to work for the benefit of the world. But by the same token there is often a kind of tension to that mindset, a perfection-seeking desperation and a bit of a superiority complex. We can distinguish between righteousness, which is very grounded, and righteous indignation which has a self-centered basis.

    Anyway, at the time I was starting training, I was very tense about trying to do no harm. I tried not to be in anyone's way. I tried not to waste anything. I tried not to offend or harm any being. Of course, to my great chagrin, it was impossible. Just living in the modern world means you're already in someone's way!

    One day I was assigned gardening for my work, and in particular pulling up weeds. Every weed I pulled up weighed on my heart. I couldn't reconcile being in the temple, trying to become a 'better' person (which, I later learned, is not the whole story), with killing plants! I knew that the garden in part provided food for the community, so I went around in my mind arguing 'for' and 'against'; but every weed I pulled felt like pain.

    After some time, the master came out into the garden and also started to work. It took a while, but I mustered up the courage to ask the question that was burdening me: How can we justify killing plants, even to survive?

    Of course, there are many possible reasons, many possible teachings here. But the master said only one thing. He looked right at me, right into me, and asked "Do you know the danger of holding opinions?"

    That was all. How wonderful! "Do you know the danger of holding opinions?"

    I responded, "Yes." And that was that. My thinking stopped; I didn't check; I didn't look back.

    Of course, our own opinionated mind makes tremendously more suffering than weeding a garden... and more than most anything we could point to in external activity. Our holding of opinion is the cause for environmental harm, cultural alienation, family disintegration, war, genocide — all the problems humans visit upon the planet. Because we don't understand our own true mind, and instead hold opinions, we are — in the words of one Zen Master — "number one bad animal!"

    Ignorance and clinging are the enemies. Let us be victorious over them.
