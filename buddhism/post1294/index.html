    As I said,
    >The difference is often more subtle than people -- even advanced practitioners of other paths -- understand. It's not that they are wrong, but they have not learned to see in that way. "Taking one step in Atlanta, you arrive in San Francisco."

    >My understanding...

    Your understanding of what the Buddha taught is not what the Buddha taught. That's not dissing you; it's reflecting the very limited nature of conceptual understanding. The three marks of existence are what? Impermanence, compound nature/no own-being, and unsatisfactoriness. But thought is fixed, set, and resolved. At least, it thinks it is. Thought can't touch Buddha's teaching.

    Buddha's view is not a view. This is in the scriptures.

    Anyone's understanding can't grasp it, because it is beyond understanding. It is not beyond attaining, but it is beyond understanding.

    And yet, everyone and his brother still believes in "my understanding" and puts understanding in the primary position, on a pedestal. This is making false idols. When scriptures warn against making false idols, this is what they mean. (Or at least, one of the things they mean.)

    Your very terminology belies a narrow view:

    >"How can you be confident enough..."

    >...to assume..."

    >...you do not need to practice meditation or precepts?

    These are all misunderstandings of the teaching of no-path. They are your assumptions, not mine, not a Zen practitioner's, certainly not a Zen master's, and most certainly not living Zen.

    Three mistaken assumptions: "It's about confidence." "You're speaking from assumption." "You're saying meditation and precepts are not needed."

    When Buddha saw the Morning Star and got enlightenment, he exclaimed "Ah! All things have the nature of awakening." When did that happen? That is the point. Did Buddha have enlightenment and perceive awakened nature only at one point in history? If you think so, that's not Buddha; that's your story of Buddha made in the image of a self.

    (In the same way, it is not so much that God makes man in His image, but that man makes God in his.)

    This has nothing to do with confidence or assumption. It has nothing to do with competing views or arguments. It has to do with direct seeing. "Ah, all things have the nature of awakening!" When?

    If you are not willing to see, or if you don't have the capacity to see, then no amount of explaining will get it for you. The extremely tragic, ongoing, addictive, wasteful insistence on trying to use thought to attain that which is beyond thought is precisely what Zen is medicine for. But who takes the medicine? Lots of people discuss the medicine, but few actually take it.

    And if you want to take the medicine, don't think that it will be as easy as popping a pill. If it were that easy, you'd already have gotten it from my writing, because I've already expressed it. But learning how to attain and apply clarity — and then actually applying it — are required.

    So before you have clarity, there is necessary movement from non-clarity to clarity. But when you have clarity, there is no movement, no self, no subject or object, no being, no world, no near or far shore, no path. But as I said, people who are attached to time and story and the idea of path can't accept that, can't attain that. So they are stuck with their conceptual rejection.

    ~

    Upaya has correct context.

    Buddha offered the gradual path for those who need the gradual path. If you can't directly access clarity, because your view won't allow it or your capacity has not yet developed, then you need the gradual path and even hearing about no-path is frustrating and seems sacrilegious.

    But those teachings were offered at a particular stage in the development of disciples, and in particular contexts. Buddha also said "In all my years of teaching, I have not said a single thing." What is that? Just poetic words? How will you attain "not said a single thing" — how will you attain that point? This takes more than just talk and thought.
