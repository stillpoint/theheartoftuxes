    That's the 64 million dollar question. You can't necessarily 'tell'; it doesn't work like that. You only make sincere effort to clarify the point. Pure practice is 'only do it', without checking.

    "What is the true purpose of my life? What is my activity today for?" These kinds of questions help clarify your direction if they are visited in the right way. They are not to be engaged through speculation, through trying to find a set answer to put into your pocket. The question starts to open something inside; right engagement is to let it open. Let the unknowing be there. Let the question itself illuminate your activity rather than grasping for a final solution.

    In Buddhism, contemplation of impermanence is considered a great motivator, a powerful way to reveal your true direction. One of the most universally practiced contemplations, among all levels of practice from beginners to masters, is The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind (from Samsara to Dharma). This is precisely the turning you are taking about, precisely the turning you are ripe for: "How do I turn away from all this dissatisfaction, all this meaningless bullshit? How do I stay on the right Dharmic track?"

    You have this aspiration now because your karma has opened up; there is a window of clarity. But soon enough, like most of us, your window will close and you will fall into habit and begin to forget the lesson you are seeing so clearly today. This is how karma moves: up and down, opening and closing. So in the time of your clear window it is beneficial to set up routines and rituals and life structures to keep you on track even when the meaningless mundanity threatens to overtake you again. Make commitments little by little, in line with your growing understanding and faith.

    One such routine is to recite and consider The Four Thoughts every morning. This will work on you deeply and turn you toward Dharmic intentions and actions.

    It doesn't happen all at once. Your life, as it is, developed gradually over many years, and many lifetimes; so some changes may also take years or lifetimes. Maintain the will to keep coming back, even over ten thousand lifetimes:

    >"Any time I fall off the path, may I quickly come back. May Buddhas and Bodhisattvas always ferry me back. May Dharma sisters and brothers always find me and call me back. May I quickly repent and let go of negative deeds, finding my way back."

    But because your true nature is true and your false nature is false, when you cleave to the Dharma it is also possible to change very quickly and miraculously. The meaninglessness, the bullshit may seem all-consuming, but with correct practice it is revealed as a very thin and insubstantial veneer -- almost nothing at all -- while your true nature has always been there, and has always been real.
