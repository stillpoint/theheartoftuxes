    If I were enlightened, would I still experience suffering/dukkha?

    There seems to be a lot of speculation in this thread as to the nature of enlightenment, and also some unclarity about the difference between pain and suffering.

    In any online forum, we can assume that posts generally start with the tacit acknowledgement "In my opinion...." In most forums, matters of opinion don't make much difference; but let's please hold ourselves to a higher standard when talking about things that really make a difference in how people think and live and relate. That means putting our egos in check a little. When we are speaking from understanding rather than experience, if we just acknowledge that it will make it easier for readers to be clear on the issues.

    As to enlightenment and pain:

    When the Buddha attained enlightenment, it is recorded that he recognized that all things have this nature -- of awakening, awareness. The substance of our everyday life and the substance of enlightenment are not two different things; but enlightenment is the clear and unimpeded attainment of that substance.

    So it is not that pain or any other reality disappears with enlightenment. With complete emptiness (as distinct from enlightenment), and indeed in many meditative absorptions, the connection to the senses subsides and there is just utter silence and stillness and timelessness. These states can indeed be pain-free and blissful. Some people will call this nirvana, while others will reserve the term for something more inclusive of both absolute and relative existence, both the still, pure One on the one hand, and the moving, relating infinite beings on the other hand.

    Complete and perfect enlightenment does not do away with relative existence; it returns to 'everyday mind', as they say in zen, only it is grounded in the absolute -- that emptiness and stillness -- and free of delusional attachment to anything in the relative realm. In this state, all things including pain are perceived clearly, but there is no sense of an "I" for whom they are happening.

    So we can talk about two different notions: pain vs suffering. Pain refers to the actual experience of an uncomfortable stimulus, like what happens the moment you touch a hot element on the stove. Suffering refers to the relationship you have with that (or any) experience, based on the false notion of a self, the sense that "I am in pain". The deeply ingrained sense of "I, my, me" makes all true experiences into dukkha: insubstantial, incomplete, unsatisfactory.

    Perhaps the short form of this is to say that with enlightenment, you will experience everything and it will all be okay. But best not waste effort speculating on it, and get to actually doing it.
