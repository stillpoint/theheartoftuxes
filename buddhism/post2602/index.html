    Is the teaching of the unlimited life of the Buddha in line with impermanence?

    I appreciate your persistence and perspective too.

    Your 'inspection' is also based on attachment. If you find what to trust in yourself, you will know what to trust in others; but without finding that you of course look for words and signs and judgments. It's true for most of us, more or less; we look to the wrong things for satisfaction. At the bottom of it, we often fail to go to the Buddha for refuge, but rather go to words and signs for refuge. That's Dukkha.

    May we all take refuge in the Buddha, and stop taking refuge in our thinking. Then we can use thinking correctly.

    >I guess what I need is a physical teacher...

    Yes, I think that would be wonderful for you. You could learn how to make good use of your analytical nature.

    It is right to take your time and research and test out your relationship with a teacher and their teaching. Practice should be free of doubt, so you have to take reasonable steps to pluck out your doubts rather than going on wishful thinking or fervent belief. But it should happen in a balanced way; and if you don't also leave room for trust and acceptance, you will not be able to receive the Dharma that those more wise than you might offer. Wonderful teaching will be offered on a gold tray, and you won't be able to accept it. So I hope you find balance in your approach and relationship with your teachers.

    >But then what have I done to deserve such a luxury?

    This is a kind of laziness (kausidya, in Sanskrit), one of the Five Faults that keep one from digesting Dharma. It is the laziness of self-discouragement, where the thinking says you can't do something, you don't deserve it, or are inherently unable to bring it to fruition, so you might as well not try.

    Your original mind — your Primary Source — is the same as everyone's Primary Source, the same as the original mind of all Buddhas. The Buddha taught that everything has this nature of awakening, this Bodhicitta; so if you deny it in yourself you go against Buddha's teaching, and against the living Buddha-nature Itself. So you mustn't think this way. Rather, you should not let anything stand in the way of finding out. Go research and study with a good teacher.

    >The reason It is hard for me to take your words at face value is because I cannot inspect you.

    >To my perspective, you could be right, or you could be wrong. Without inspection, following your advice would be a foolish thing.

    I could be right or wrong if you base your inspection on my authority. But if you put down your opinion and your attachment to words, you can start to see for yourself.

    For example, if I say that apple juice tastes like apples, you could say that I might be right or wrong. If I am a great enlightened teacher maybe you believe what I say. But even so, you don't know for yourself. You have to go drink apple juice to find out.

    If you are too attached to words, maybe you don't even believe your own experience. Maybe you think "well maybe something is wrong with my mind, and this really tastes like chocolate". Nevertheless, before words and thoughts appear your awakened nature sees clearly, hears clearly, and tastes apple juice clearly. No need for an authority to tell you what you taste.

    Where we do need wise guides and elders is to keep pointing us back to our awakened nature when we inevitably stray into attachment and habit and delusion.
