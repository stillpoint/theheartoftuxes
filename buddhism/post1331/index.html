    Zen isn't about thinking and figuring everything out; it's about being awake and participating in things-as-they-are.

    So there's a great emphasis on simplicity in Zen: keeping a simple mind, not a complicated mind that always seeks to be the 'expert'. Much of practice is about bringing the mind down from its flights of fancy, down to the earth and to one's plain activity in the moment. It's not only sitting meditation that has this effect, but also settled, non-grasping, non-striving presence throughout the day, and especially physical practice. When the body and breath have moved vigorously, you're more settled and ready to just be with things rather than work on the 'master project' of figuring it all out.

    Never mind special experiences. Don't make ideas about "I got oneness" or "I experienced..." whatever. If you attach to special experiences you'll get laughed out of the Zen Centers. Just realize that when you do practice, new things will sometimes happen. No big deal. New experiences happening is an old story that's happened to millions and millions of other students. Don't get excited about an experience the way a baby gets enthralled with peek-a-boo ("OMG, did you see his face just disappear!!? Holy cow, this is incredible!"); just see it as something new that happened, and return plainly to your practice like an old veteran.

    What will bring about these beneficial attitudes and behaviors? Correct teaching. Frequent, consistent, ongoing access to proper teaching from a capable and confirmed (ideally awakened) teacher. Without the consistent influence of good teaching, your own opinions, preferences, habits, and blind spots will swamp your practice. It will be like a little boat on a rough ocean: even the first few waves can sink it. But proper teaching provides you with a more substantial vehicle, and the support to right it when it turns over or goes off course.
