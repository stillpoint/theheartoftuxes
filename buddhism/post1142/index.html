    The man reading a newspaper on the train is different from the man who got on it. How?

    This is how we are tricked by language. You say that you know that you'll be going to work, and you have a notion of what "going to work" is. But that notion is just a notion. It doesn't have the actuality of the moment unfolding for you:

    • the particular air currents,
    • the particular raindrops,
    • the particular people moving in their particular ways,
    • the events that arise and the specific ways in which they arise,
    • the fact that you have two pieces of toast and a black coffee influencing your body and mind rather than a half pound of pork,
    • the fact that you are now thinking of your ill mother rather than last week's ball game,
    • and really countless, countless other unfoldings and the new relationships they create....

    "Going to work" is only shorthand. The real thing is utterly unique and momentary.

    The next big language problem is the confusion between thinking and knowing. It would be more correct to say that you think of yourself going to work. You check that thinking later and see that certain aspects of your experience correspond to what you had thought (for example, you do end up at the office), and so you conclude that you "knew" you were going to work. But really at no point did you know what was unfolding. You didn't know how all of the incalculable atoms in the universe would be arranged at any given moment; you didn't know that the morning would unfold with you standing on the subway next to a woman with a lilac scent or that the river of thoughts in your mind would play out in exactly the way it did. So your idea of knowing is selective. It is really just thinking, and very limited thinking at that, only keying on certain landmarks and ignoring others.

    The third big issue with language here is that you believe the words or ideas have a reality in themselves â€” especially, and most significantly, the idea of "I". The assumption that the word and the thing are the same is what allows us to fool ourselves, and is what creates this vast shared illusion. This is not "a computer"; we name it a computer. In Czech it is named "pocitac." In Finnish it is named "tietokone." But what is it really? It is a collection of resources temporarily formed in particular shapes and relationships (which are always changing) to which we ascribe the name "computer". Before it was formed, there is no computer; after it is destroyed, there is no computer. The whole world of things is like this.

    Perhaps the most striking part of your message is the sign-off, "Please help," One gets the sense that all of this thinking is a house of cards about to fall down. The ideas and arguments for or against are not satisfying. They do not calm you or nourish you or provide you with contentment.

    So I urge you to look for what is real beyond words. What is there before thinking appears? If for example, you determine to experience a tree, without assuming that you know what it is just because you can label it with a word, then you will experience something profound and real, and ultimately satisfying. It will likely feel empty, because it is empty of the word-meaning, the "thingness", we usually add to it; but this emptiness is wonderfully peaceful, and at the very least you will be able to touch something true, not just something that you think.

    The great Zen Master Dogen said "You should entreat rocks and trees to preach the Dharma." Entreat. Go and look, not with arrogant detachment, but with curiosity and humility. Ask the tree to tell you what it is; ask it to tell you what is real.

    And if you do, please report your findings!
