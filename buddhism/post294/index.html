    TheHeartofTuxes

    Learning and practicing together with others is important, even if it's not your favorite. Thought we may hope to hide and control our situations, true freedom is trained by being exposed, ultimately hiding nothing.

    Of course, this happens in proper increments. At first you just learn together in a protected, if structured, environment with others. Then you get to learn that others are like you and you are like others: whatever differences we may have on the outside, our inner need and purpose are the same. When you realize this through direct life experience, it is a significant milestone. It is one of the gateways to great compassion and freedom.

    Another important aspect of practicing together is that it purifies your character. When you're in isolation, you have everything your way — your own favorite pace and schedule, your own cherished opinions, your own whims. This only deepens ego's grip on you, keeping you narrow-minded and fearful of new situations.

    But when you live, work, or practice with others, you often need to give up your opinions and preferences. Your crusty exterior starts to crumble, and a softer, more adaptable, more mature character appears. It is like when sharp rocks rub on each other at the shoreline, making each other smooth.

    At higher levels of practice, when a master senses you are close to breaking through to awakening, or if you commit to a very uncompromising path, the master may intentionally pull the rug out from under you, exposing the neuroses and strategies that form the last refuges of ego, completing the process of purification.

    Whether you act courageously as a beginner in merely trying out a new practice, or whether you act courageously as an advanced practitioner, facing every last demon and owning up to every last flaw, the practice attitude — the intrepid spirit that is willing to give up attachment to "I, my, me" — is the hallmark of the real spiritual seeker.

    Practice is not intended to be comfortable to the ego. That is not it's purpose.

    From a practical perspective, in order to pass the precious teaching on broadly and without favoritism, it is usually necessary for a teacher to take on group classes rather than laboring in a series of solo meetings, passing on the same basics over and over. When a student progresses to advanced levels, there may (or may not) be more private meeting. Often it's the attendant, the one in complete service, who gets the most private time with a master.

    In some traditions like Zen, private interviews are part of the training. In most Zen centers there are weekly private interview opportunities as well as one-on-one formal koan training in residential and retreat settings.

    The masters of some lineages tend to be more isolated and have fewer students. For example, Dzogchen teachers in the Ngakpa tradition sometimes retire to mountain hermitages, and their students are the few who take on the inconvenience and discomforts of the setting.
