    In Buddhism, where would you place your trust in hard times?

    Your question is so sincere; I wanted to revisit it, as my previous response is only what came up in the moment, only one aspect of the issue.

    I also have had very deeply-felt connections to both Christianity and Buddhism, and also at times have been lost as to where to place my trust. It now seem clear — and I want to encourage you with this as well — that the thing that can receive our trust remains the same; it is only our thinking about it that changes. Whether we name that thing (non-thing, really) 'God' or 'Christ' or 'Buddha' or 'Mind' or 'Truth' or 'Emptiness' or 'Tao' or 'Source' or 'Unknowing', it is neither the name nor our conception of it that matters. What matters is that everything has one root, one substance, and that is what we owe our trust.

    There is no actual thing or being we let go to; we only let go. The non-thing we let go to is in constant and eternal communication with all things. Its substance is pure and unmoving, but its activity is utterly fluid and infinitely interpenetrating with all things. And if it is more helpful to us to conceive of a thing or being with an associated name and feeling, because that's what helps us to actually trust, then that may be a necessary and helpful tool until we reach the level of trust where we can let go of that as well.

    >Jesus said "I am the Way and the Truth and the Life."

    >Buddha, when he was born, said "In heaven above and earth below, I alone am the World-Honored One."

    >Islam's Quran says "There is no God but Allah."

    >Judaism (per Isaiah) says "I am the L-rd and there is none else."

    These don't refer to different things! But we must ourselves realize and actualize that they are not simply talking about concepts. There is a momentary (timeless), present, living reality that they all refer to. And there is only one — nothing outside, no other source, no other God.

    Until we attain that reality completely, it is up to us to trust. Once we attain it completely, then we are the repository of trust, through and through.

    And we already are perfect trust. When the sun is in our eyes, we squint. After we work hard, we are tired. When we receive someone's love, our heart responds. This is perfect trust, if only we see it.

    ~

    I also want to suggest that the loss you experience, of a being to whom you can easily relinquish, may indicate a growing maturity. There is perhaps some greater self-responsibility, so it isn't as easy to wander innocently through the world. You see more, so you see your own part more.

    This means that you have to develop a trust that does not in any way deny self-responsibility. On one hand, never think that you alone are doing something, achieving something, getting something. God (or Buddha or Original Mind) is doing it. But on the other hand, be very meticulous in your activity. Follow the changing situation like the sensitive needle in a phonograph follows the groove of the record. So trust is no longer broad and vague; it functions very precisely moment to moment.

    OK?

    That momentariness and precision will come about by letting go of your inner dialogue about the whole deal.

    Across time, you don't know how to live. Me neither. But right now, you know. Only reading Reddit. Later, just eating or just helping your housemate make dinner or just enjoying the evening. Don't worry about 'how'; allow your knowing to express itself according to each situation, each moment. And that includes sometimes knowing that you're lost, and that it's time to be lost. If you proceed in this way, no situation can defeat you.
