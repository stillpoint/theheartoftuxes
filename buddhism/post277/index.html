    TheHeartofTuxes

    It doesn't matter whether it's common. What matters is how you keep your mind. Will you practice compassion or not? Will you make a sincere effort or not?

    The thought "I'm not cut out for Buddhism/I'm not cut out for compassion/I'm not cut out for awakening" is a way of distancing yourself from responsibility for your own action. This isn't about Buddhism demanding something of you. This is about whether you continue to see the worst in someone, without bothering to change, yourself.

    If someone is very petty, cruel, boorish, sinister, etc., it means that they harbor a great deal of ignorance. It is the ignorance that is the enemy, an ignorance we can all claim part of. They don't have the awareness and peace of mind others can claim. This is worthy of pity.

    It also means they are suffering a great deal of disconnection, and will suffer a huge amount of negative consequence. This is worthy of compassion.

    And Buddhadharma teaches that we have all cycled through similar situations countless times. We have been that person with seemingly no redeeming qualities -- not because it was our wise intent to be like that, but because of a stream of causes and conditions we were and are still stuck within.

    And, if we fail to have compassion and fail to see the Dharmic nature of the person and situation, we create causes that will bring us back to that kind of existence: self-involved, fearful, self-righteous, judgmental, reactive, and so on.

    At any juncture, there are some things we can do well and some we can't. That's why we practice. If we were already completely stable in equanimity and compassion we wouldn't have to develop, we wouldn't have to train. So it is appropriate that at times we will 'meet our match', so to speak -- we will come up against a situation that's difficult to face.

    Therefore the path is not in always being free of difficulty. The path is in facing difficulty with positive response. We try, try, try.

    All Buddhist vehicles agree on three basic points: purify negativity; cultivate positivity; train the mind. Every situation is an opportunity to uproot our pride, selfishness, judgmental thought, and other negative traits.

    Every situation is an opportunity to practice positive action like openhanded presence, generosity, vigor, forbearance, wisdom, kindness.

    Every situation is an opportunity to train the mind not to attach to any external or internal object -- not to be swayed by being and events.

    So the common person laments difficulty and blames the ignorant; but the practitioner on the Buddhist path uses difficulty to perfect practice, and is grateful to the ignorant for reflecting back their own ignorance so it can be acknowledged and overcome, and for providing the opportunity to bring forth greater patience and love.

    One needs to practice hard even when times are good, because one soon encounters trouble. It's like building a survival shelter: the time for making it strong and stormproof is before the storm arrives. If one only starts patching up the roof when it's already collapsing in the rain, it will be very hard if not impossible to avoid risk and ruination.

    So you need good teaching, good method, and good practice. Then your karma will change and you can be stable and open around difficult people.

    If you practice Zen, do more Zen sitting. If you practice Vipassana, do more Vipassana. If you practice Metta, start by sending friendliness to someone who has been unconditionally kind to you, or someone you greatly admire and love. Also practice radiating Metta toward neutral beings; and finally, offer it to difficult beings.

    Spend time around those who can be utterly stable in any company -- teachers and experienced Sangha members. Receive their example, and let it seep into you. Become that.
