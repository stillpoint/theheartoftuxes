    TheHeartofTuxes

    I recommend a ritual of repentance. It's a practice meant for just such an occasion, to finish with the mistake and regain your correct relationship with yourself and Dharma.

    The rituals differ from tradition to tradition, but in general they involve contemplation of regret, followed by purification practices, and resolution or commitment or vow.

    1. Consider in detail the pathways of cause and effect that brought you perform this mistake, and that may unfold because of it. This has two aspects: first, you open your eyes to your responsibility and how you influence the lives of yourself and others; second, you augment your awareness of karma — the states of mind and decisions that led to the mistake — so you can change your karma moving forward from this point.

    In this step it is important to not only consider your regret in a conceptual way, but to feel it deeply. For instance, feeling your love for your family and other beings, you can let your regret for putting them at risk impact you at a deep level. This has transformative effect.

    1. If you have taken refuge or are willing to take refuge in the Three Jewels, do so. You can do it in your mind, with your heart, with your speech, and also with your action by performing bows as you recite a refuge prayers. It can be as simple as "I go for refuge to Buddha... I go for refuge to Dharma... I go for refuge to Sangha...." (Repeat 3 times, bowing each time.) Using all your bodymindspeechaction together is powerful. This is where you give yourself and your error over to the Three Jewels, and welcome their blessings and guidance.

    If you want to do a heartfelt prayer for support, you can do that. ("Please help me do better... Please help me help my family..." Etc.) This is actually an important daily renewal: to release our inevitable flaws and errors and have the opportunity to 'come clean' and re-commit, and especially to receive the blessings of those exceptional, boundlessly powerful and compassionate beings, who are committed to helping us be well.

    Then some kind of purification activity is done. This can mean doing 9 or 27 prostrations (or 108 or 500 if you can handle it); or making a number of water offerings (you can empty and refill seven small cups at a time); or making an offering of flowers or incense; or doing mantra (most Buddhist mantras will do, or you can use one specially aimed at repentance: Om salba motcha moji sadaya sabaha); or doing chanting (chanting a mantra out loud or doing Great Compassion Dharani); or doing a session of meditation.

    1. Finally, resolve strongly to uproot the negative mind states that led to the error (distraction, pride, indifference, whatever negative causes you identified). Resolution is useless if it is not carried out in action; so some kind of definite commitment or vow is useful. A commitment is something you apply yourself to in general; a vow is something specific that you absolutely hold to the very best of your ability. You may commit to more regular mindfulness practice, or to finding a teacher to guide you. These address the mistake in a broad way. And/or you may vow to always turn your cell phone off and keep it off when you drive your car.

    You may also take concrete steps to make amends to the beings harmed by your actions: the tree you hurt, the property owner, your family, etc. Taking positive, concrete steps to make amends heals this issue in your mind and makes you clean.

    1. Finish the ceremony by dedicating this experience and everything you have — all possessions, all relationships, and every good and bad experience — to the Three Jewels and to the benefit and awakening of all beings. This step is the final alchemy transforming error to vast benefit. You are not just cleaning things up to get a better situation for yourself, but rather you are changing garbage into gold, making benefit for infinite beings.

    You can bow three times or otherwise finish the ceremony; and then you are done. Don't call up doubt again in your thinking. You have given yourself over to Buddha, so let Buddha have it now. Only take care of your part, only channel your energy to positive activity; and have firm confidence that you have received blessings and repentance, and that you are completely clean. Go forward in your new life.

    ~

    Most traditions have regular daily repentance or purification built into the training. This gives you a clean start every day. What a gift! What a chance! To start from zero again, to be fresh and alive.

    There are also these kinds of special ceremonies and practices for bigger mistakes that don't only hurt ourselves but others as well. The aim of Dharma is not punishment or retribution, it is to return us to our right mind, our right happy and awake nature. It is very positive, very compassionate.

    For ingrained issues, including deep karma built up over many lifetimes, powerful purification practices exist. Many people do 500 or 1,000 prostrations every day. Special practice may entail 10,000 prostrations in a day! In Vajrayana, many people do purification practices associated with Vajrasattva (which must be taught and empowered by a guru). Each lineage has its way. The main thing is doing it, so you can get clean and start fresh.
