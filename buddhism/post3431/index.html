    > but I read somewhere it says visualizing is fooling oneself, it can lead to insanity.

    Who says this, and why?

    This is a good example of how plucking points out of context destroys meaning, and of how mixing teachings leads to confusion. If you have a good teacher of a valid tradition, learn from your teacher. They will teach in the proper order, at the proper pace, with the proper overview. They will attend to issues you can't even be aware of yet.

    If you don't have a teacher, it is best to find one; and barring that, stick to one authentic, reliable, traditional source of teaching, and don't play one tradition or teacher against another.

    Teachings are given at different levels to people of different capacity and attainment. This is appropriate. But we have strong habits of attaching to words and clutching at understanding; we think we can get something significant just by downloading enough data. So people comb the internet and books for more understanding, without seeing the proper place for the understanding â€” knowing its seed and root, it's nourishing context, its proper field of growth, its proper seasons of flowering and fruition.

    At a more pure level, nothing needs to be added. This is quite true, and quite a good teaching.

    But we already add untold mountains of material with our habit and attachment and assumption. So just to give this teaching to someone who has not fully realized the extent of their negative conditions can lead to complacency.

    Because we have so many kleshas, so many clouding influences, specific practices are given to weed out the negative clouding influences and support the positive awakening influences. Since our hearts are dead and closed, we are given devotional practices. Since our sight is unclear and our creative capacity withered, we are given visualization practices. Since we are ungrounded and disconnected from the wisdom of our bodies, we are given physical practices. Since we are impatient, indulgent, and distracted, we are given practices with specific steps. Since our aspiration tends to burn brightly in short spurts and then sputter in the face of self-centered intentions, we are given practices of remembering great compassion before, during, and after spiritual activities.

    There are 82,000 kinds of practices for 82,000 kinds of mind sickness.

    For those who need to learn a beneficial path while they are still attached to form, rule-based and scriptural-based traditions appear.

    For those who have digested the rules, traditions appear that point to original Wisdom and how action can benefit many others.

    For those who are beginning to move on from these forms, traditions focusing on trans-formation appear.

    And for those who understand better the empty nature of all of these, very pure and direct traditions appear.

    Neither tradition is better or worse, and all have produced enlightened beings. What is better is if the teaching is appropriate for the student; and what is worse is if the student takes the teaching out of context.

    Don't check your teacher by asking for the opinions of others. Of course, some preliminary research and trial is necessary before you put your trust in someone; but after you have reasonable evidence to trust your teacher, don't engage in wrong speech behind their back. If you have a doubt about something, consult your teacher. Help them be a better teacher to you by sharing your doubts sincerely.
