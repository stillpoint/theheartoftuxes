    Eastern yogic traditions recognize the purification of energy as part of the process of spiritual development. The base energetic substance of the body (known as jing in Traditional Chinese Medicine and Chinese internal arts) is said to be developed and refined by spiritual conduct, including ethical activity and presence or meditative concentration (or it is squandered through petty or materialistic activity and lack of presence). In achieved masters, this purified substance forms dense and brilliant relics that remain after death. These are called sari or sarira or shari.

    When a high monk or master dies and the body is cremated, in many traditions other monks will sift through the ashes to retrieve these pearl-like objects, and any bones or other material not destroyed by the fire. These are said to not only indicate the high level of attainment gained by the deceased, but to have an intense spiritual power of their own. Just to see them is in inspiration and a profound blessing.

    The relics will then often be housed in a reliquary that becomes an auspicious focus of practice or place of pilgrimage that can connect people deeply to the Dharma.

    These sari are extremely beautiful. The relics of some of the past masters we hear about in the sutras and historic tales are still in existence.

    There is a tour traveling the globe, displaying the sari of great masters. If you have even the slightest possibility of attending the tour, it would be a very auspicious thing to do. Just seeing the sari in real life is amazing. I've had the great good fortune to view the relics (of Buddha, Sariputra, Kasyapa, Padmasambhava, Milarepa, past Karmapas and Dalai Lamas, and many other exceptional masters) twice; and I would wish that everyone could encounter them.
