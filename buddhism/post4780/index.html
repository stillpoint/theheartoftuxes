    How many malaria vaccines could you buy with the money from one golden buddha?

    When we start to open to the extent of suffering in this world — when we begin to let it in and feel the magnitude of it — we can hardly help but have this kind of doubt. It is a sincere and vulnerable doubt that we all share in the depths of our psyches. Shouldn't we help more? Aren't we wasting effort, aren't we wasting money? And what is the use even if we do try? Following the train of thought to any significant degree, we can fall into helplessness, despondency, confusion.

    We have to realize that we will never have the perfect idea. Though we try and try to figure it out and understand the way, no plan will suffice. No ‘doing’ will do it all.

    Mother Teresa said that we can’t do great things in this life, we can only do small things with great devotion. The way that help unfolds is through individual, momentary action. “Making a cup of tea, I stop the war.” Not just any cup of tea stops the war; and not just any helpful action actually helps. But bringing peace and presence to the moment brings peace and presence to the world. The only thing we can take care of is our own moment.

    And each person, each being, has its own moment, unfolding uniquely. What you are able to unfold, I am not; what you see, I may be blind to. So I would not begrudge anyone with a sincere heart who wants to offer their effort and resources… to save the whales …or improve the situation of poor people …or build temples …or assist businessmen in being more successful. Helping is helping; it appears in the moment; and each person has his or her own place in the grand scheme, his or her own talents and perspective to offer. Would we demand that no fragrant or colorful flowers be planted in our gardens, lest they take space and nutrients from vegetables that provide food? Would we insist that artists become contractors, and musicians become farmers because that suits our idea of ‘helping’?

    Until I can embrace all of it, I won’t claim to have the final word on helping. Until we ourselves have finished clarifying the great question of life and death, all of our activity, all of our so-called help, will be rooted in ignorance and have unsatisfactory results. And yet, faced with this mess, we have to try. In all our imperfection and insufficiency, we have to try.

    Could it be that a massive golden Buddha, overwhelming in its magnificence, might for an instant strip someone of their doubts, their views, their self-delusion, and provide an experience of something beyond that will make all the difference?
