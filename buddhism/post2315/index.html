    Here is a prayer from the Tibetan Buddhist tradition. You can consider it a mantra if you like:

    >For whatever harm I have caused others, may they forgive me.

    >For whatever harm others have caused me, may I forgive them.

    >For whatever harm I have caused myself, I forgive myself.

    You can repeat the phrases wholeheartedly, 3 or 9 times, or in multiples of 9.

    ~

    Forgiveness is an important part of practice. Forgiveness of others and forgiveness of self are essential to purification, because if we are saddled with resentment that moves outward or inward, dominated by spite or shame or any of those kinds of agitation, we can't do our practice properly.

    So coming clear is a duty, an ethical responsibility. It is our job to get on with our practice of clarity, compassion, and integrity. Therefore we have these many forms, many rituals of repentance, forgiveness, self-forgiveness, and other kinds of purification. Resisting purification, even out of a sort of false humility, amounts to avoidance of right practice; and that's a form of laziness or even passive-aggressiveness. We should take every opportunity to clear out the garbage and start fresh.

    Another simple mantra used in the Theravada tradition is the formula:

    >May I be well
    May you be well

    It can also be rendered as:

    >May I be well
    May all beings be well

    These phrases are often used in the context of Metta (Lovingkindness) practice.

    Prostration practice is considered a kind of purification. It can be done simply, just by bowing to an altar or visualized or felt 'Buddha'; or it can be combined with going for refuge. It is a simple but profound practice that heals you and other living or dead beings.

    I've written at length on prostration pratice in other posts (you can search my posting history).

    In the refuge practice, you bow to a visualization of the Buddha or an enlightening being in Buddhism such as Kwan Yin or Tara, or any number of saints or masters. If you have a close connection to a guru you can regard them as a representative of Buddha, of the awakened mind. If you have a full-fledged altar, or a statue or painting or photograph of one of these representative beings, that is also great for this purpose. And barring any of that, just some representation of the Three Jewels (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha) is wonderful to bow to. (But you shouldn't bow to an object from nature unless it is completely clear to you that it is a representation of the Three Jewels. We don't bow to Nature, because Nature is part of Samsara.)

    Begin by remembering your compassionate motivation to wake up and help all other beings wake up and be free of suffering. Set your intention of healing and cleaning all your relationships. You can make a simple offering of candle-light, incense, or clear water if you like.

    Have the sense that not only are all enlightening and blessing beings there with you, also all beings (including your abuser) are taking refuge in them together. Feel or picture their presence and the presence of all beings near and far, going to the Three Jewels for help and protection.

    Recite the refuge vows in the language of your choice (English, Pali, or Sanskrit are popular, or you can use the language of your practice lineage). Here, Pali and English are interspersed; you can choose either one:

    >Buddham saranam gacchami
    I go to the Buddha for refuge.

    >Dhammam saranam gacchami
    I go to the Dhamma for refuge.

    >Sangham saranam gacchami
    I go to the Sangha for refuge.

    > Dutiyampi Buddham saranam gacchami
    For a second time, I go to the Buddha for refuge.
    ...(and so on)....

    >Tatiyampi Buddham saranam gacchami
    For a third time, I go to the Buddha for refuge.
    ...(and so on)....

    So you do three repetitions of the three refuge vows.

    As you recite one line, perform a full prostration, from standing with hands in prayer posture to kneeling with hands and head touching the mat or floor. Then stand back up. Bowing down gives you the opportunity to let go. Standing up gives you the opportunity to return to be renewed.

    Have the sense that all beings are bowing with you, in humility, sincerity, respect, and supplication for healing blessings.

    When you finish, do one final standing bow, confident and happy that you have been received by the Buddha and field of enlightening beings, and that you and all others have received the benefit of the practice.

    If you are a parent or if you have ever been any kind of teacher or caregiver, you know how natural it is to wish to help those who come to you. How much more so does the perfectly, boundlessly loving Buddha wish to do so! So we can be confident if we bring ourselves forth with the proper attitude, of course we will receive blessing, healing, and refuge. So when you finish the practice, you have given oversight of your issues to the Buddha; you can now let go.
