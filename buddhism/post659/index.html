    What exactly is "Zen"?

    You are quite right that Zen is not what one thinks they understand about it. It's beyond understanding or not understanding. But contrary to much of the talk and writing that obscure it, Zen is very real, very present, and of utmost significance to us.

    For instance, you know how to use a fork without thinking about it. If you think you know what a fork is just because you know the word "fork", that is delusion. You don't know where this matter and form came from and where they go. But when you use the fork, that is a knowing that is beyond words.

    Similarly, when someone calls your name you can respond instantly. If you try to define yourself in words and thoughts, you could talk for your whole life and never reach it; but someone calls and you answer — that is a knowing that is beyond words.

    Zen is like this. Very plain and simple. God, Buddha, Tao, Spirit, Mind, Self are all there, before thinking appears.

    >Is it zen, or is the understanding of it being drastically abused by what seems to be an adolescent attempt to formulate the most preposterous dichotomy of meaning possible?

    I couldn't agree more that posing and obfuscation are rampant in discussions about Zen. It's so disappointing, because all it takes to save ourselves from that idiocy is sincerity — the sincerity to admit that we don't know. And that's where Zen is: in that sincerity, real life appears by itself.

    It is not Zen that is frustrating, it is our attachment to words and concepts. Our own minds are too busy to see what is simple. Our own minds are dissatisfied, so we can't accept that this is it. A real, simple moment can't be touched by words; but that is profoundly terrifying to the ego that seeks to pin down and control experience. So returning to the real moment means the momentary death of the ego. Those who are not willing to make that exchange instead substitute clever phrases and smoke-and-mirrors sayings, taking glee in not having to be responsible for meaningful communication.

    You know, Zen is far more easily transmitted in live, person-to-person contact. These online forums are maybe the worst possible place to learn about Zen. When you witness how a Zen Master carries him/herself, or when you pull weeds and till earth in silence for 90 days, or when you taste tea as if for the first time ever, or when you find the response to a koan emerging from you in the presence of your teacher... then you experience some of Zen directly, without the need for explanation. And in fact, when you experience something plainly, no amount of explaining can add a single thing.

    So if I say that Zen is your ass on a chair this moment (or if you're standing, your feet on the ground this moment), that is not an obscure or poetic statement. There is nothing confusing about that until your own mind starts meddling with it. Maybe you think "that can't be Zen, there's nothing special there." Maybe you look for some meaning besides your ass on the chair. That confusion comes from what you add to the situation. Originally, the situation is clear: just your ass on the chair. Moment to moment, each thing is true. That real life is called "Zen".

    There is no other moment than this; there is no other truth than this. But can the mind be simple enough to allow it?
