    Is it the writing style of Thich Nhat Hanh's Heart of Buddha's teaching, or my understanding of it that is confusing? Any other recommended books in this style?

    Wow, Thich Nhat Hanh has written so many books, I wouldn't know where to start in terms of reading recommendations.

    But this is important: When you have eaten a meal, you need to give space so that digestion can happen. If you only keep eating, thinking that more eating will help you to digest, soon it will all be out of balance and no matter how much you eat you won't be able to retain it. Furthermore, the excess food will actually become like a poison, and harm the body.

    Similarly, when you take in teachings or any information, the most important part is how you digest it. It doesn't help to take in more, more, more. First digest one thing deeply; then you can move on.

    Unknowing is what digests your experience. When you receive a lot of information, your object-attaching habit gets stronger; you have to let that go, and counteract it; be free of object attachment. The name for this is Unknowing, or open awareness. Unknowing will digest anything you take in or go through. And when your experience is properly digested it becomes wisdom. That wisdom is very clear and stable; it is yours, and can't be challenged or upset or taken away.

    So digestion is important. You should do several parts practice to one part reading. That is the correct proportion. You will understand much, much more by getting your own practice experience. And your understanding will become much, much more functional when you repeatedly let go of it (your understanding, that is). That is to say, don't try to hold on to things and establish a 'philosophy of the universe'; don't fool yourself into thinking your know something. Cherish Unknowing.

    If you commit to doing several parts practice to one part reading, you will progress very fast. Other people may get a lot of ideas and so-called knowledge; but all that thinking won't function well — they won't be able to use it to change their character, relate better with others, and wake up out of delusion. In fact, the thinking will add delusion — spiritual-style delusion — and become a poison, like too much food. Get the proper balance, and make use of Unknowing, and your wisdom will grow. You can trust that.

    Assuming you undertake that balanced approach, one book I'd recommend to you is What The Buddha Taught by Walpola Rahula. It's a very well organized explanation of the basics of Buddhism from the Theravada tradition (which is a valid basis through all Buddhist traditions). The book has a scholarly tone, but is written in regular language and isn't bereft of inspiration. It may give you enough of a framework to help make sense of other works by other authors.

    In terms of Zen books that are very well organized, the magnum opus is Compass Of Zen by Zen Master Seung Sahn, which gives an overview of Theravada, Mahayana, and Zen, and how they support each other, presented in logical progression (albeit from Master Seung Sahn's approach).

    Another author of Zen books who presents material in a very organized way is Zen Master Robert Aitken. The warmth in his writing takes on the character of very dignified, but full-hearted, devotion. Taking The Path Of Zen and Mind Of Clover are classics.
