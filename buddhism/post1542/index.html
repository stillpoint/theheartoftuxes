    TheHeartofTuxes

    Are you willing to take on painful karmic consequences for the sake of others? How deep does compassion go?

    Remember that stopping a hijacker may save the lives of others, but it also saves the hijacker from generating tremendous negative karma himself/herself.

    If you are willing to suffer so that others can be saved, that is a level of compassion that turns around negative karma: the intention-action (literally, karma) is very pure and not bound to the idea of self and to self-gain.

    These kinds of questions tend to perpetuate a rule-based approach to life and spiritual practice — one seeks to gather the complete list of 'dos and don'ts' and then access it at each turn of life; but that is a low-class approach, and the list can never be complete enough or flexible enough to address all situations, and one will never be able to access it flawlessly in every eventuality. Although the rules are necessary at a certain level of practice, beyond that level they constrain the spontaneity and flow of life itself.

    The ideas of right and wrong are contrived for the sake of low-class consciousness that doesn't yet understand basic right and wrong and hasn't yet fully met the mind of wisdom. For example, we may tell children that they shouldn't do a certain action "because it is wrong"; but later when they understand causality better we can explain the reasons and do away with the label 'wrong'.

    Originally these categories don't exist; before the cause-and-effect of the manifest world, there is the pure emptiness underlying it (or shall we say, the pure emptiness of it). Rather than relying on a set of imperfect, contrived rules the higher path is to rely on wisdom itself — that pure, empty point. This means there is no way to know ahead of time what to do: clear action is completely momentary, in utter harmony with the unfolding situation. In this path, you are not moved by concepts that you hope to remember or the emotional and habitual reactions that usually take their place in times of crisis; instead you rely on the bright, spontaneous Unknowing of your original mind. It is this mind that responds with absolute appropriateness, compassion, and freedom from karma. Thus training in identifying, trusting, and embodying that mind is key.
