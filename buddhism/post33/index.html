    You're not wrong; but since it's only an idea, you're not right.

    Non-self is not an idea. Buddha pointed to it because he realized and attained it. If it's only an idea, already that's not it. If it's a conclusion, it can't embrace movement. If it's an opinion or belief, it's by nature already limited. But your true self is unlimited and all-embracing.

    Concepts are only more or less approximately in alignment with some part of reality. The interesting thing is to actually attain reality — to attain your self's/non-self's true nature, beyond all concepts.

    Furthermore, although your words could be thought of as something like 'correct', you may not understand what those words really point to. It is exceptionally easy to fool yourself into thinking you understand something, just because you've put a few words and thoughts together. This is the great illusion of self, after all: it's given a name, so we think of it as existing. So just coming up with your own ideas is not the path. The path is absorbing and embodying Dharma. For that, you need a proper, consistent teaching and practice — an influence that can continually reflect your mind back to you and weed out the ongoing errors, ingrown tendencies, and resident blind spots that plague you. If you try to do it yourself, the ongoing errors and ingrown tendencies and resident blind spots will be at the steering wheel.

    A stable beacon is necessary; the wavering mind that has not yet been purified is not qualified to guide you along the path.

    We always like to have something 'in hand'. ("This is my conclusion. Am I correct?") That's still the function of the illusory self-identity, trying to maintain its illusion, trying to gain a semblance of control. But if we really begin to understand the nature of self, we will begin to trust letting-go and letting-be. Instead of putting words into the mouth of self/non-self, let it speak for itself. That is very interesting.

    Even if a thousand people agreed with you and even if there were all kinds of scriptural evidence supporting your notion, that would be an empty prize for the ego: thought of as desirable, but not contributing satisfaction. Soon you would feel that constant gnawing uncertainty, that doubt that's always in the background. "I thought I had a conclusion; but my conclusion is not helping me."

    So a great Zen Master said, "Understanding can't help you."

    That is to say, it can only help in a limited way; it can't help you attain freedom and satisfaction.

    Therefore, practice is essential. Only having some ideas is not enough. Actual attainment is necessary; and that happens through putting down concepts, not by picking them up and holding them.

    Next, although appropriate understanding is an important basis for subsequent practice, getting a smattering of opinions from diverse strangers online is absolutely not the right way to develop right understanding. Many views and opinions, many levels of wisdom and ignorance, and many different conscious and unconscious agendas appear here, all mashed together; and you don't know how to tell the difference — both because the sources are anonymous and because you have not yet received the training and refined your perception (through practice) to the point where you can distinguish.

    So rather than asking potential idiots like me what they think, for such a very fundamental and important issue — the Great Issue Of Life And Death — you ought to go to someone who has seen into the matter, and who has been confirmed by other masters, and who has been trained to teach, and who has been confirmed as a teacher. And then you ought to listen and learn carefully, practice hard, and resolve the issue for yourself beyond mere concept and belief — beyond all doubt.
