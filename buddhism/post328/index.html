    I want to runaway to a temple. Help?

    You and me both, friend.

    There is a time to retreat and protect ourselves, and there is a time to stand fast and see things through. You may not always know which time you're in, but you should try: do you need to stop it all and re-set, or do you need to redouble your efforts and help move things forward? Each way has its repercussions!

    If you are aware that a monastery can't keep you from your issues (including the family dynamics that live in you, the family relationships that you will continue to have, and the self-responsibility that life will always demand from you), then you can go to a monastery.

    Monasteries are practice places. Conditions like schedule and environment are cultivated to eliminate distraction; but the purpose is not to run away. Rather, the purpose of the relatively distraction-free situation is to offer a very focused and energetic effort to the central point of life. Hard work is the lifeblood of a monastery.

    Monasteries and temples also have a very important relationship with society. They should not be places where people go to selfishly engage in their own personal pursuit of peace, but should be where people go to cultivate peace for the sake of everyone. Depending on the culture of that particular place of practice, they may be more open to outside visitors on one hand, or more protective of the residents' practice (and so more isolationist) on the other. But a strong practice place should have resources for someone like you, whose mind is in turmoil. The environment and hopefully a resident teacher or guide may at least be able to help you get your bearings enough to understand your next necessary steps.

    One thing you should not look for is a free ride. These days, with this economy, it is rare for a temple or monastery to offer free accommodation. If they are in a position to offer space or teaching, you should still expect to work hard either within the monastery or by finding a job outside in order to support yourself.

    Temples are not necessarily the same as monasteries; temples may not have residents, and in any case they will usually be more open to the public during daily activity or at specified times of worship.

    Other options besides 'getting away' to a monastery may be to find a practice group where you can be supported in your effort to clarify your own life through meditation practice, or therapeutic services like counseling or psychotherapy where you can actually talk through and process troubling patterns (check local social services info to see where you can get free counseling).

    I agree with you that the right emotional support can give you the strength to help yourself. Just know that in many monasteries the type of help that you get will focus on meditation practice and work practice — you may be left to work through things on your own via practice rather than getting a lot of one-on-one time with a guide.

    When your center is weak, then life's movements shove your mind around easily, and you lose your balance. In that case, retreating to a place where outside conditions are more supportive can be very helpful. This may mean a practice center, or it may just mean leaving a toxic environment and finding a better one elsewhere. But you will never find the perfect situation.

    When your center is strong, then life's movements won't move your mind as much, and you can engage with all kinds of situations. You will not depend on outside situations, and can in fact go into places where there is a lot of turmoil, in order to help. All imperfect situations will be no problem.

    What makes your center strong? Momentary unknowing, practiced repeatedly over the long term. In a moment, the effort of letting go of (not controlling) your mind helps it become a bit more peaceful and a bit more centered. When this is continued moment to moment, the peace and centeredness become established strongly. This should be your focus — to develop a strong center. Build your safe haven within yourself. Any environment or conditions you seek should only support that essential practice, the point should not be to run away from things.

    ...Even if we desperately want to run away. (I can relate!)
