    >Trying to wrap my brain around the idea of...

    Yeah, don't.

    Zen is doing, not figuring. Only put it all down; then all knowledge is already there.

    Repeat this, and the knowledge that is already there will deepen and eventually emerge in your thinking. But for the most part, thinking-understanding can't help you.

    >A zen teacher would be very helpful, I think, but the only group near me meets in the city at 6:30am.

    Does the group have a teacher? Not all groups are equally supportive for the new person learning Zen.

    Don't accept that there is no way. If you make "I can't" then your result is "I can't". If you make "I can", then your result is "I can". Contact the group. See if there are provisions. See if there are occasional workshops or daytime sittings. See if folks ever travel to a teacher or invite a teacher.

    Travel to meet a teacher, if necessary. One meeting can put an entire life into motion. Some teachers will give instruction by email or Skype, while other parts of training will require face-to-face meeting. You might start on basics and then in the future when someone else can take care of your child you can have a day or weekend devoted to learning from a Zen teacher.

    >Should I stop reading about Zen and trying to intellectualize it and just focus on meditation?

    Yes, stop reading about Zen and trying to intellectualize. Before you know how to practice, reading makes more and more problems. It will be more difficult to learn and practice with a lot of undigested concept in the head.

    After you know how to practice, and if you can maintain practice that doesn't attach to thinking, then little by little certain readings can fill in the conception. But the readings should align with the style of practice you are doing. Different styles are different for good reasons; and the teachings and recommendations don't always go together, or the words are not always used in the same ways.

    It is not the book that makes the headache; it is the grasping, concept-making mind. If you don't grasp and don't make anything, then everything can teach you — book or no book. But when you try to have something to hold onto — whether it's an opinion or a feeling, a state of mind or a possession, a relationship or an identity — then you send yourself to hell.

    Zen is not holding anything. Can you do that?

    Sometimes a meditation method has one single, simple form that you return to in order to let go of all other holding. So it may seem like there is one thing to hold onto. But even then, the returning should not involve grasping or making. Only return to the method very simply, with a plain, unadorned, almost stupid mind. Don't think about what you want or what you lack or what you should be. If you don't make anything, you and your life moment are already complete. That is the main point.
