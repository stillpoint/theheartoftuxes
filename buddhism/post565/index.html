    Death, like birth, is considered an inherently sacred time, when the gateway between realms opens and meaning is brought into sharp focus (if one can manage to avoid despair and confusion).

    On the occasion of someone's death, it is common to do mantra or chanting to help guide the mind-stream of the deceased person, and to help practitioners use the occasion to spur them to awakening or at least tremendously increase their connection to Dharma, and to help all survivors experience compassion, intimacy, and peace.

    It is customary to chant or recite the Heart Sutra (Sanskrit version at the bottom of this post)

    or

    the final mantra of the Heart Sutra:
    >gate gate paragate parasamgate bodhi svaha

    >(gone gone gone beyond completely gone beyond awake evoke)

    or

    repeating the name of the Bodhisattva Ksitigarbha, who ferries people through the realms after death:
    >jijang bosal, jijang bosal, jijang bosal....

    ~

    In some traditions it is considered very important to maintain an atmosphere of calm equanimity and Dharmic intent even well after one's passing. It is said that the deceased still lingers around the body and around dear ones (the mind-stream tends to follow what it attached to strongly in life); and in order to support clear passage toward clarity and awakening it's important for survivors not to engender confusion, great distress, and clinging.

    Calling out for the person to come back is far from a lovely, romantic sentiment; in fact, it is said to be a confusing influence leading to suffering for the deceased. We should instead practice openhandedness and clarity, encouraging the dying or dead person to move on freely and clearly, without attaching to form. This gives them the greatest chance of direct awakening or beneficial rebirth. Confusion and clinging at the time of death can lead the deceased to rebirth in a hell realm or other low and suffering rebirth.

    That's not to say that honest emotion is wrong. Suppression also has a confusing influence. Rather, rigorous honesty, clarity, and focus is called for. At this time when the deceased has lost the stabilizing effect of the body and entered uncertainty, the focus and prayers of the living can serve as a beacon. That is why Buddhist funeral ceremonies first call the deceased, make them comfortable with kind speech and sentiment, and then beseech them to be clear and not attach to anything (and bells are rung to transmit the awakening wish).

    • On Contemplation of Death and The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind.

    >There are two contemplative practices in Buddhism that very strongly clarify direction and build motivation. The first of these is The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind (toward Dharma and away from Samsara). The second is Meditation On Death. Many masters credit these practices with maintaining the motivation that allowed them to reach awakening. Both are performed as daily practices by laymen and monks alike.

    ~

    >Death meditation can certainly be beneficial in the right context. In the wrong context, without the proper supporting attitude and teaching, it could prove too provocative for some people, and could drive them in the other direction, causing them to write off the whole issue as too morbid or overwhelming.

    > The Human Route

    >Coming empty-handed; going empty-handed: that is human.

    >When you are born, where do you come from?

    >When you die, where do you go?

    >Life is like a cloud that appears.

    >Death is like a cloud that disperses.

    >Originally, the cloud itself does not exist.

    >Life and death, coming and going are also like this.

    >But there is one thing that remains clear.

    >It is pure and clear, not depending on life and death.

    >Then what is this one pure and clear thing?
