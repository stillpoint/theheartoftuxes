    It's not that you have no control, but that you have a great deal of control: your life unfolds according to what you have intended and done previously. Your mind makes your life.

    'Buddhism' is a religion with many paths and voices; 'it' doesn't say anything by itself.

    Suicide goes against the first precept: refrain from taking life. There are serious karmic consequences to killing. There are serious karmic consequences to strongly attempting to avoid a situation. The intensity of effect varies directly with intensity and frequency of cause.

    Aggression/avoidance is one of the 'Three Poisons' -- afflicting attitudes which are guaranteed to lead to suffering. The Three Poisons are grasping desire, aggression, and ignorance. Simply put, in trying to clutch at what we think we like, or trying to destroy or avoid what we think we don't like, or ignoring what we regard as neutral, we create the causes for our suffering and the suffering of others.

    If suicide is an avoidance and killing, it leads to suffering. If it is an intense avoidance and killing, it leads to intense suffering. But some Zen Masters, for instance, will choose or be aware of their time of death. Nominally it may be seen as a kind of suicide, but it is an exceptionally surrendered and harmonious event, with no 'Poison' involved -- no grasping or avoiding, and with utterly aware presence. In this case, no karma is made, and certainly not negative karma.

    There are also rare cases of suicide committed for the benefit of many others, such as the self-immolations performed by monks in order to focus world attention on acts of injustice. In these cases, presumably the monks' minds were in deep serenity of samadhi, and their acts were not selfish; so negative results would not be incurred.

    Depending on other mental conditions and situational factors, and on the site of positive or negative merit one has accrued, a violent suicide could lead to tormented rebirth as a hungry ghost or animal. If one has accumulated the merit for it (thorough Dharma practice and compassionate deeds), one might be fortunately reborn as a human, but with ongoing repercussions of self-harm or other kinds of suffering that would eventually unfold.

    One can't escape the results of one's own intention and action.

    How you go to sleep conditions your nighttime existence. How you wake conditions your day. Much more so does your mind-state at death condition the after-death transition.

    How you live your days and how you keep your mind moment to moment, day by day, year by year, sculpts the shape of your future. This is very clear; anyone can see this cause-and-effect relationship if they look clearly. The subtle impressions accumulated in this way are all that you have at death; everything else is stripped away. But how you keep your mind at the moment of death (in Indo-Tibetan terms: which orifice the consciousness leaves the body through) determines how the mind stream will be propelled, and whether it will follow a relatively obscured, or relatively liberating, or completely liberating path.
