    I want to try out chanting. Where do I start?

    Besides the many chanting videos on Youtube, here's a page with mp3s and text to follow along. You can try longer chants, or you can do mantra practice out loud (which is known as japa practice in Hinduism). The main thing is that you stay present and do it sincerely. Just use your voice to help yourself and others.

    Chanting practice is traditionally done for long periods, anywhere from 10 or 20 minutes to hour long, several hours long, overnight, day-long, weekend or week of day-long, and more. If you want, you can practice by duration; but it's not required. You can also just use your voice whenever you find some time or have the need or desire to practice.

    Chanting is especially good for changing your mood and purifying your emotions. It is also considered a practice of Right Speech, one of the eight elements of the Eightfold Path. It is a remarkable practice in that it can be performed very simply and easily, but can also be a sophisticated and sometimes esoteric path (toward purification and beneficial expression through the Speech aspect of Body-Speech-Mind).

    Chanting also connects to shamanic and energetic practices, some of which predate Buddhism but were absorbed into its repertoire in various locales.

    When you first start chanting dharanis (long mantras), it may take all your concentration just to follow along, and you may not even be able to keep up with some chants. That's no problem; only keep going. You are still training your sincere mind, your concentration, your breath, and your indomitable flow â€” the mind that keeps going no matter what.

    Once you start to memorize the chant it will be easier to follow along. Then the task will be to keep returning to the chant and not letting your mind wander. Since you no longer have to use all your resources just to follow, the mind will tend to move to other things; so you just bring it back to chanting wholeheartedly.

    In some respect, just repeating the chanting will grow your practice, and there's nothing special you have to do. But do notice when attention wanders or when insincerity creeps in; then let it go and return.

    If you want to practice Zen style, once the chanting has become fairly one-pointed â€” that is, the mind doesn't wander much and you simply chant â€” you can once in a while gently turn the attention back to the source of mind: Where is the chanting sound appearing? Who is doing the chanting? What or where is the mind that is aware of this? These are all the same question. The important part isn't the words, but the open looking, the questioning attitude or Unknowing as you turn back to find the center of your experience, the center of yourself. No need to expect something in particular to show up or be grasped, but just occasionally gently look into the one mind behind it all and rest in that Unknowing.

    But if you don't want to practice that style, just simply chanting is very good. You can do it in meditation posture while sitting or standing formally; you can do it devotionally, pouring your positive or negative emotions into it and letting it clean them; and you can do it throughout the day just to bring clarity and joy into your life and the life of others.

    Chanting is considered one way of helping those you can't help otherwise. You may not be able to change someone's situation immediately, for instance, but your chanting plants the seeds of awakening in them. Just hearing the name of a Buddha or Bodhisattva sets a person on a path of inevitable awakening in future lives.
