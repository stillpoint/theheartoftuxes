    What I'm seeing in this thread is the gap between theorizing and experience. Those who have advanced significantly (and verifiably) in training understand the issue, because they see it in their own mind and in the way things play out in community. Those who practice in isolation only have their own opinion to go on, which weaves bias into their experience.

    Those convinced by their own 'reasonable' arguments don't recognize their underlying assumptions. Without the reflection and feedback of a clear-eyed teacher these assumptions ferment in the dark and produce all kinds of mistaken views.

    One such assumption is that one necessarily recognizes one's own enlightenment. This is not the case. And likewise, what many think of as their own enlightenment is often nothing of the sort. It takes a great being to recognize a great being. But someone who only thinks of themselves as a great being will create views based on that concept rather than clearing away views, from the basis of attainment.

    There is a tradition of circumspect reticence and humility among masters, because masters actually share attainment and perceive the effect of self-referential speech on others. There is a tendency toward pride and self-trumpeting among isolated practitioners because despite their sometimes true insight experiences those experiences get couched in their self-referential frameworks and unchecked views.

    Shallowness can't conceive of depth; but depth can embrace shallowness.

    I've encountered enlightened beings who, in intimate private exchange, have spoken of their attainment. It was appropriate; it was compassionate; it was tremendously beneficial (life-changing); it reflected the mind of the questioner back to them; and it was aimed precisely, like a hunter's arrow piercing the heart of the prey.

    I've also encountered a disappointing, tiring litany of proclamations of enlightenment from a long, long line of self-deluded people who make things worse not only for themselves but for those who encounter them.

    The distance and difference between the two extremes makes me cry.

    And the continued good teaching of kind, experienced, attained practitioners here, despite the many clouded arguments of others, makes me thankful. The fact that these noble brothers and sisters don't themselves lazily trumpet their attainment as a short-cut ("trust me; I'm a Dharma teacher") is worthy of honor. I bow to you.
