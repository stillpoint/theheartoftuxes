    >I honestly don't know how to feel about it.

    That is appropriate. Your feelings are your feelings; they are true in themselves, regardless of what you or anyone else think of them. They have their own inscrutable life, their own mysterious power.

    Feelings are like the Trickster that is sometimes benign and sometimes hostile, but nevertheless brings awareness and new life.

    Although we do our best to work through negative emotions and move on from past events, still we don't have total control over feelings. Not only do we have a right to emotional sincerity, it is also most healthy and practical to receive feelings with spaciousness and compassion.

    Fear brings awareness. Anger protects the organism. Sadness purifies. Relief follows when we give feelings the space to move. Love emerges when we own our feelings completely.

    Revenge is an intent, which is different than feeling. There is often an honorable desire to 'take the high road' and become spotlessly pure in our response to past traumas; but healing requires us to be honest about where we are in the process. The dispassionate meditative part and the passionate will to live co-exist. We can commit to non-violence while still acknowledging grief and rage (for instance), and letting them be expressed.

    The issue seems to be finding the proper venue or channel of expression, the safe container for all truths to be experienced and released. We just avoid acting out unconsciously or irresponsibly. Rage, for instance, is not directed at someone else; but it can still be owned and expressed â€” in art, in therapy, in journaling, in life activity, or (if one's practice is deep enough) in sitting with it. Pain can be transformed through expression. And indeed, these feelings need to come to the surface and move on before we can really be free of them. "You can't change what you don't acknowledge."

    Not knowing what to do with the situation is not necessarily wrong. We have this conditioning that says we always need to have something to do, we always have to be in control. That's just ego. Actually, Buddha does it; Buddha takes care of everything. So your not-knowing is okay. Give it to Buddha.

    You can sing it out or create visual art or dance it. You can let the whole truth live in dialog with a trusted ally, like a therapist. You can perform rituals of giving-over to Buddha by doing altar practice and prostrations (for example).

    There are many ways, but the two basic phases are (1) express it, and (2) move on.

    It is also very helpful to have a ritual of returning, a way of closing cathartic sessions and finding groundedness again. Perform this ritual after art, therapy, or practice that visits with powerful emotions. For some, this may involve washing hands, face, and feet; for others, it may be taking a walk with mindfulness of the feet on the ground, or chanting a vow, or doing some yoga. Whatever your way of returning to simple reality. Make use of the senses to bring you back. Feeling water or earth, smelling incense, hearing a bell ring... what helps you return to simply being here and now?
