    Zen practice is based on compassion, radical openness to reality, and concentration. All of these do better with open eyes.

    In the first case, rather than withdrawing into one's own small circle, one merges inner and outer, showing up for all beings. The movement of the heart doesn't circle back into smallness, but moves out to all beings. Thus the representatives of perfect meditation and compassionate activity in Buddhism, the Buddhas and Bodhisattvas, are all depicted with open or half-open eyes, either radiating their blessings from stillness or poised and ready to respond.

    In the next case, rather than holding on to a particular stance (inner) or state (sleepy comfort), one allows all ordinary and extraordinary things to be what they are, with one's full awareness.

    And in the third case, open eyes both supports brightness and trains concentration in the presence of everyday phenomena, which is necessary if one is to grow the practice beyond the boundaries of the sitting mat.

    Practically, keeping the eyes open has a tremendously positive influence on wakefulness and clarity. This becomes more telling and more significant as one progresses, especially during long sittings and retreats. Meditators who close their eyes almost inevitably drift off in some way, and seldom know that they are drifting off; the vague soft feeling simply feels more comfortable, and because it is pleasant they tend to attach to it and think of it as 'good meditation'. This is a mistake.

    Open eyes also trains the meditator in the ability to be with the waking world; that is, one doesn't seek to run away from or control things, but establishes one's stability and freedom within 'the ten thousand things' of life.

    The exceptions to this rule are as follows: some meditative methods with a different aim, such as deep relaxation, visualization, inner energetic work, or dream work; and rare cases that could be called 'advanced', where the meditator has exceptionally strong and clear motivation — motivation that very naturally doesn't ebb or waver — as well as the ability to concentrate almost without interruption.
