    Avalokiteshvara is the compassion aspect of your own original mind. The pure awareness that is without intent, without object-relationship, is the attainment of Avalokiteshvara. If you keep that mind, without contriving any way of trying to be or seem or feel something in particular, that is the true practice. It is called Unknowing.

    All Mahayana, Vajrayana, and Zen training proceeds on this basis; it is all predicated on the original mind of compassion. Theravada also, but it is not overt in the teaching. But if you let go of intention ('I' am doing 'practice', 'I' am making 'something' happen) and only do what you are doing 100%, that is already compassion. This is not easy for practitioners to understand, especially when they are habituated to practices that depend on forms and rules.

    There is a Mahayana/Zen practice known as the Kwan Yin method that traces sound back to its source (the uncontrived mind). Although it is a method, once one has traced back to the source it amounts to the same thing: resting in the primordial state of awareness. There are many methods that return to that state, whether through circuitous steps or very directly by dropping everything in a moment and flashing awareness.

    Informally, since you have a karmic connection with Avalokiteshvara, you can keep Avalokiteshvara in your heart and mind. In the outer aspect, you can realize the Bodhisattva above your head or in the space in front of you at all times. You can also keep a picture or figure as a home for the enlightened being, and use it to connect to that mind. In the inner-inner aspect, you can maintain clear, uncontrived mind, moment to moment.

    Mantra is good. Om Mani Padme Hum. Chanting is good. The Great Compassion Dharani, or repeating the name Avalokiteshvara (or Kwanseum Bosal, or variants in different languages). Bowing and taking refuge with Avalokiteshvara as the object of focus representing the Three Jewels is good. Circumambulating an Avalokiteshvara figure or sacred site is good. /u/Little_Morry has already mentioned Avalokiteshvara sadhana, and training from the Bodhisattvacaryavatara. There's also a fasting retreat that goes along with the sadhana. (It's said that if you complete this retreat having practiced sincerely, you are guaranteed not to be born in lower realms in the future.) But these all bring you back to the basic point, which is your own innate mind.

    Doing generous, compassionate, suffering-reducing action for yourself and others is of course the expression of Avalokiteshvara in relative terms. But the 'I' is not the one doing compassion. This is a very important point. Many people around the world think very reasonably that they will help others with their idea, their effort, their way of doing things. This is actually a kind of violence. It originates from duality that assumes a self, an other, a broken situation, and an action that is supposed to cure the situation. This is already very fragmented! But we must realize that what originates from a separate self is imperfect. Even ISIS warriors think they are helping the world with their idea! But of course, it is an imposition; it comes from a place of duality and ignorance. So first putting down self and all aims and opinions is necessary. Return to the mind that is not made. Then that mind/heart can function as compassion.

    In other words, true compassion doesn't have an aim. It is not the parent's concept and subsequent imposition of what should be happening in the child's life. It is the parent's open listening and showing up in all kinds of support — gentle or fierce, as the situation demands — without any prejudgment. This comes from the original pure mind.
