    Can Buddhism replace psychology

    Your question is not that different from asking whether Buddhism can replace car mechanics. Well, yes, but why?

    Many great Buddhist teachers are also psychologists or psychiatrists. I know of several enlightened beings who also have expertise in psychology. It would seem that the two could enhance each other.

    The Three Jewels of Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha should be the only refuge in Buddhism, but that doesn't mean you never use anything. You don't stop breathing or eating or living under a roof because 'Buddha is my refuge'.

    You just have to use things wisely. If you need medicine to bring you back to health and balance so that you can practice and live well, then taking the medicine may be a good step. When the side effects of medicine begin to interfere with clarity or contribute to addictive behavior, you have to use your best judgment and discriminating wisdom to decide when to continue and when to stop... and when to rely on other options... and how much. These decisions can be informed by the guidance of those who are expert in their respective fields: doctors, psychologists, psychiatrists, alternative therapists, other meditators who have gone through what you're going through, Buddhist teachers, enlightened beings, and so on.

    It's also apparent that some Buddhist teachers have less experience and knowledge regarding psychology; and their opinions in matters beyond their area of expertise should be taken with a grain of salt. In fact, if a teacher dogmatically states things that he/she is not qualified to comment on, that should serve as a warning sign. It doesn't necessarily mean you have to write the teacher off, but it could mean you need to help them wake up to their blind spot, or at least take caution with their teaching and not follow it blindly yourself.

    The aspect in which Buddhism is a 'better' medicine than much of psychology is that it can offer liberation from the self, altogether. While psychology has many skillful ways of healing and teaching (which could enhance one's ability to do Buddhist practice) and in many ways offers skillful remedies not in the repertoire of some Buddhist teachers, these remedies all tend to aim at the psyche of a supposed separate self: it's conditional healing, whereas Buddhism has the ability to uproot the whole original mistake about self, undercutting the entire issue that most psychology addresses.

    But we still need to live and function in the world. Going back to my original analogy, Buddhism can overturn your dependence on any possession, so if your car breaks down its no problem! Maybe you lose your job because you can't travel to work, but that also is no problem! Even dying is no problem! But also staying alive, fixing your car, and keeping your job is 'no problem', and allows you to carry out your good activity and awakening practice in this life; so you go to a mechanic. In Buddhism you can use medicines and remedies appropriately.

    ~

    Edit: Yes, it can be said that since mind is primary, it is possible to cure anything with mind alone. But there are two important points here:

    First, what level of attainment is necessary for that to happen? Even exceptional, luminous teachers have fallen to cancer and now AIDS.

    Second, doing miracles like this is wrong practice. If this path is recommended, it is an outside path and you should move on. Great masters often have the ability to perform miracles, but they should not flaunt those abilities. The correct miracle is opening your eyes when you wake up and closing your eyes when you go to sleep.

    Your own well being depends on your own karma, which no one else can take away. That should be the central aim of your practice: clarifying your karma through your regular, everyday life. If you clarify your karma, your conditions for practice, including your health, will improve in this life or in future lives.

    ~
