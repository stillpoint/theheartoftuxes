    TheHeartofTuxes

    Various schools have different forms; you can choose any. With experience you tend to find that the general intent is the same across various schools, and that the building blocks, as it were, are all there.

    First thing in the morning, or when you start your practice, you can offer clear water and/or candlelight and/or incense to the Three Jewels represented on your altar. If you don't have an altar you can visualize it. What you make real in your mind is actually real.

    1. Begin by revisiting and regenerating your intent. This has two aspects: devotion and compassion. On this point, the great master Khandro Rinpoche has this to say:

    >Devotion is best expressed in the form of a homage; and a homage is best expressed as the strong determination to truly work with one’s own mind to transform this precious human existence into a vehicle of liberation from delusion for oneself and others.

    >Through that devotion, generate the second essential quality, which is bodhichitta: the strong intention to make every effort on the path of realization to be for the single purpose of liberating all sentient beings from suffering.

    1. It's not mandatory, but it's a very, very good daily practice to consider The Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind away from Samsara and toward Dharma; or to incorporate some other form of recognition of death and impermanence and the preciousness of this human life and the opportunity to practice in this day, in this moment. Alternative contemplations are The Five Recollections and The Nine Point Meditation On Death. I list The Four Thoughts below:

    >The freedoms and the favorable conditions
    of this (human birth) are extremely difficult to obtain.

    >Everything born is impermanent and bound to die.

    >The results of virtuous and unvirtuous actions (which are causes) are inexorable.

    >The three realms of cyclic existence have the nature of an ocean of suffering.

    You can look up The Four Thoughts or the other contemplations on many sites online. Millions of people around the world do this practice every day.

    1. There are three simple versions of going for refuge:

    >Namo Buddhaya
    Namo Dharmaya
    Namo Sanghaya

    (Literally, 'honor to' or 'homage to' or 'hail' Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha.)

    or

    >Buddham saranam gacchami
    Dhammam saranam gacchami
    Sangham saranam gacchami

    >Dutiyampi Buddham saranam gacchami
    Dutiyampi Dhammam saranam gacchami
    Dutiyampi Sangham saranam gacchami

    >Tatiyampi Buddham saranam gacchami
    Tatiyampi Dhammam saranam gacchami
    Tatiyampi Sangham saranam gacchami

    ("To the Buddha, to that refuge, I go. To the Dharma, to that refuge, I go. To the Sangha, to that refuge, I go." "...For the second time, to the Buddha...." "...For the third time....")

    The second version is more specifically going for refuge. It is the same recitation across many different schools of Buddhism.

    or

    With prostrations:

    >(Bowing down)
    From now until attaining the heart of enlightenment
    I take refuge in [the Lama,] the Three Jewels.

    >(Standing up)
    From now until samsara becomes empty
    I shall accomplish the benefits and happiness of all sentient parent beings.

    This last form is common in Vajrayana Buddhism, and involves the three practices of body, speech, and mind (you prostrate with the body, you speak the vows, and you visualize all Buddhas in front of you and all beings taking refuge together with you). One may include refuge in the Lama, who is one's guru or the great enlightening being one chooses (such as the patriarch of a lineage or a great Bodhisattva), who are considered to embody all three Jewels — when you go for refuge to the Lama, you automatically take refuge in the Three Jewels.

    Other schools have more involved refuge ceremonies. Korean Zen, for instance, has a longer chant including not only the Three Jewels but also specifically acknowledging certain Bodhisattvas, and groups of great beings and Arhats, all while performing 108 prostrations.

    1. Then you can do your chosen practice, or finish and continue your day.

    2. To finish, in order not to waste or taint your practice, you can recite a verse of dedication, that whatever benefit you gain from practice is shared with all beings everywhere. This verse comes from the Jataka tales of Buddha's previous lives:

    >Through this merit, may all beings attain the omniscient state of enlightenment,
    And conquer the enemy of faults and delusion,
    May they all be liberated from this ocean of samsara
    And from its pounding waves of birth, old age, sickness and death!

    This is a good and complete practice session. Practitioners from various traditions will recognize its elements in their own daily ceremonies.
