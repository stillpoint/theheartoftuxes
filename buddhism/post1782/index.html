    Could somebody explain to me the various texts?

    The distinction you offer may be okay as a generalization, if we recognize it as one; but the central point is to perceive and attain this "Buddha", this awakened one, yourself.

    As the famous martial artist Ip Man said, it's about the person, not the school. Differences between Hina-, Maha-, and Vajra-"yanas" posited in popular writing don't play out as significantly in real life. The main issue is how the individual digests the teaching and embodies it.

    While there is much evidence for a historical Buddha, who was a person like the rest of us, the historical Buddha himself cautioned us not to mistake his form with who he really is. The body and speech of the historical Buddha are not the real Buddha.

    So what is Buddha? What is the awakened mind?

    All kinds of sutras and other teachings may point there in their own way, but they can't actually take you there. So the best place for you to start — and a good place for you to start whenever you study — is letting go of the grip and putting it all down. First, clear mind; then teaching can be digested. If teaching arrives before clear mind, it just becomes garbage. It congeals into opinion and becomes your big problem.

    ~

    If you want to follow a systematic approach, I might recommend working with an overview text. Walpola Rahula's What The Buddha Taught organizes the Pali material in terms of the basics of Buddhism, with much reference to original sources. Once the basic framework is there you might venture to related sutras.

    A brilliant overview of Theravada, Mahayana, and Zen paths (although with the flavor of his particular school) is Zen Master Seung Sahn's Compass Of Zen.

    Many Tibetan schools will innately provide this overview, since their training is organized in that way. If it's a good fit for you, you might do very well to read the scriptures in concert with actual practice and study within a Tibetan school of meditation.

    The other way, of course — and a valid one — is to follow your own interest. What is going on in your own life, your own awareness? Studying sutras that address your alive situation may bring them home that much more. The risk, of course, is that study may be less complete and that one may ignore the things one always tends to ignore.

    In terms of original sources, the traditional beginning would be the sutta recording teachings that Buddha first expounded after his enlightenment, the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta, "Setting In Motion The Wheel Of Dharma". After that, the Adittapariyaya Sutta (Fire Sermon), the Anapanasati Sutta, and the Dhammapada are all good subsequent sources generally associated with Theravada but forming some of the foundation of Buddhism at large.
