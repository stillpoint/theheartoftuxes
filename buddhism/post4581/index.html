    If you get rid of all of your wants, how do you still want to live?

    >>"There is nothing actually graspable in life."

    >Then why reach for anything?

    Since nothing is graspable in life, you can actually do something. Movement is possible because of it. You make effort toward fulfillment, though you realize it is not something you can hold.

    Depression, false neutrality, doubt, and giving up are all forms of laziness.

    ~

    The detachment of numbness and avoidance is very different from the equanimity developed in practice. But those who practice alone, or who indulge in thinking more than they practice, often have difficulty distinguishing between the two.

    You will have trouble getting out of deep-seated patterns on your own. The issue is that the one guiding the practice is the one with the blind spot. Practicing with a teacher and group will provide a way to regularly get the teaching that provides correct view, and regularly get checked and saved from wandering far from the path.

    Underlying neurotic patterns co-opt practice just as they influence the rest of our lives. Addictive habits or patterns of resistance, avoidance, and denial often control the way practice is undertaken. The meditator may think he/she is using the mind in a reasonable manner, but the secret agenda of neurosis can be running the show. Once it starts to come clear that there's an issue, many meditators will quite sincerely put more energy into mediation; but if the mindset or approach of meditation is already flawed, transformation won't be possible. Or if meditation itself is an avoidance of other issues and activities, just doing more meditation plays into the pattern of avoidance.

    So some psychological insight and processing may be required in order to continue meditation with integrity.

    You have a lot of thinking and opinion-making about Buddha and his enlightenment. You and I are inconceivably far from that state; it doesn't avail to speculate about it. The Buddha was not like we are; there was absolutely no trace of the illusion of selfhood in him. Even beyond realizing his true nature, he had eliminated even the most subtle habit or reactive tendency. His mind was pure and boundless in ways that we can't know, even if we have spectacularly revelatory awakening, because we still have countless impurities functioning in us -- the result of lifetimes of ignorant activity.

    So you don't know why and how the Buddha acted. There was no trace of intent in him, no self to devise a strategy, no other to respond to. Self and other were (are) completely embraced in his boundless awareness. In any case, you are not Buddha, so you can't draw any conclusion about how you should act from his action.

    If you look at the lives of enlightened beings, both the luminaries throughout history and the ones alive now, you will see tremendous energetic activity and devotion. For example, the great master Tsongkhapa performed three and a half million prostrations in his lifetime, continuing practice well after his original enlightenment. A once or twice daily practice along with long stretches of discursive thinking simply can't compare with this kind of effort.

    By far the majority of masters have been very active in teaching or socially engaged actions. Relatively few retreat into seeking inactivity; but if they do, it is out of great compassion and surrender, not from strategizing or trying to figure out their path.

    You will not come up with the answer by thinking about it. Your processes of engagement are all subject to the ignorance you're working under and the neurotic views and habits already controlling your relationship with practice. I suggest working with a teacher and group, putting the emphasis on non-mental forms of practice (e.g., chanting, prostrations, movement meditation, physical work, helping), and looking into emotional healing. Seek out help on the emotional front if necessary.

    You might find some relief through heart-centered practice like Metta, Karuna, Tonglen, and devotional chanting; but you'll do much better if you work with a teacher.
