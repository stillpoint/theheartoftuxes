    When I was younger I was a staunch, idealistic vegetarian. I suffered with the suffering of animals, and I was greatly burdened by the harm we all cause the environment. This sensitivity carried over into my first weeks of residency and retreat at a Zen center.

    One day I was assigned to work in the garden, which was really a patch of lawn and hard, dry earth that was being prepared for future planting. Every cut of the shovel that uprooted grass and every pulling up of weeds stabbed at me. "How can we continue to do harm like this?"

    (For that matter, I also had quite an issue with the fish tank at the top of the stairs in the temple hall. "How terrible!")

    This was my mind. I was full of angst and blame for others, and I was very judgmental about myself. I pointed the finger everywhere, trying to be good, trying to be right.

    When the Zen Master came out to work in the garden as well, I took the opportunity to ask about doing harm to plants. He didn't have to ask for details; he saw my mind. He replied "Do you know the danger of holding opinions?"

    And that was that. I was able to drop it, and just work.

    All of these imperfect situations I was pointing at paled in comparison to my own clinging to opinions. Because opinions have the power to cut us off from truth altogether.

    Thoughts come and go. This is not a problem. In fact, it is a benefit. It is our great human talent to make connections and envision possibilities and organize movements. But if we believe our thoughts are real, that's a big problem.

    Thought, by its very nature, can never contain reality. Reality is moving; thought is frozen. Reality is interconnected; thought is defined. Reality is boundless; thought is necessarily limited.

    The notion "I am right" is delusion on three accounts: First; the thought itself cannot contain reality. Second; because reality is not contained in thought, there is no 'right'. Third; there is no 'I'.

    The thought "I am right" perpetuates the wheel of Samsara. In the chain of Dependent Origination, clinging is the ninth link and becoming is the tenth. When we cling to an idea, we become an 'I' who 'is right'; and that's the illusion that sets in motion all the suffering that follows.

    So holding opinion takes you away from Dharma. Seeing is necessary.
