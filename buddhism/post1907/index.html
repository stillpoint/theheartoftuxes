    Does any part of Buddhism say to give up everything you like/want?

    There's a difference between giving up everything and not being able to have anything. You can give up everything in a moment, completely letting go of your hold, and in the next moment you could have a reasonable desire to eat ice cream, or to sing a song for friends, or to buy a new shirt. You can give up your attachment to your bed, being willing to sleep in another bed, or on the floor, or in a hammock, or on a sofa, or on the street; but you can then still use your bed and enjoy it (and let it go when it's time to move on).

    It's interesting, the number of people who begin to learn about Buddhism and quickly get caught on the point of openhandedness. It's a habitual fear reaction, a recoil against the suggestion that one might be required to actually let go. This shows how deep-seated and automatic the clinging habit is. The mere suggestion of loosening the grip starts the mind fretting and looking for loopholes.

    Don't worry; you can have a regular life. But if you want to attain your 'big self', your true nature, you will have to completely let go of your small self.

    Far from being a burden or causing deprivation, letting go is the cause for liberation, happiness, and effectiveness in life. You are much more effective as a free agent, able to consciously choose and respond, than as an automaton that always chases pleasure and runs away from discomfort.

    There are two essential points that come to mind. First is the insubstantial nature of reality: There is nothing actually graspable in life. You can never get your perfect situation, and when you do get something you want you can't hold it forever; it will change, it will become insufficient, and it will pass away.

    This insubstantial nature of life, paired with our insatiable grasping, makes inevitable suffering. Everywhere around the world, people can't get and hold what they want. Then they compound the problem by imposing on others, reacting with anger or greed or lust, taking what doesn't belong to them, and doing all other kinds of bad action. So this grasping habit makes our suffering world.

    The second point, then, is that for our happiness and the happiness of others our action should not be for ourselves alone. Our action should be for the sake of others. When things are owned and used for the sake of others, we can enjoy them without being devastated when they go away. On the other hand, whenever we do action only with ourselves in mind it already plants seeds of suffering for ourselves and others.
