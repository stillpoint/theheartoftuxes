    What is Buddhism exactly? Anyone care to give some insight to a spiritually lost teen looking for spiritual guidance?

    I was once hiking alone in a snowy mountain wilderness, and was two day's hike in when, for a short while, I got lost. The sun had melted my footsteps in the snow so I couldn't backtrack. I knew where I was in general, but I couldn't find the trail for an hour or so. Even though it was a short time, it was still very distressing.

    I realized that panic or hopelessness couldn't help me; I had to be my own friend. So I encouraged myself and settled down and started to consider the resources that I could call on.

    That whole time, in the deep mountain stillness, little birds would flit in and land on the ground to peck for food. Juncos and chickadees, with their bright and clear energy, would flutter in from nowhere and just settle on the snow. They helped me realize that I was not "lost". "Lost" was just the label my thinking made — my story about my situation. I was just here, having flitted in from nowhere; just here along with the birds and the trees and the snow. How I related to the situation was entirely up to me. (I took a cue from the birds and allowed the bright and clear energy in me to emerge.)

    I realized I had some tracking skills that could help, and upon applying them I soon found my way out.

    All of this by way of letting you know:

    • You are not lost. "Lost" is just the name you are giving to your situation and feeling. You are right here, right now.

    • How you relate to your situation from here on in is completely up to you. Buddhism may offer wisdom — good examples, good suggestions — but you have to get that wisdom through your own appropriate action. The history of Buddhism has many examples of people who did very bad action but turned it around and became great leaders and teachers. (See the stories of King Ashoka and of Milarepa, for example.)

    • You already have resources within yourself that can help you.

    • When your mind starts to seek the way sincerely, many teachers and helpers will appear. Birds, trees, Reddit people — who knows what form these guides will take. They are always there, but you can see them once your mind becomes sincere and calms down a bit.

    Feeling deep regret can be very painful and upsetting, but it also has a cleansing ability. If one opens to the regret rather than trying to escape it, it can provide the strong and decisive energy to propel one toward a better way of behaving. This doesn't mean one should think about past bad actions or judge oneself; it means one should let the feeling sink in deeply so that it can turn the mind around.

    (It was "Brutal" King Ashoka's willingness to see the results of his horrific violent actions, and his willingness to feel the regret very deeply, that caused him to adopt Buddhist teachings and turn his life into a tremendous benefit for a great number of beings — people, animals, and environments. His famous realization "What have I done?" was the turning point between a life of great violence and suffering to a life whose beneficial influence is now evident even in our modern times.)

    In short, don't focus on past bad actions. That all has happened because of a whole stream of cause and effect. But take it to heart and let it turn the mind and heart toward the practice of awareness, compassion, and good acts. "Be a light unto yourself."
