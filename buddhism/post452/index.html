    TheHeartofTuxes

    The Buddha said,
    >Be a light unto yourself.

    It is up to you to take the necessary steps to wake up out of suffering patterns. Go to a good source of teaching, preferably a living teacher, and learn. If you choose one proven traditional method and stick to it, that will become your tool, your medicine for transforming your life.

    Of course, mindfulness is necessary. If you are not aware of your reactions in the moment, you won't be able to apply anything you've learned. So developing ongoing awareness is a basic necessity of transformation. You have to become more present.

    When you are more present, then already some negative emotions will be resolved; because many are only triggered by thoughts of future and past. You can then deal with what remains in the moment because you are present and aware of what's going on, and you can apply what you've learned.

    ~

    In order to transform your habitual, unconscious negative action through Buddhist practice, you will have to be very committed to a form. Until one becomes independent in advanced practice, one requires the support of a wise and compassionate guide who can see beyond ego, and the supporting structures of practice group, practice space, schedule, and commitments and vows. You can't do it yourself, alone, because your negative emotions and reactions develop unconsciously. It's not just a matter of 'trying to be good' and suppressing them â€” they will only come back stronger. So proper commitment and support are necessary.

    But this raises the question of whether Buddhism is actually the first step for you. Of course, it is an excellent path and I encourage you to practice it anyway; but in terms of changing your habits and unconscious reactions, shadow work and psychotherapy may be quite a bit more effective at this point.

    (If you want both Buddhism and therapy, luckily there are many Buddhist therapists.)

    The reason these reactions come up so strongly and frequently, seemingly beyond your power to control, is that they reside in your shadow â€” the parts of you that you've denied, ignored, or pushed away. For instance, 'trying to be good' is one way that parts of oneself can be denied, and thus thrust into the unconscious: one tries to distance oneself from, say, anger; so angry reactions are disowned and one tries to see oneself as only calm and nice. Thus one becomes less and less aware of the way anger does arise â€” when and where and how â€” and anger begins to control one from beyond one's awareness. The reactions become unconscious.

    And since whole swaths of self have been disowned, one perceives the source of the anger as coming from outside of oneself â€” from other people or from the situation, or from a sort of 'curse'. This is the dynamic of the human shadow.

    Shadow work and psychotherapy may be more useful to you at this point, unless you find a very clear and strong container of practice and commit to it completely. In that case, the clear form and commitment shine light in every corner of the being, and the shadow becomes revealed. But if you remain complacent and give yourself an 'out' with practice, for example doing it only when you feel like it or only in private, then parts of you will easily remain hidden from practice and the shadow will continue to control you.

    ~

    Psychology works at the level of the psyche. Buddhism has psychology as well, and actually very skillful psychology; but overall it aims at a deeper level, cutting off the very root of ignorance about selfhood. It's just important to get things in proper order, and to have a somewhat well-functioning self before you start to cut it off. Otherwise it's like someone who never really grew up trying to function in the adult world: at some point they actually have to go back and work through that old material, the old wounds, the things that hadn't been learned, and so on.
