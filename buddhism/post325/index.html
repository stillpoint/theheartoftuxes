    Choosing a Buddha representation for the altar, and setting it up

    What is the purpose of having an altar? This question will guide your decision-making process.

    An altar helps you return to your clear mind. It may do this in different ways: through a devotional (heart) connection; by reminding you of the wisdom, purity, and compassion you are moving toward; or by providing a focal point or station where you feel comfortable and settled, and where your mind is quieted.

    While temple altars are created according to the rules and rituals of the tradition, personal altars can, if you wish, involve more varied form and a more personal touch. For some, it seems important for the home altar to be “official" and formal, to provide a more rigorous and meticulous encouragement. For others, accessibility, comfort, and a warm feeling seem to be more significant, to invite the person in, and give them a sense of ease with practice.

    It is not necessary to use the same mudra depicted by the altar statue. Mudras express different energies, different aspects of the enlightened mind, but in the end they all point to the same destination. You may find a figure with a mudra that is significant to you -- depicting an area of practice that you need to be reminded of -- but unless you are training in esoteric practices or are instructed to use the figure as an aid to a specific visualization practice, it doesn't matter what posture is displayed. What matters is your own faith and practice.

    In the early days of Buddhadharma's spread, the Buddha would be depicted by an empty space, such as a seat under a tree. This is maybe a good reminder not to attach to form. The physical or mental form is not the true Buddha!

    There is also the famous story in Zen about the master who threw the Buddha figure into the fire -- it was just wood, after all. (See story below.) This awareness of non-attachment to form is balanced by proper respect. It's what the image or form points to that's important. The form of the Buddha is just a stand-in for your own original mind. That on the altar is you, not anything special or distant.

    To fulfill the format of an altar, all of the elements should be included. The altar itself, and the rice or sand in the bowl represents Earth. The water bowl represents Water. The candles represent Fire. The incense represents air. And the Buddha figure and empty spaces represent Space or Spirit. While there are traditional and esoteric explanations for all of these, in general having them all present together provides a sense of completion. With no part of the world left out of your practice, your mind can relax. There is nowhere else to go, nothing else to be done; it's all here.

    Handling the items on the altar is a practice in mindfulness and correct relationship. You can call forth you humility and devotion, the part of you that it's capable of honoring that which is worthy of honor. No special feeling or thought is necessary; just offer your attention.

    When you keep the altar clean, you are practicing keeping your mind clean. When you respect the altar without making a big deal of it, you are expressing your own noble nature. When you replenish the water and candles, and clean out the incense bowl, you are practicing right relationship with the world.

    Some incense bowls use clean ash (sometimes available in a city's Chinatown or at New Age stores); some use rice. Rice gets dusty and dirty more easily. Some altars have a small rice bowl kept separate, while incense is placed in ash or sand. Be aware that sand will not extinguish some thick incense sticks (nor moxa, if you're into Chinese Medicine), so don't leave incense unattended in sand.

    It's customary to change the water every morning. The first act of the day is to offer your pure mind and heart -- like pure water -- to the Buddha, to the Great Way.

    You may also want to offer something fresh and alive on the altar. Flowers, fruit, and incense are traditional offerings. Remember: you are not offering to something that is special or distant; you are recognizing your own true self and putting yourself in right relationship to it.

    In the final analysis, one moment of clear mind is the true altar. That is the altar that is never destroyed.

    > One cold winter, a monk came across Zen Master Dan Xia throwing a wooden Buddha image into a furnace. The monk was aghast, and demanded an explanation.

    >Dan Xia said “Don't worry, I'm just seeing if we can get some relics (sari). [It was the custom when a master died to burn the body and search the ashes for luminous pearlescent relics, signs of a certain attainment and purity of character.]

    >The monk replied that it was ridiculous to expect relics from a wooden image; to which Dan Xia responded, “If that's the case, let's find some more for the fire."
