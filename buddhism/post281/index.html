    No, it's not at all the same; but of course people have difficulty knowing the difference between their original mind and their attached mind. Spontaneous response is not the same as blatant reactivity or indulgence. And this is where so many go wrong in their misunderstanding of teachings like non-action.

    Without having the difference pointed out to you it's difficult to recognize it; because you are looking for some principle upon which to base your compassion, some idea. But compassion comes from your center, your essential mind; it's not imposed or contrived.

    When you let go of all concepts, your mind is not separate from others. Originally, you are not separate. So before thinking and conditioning (and lust and anger) take effect, you naturally act with compassion.

    I witnessed the activity of the basic mind clearly for the first time early on in my training. I was instructed to chant often throughout the day, and I took every opportunity to do so. I'd chant in front of the altar; I'd chant while cooking and cleaning; I'd chant while showering and getting ready for my day; and I'd often chant while taking a stroll or walking from place to place.

    One day as I chanted on the way to the bus stop, a woman in front of me threw a cigarette package wrapper on the ground. Without thinking, I picked it up and threw it out in a nearby bin. She happened to see me do it.

    After I boarded the bus it dawned on my what had happened. I had only been chanting, with no thought other than the chant. Very clear! Only some minutes later, after I stopped chanting and sat down on the bus, did my mind start to have enough activity to reflect on the events. I realized that my action was completely spontaneous, unplanned, uncontrived, and appropriate. I had no thought of right and wrong. I had no thought of being seen or not seen. I had no thought of sending a message or saving the environment. No anger, no pride. Only appropriate action.

    This was a very thrilling realization. I recognized what the scriptures and masters were pointing to when they talked about the function of original mind.

    Since then, I've seen this mind in its spontaneous, appropriate response countless times. Each time is a blessed confirmation of our essential goodness; and each time is a deepening of faith and trust in that goodness.

    For those around the periphery of it, the original mind of compassion may manifest as conscience or intuition — mere intimations of inner movement. But with correct before-thinking practice, the reflecting/responding capacity of that mind becomes more and more clear. It is able to act with great skill and subtlety. It is able to join with personal experience and Dharma study to become wisdom-in-action.

    But the first point is always the return to clarity, to zero (sunyata). Whether this comes through a meditative process or is accessed in a flash, one has to return to complete openhandedness or Unknowing.

    The master Tilopa gave concise teaching on the kind of practice required to return to original mind:

    • Don't recall (Let go of what has passed)
    • Don't imagine (Let go of what may come)
    • Don't think (Let go of what is happening now)
    • Don't examine (Don't try to figure anything out)
    • Don't control (Don't try to make anything happen)
    • Rest (Relax, right now, and rest)

    And Zen Master Bankei said it even more concisely:

    >Don't get born!

    To paraphrase one of Bankei's teachings: If I were to ask you your name and where you're from, you could answer easily and without hesitation. This demonstrates the brilliant reflecting quality of original, uncontrived mind. This is the activity of compassion: need appears > appropriate response appears.

    When you're driving or walking, you stop at a red light and you go on a green light. It is very clear activity. Likewise, when your friend waves, you wave back. When you are hungry, you eat. And when someone else is hungry, you feed them.

    It is the same clear, reflecting mind functioning in each case, according to each situation. But our clear mind gets covered up with ideas and arrangements. We look for some principle on which to proceed; and we add layers of moral philosophy, psychological suppression and frustration and shame, cultural customs, peer pressure, and base indulgences. These all cover up our original brilliant reflecting function. That's why we need to find a good teacher and practice — to liberate what we originally are from the layers of garbage covering it and perverting it.
