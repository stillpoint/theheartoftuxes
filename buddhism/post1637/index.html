    Modern metta masters? Is there metta in Zen?

    You are extremely fortunate that you (first) recognize this, and (second) see that it is not helping you, and (third) have the willingness to address it. For all three of those conditions to arise for you is very, very auspicious even if it may feel crappy.

    It is very important not to engage in stories about yourself either way. "I am fantastic" and "I am no good" are both ego. When the opportunities for story-making appear — such as when you win or when you lose — decline to follow them. Make an extra effort to stay momentary rather than looking backward into the past or forward into the future.

    Next, commit to situations where you have to act together with others. Ideally, associate with clear and noble-minded people in beneficial pursuits. Joining a meditation center or charitable work group may be very good for you. The point is that when you work together with others you will no doubt have to let go of your own opinion and your own favorite way, many times. (Many, many times!) These are the little deaths of the ego. The ego dies so that something new and alive can be born.

    If you know that you have a heavy ego you would probably do well to put yourself into situations like this where you have to work or practice with others — situations from which there is (temporarily at least) no escape. You commit to a period of practice, or you commit to a number of weeks of work together, or to a retreat, or to a committed relationship with a partner. You set aside the option of escape so that the situation and the reflection of your companions will bring your ego to light. It is something that is nigh impossible on your own, but becomes very possible with the mutual support of others.

    In turn, since you understand very well what it's like to live with a heavy ego, you can help others with the same problem. Working or practicing together is not just for you, but for each other. With this intent, the ego becomes formed into an ally, something that works toward awakening for everyone.

    When that direction toward awakening is established deeply, your strong ego becomes a strong awakening force. It becomes your great gift to the world.

    If you check the stories of Bodhisattvas and Masters through history, you will find many who started out with very strong, difficult egos. They required very strong, hard practice in order to transform; but their strong egos did transform into very bountiful energy that attracted and assisted many, many beings.

    >Things aren't that well right now and I think the healing process starts with looking at my own faults and behaviour, but even 'finding myself' seems somewhat of an impossible task.

    You may at many junctures look at your own faults and patterns; but where you start is only ever just here. You start by letting go of agendas, letting go of plans, letting go of judgments, letting go of labels, letting go of past and present and future. Just let it all go. Then what you are appears by itself.

    If you need help re-setting, take a breath. Have a cup of tea or glass of juice. Take a walk. This is already finding yourself; you just have to develop your faith in it by returning repeatedly. Keep on letting go, keep on being momentary.

    Yes, Zen or other Buddhist teaching and practice may be helpful to you. If you are moved to explore, try it out. Even if you find it's not for you, you will at least have found some movement out of the old patterns and you will have learned something new. If you are interested in recommendations for practice groups or teachers, you can do an online search, post in /r/Buddhism or /r/meditation, or I may be able to offer an idea or two.

    Here are Tilopa's six helping words:

    • Don't recall (don't recall the past)
    • Don't imagine (don't imagine the future)
    • Don't think (don't think of the present)
    • Don't examine (don't try to figure things out)
    • Don't control (don't try to steer the way things are moving)
    • Rest (let it all be as it is)
