    In Zen especially, everything you have is harnessed in service of the moment. Someone says hello, you reach out your hand. Very simple relationship; nothing fancy.

    If you train in a traditional course of practice, all the knowledge and all the aspects of life will be included. This is different from the flitting about and reading and figuring that many people do when trying to cobble together their own practice from books and online sources. It is a very, very different way. Traditional courses of practice include body, speech, mind, relationship, ethical activity, health, work â€” life as a whole.     Chakras can be understood as processes or systems instead of objects.

    When you gaze at a stream or river, you can perceive swirls and eddies that seem to stay in one place; but actually there is no such thing as a swirl or eddy, it is only the constant passage of water. Similarly, we can point to chakras that seem to be there, but they are more like patterns of movement, appearances of something that is always moving, always disappearing.

    And just as the swirls and eddies are indications of the river's form and function, chakras just have to do with how things are moving, how things are working. We say someone is 'headstrong', or 'doesn't have their heart in it', or 'has a gut feeling'. These are talking about the functioning of the chakras.

    Really, we don't necessarily need to create the idea of chakras at all to talk about most functions; but through profound meditation some people became sensitive to the swirls and eddies, the subtle inner movements and their outer expressions. These people identified patterns of relationship that can inform us how to be complete and flowing, like a living stream or river.

    At higher levels of yogic practice, especially in Vajrayana Buddhism, there may be direct practice with the chakras. Zen doesn't bother with the terminology or the various practices as much. These represent different ways of getting to the same point. One way has many different skillful practices to address countless blockages and disruptions in our inner streams. The other way has (basically) one practice and one practice only, which is applied in all situations. The former requires faith and surrender to the guru passing on the detailed instructions. The latter requires faith in oneself and one's application of the one practice.

    This is a gross generalization, and there is much overlap between paths. Vajrayana, of course, also requires faith in oneself; and Zen, of course, also requires faith in the teacher and practice. But in a very broad sense, the styles are like this.

    Another way of saying it is that one way may become very hands-on in dealing with chakras, removing obstacles and purifying your being, and eventually dawning in realization. Another way keeps returning to openhandedness, openhandedness, neither touching nor manipulating anything; and this allows chakras and all forms and movements to find their own correct way, eventually dawning in realization.

    Bottom line: you don't have to know it unless it comes up in your training. You already use your hands perfectly well; their use just has to come into alignment with proper intent and awareness. And you already use your chakras perfectly well; and when you keep proper intent and awareness they will naturally function better and better without you having to tinker and fret about it.
