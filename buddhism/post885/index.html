    Home Silent Retreat....

    Dharma texts are not necessary. Working with a single phrase is better, as it can point you back to practice quickly rather than serving as an escape or distraction. There's no need to make ideas or ruminate on many topics. Consult your teacher for advice on the style and subject matter to be engaging in. Don't think of sprinkling entertainment in. Don't bring distractions with you. Trust that your practice will digest what you need to go through.

    When doing a solo retreat, if it is specifically a practice retreat it is best to work with a very clear schedule. The less room you give yourself to compromise or make excuses to avoid practice, the better. In a group retreat, you are carried along by the group energy and activity, and there's no thought of going against the schedule; but when you're on your own there may be temptation to make things more comfortable. This often manifests as the loosening of daily commitments or the introduction of break times and other activities that get longer and longer as the days pass.

    There's also the possibility of overthinking the retreat and trying to include too much. That's why it Is often best to work with a traditional form. Traditional forms tend to be complete, and usually don't need to be augmented or reworked. Practicing with body, speech, and mind is a balanced approach; so for instance, a set of prostrations or taiji or yoga once or more each day as physical practice, mantra and/or chanting as speech practice, and meditation as mind practice, is a good combo. Some retreats include work periods or walking meditation periods.

    You should decide ahead of time what the purpose of the retreat is, and devote yourself to that purpose. Cut away the nonessential parts. State your purpose clearly at the beginning of the retreat, and at the beginning of each day or each practice session. Dedicate your practice to the benefit of others. This is important, to avoid sinking into a self-serving, totally self-involved mindset.

    It is very helpful to have your food situation organized ahead of time so you don't have to spend a lot of time and energy figuring out and preparing meals. I found it useful to have a rice cooker with me, in which I would cook not only rice but other accompanying foods. If someone else can act in support and bring meals, so much the better.

    You may also be well served by scheduling time to taper back in to your regular routine after the retreat. It can be difficult and painful to go straight from retreat to regular daily life. Sometimes it's good to have a couple of days to recuperate and digest the retreat.
