    I work in a liquor store. Wrong livelihood?

    Nothing is good or bad in itself. There is only cause and effect. The effect of intoxication — reveling in ignorance — is the enemy, not alcohol. Sometimes alcohol, like any substance or experience, is medicine.

    Don't make "good" or "bad"; only act with clarity. Moment to moment, acting with clarity will take you along the clear path. It may turn out that at some point you see a cause-and-effect that you can no longer abide by — that harm outweighs benefit in your job — and be moved to find new work; or it may turn out that your moment to moment clarity makes this "wrong livelihood" into a benefit for others.

    Traditionally, nominally, selling intoxicants is wrong livelihood. If we take a broad definition, it is sometimes difficult to find a pure job. Selling entertainment, moving money around, decorating spaces with non-essential items — there are many jobs that don't live up to a totally pure standard. But some schools of Buddhism don't focus on purity. Rather, they focus on how you can use any situation.

    We all have our karma, the situation that we are constantly emerging in and working with. Yes, we need to work on purifying that karma and getting free; but in the meantime we can also use it skillfully. This skill doesn't come from concepts. You can't plan your relationship with the moment. Rather, let go of preconceptions and meet the moment with clarity. This will allow you to use the moment skillfully.

    This means you should not think of yourself as either a bad person or a good person. Many mistakes, many harmful actions have been made in the name of goodness. (Religious terrorism, for instance.) If you hold an image of goodness and try to act that way you will be limited by that idea. Sometimes softness and niceness are necessary; sometimes clear boundaries and decisive action are necessary. Sometimes it's time to listen; sometimes it's time to act. The clarity of responding appropriately to the ever-changing situation is a different kind of goodness.

    One thing I so appreciate from my teachers is that they set high standards without judgment. Not judging alcohol-buying customers (or yourself) doesn't mean you have to give up on high standards. Every job will have some difficulty, some aspect that makes it more challenging for you to stay clear and maintain your correct direction. That is why working in the world is good practice. If you indeed practice with it, it can make your clear center stronger. The main point is that you make the effort to keep your clear center — to stay open and responsive, moment to moment, rather than be dragged around by your thoughts or habitual reactions.

    There are stories in the history of Buddhism of prostitutes who would have an awakening, enlightening affect on their customers. Nominally, prostitution would be considered wrong livelihood, but they used their karma to help others. One can imagine the shamed, craving, wounded mind of someone seeking out a prostitute, and how it might be liberated by a present, clear, loving, non-judgmental soul. Likewise, if those mistakenly seeking to "drown their sorrows" in unclarity meet a present, clear, loving, non-judgmental soul at the counter of the liquor store they may be similarly influenced. Put down all figuring and let your original clarity shine moment to moment.
