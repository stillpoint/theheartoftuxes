    You're in the same boat as a lot of people. I started out studying Zen from a book, many years ago.

    The online community is a mixed blessing. The (hopefully) mutual support and ease of access to information is an amazing resource that has only been in existence for a very short time, less than a generation. But the lack of rigor (without, for instance, a confirmed enlightened being pointing the way), the insidious co-opting activity of ego, and the confusion/profusion of paths, opinions, and varied (sometimes questionable) levels of attainment can severely degrade and at times completely obscure good teaching.

    Whether in person, online, or through books, I'd suggest that as much as possible you stick to one lineage and one method. Each tradition has its own way of presenting the truth. There are many good ways, but you can't necessarily mix them and get good results.

    Next, part of your daily practice should be clarifying your life direction. I don't mean that you need to cling to an idea; but you should ask the question: Why am I here; what is my life for; what is the true purpose of Zen? Asking the question makes your direction more evident, and when your direction is evident you can know why the methods and forms are the way they are. Then if you have an issue with your eyes or some other adjustment is necessary, you will still have your guiding purpose.

    In Zen and Mahayana communities, the Four Bodhisattva Vows (sometimes called the Four Impossible Vows) are the daily re-connection with purpose:

    >Sentient beings are numberless; I vow to save them all.

    >Desires are inexhaustible; I vow to end them all.

    >The Dharma Gates are infinite; I vow to enter them all.

    >The Buddha Way is unexcelled; I vow to attain it completely.

    In Vajrayana, the Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind have a similar function.

    If you are sincere about your purpose, forms can be adapted. It is like having a compass: if you wander off the path, you can take out your compass and find North. No matter where you are, what road you've taken, how far away you've gone, if you check a working compass you can find your 'North'.

    Maybe sometime you will travel to sit with a strong group and receive teachings from a luminous being. Bring your index card of questions and issues and try to get an interview with a senior student or teacher, to get clear on practice. Even just meeting an awakened being one time is an extremely precious, life-altering event.

    For Beginner's Mind — the mind that is very sincere and only wants to try — nothing is an obstacle. I traveled all around North America for about a decade before finding my master; and if I had done it ten times over it would have been worth it! And it's not like I had a life of wealth and leisure; I had to struggle to do it. But nothing else can compare. What else is more valuable than the relationship that can bring you back to your original self?

    In the meantime, as you practice largely on your own, you will have to be your own coach and your own cheerleader. Equal portions of reasoned consideration and kind encouragement will be necessary.

    One thing is clear: if you keep trying, you will do it. Overcome all obstacles; let nothing remain in your way. Preserve the sincere motivation to practice Zen, and even if you lose your eyes, it will be no problem. Only do it.

    If you want to keep in touch with me I'd be happy to help, if it's something I'm qualified to speak to.
