    feeling that your way is getting lost, past self is back

    I'd ask anyone to please look into it through direct experience rather than reacting from opinion. Disagreeing is one thing; whether it is true or not is another. "The more things you understand, the more things you have to handle." That sentence doesn't have judgment or moral statement to it, it just describes the situation. There is also no statement about ignorance here.

    Lack of proper understanding is no good; but don't think that ignorance is the mere absence of the thinking's understanding. Thinking-understanding is necessary for functioning in the relative world. We need words and ideas to get by in daily life, but words and ideas — and thinking-understanding — can't grasp reality. The very nature of thinking separates a universal unity into disparate objects; it takes what is ever-flowing and ever-changing, and freezes it into solid concepts; it forces boundless and inextricably interrelated events into limited 'boxes' of words, labels, ideas.

    So we need thinking, but thinking is not nearly enough. And if you see your own mind, you will see the proliferating nature and veiling effect of thinking, and you will have no argument with what I've written. It is not a matter of opinion; it is a matter of seeing directly. Those who actually glimpse the nature of their mind in meditation practice will understand this in a way that thinking and explaining can't touch. Perhaps there is confusion about my use of the word "understanding"; but someone who does the practice of looking into mind will be able to identify that difference.

    Actually, letting go of understanding will allow it to function properly. It doesn't mean that you no longer understand; but attachment to understanding will no longer hinder you.

    The in the Tao Te Ching, Lao Tzu says:

    > The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao. The name that can be named is not the eternal Name.

    > The unnameable is the eternally real. Naming is the origin of all particular things.

    (Stephen Mitchell Ch. 1)

    Feng and English have the last line as: > The named is the mother of the ten thousand things.

    That is the point we're looking into. Whatever we think or say about it, that's not it; thinking-understanding can't grasp it. And when one thought arises, the "ten thousand things" — the world of proliferating objects — arises.

    ~

    Lao Tzu also says:

    > Empty your mind of all thoughts. Let your heart be at peace.

    > Watch the turmoil of beings, but contemplate their return. Each separate being in the universe returns to the common source.

    (Mitchell Ch. 16)

    Feng and English's translation:

    > Empty yourself of everything.

    > Let the mind become still.

    > The ten thousand things rise and fall while the Self watches their return.

    ~

    And:

    > Stop thinking, and end your problems.

    (Mitchell Ch. 20)

    Feng & English: > Give up learning, and put an end to your troubles.

    How does that reconcile with the need to overcome ignorance? It points to a radically different type of 'understanding', something beyond or before thinking. It points to allowing all things to rest in themselves. This ungrasping is real attainment. But just explaining it is not enough; actually doing it is necessary, and that means putting down your understanding.

    A famous zen dialogue elucidates this point very well. (So well that one of the people involved, able to let go of his thinking-understanding, attained enlightenment on that occasion.) The dialogue occurs between Zen Master Nan Quan (Jap.: Nanzen, Kor.: Nam Cheon) and his student and eventual successor, Zhao Zhou (Jap.: Joshu, Kor.: Jo Ju).

    > Zhao Zhou asked Zen Master Nan Quan, "What is the true way?"

    >Zen Master Nan Quan replied, "Everyday mind is the true way."

    >Then Zhao Zhou asked him, "Then shall I try to keep it or not?"

    > The Zen Master replied, "If you try to keep it, you're already making a mistake."

    >Zhao Zhou asked again, "If I do not try to keep it, how can I understand the true way?"

    >Then Zen Master Nan Quan said, "The true way is not dependent on understanding or not understanding. Understanding is illusion. Not understanding is blankness. If you completely attain the true way, it is clear like space. So why do you make right and wrong?"

    >When Zhao Zhou heard that, he got Enlightenment.

    The teaching question is: What did Zhao Zhou attain?
