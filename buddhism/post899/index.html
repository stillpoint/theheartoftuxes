    Computer use and mindlessness

    The computer and other electronic devices are so insidious because they closely approximate the thinking mind. They are thinking machines, so the habitual lure of drifting thinking is much, much more prominent with their use. Such is the difficulty of maintaining clarity while using "thinking machines" that it would be best to limit their use and introduce remedial activities (like walks in nature or silent time at home) rather than depending solely on the strength of one's mindfulness.

    • Repeated mindfulness signals like a timer alarm or computer event scheduled every 10 or 15 minutes can help you at least reset that often if you lose your contact with reality.

    • Scheduled (mandatory) breaks can also be a big help.

    • One of the big issues with the modern-day electronic lifestyle is finding the root of awareness in the body, so physical activity or the lack of it will be a major influence. Vigorous whole-body-and-breath movement throughout the day, or at least at some point in the day, will go a long way toward keeping you more grounded in the body and less lost up in the head.

    Some people find great benefit in using standing desks, so they can have a more engaged and dynamic physical posture while working. Some executives even work while walking a treadmill. Seating that allows for constant movement is similarly engaging. Students with ADHD have shown marked improvement in attention when sitting on exercise balls rather than regular chairs. It is thought that the little changes of balance and other opportunities to move keep them more rooted in their bodies, and hence more mentally grounded.

    • You identified posture as one of the key components: the physical foundation of mindfulness through the posture. Staying mindful of the posture will work hand-in-hand with vigorous exercise in establishing more physical grounding overall. If some background mindfulness of the whole body posture is not feasible while using the computer you can keep a background awareness of just the feet on the floor, or of the feet on the floor plus the butt on the seat — whatever the physical foundation happens to be moment to moment.

    If this is still too difficult to maintain you can use a point of contact. One Zen master uses awareness of the thumbs touching the steering wheel when he drives; you may feel the fingers on the keyboard, or if you can have bare feet or just stockings you could use a textured item for more sensory feedback (a textured rug, a spiked massage ball, etc.).

    • Your environment can also be arranged to support mindfulness. Keep reminders — objects that help you return to centeredness and calmness — within view. The place where you naturally look when you move your eyes from the computer screen — place a centering object there. Let it be something that works at the gut level, like an image or color or pattern that influences you without even thinking about it. Leave verbal reminders for secondary locales, if at all, since they will also at first glance stimulate rather than calm the word-associated thinking.

    Sound can be supportive. The natural sounds of a water fountain or rustling leaves or wind chimes can enhance mindfulness. There are many CDs, mp3s, and computer and phone apps with soothing background sounds. The important point is that these are used to enhance your own efforts to stay present and grounded, not as alternative escapes from the real moment.

    • Electronic fasting is a thing. The way people used to schedule a dietary fast in order to return to balance, you can schedule hours of the day and days of the week (and weeks of the year) in which you refrain from any contact with thinking machines like computers, phones, video games, and TVs.
