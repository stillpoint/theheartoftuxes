    Sit on something firm so you don't bobble or bounce. Also, the longer you sit, the less comfortable soft cushions will be, because they compress soft tissues.

    A folded thick blanket works for many. Some people use a rug that is thick enough to prevent bruises on the anklebones and feet where they contact the floor. A yoga mat works. And if you have a thick carpet you may do fine without any kind of mat at first.

    Likewise, the cushion under the sit-bones should be firm. Another folded blanket or folded rug may do for this. After practice becomes consistent you might want to splurge for a kapok or compressed cotton meditation cushion.

    Most westerners find that the pelvis has to be raised a few inches, both to ensure that the knees rest firmly on the ground, and to relieve the muscles around the upper thighs, pelvis, and lower back. Raising the pelvis helps prevent the collapsed outward curve of the lower back that requires muscular tension to counteract. If you raise the butt more than 5" or so it starts to become precarious, and you might then consider alternative postures.

    You can also use a chair, again setting it up so that the pelvis is slightly higher than the knees. These are not hard and fast rules, but general guidelines that work for many people. In the case of sitting with a chair, feet should be solidly flat on the floor.

    In either posture, create a solid base and then let the upper body waft upward atop it. When the base is solid, the upper body can be relieved, resting on the skeletal structure rather than struggling for uprightness and balance.

    Keep your eyes half-open, like you see in images of the Buddha. You can softly gaze at an angle toward the floor. You don't have to focus on anything in particular. If you pay close attention to your method of meditation the sight will relax and won't be an issue.

    If you meditate facing a wall, as in some traditions, it's fine to gaze straight ahead. And if you are tired you can raise the eyes and open the eyelids a bit more.

    The main thing is to be both wakeful and relaxed. Be upright and noble in both physical and mental posture; but do what you can to relieve strain. If you need to stuff a support cushion here and there, do it for the sake of stability and then forget it.

    Don't mess around with cushions and posture during sitting. If you need to learn about posture, do some research or take a course in yoga, or meditation, or Alexander Technique. If you need to figure things out with body and supports, schedule separate sessions just for that purpose. But when you're meditating, only meditate; don't mess around with anything else.

    If the body wants stretching, go ahead. Do it gently, to about 75% of the limit. Listen to the physical situation rather than forcing an idea or agenda onto the body. Also know that just stretching is not enough; you need strength and tone. Looseness without tone is not good; it's easy to injure your joints seriously that way. Yoga or Pilates are good for developing flexibility and strength in a balanced way.

    Some people get better at the physical part of sitting just by doing more and more, and gradually removing supports over months and years. But for others this approach doesn't work, and they need other kinds of practice or training to help them in their posture.

    In some Zen lineages, it is taught that correct posture is already meditation. You can experience this: if you sit upright, awake, and released, nothing else is necessary. Just to be present and awake is practice.

    Posture is very influential on your mind (and your mind is very influential on your physical posture). But if your aspiration is very strong and clear, it doesn't matter how you sit; your mind's sitting is key.

    There have been many posts on posture in /r/Buddhism and /r/Meditation. You can use the search function, and you can also search my posting history for many detailed posts. You can also find general guides on some Zen center websites.
