    Is Buddhism right for me?

    I'm not annoyed at all by your post. Just the opposite: I think it's a wonderful investigation. How fortunate to be considering these things!

    Buddhism is very broad. There are many ways in. Just don't expect to remain unchanged; don't expect to always hold on to your current view, whatever it may be.

    Community and warmheartedness between people and between living beings — and indeed with the whole world — is the foundation of Buddhism. Buddha didn't just keep his realization to himself, he offered it to help others grow in mutual awakening. That is a basic warmheartedness at the very roots of Buddhism.

    The fundamental issue we are all saddled with is that we don't understand our true self, our true nature. But this one issue manifests in countless ways, so people go to Buddhism for many different reasons and along many different pathways. Different reasons, but at the bottom of it, just one reason. The important thing is that you do something to wake up and understand the true self — the motivation behind your yearning or inquiry.

    The Dalai Lama spoke to a diverse audience about all these different pathways. He noted how some of us may go to a Chinese food restaurant, some may eat Italian, some may prefer Mexican, some seek Thai cuisine, and so on. All of that is fine, he said; of course, we have different restaurants serving our different predilections and needs. But the most important point, said the Dalai Lama, is that we get food in our belly!

    So rather than worrying about where to go and arguing about which restaurant is better, it's important sooner or later to actually get the food in the mouth and digest it. Likewise, it's important for you to actually try something, and get some experience in your belly. Using your best judgment, along with trust in the right guides, you can try some Buddhist style practice if you like. Then you can get an experience and understand better if you'll return to this 'restaurant'.

    > There is a famous story about Buddha's closest disciple, Ananda, who at one time very much like you was contemplating the role of relationship and community in the spiritual life. With glee he had a realization that he wanted to share with his teacher:

    >>Venerable Ananda approached the Blessed One. Having approached, he paid homage to the Blessed One, sat down to one side and said to him, "Venerable Sir, I believe that good spiritual friendship is fully half of the spiritual life."

    [We can imagine Ananda realizing something like "Wow, good relationship is so important; it's probably accounts for at least half of everything we try in the spiritual life!"]

    >>The Buddha replied, "Not so, Ananda! Say it not so, Ananda! Good spiritual friendship is the entirety of the spiritual life. When a Bhikkhu has good spiritual friendship, he will surely develop and cultivate the Path of Awakening."

    Spiritual friendship means not only your relationship with other sincere seekers of truth, but your relationship with the lovely Truth itself. The basic, original sincerity we possess even before birth is the basis of real community. Without finding your basic, original mind you can't have complete community; but the more you move toward it, the more you find true community.
