    Looking for devotional practice

    Devotion doesn't have to be thought of as secondary. If you practice devotion without making ideas about it, it is the same as clear meditation. The heart can be a great impetus toward unification.

    The problem arises when you hold a sense of "I am doing the devoted practice". When devotion becomes an exercise in pumping out emotion or creating a drama about how much you love the object of devotion, it regenerates the illusion of selfhood, which means that dualism and separation from reality persist.

    There are countless devotional practices. Many popular practices can be performed with a devotional quality, by throwing yourself into the wholeheartedly. Chanting and mantra are natural candidates. Using the voice in chanting or in voiced mantra recitation (japa) involves the heart and is healing to the emotions. The pace and quality of chanting can change with the mood and energy of the moment. Slow chanting has a still and sacred quality; fast chanting matches a racing mind; soft chanting is intimate; loud chanting can be rapturous.

    Prostration practice can be a quintessential devotional practice. The mind, the heart, the will, the body, and the breath all act together in offering humble respect to the enlightened ones. You can bow to an image of enlightenment like a statue or painting or picture of a Buddha or Bodhisattva; or you can bow to something else that represents your Buddha-nature, like a Zen circle. Take care not to increase subtle pride by bowing to something you make yourself; and don't bow to objects or beings from nature as nature is still part of Samsara.

    [Edit: I just want to clarify this last point. It is wonderful to bow to anything. In regular life you can bow to good and bad people, high and low beings, living beings and inanimate objects. You can bow out of respect and thanks. You can bow with humility and honor. But you should only do bows of refuge to the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha or their representative in the form of a guru or enlightened deity.]

    The repetitive and vigorous quality of prostrations is well suited to devotional work. You put out an effort. You humble yourself to the divine, and you stand up for awakening. Again and again, you drop all clinging, shed all obscurations, bring yourself into contact with the ground of being; again and again, you quickly stand up out of pettiness and suffering, you rise into your noble essence. If you wish, you can recite the Tisarana (Threefold Refuge — probably what you refer to as the hymn to the Three Jewels) while doing prostrations. The altar you keep can be your ritual place, the place that provides focus and helps you create a sacred field. It can hold whatever image or symbol you prostrate to.

    Making offerings is also a very lovely and natural practice for a devotional style practitioner. First, creating and maintaining an altar engages you through the vision, physically through your action, through the sense of touch by handling the objects on the altar, through the sense of smell by burning incense, through the voice and energy by reciting mantras and prayers. Every morning take some time to clean the altar, refresh a water bowl, light candle and/or incense, and give thanks and prayers of connection with the enlightened (and enlightening) beings. Every evening, pour out the water bowl (preferably into a plant or onto the earth outdoors), light a candle and/or incense, and take in the beauty of the altar. Sitting and gazing at a well-made Buddha figure helps you to absorb the qualities it depicts, including presence and compassion and poise. This is a lovely and simple practice to do before bed.

    Next, water offerings are custom-made for devotion. This is because water is widely available, at low cost. So it is something that you can offer repeatedly, many hundreds of thousands of times, or perhaps millions. Buy or assemble a set of 7 cups, arrange them a barley-grain away from each other (for large cups; or a rice-grain away for smaller cups), and fill them with clear water from a pitcher. If you want to purify the water, drop a few filaments of saffron in it. Fill the cups with water up to a barley grain or rice grain from the rim. You can do this practice silently, or while reciting mantras and prayers. The main thing is that you are offering yourself and your very best wishes and intentions along with the water. Picture the enlightened ones entering supreme bliss upon receiving your offerings, ecstatic about your sincerity and offering; and see and know that their blessings enter you and transform you into original enlightenment. If you want to do many repetitions of offering, you can empty the cups into a bucket and refill them repeatedly, and then later empty the bucket respectfully onto soil or give the water to trees or plants.

    Thanking your past life is not so much a devotional practice. The past is already past; it is not real. And if you think about yourself too much you can develop pride and attachment that hinder practice. The point is to keep letting go of even the smallest negative thoughts and actions, and keep performing even the smallest positive thoughts and actions; that is, not to rest on your karma from the past (which may be on its last legs) but to create very good karma right now.
