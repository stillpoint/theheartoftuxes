    > How do I find out these biases that I have...?

    I wanted to address this question in particular, because it's a sticking point for many practitioners, and especially many on Reddit who think they can be their own masters by just picking up snippets of teaching here and there.

    There's this common illusion that equates awakening with thinking. "As soon as I get the right ideas," one thinks, "I will be finished with the path." When it's stated like that we may see how limited, how ridiculous that line of thought is. And yet it is the default for the vast majority of seekers. The goal is seen as a matter gaining enough understanding.

    This is how we co-opt spirituality. The great master Chogyam Trungpa called this mistake Spiritual Materialism: the idea that it's a matter of getting. Since our only strategy to this point -- our modus operandi -- has been to address life through our thinking, we assume that's what's required in spiritual life. Without proper teaching and training, and lots of it, that remains our default. It remains the filter through which all life is encountered.

    But true spiritual practice has more dimensions to it. It is largely a matter of direct seeing, and doing. It requires the seeker to go beyond old patterns of interaction and beyond thinking.

    The westernization of Asian spiritual paths initially took them out of their context of devotion and physicality. It dropped what many westerners saw as empty mysticism and unnecessary 'grunt work', and tried to attain the path through the same old theoretical, philosophical methods they had always employed. Essentially, the heart and body were disconnected from the practice, and the head -- not insignificantly, the domain of ego -- was made king.

    Correct practice embraces and integrates mind, heart, and body. And it has a clear, consistent Dharmic direction that can't be applied by one's own ego. In isolation, the very ignorance we are trying to overthrow is quite often also the mind that's running the whole show.

    That's why we seek external support, external structures, to integrate mind, heart, and body in ways we have not yet digested; to provide a reliable, consistent view beyond self; and to reflect us back to ourselves. And this is apropos to your question: you don't necessarily see yourself until you are reflected. So the supports of the training situation are there in large part to serve as clear mirrors in which you begin to see your own mind. In the practice mirror you see your own karmic patterns so you can go beyond them.

    Heart, mind, and body are the spheres of the Three Jewels of Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha. True refuge in Buddha embraces the heart, and is expressed in devotional practice. True refuge in Dharma embraces the mind, and is expressed in understanding and mental practice. True refuge in Sangha embraces the body, and is expressed in ethical activity and physical practice. Traditional training takes all of these into account. It's not just tradition for the sake of being traditional; it becomes tradition because it's deep and wide and proven effective. (Though some things become traditional because of cultural conditioning and other attachments -- no path is without its shadow.)

    So the course of training holds many aspects of practice that we are not yet able to hold for ourselves -- if we're even aware of them!

    The Three Jewels also emerge in the ways training forms reflect our minds back to us. The teacher, representative of the Buddha, often sees us more clearly than we can, and provides reflection through interaction and feedback as well as by providing the lessons that are appropriate for us.

    The teaching and method, representative of the Dharma, is often a very direct mirror, especially in meditation where -- if we adhere to the method closely -- we can see very clearly when and why we depart from practice. All of the interactive forms, from meditation to ritual to liturgy, provide mirrors for us. If we are lax about form, the mirror is unclear; if we are clear about form, we get to see ourselves and soon transform ourselves.

    The practice group and community, representative of the Sangha, reflects us back to ourselves through relationship. When you have to live and work with others, you get to see and feel your own opinions and preferences rising; and you have to discover how to get past them, how to give them up and return to clear, compassionate together-action. In the context of commitment (whether it's a purposeful marriage or taking practice vows with others), relationship quickly shows us our selfishness, our attempts to hide or control.

    ~

    Buddha > heart > devotional practice > samadhi/faith

    Dharma > mind > study and meditation > prajna/wisdom

    Sangha > body > ethical activity > sila/purity

    ~

    For more on Spiritual Materialism, see Chogyam Trungpa's classic book Cutting Through Spiritual Materialism.
