    A Lama Comes To Town

    Is the issue that you don't trust the teacher?

    Or is it that you don't really want to pay; you'd rather get teaching for free?

    Or are you trying to find your balance between complete giving and practical financial concerns?

    Those are three very different issues, so you should be clear on which one is the concern. It's possible that there are elements of all of these in operation.

    Just don't fool yourself about it. It's easy to think that skepticism is great; but it's also very easy to let your own fears and your own stingy character express themselves as mistrust or criticism of a teacher.

    We run into trouble when we just label things 'good' or 'bad'. We lose subtlety, interconnection, and reason with those labels. The words are just shorthand, just quick ways to tag a much broader situation. It's the situation that you must deal with, not the word. So please do away with the notion that â€œskepticism is good". In many cases it is good, in many cases it is bad. It is, of course, valuable and necessary to get familiar with your teacher and chosen tradition, to try them out, and to investigate whether they are beneficial and trustworthy. It's important to have this kind of evidence-based inquiry, and to be responsible for your own progress and safety.

    But by the same token, it's also valuable and necessary to put your trust in something once you have determined it to be reasonable and beneficial. At some point, you have to let the Dharma in. At some point, further mistrust blocks your progress, blocks the guidance of those with knowledge and wisdom you don't yet possess. Your salvation is everywhere around you, but you cut yourself off from it. Much of mature practice is in discovering all the various ways we block this ever-present blessing.

    The mind of faith is an extremely powerful ally on the path; and without it your progress may be limited. Yes, this faith should be based on reality, on evidence and experience. But it is necessary to let go of the stingy, fearful, clinging heart. This is true in terms of letting go of cynical doubt so that the teaching may enter your life; and it is true in terms of letting go of miserly greed so that the blessings can enter your life.

    It is also important to remember that your spiritual path is not just for you! We spend so much time and effort trying to get our perfect spiritual situation (great teacher, lovely practice space, liking everyone we practice with, not having to pay -- or paying only enough to think of ourselves as generous), we try to get everything just the way we like it. But really our practice is for others. It's so we can wake up and stop being pretty little beings harming each other and ruining our planet.

    So it's necessary to break out of that narrow-minded preference that we habitually trust and struggle to maintain. This is one of the main points behind practices of generosity: it is for you, to practice being your greater self, to bring you closer to fulfillment. The enlightened ones don't need your money! In practical terms, if the money is not there they can't travel and teach, or maybe they have to go without medical treatment or other supports we take for granted. But that doesn't matter to them. Their compassion is vast; they are only here to help you.

    So the enlightened ones don't need your offerings. Offering is so that you can start to remove your own petty, self-clinging nature, and receive the gifts -- the teaching, the good deeds, the spiritual nourishment, and the mystical blessings -- that are constantly being radiated by enlightened beings and by your own enlightened nature.
