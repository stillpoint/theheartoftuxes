    It depends on how you understand them. Absolute Truth is Absolute Truth, regardless of what name it's given. The point is to attain that truth and then let it function in your life.

    So what a Christian calls 'God', if it refers to the actual truth rather than dogma, points to the same truth attained in Buddhism (called 'Sunyata' or 'Emptiness' â€” the ungraspable, boundless, brilliantly awake basis of all things; in some traditions also called 'Original Mind').

    But if you attach to the dogmas as literally and exclusively true you might have a problem. For instance, Buddhism doesn't have a creator god. It teaches about how things co-create; and at a deeper level points to the timeless basis of all things: that absolute, empty, aware source. Rather than seeing creation as something happening over time, which is an illusion created by thinking, it recognizes all things as manifestations or projections of Emptiness/Original Mind, like dream images that are all made of the same mind-substance.

    When asked whether Buddhism and belief in God can go together, one Zen Master responded "Who made God?" This is a crucial point. Originally, God is before thought and beyond speech; but if one clings to an idea of God, then for that person 'God' is man-made. In this sense, you make 'God'. In order to get at the Absolute Truth (whatever name it's given) one has to cut away all the stuff one makes with thinking, all the concepts that get confused for truth.

    In part, meditation is the practice of letting go of things you make up with your thinking, in order to allow the Absolute Truth to show itself. That is why meditation practice often plays an important role in Buddhism (though there are other ways to get at Absolute Truth).

    We use names and words so often we convince ourselves that just because we say them, they exist... and that just because we say them we know what they are. If you can get beyond this mistake, there is no problem with reconciling the heart of Christianity and the heart of Buddhism; both point to the plain truth beyond words.

    Most religions point to one source:
    Christianity says "The Lord is One", and Jesus says "I am the Way and the Truth and the Life."
    Islam says "There is no God but Allah."
    Taoism says "The nameless Tao is the source of the universe."
    And in Buddhism, the story goes that when Buddha was born he said "In Heaven above and Earth below, I alone am the World-Honored One."

    It is funny that while people may believe that there is only one source, they think that their 'one source' is different from another religion's 'one source'.

    Buddhist teaching adapts to the needs and level of the audience. So if someone is fixated on the idea of the 'one source' as existing in time and space, a Buddhist teacher may use that kind of language until the person begins to understand interdependent co-arising. And if someone is fixated on the idea that there are 'things' that are interdependent, the teacher may use that language until the person starts to see the empty nature of everything. The different levels of teaching don't contradict each other; they move the student on to deeper levels of understanding.
