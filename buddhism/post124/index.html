    The offering of something precious isn't for the Buddha's sake, or for the sake of whatever enlightened being you're offering to; it's for your sake, so you can practice offering to the most valuable part of life.

    The Buddha is already complete; he doesn't need anything. But our stingy, self-concerned nature keeps us from experiencing our own completion. So generosity in daily life and offerings in formal practice are important practices, to break down our selfishness and our attachment to form.

    Gold is only valuable in conditional terms; that is, it's only so because we say it's so. Originally, gold, jewels, money, and 'valuable' possessions are empty. They come from nothing and return to nothing. But we don't fully realize that. It's only an idea to us. Therefore, since we still think of them as valuable, we offer precious and beautiful things, to establish our right priorities and to create a strong karmic connection to the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha.

    It is fine to have a humble shrine that emphasizes and reminds you of your humility; and it is fine to have a rich, lavish shrine that emphasizes and reminds you of your original wealth-beyond-estimation. Just don't attach to either one.

    One of the major functions of an altar is to take the thinking mind away. Huge or amazingly ornate Buddha images have an awesome impact on the viewer, giving them (perhaps) a moment of speechless awe, which is their own Buddha Mind appearing. Another function is to convey deeply, beyond the level of speech and thought, the positive and boundless qualities of enlightenment: beauty, nobility, compassion, peace, blessing, equanimity....

    There is a western, Judeo-Christian-Islamic conditioning of mistrust or fear toward 'false idols'. Many of us have received the programming that says a gold statute on an altar is Baal or some pagan or demonic deity. So we just have to be clear that we are not worshipping by way of subservience or slavery, but practicing reverence and humility so we can shed the egotism that creates suffering.

    And what do we revere? If we have a separating sense of self, the Buddha is external. But once we have an experience of our true primordial mind, we begin to know that the Buddha is none other than that mind. In essence, we are returning to our own true being; and the Buddha image provides one reminder or signpost pointing the way.
