    Trouble in Paradise - a question for buddhit

    To tell the truth, I also prefer -- and trust -- cutting off all thoughts. It is a simple surrender that I trust and respond to at a very deep level. But there's also a different issue here: You would rather cut off thoughts than be present with reality. That in itself is self-centered.

    Equanimity means being unmoved by like and dislike. The mind-habit is to go toward what we take to be pleasant, to try to avoid what we take to be unpleasant, and to ignore what is neutral to us. These are the three poisons: grasping (craving), hating or rejecting (aggression), and dullness or confusion (ignorance). Mindfulness is a practice that dispels the three poisons. You don't grasp at something; you just pay attention. You don't avoid something or push it away; you just pay attention. You don't sink into dullness or tune out; you just pay attention.

    Cutting off all thought is a very excellent and primary step. That is returning to true nature. But it is important to take the next step, which is letting the true nature function in relationship with the world and with others. So actually, you should cut off the thought of 'benefit', the thought of avoiding mindfulness practice, and the thought of retreating to a pleasurable vacant state; then the mind will function as it needs to function.

    All of the above is to provide a direction. When you have a strong direction, then the practice of cutting off thoughts is a very good one. You can go deeper and deeper, and experience bliss and samadhi. An experience coming out of deep samadhi can completely re-organize your life. But it's very possible to become attached to blissful oneness and utterly lose your way. If you don't have a clear and strong direction, cutting off thoughts is self-serving and even cruel to others, not so different from alcoholism. So clear direction and a good teacher (who can regularly check your mind and bring you back to the correct path) become very valuable in 'cutting off' practice.

    Two further notes: First; be aware that people sometimes veer off with mindfulness when there is too much self-reflection. Rather than just perceiving or acting with presence, the practitioner constantly generates the thought "I am seeing this... I am feeling this... I am doing this..." That is uncomfortable and self-defeating.

    Second; we are often the last to know whether something is of benefit. In the midst of our experiences and our personal dramas, we don't have the perspective to see change and judge it objectively. For example, everyone has the experience of sitting in meditation but being in turmoil, fighting with their own thoughts. But from the outside, someone seeing you will likely have a feeling of peace and groundedness, and will think "how wonderful!" From inside, it seems to us like there's no progress; from the outside: "how wonderful!" Another reason to have regular contact with a teacher, who can see more clearly what progress is occurring.

    It may be nothing, but I get the sense that you may be suited for, or at least enjoy, zen practice.
