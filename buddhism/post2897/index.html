    How do I find motivation to do anything?

    > Buddhism doesn't seem to stress work ethic. As a result of this way of thinking, my motivation to study and do work has gradually decreased.*

    That's your thinking, not Buddhism's thinking. So your motivation or lack of it comes from your own mind. It's not something you can pawn off on Buddhism.

    If you're concerned about waning motivation, that means that you still have motivation. If you totally didn't care, there would be nothing to be concerned about. But you still have some awareness of the cause and effect involved, among other motivating factors. You at least know in the back of your mind that study and work will shape your future for the better, and you may also actually wish to be interested and inspired about study and work.

    So then you need to look into the reasons you are not connecting with your interest and inspiration. You can't blame Buddhism; you have to take responsibility for your own situation. Is it the subject matter? Is it the setting or teacher? Is it depression? Is it laziness or fear?

    There is nothing evil to be found if you look into your own patterns; just cause and effect. When you find the cause, you can change it and change the way your life unfolds. If you don't find the cause and effect in your own mind, it will continue to control you from the shadows.

    This is your life. It's completely up to you how you will approach it, the thoughts and attitudes you will apply, the values you will uphold and the ones you will abandon. Completely up to you.

    Furthermore, this life is very fleeting. Not only will you die within a few years, or a few dozen years if lucky, but you don't know when that death will come, and you don't know when other limitations will come like crippling accident, serious illness, dementia or infirmity as you age, financial ruin, loss of friends or support network, and so on. You don't know about these things, all of which are very possible, and all of which happen to many, many people every day.

    Therefore this life which is in your hands is extremely tenuous and precious. So it would be beneficial to consider the best use of your time and energy. If you find that study and work are not worth another thought, then drop them and move on to what is important. If you find that study and work are indeed important, then you have to look into the obstacles that are coming up in your own mind: what is being held onto, and what is being avoided?

    In case you need a testimonial, devoted practitioners of Buddhism — in all its main traditions — are among the most motivated, active, determined, vigorous people I know. The truly compassionate heart is tireless in its work for the sake of others. When one connects with one's true purpose, which is to unfold this compassionate heart, one becomes consistently joyful, thrilled to undertake more and more work; because all that work is self-expression. It's the expression of who you really are.
