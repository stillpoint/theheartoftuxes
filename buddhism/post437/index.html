    Why uphold the precepts?

    If you see reason and you connect to the reason in your heart, then whether it is difficult or not is of no consequence. In fact, if you understand the cause-and-effect relationship of the precepts and you connect to that understanding with your heart, any difficulty brings forth your devotion; it calls on you to show up.

    Don't conceive of precepts as prohibitions or commandments. If you make it about "can I or can't I" you will fight with your own mind -- you'll make it about your own success and failure, which is an essentially self-centered, ignorant state.

    It's not about your personal drama but about your influence on others. There is a radiant warmth to the precepts: they move outward from your pure, compassionate heart as a gift to others.

    In particular, the precept on sexual behavior isn't (in lay life) about celibacy but about misconduct. On the outer level, don't hurt yourself and others with sex. On the inner level, don't hold the self-centered mind for which 'other' and attachment appear.

    And when you fail to keep the precept on sexual misconduct, quickly return to correct practice. Don't think poorly of yourself. Don't dwell in negativity, self-blame, or despondency. Doing so is also "misuse of sex". Return quickly.

    >It is no good condemning promiscuity as immoral behaviour, for it is only a symptom of general immaturity. Like anybody else, the [person addicted to a particular behavior] needs guidance to find a way to forget the self.

    > Robert Aitken, Roshi

    This raises the important point that proper guidance and external support of teacher, path, and community (Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha) are necessary. We are for the most part not capable of overcoming our karma by ourselves. Imagining that we are is another instance of our arrogance.

    We have been going around and around on our own, controlled by karma. But there are clear guides who can maintain the clear view, and who can teach us the common and deep meanings of the precepts. And there are courses of training and external structures that serve as mirrors for us, to help us see our own minds and behaviors. And there are communities that bear witness, offer encouragement, remind us of our humanity in both its fallible and sublime aspects, and keep us accountable.

    Also, by way of 'returning quickly' as mentioned above, teaching, tradition, and community offer mechanisms such as daily and special repentance ceremonies and purification practices to help us return quickly to practice when we break precepts, and to help us digest addictions that may not have been well addressed by our current resources.

    This is the first thing you should do if you're serious about taking on the precepts: learn them from a proper teacher, learn what the precepts really mean, and not only your idea or guess. Find a teacher, join a community, commit to a course of study and practice with them. Without right knowing the rest of the Eightfold Path is blocked.

    Next, keeping one precept is already very, very wonderful.

    Keeping two precepts is very, very wonderful.

    Keeping three... four... precepts is very, very wonderful.

    Keeping five... six... seven... eight precepts is also wonderful.

    But all precepts have one central precept: only keep your awake, non-attaching mind. If you don't make or attach to anything with your mind, that is already all precepts. Because your original mind is already the same as Buddha's mind.

    ~
