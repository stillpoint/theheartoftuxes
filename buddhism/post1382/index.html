    Maybe. Maybe not.

    I agree that it's a major issue for many thinking of ordination: will doubt and regret arise? Are aspects of self being atrophied; are aspects of life being avoided? In many cases the answer is yes. In some cases, no.

    Captain America knows he's a soldier; he knows where 'home' is, and how his life can unfold most sincerely and beneficially. Some people know they're monks, or have a strong sense of it and come to know it through their commitment.

    One might call attention to the value of living in sane environment from a young age, with sane livelihood and sane teaching, being called repeatedly to the Dharma, and applying oneself devotedly from a young age so there's less room for evil influences to take hold, and one's life becomes a profound deepening and flourishing. This works for some people. When I think of all the useless, negative influences we're exposed to daily in lay life, I feel sorrow for the tragedy of it.

    Yes, having some experience in life may help stem thoughts that you've 'missed' something. But who's to say that the experience of monkhood is not full, and that failing to ordain as soon as possible might not be cause for deep regret later? As well, consider that the well-trained monk will practice mindfulness, not ruminating about what could have been.

    If one realizes that becoming a monk was a mistake, it could yet be a very good mistake, one that clarifies one's life path and that brings with it very positive experiences and lifestyle patterns â€” relatively unique ones, that could serve well in family life, business, etc.

    You can't say it's one way or the other; you don't know the right path for any given person, or what their aspiration and capacity entail.
