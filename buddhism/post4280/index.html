    Would this be adequate practice?

    > It's not as simple as perfecting moral conduct and establishing unwavering mindfulness.

    If you can do it, you don't need a teacher or tradition or method or ritual. Can you do it?

    You seem to have a good head on your shoulders, and a balanced view of the landscape. It will all work out if you keep moving and stay openminded.

    Rituals have a good purpose. Some people have attained enlightenment through ritual, some through study, some through going straight to unknowing. There are said to be 84,000 kinds of mind sickness, so there are 84,000 kinds of practice-medicine. All methods are a waste and distraction if the way you use your mind is incorrect; and all methods are awakening if you stay aware and open-handed.

    In any practice there will be times when you don't understand why something is there or don't feel like doing something. There will be parts of it that you find too complex or not enough explanation or uncomfortably weird. There will be times when you feel something foreign is being imposed upon you. If it were completely familiar and comfortable it wouldn't be at all transformative, it would just be your same old mind finding its 'favorite' and escaping its 'unwanted'. If it were completely familiar and comfortable, there would be no challenge to the ego that always wants things certain, graspable, and most of all 'my way'.

    Aspiration and sincerity can go a long way in solitary practice. For some, the solitary time is very important; that is how some people digest their experience. In Carlos Castaneda's writings, his teacher Don Juan said that some seekers are trackers, using great acuity to see and follow signs along the trail; and others are dreamers, connecting deeply to the messages that arise within them. The two paths are not mutually independent, but it's a way of understanding how we may best engage with practice.

    For those who do well in solitary practice, the danger is that the ego takes control. We always follow what we want and try to avoid or destroy what we don't want. Furthermore, we ignore the things we don't think of as significant. So that is the configuration of our ignorance — a pattern that repeats on and on and on. So when we adopt a practice and filter it through what we see as our own good judgment, unbeknownst to us we are still going toward what we like, away from what we don't like, and ignoring much of the rest. It is very hard to see and very hard to overcome. Without an external support, someone holding the higher view, how do we uproot our like-and-dislike habit and become aware of what we habitually miss?

    So it's not that solitary practice is bad, but that it's very easy to be unaware of what's missing. When you do good training with a good teacher, I'm 100% sure you will have boundless gratitude for your teacher, and later offer the same encouragement and caution to other students.

    I think you're already doing well. Good luck going forward.
