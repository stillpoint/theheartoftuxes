    Ideas can't help you. After a certain point, you have to do it.

    But we have a way of constantly limiting and oversimplifying ideas. We may have an experience that brings us to new awareness; but then soon we try to sum it up and put it on our keychain. And then that summing up itself becomes just an empty slogan. A shadow of a shadow of the real experience that has already passed.

    Furthermore, our summing up is not necessarily accurate summing up. For instance, the earliest Buddhist scriptures don't say much about "only ever the present moment"; but they do say "the three times" (past, present, and future).

    The most common, persistent mistake in practice is checking: stepping outside of what you're doing to have a peek at how things are going, sum them up, compare the situation, judge your condition, give them a label, and try to manipulate them. This is checking activity.

    An example that we see frequently is that although people decide to practice being in the present moment, they try to do that across a span of time. "I need to be present always." "I need to be present all day/week/work period/vacation/etc." This is another form of checking, stepping outside of the moment and trying to see how well you're doing at presence, judge it as sufficient or insufficient, and control the outcome (assuring that it will continue across time). But you can't be present across time; you have no sway over unreal future. Trying to do so only creates stress and exhaustion. You can only return just now.

    So Shunryu Suzuki Roshi gives us this practice gift:
    >When a gaining idea arises in our practice, it is a sign that our practice is in trouble.

    The three times (past, present, and future) and the present moment are actually the same thing; but that thing is not a concept. It's a living reality unattainable by thought. So thinking can't help you. In fact, the checking, thinking, and gaining mind make the discomfort. But when you actually get a moment as-it-is, then there is no problem.

    The initial point here, the warm-up, is don't believe in your catchphrases so much.

    Following through on the swing, put it all down.

    Put down what you've learned, what you are now, your conundrums, 'Dharma', reconciling, 'normal life', society, striving, not striving, improving, step zero, having it or losing it, goals, accomplishments, fitting in, and especially "I… I… I… I… my… my… my…."

    Put it all down.

    If you put it all down, already you have fitting in, already you have completion.

    Sometimes the checking, comparing mind just takes us for a trip, and we start to believe that fitting in and completion has to look like something in particular. We get an image or philosophy in our head and try to make things turn out that way. Put it all down, and you have your real life.

    You have done a lot of practice already, and have (presumably) had some good teaching. Return to your touchstones. Return to practice. If you have to wake up ten minutes earlier in the day, make it so. Return to your teacher and sangha, or find a teacher and sangha to keep in touch with. Use life structures to aim you back to the main point. Rituals, commitments, vows, lifestyle changes, living space changes, clear relationship, spiritual relationship... letting go of distractions...: these medicines can be used in small doses to help you keep in touch with your direction.

    But realize that your thinking makes distinctions that are originally unreal. You already have the Buddhist path. This is the Buddhist path. Only your thinking makes it not so.

    Put it all down.

    Then: be present Be kind.

    Enough.
