    Do you know what Zen is?

    It is not hidden; it is not special. It is right here at your feet, right in front of your nose every moment. It is just more simple than most are willing to accept. So it is necessary to let the mind be just as simple.

    You say that encountering the words without any of your own mental commentary involved no joy but only indifference. That, then, is not yet the open, unguarded presence we're looking for. Indifference is actually a subtle kind of mental activity, a dullness that turns away from the moment. However, it is close to a very beneficial and enlightening quality: equanimity.

    The difference is awakeness, engagement. When you can be vibrantly present with the moment — engaged, but without discriminating between what you want and what you don't want — that is the kind of presence that is sought in meditation practice. It is talked about in many different ways; but the important point is to try it, to practice it, to put it into action.

    Many ways to talk about it: Awake equanimity. Not grasping at what you prefer, and not avoiding what you don't want, but being open to the moment as it is. Settling in to the moment. Merging with the moment. Letting the moment be 'enough'. Putting down what you're holding. Relaxing. Surrendering. Being the unchoosing witness. Un-knowing (which is different than not knowing).

    The important thing is to try, and to keep trying. Even if your practice is not perfect, if you keep trying you will get it. And if you keep trying purely, without grasping at the future, then that is already enlightened activity, already enough.

    >I want to experience the moment.

    That's great! Yes, keep practicing and you will get your own experience about it, which is better than any discussion.

    You will be well-served, though, by letting go of assumptions about what reality should feel like. You are not open to the experience as-it-is until you put down all that you're carrying and let all experience be what it is.

    So structured meditation can be very helpful for you. Something like following the breath or counting breaths can provide an anchor, an object to return to again and again. Over time, this will teach you to relinquish the things your mind-habit does to get in the way of real experience. If you continue gently, without an acquisitive approach, you will soon get a taste of Zen.

    Objectless meditations — pure choiceless awareness, shikantaza — are better for those who already know how to relinquish their own mind-habit and who have the aspiration and determination to embody the Zen mind continuously. But for you, something with structure will be much more helpful at this point.

    For those who live up in their heads a lot, analyzing everything, physical practices can also be extremely useful. Some teachers assign physical activity like full-body bowing, or simple hard work like chopping wood or garden work as the primary practice in order to help the energy come down from the head (where it manifests as confusion) and into the belly (where it manifests as a more stable, centered mind). Practices that help the body to let go, like yoga or free movement and dance, can also be useful for people who are tense and closed because of their attachment to ideas.

    Do you have a teacher? A teacher and community will also be tremendous supports, in ways that are difficult to imagine before you encounter them.

    I encourage you to experiment, a moment at a time, with letting go of all beliefs, assumptions, and wanting. Your place of meditation is your laboratory, where you experiment with what life is if you don't interfere in the least. Be more devoted to truth than to what you think it should be; let the chips fall where they may.

    Good luck! (To all of us.)
