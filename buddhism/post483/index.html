    TheHeartofTuxes

    This is all just talk. It's conceptual, speculative, and disconnected from real life experience. It seems the kind of luxury afforded sheltered first-world inhabitants, and the kind of world view that gets upended when one actually encounters some of the hardship in the world (like for instance the garbage-pickers of Calcutta, the child soldiers of the Darfur genocide, or the generation of deformed children at Chernobyl). These kinds of beliefs develop within an isolated and disengaged bubble, in a situation without a great deal of compassionate and organic connection with others.

    If you were to walk down the street today and have your life threatened by a gang of muggers or by sudden bombing raid or other attack, no doubt your terror and gut reactions would kick in. We could guess that nothing would be so important to you in that moment as your survival and safety; and your reactions would overtake you beyond level-headed reason: your heart would race, your mind would spin, your knees might buckle and you might lose control of your bladder and bowels. You would not be so dispassionate in your assesments: "Oh, death is going to happen anyway; and who am I among other animals to deserve life?"

    Or perhaps more fitting, if you were to live in a place devastated by climate change — say, within an Inuit community driven off its ancestral land, rife with illness and depression and suicide — that is to say, when you are surrounded by suffering and faced with the same ugliness and hopelessness, when it's more about you, your view might change.

    You've not gone beyond attachment to survival; but rather, other attachments (to thinking) have created this bubble.

    It's because you fail to connect with others that this kind of thinking can arise. Your view seems reasonable simply because — at the level of your awareness — you don't personally care about things beyond your own limited circle and what is created in your head. If, on the other hand, you actually love other people and animals and the Earth itself, you would care deeply what happens to them today, and tomorrow, and in the future; and you would do whatever it takes to help them.

    Furthermore, it's not true that you can't help thinking that way. You have chosen to think that way, and you've made it a deep habit. That doesn't mean you are somehow magically unable to change. Your mindset is an effect based on causes; if you change the causes you change the effect. The problem is that you habitually encapsulate the issues in a speculative conceptual process rather than opening to other beings in your heart and in real life experience. In this sense, thinking becomes the ego's distancing strategy — the 'ten foot pole', so to speak, keeping real life at a comfortable distance. Unwilling to take in suffering and horror, one is also rendered incapable of experiencing true awe and loving response.

    The claim that "it's inevitable" is also insincere. This is a sub-category of one of the Five Hindrances in Buddhism; it's a type of laziness (kausīdya). This type of laziness is quick to say that the situation is hopeless or that one is incapable or unworthy, and thus shirk all responsibility for the unfolding situation. Even if some things were hopeless (which we don't really know), other things are not; and there is much, much, much that we can do to improve the situation for each other and for countless other beings.
