    Yes, that is the difference between the Five and Eight Precepts: the Five Precepts call for no sexual misconduct; the Eight Precepts call for no sexual contact of any kind.

    But the wording is significant. What is translated as "I undertake the precept of..." can also be rendered as "I understand the training principle of...."

    "I understand the effect of celibacy on training."

    Without having learned the meaning of the training, and without having digested one's own addictive situation, just imposing prohibitions is a recipe for disaster. I think some people try to use the precepts as a form of control, thinking or hoping that in one fell swoop they will eliminate all their faults, all attachments, and won't have to really face their humanity. It's an egocentric approach, and it creates a shadow. Merely imposing control without meeting, feeling, embracing, and digesting the causes of attachment leads the mind to rebel strongly and unconsciously.

    In this regard, the teaching of Spiritual Bypassing is a very helpful and important companion to the precepts. We should be aware of how we try to sidestep full contact with our human situation.

    Both sets of precepts have their context. The Eight Precepts lean more toward support for meditation practice. I would suggest that the Eight Precepts be practiced in the context of training guided by a teacher, and at first only on special practice days. Traditionally, this is twice a month on the full moon and new moon days. Presumably, one would engage in extra meditation practice on those days. Sometimes special ceremonies require it also.

    If twice a month is proven do-able, one might try weekly (adding half-moon days). It should be done in the context of ongoing training, with that support, not in isolation as a form of wrestling oneself to the ground.

    Another occasion where Eight Precepts may be undertaken is retreat, where, again, there is an appropriate supporting structure: the teachers, the teachings, the venue, the schedule, and the efforts of the other practitioners all help one to keep the precepts. (And the precepts help keep the retreat.) It is a reasonable relationship.

    The Five Precepts themselves can be a lifelong investigation and project. In some ways, it may be better to hold fewer precepts, but hold them completely, than to hold more precepts in a way that is perversely rigid or shoddily lax. The aim is awareness of cause and effect.

    It is also helpful to know that different Buddhist vehicles have different approaches to the precepts. The precepts are the same, but there are different ways of training with them. The Theravada approach typically has emphasis on purification, the Mahayana on compassion, the Vajrayana on transformation, and Zen and Dzogchen on original completion. All of these paths can lead to enlightenment!
