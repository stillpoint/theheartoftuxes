    Did you have a painful experience and then make a story about it to yourself ("this always happens")?

    Do you make a story about your situation that only adds to the suffering?

    The Buddha called this the 'second arrow' -- making a story that creates suffering. Someone asked the Buddha if he experienced pain like the rest of us (because he seemed kind of superhuman in his even-mindedness and joyful, loving nature). The Buddha replied, "Yes, I experience pain, but I don't get the second arrow."

    That is to say, "I don't make the idea 'Oh poor me, I am in pain!'"

    Essentially, practice is being as-is, so we can realize that 'as-is' is already complete and perfect. It's not necessarily how we want and hope and dream things will be, but it's already complete and true. And if we allow it, our own complete, awake nature shines through.

    So any progress you can make in settling down, letting it be, and refraining from judgment and manipulation will be a benefit and will take you in the direction of being complete.

    There's a very broad range of 'normal'. You could also say that there is no such thing as 'normal'. I certainly don't think of myself as normal. And I think that in general people who pursue Dharma are kind of different. We're a motley crew.

    I wonder what meditation methods are recommended for people with Asperger's. Are you aware of any work being done in that area? As I said, physical practice may be very helpful. Some monks who physically can't sit for long periods gain attainment through walking practice, for instance... or prostrations, or work, or generous giving and social activity. There are many ways.

    Next, our situations arise from our karma, our preceding action. This situation is helping you purify yourself. If you understand karma, you won't blame your situation but rather be glad that it is clearing you of negative karma. If you want a better situation in the future, do lots of good action. Meditating is not the only facet of practice -- not at all! Make positive intentions and actions, don't make negative ones. Be meticulous about this. Give generously of your time, effort, money, and possessions. Make offerings to the enlightened ones. Make positive vows and carry then out. Establish connection to an enlightened lineage, and re-commit on a daily basis. Your good intentions and actions will create a store of merit upon which future practice will be based.
