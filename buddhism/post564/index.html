    On another note, I'm extremely disappointed in the conceptual answers here. Of course, everyone wants to show off what they understand. Everyone wants to think they understand something. Everyone wants to help. But this is all very much beside the point of koan practice. So much speculation, guessing, idea-making. This has nothing to do with koan practice. It's basically shitting on koans to put forth philosophical notions, vague zennish phrases, or things one has come across and memorized.

    "The oak [cypress] in the courtyard" was correct at the time, centuries ago, when the questioner and responder were there near the courtyard. In this situation here, on Reddit, it's a million miles off the mark. It's like someone asking for food because they're starving, and you say "I had an awesome meal ten years ago at Flo's diner!" You can say it with bravado, but it's completely beside the point, as are the other conceptual answers here.

    I just want to see one person who answers sincerely. Please, just someone have the courage to say "I don't know" or "I have no idea what you're getting at" or "that's all just words to me; though I think I experienced something a while back, I can't voice it".

    One second of sincerity is worth ten million empty words.

    ~

    There are a couple of responses here that could be authentic, but we wouldn't be able to tell just from the words. Some koans are set up in a way that you can tell somewhat from the words, others require an action or some other component; but much of the response has to do with how someone presents the answer. It must come from the non-thinking mind. No guessing. No opinion. No running to your inner computer to do a Google search. Also neither faint-hearted nor swaggering.

    Only plain and clear — because it has come from the mind that is like a mirror, not from the thinking mind that is like grease on a mirror. The correct response is as plain and clear as the image of an apple when an apple is held before a mirror. Apple: apple. Koan: clear response.

    Therefore, these responses don't help because (A) no one here can tell if the possibly 'correct' ones actually reflect clarity (or if they're stabs in the dark), and (B) the question itself along with the responses all encourage conception and speculation rather than getting at the real point.

    Don't let this subreddit become a mutual orgy of opinionation and posturing like r/Zen. Koan is meant for you to attain. It has to be your wisdom, not someone else's wisdom — much less someone else's lame idea, which is what we mostly see here.

    If you don't know how to do authentic koan practice, find proper teaching.
