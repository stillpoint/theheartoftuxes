    What am I in terms of Buddhism if my goal of researching Buddhism is not to reach enlightenment but to live a better life?

    There are two kinds of happiness, two kinds of 'better': lasting and temporary. Which do you like?

    In Vajrayana Buddhism there's a teaching of the levels of motivation. The motivation for a better life is the lowest level, but still positive and still a motivation worthy of applying to the study and practice of Dharma.

    The middling motivation is to gain complete unexcelled enlightenment. The highest motivation, and what is taught as the aim that causes the greatest progress and fruition in practice, is to gain complete unexcelled enlightenment for all beings.

    Your intention is perfectly fine, if your idea of 'better life' is in accord with the Dharma. (Some people think a better life means getting rich or famous based on the suffering of others; and many more think it means getting a bit more for themselves and their family and ignoring the suffering of others. Although these are extremely common views, they would not constitute positive motivation for the practice of Dharma.)

    For all of us, if we don't have the absolute highest motivation it means there's a misunderstanding of karma and of our true nature. If we truly see interdependent causality very clearly we will know that real and lasting individual benefit is not possible without benefit to all others.     It is possible that, feeling some greater relaxation about your current motivation, you may be more inclined to practice wholeheartedly. Then you would gain the karmic benefit leading to future enhancement of your view and attitude.

    Let's be honest: many students of Buddhism talk about offering themselves to the ultimate benefit of all beings, but when it's not a formal practice situation and the ritual supports aren't there to help the student remember and regenerate that compassion, very often the motivation falls away and habitual ways of relating reappear. Until compassion starts to function in us continuously and spontaneously, we're not so different in the actual application of motivation for enlightenment.

    Seeing a higher path is different than looking down on another practitioner. If someone looks down on you, that's a failure of their own equanimity. Maybe you can understand that and have compassion for them.
