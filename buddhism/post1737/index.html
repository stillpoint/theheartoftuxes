    How does one eradicate the Five Poisons from themselves?

    Five Faults and their Eight Antidotes

    > Laziness (not even starting):

    > * Laziness of not wanting to do anything > * Laziness of thinking oneself unworthy/incapable > * Laziness of being busy with worldly things.

    • Antidotes:

    • Faith & Trust

    • Aspiration

    • Effort

    • Suppleness/Readiness/Training

    > Forgetting the instructions (losing contact with the motivation or method)

    • Antidote: Mindfulness

    > Agitation and dullness (too tight and too loose)

    • Antidote: Awareness

    > Non-application (you know what the problem is, but you don’t do anything to help yourself)

    • Antidote: Application

    > Over-application (you keep applying cures that aren’t necessary)

    • Antidote: Non-application

    There are said to be four cures for laziness. When one doesn't initiate practice because one thinks poorly of oneself, doesn't see the point, loses touch with its benefit or urgency, indulges in sensory pleasures, or distracts oneself with busywork, faith helps because it prompts one to take a step regardless of negative thinking or distraction; remembering and regenerating aspiration helps because it brings one back to wanting to practice; effort helps because it directly overturns laziness; and the mental and physical suppleness that comes from training helps because one's habit mind is diminished and one's comfort in all kinds of practice situations is strengthened. Practice helps practice.

    The cure for forgetting what to do and how to do it is mindfulness, the practice of maintaining consistent awareness of one's activity. One keeps coming back to the here and now, remembering "Okay, what am I doing now? What's the point, again?"

    The cure for both agitation and dullness is awareness of one's condition. If one is just caught up in a situation without seeing one's own state, it's easy to get carried away by that state: if dull, one continues to be dull, and reacts through dullness; if agitated, one continues to be agitated, and agitation will control the activity. But if one is aware, one can let it go. "I'm dull now; let's wake up!" "I'm agitated now; let it go."

    The cure for non-application is simply application — actually doing it. The fault of non-application is distinguished from laziness in that one may be practicing, but is not doing it skillfully, not using the teachings according to the situation. For example, many people try to invent their own practice or put their own slant on the teachings. In other cases, a person may be willing to try one practice, but avoids another more applicable one ("I'll do meditation, but don't give me that crap about being generous.") It's like seeing the doctor, understanding your disease, buying the medicine, knowing that you have to take the medicine, and how, and when, and then not taking it — that's non-application.

    Checking is so habitual that even when one's practice is going well one may check it and think about applying cures, or somehow try to force it to progress faster. One gets in the way of oneself by over-applying techniques. The cure, then is non-application — let it be, and let things take their own course of development.
