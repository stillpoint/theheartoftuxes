    Compassion for oneself and others is primary and overarching. This is how it is done: with compassion.

    My question to the person feeling shame about sexual activity would be whether celibacy actually brings light to the shame and helps move it out, helps resolve it; or if it is merely an avoidance of the issue.

    Correct celibacy must also fully embrace sexuality — the feelings, the energies, the physical and mental repercussions, the inner and outer politics of it. Nothing is to be driven into the shadows.

    Regulating one's environment is necessary. Unless one has complete mental stability and unshakeable faith in the path, one must use teachings, commitments, relationships, and environment wisely to guide one's karma. Taking yourself away from stimulating or provocative situations for a time may help settle your reactivity and get you established in an understanding and habit of correct practice... if you are practicing correctly. But as well, cutting off the mutual nourishment of human contact and avoiding the opportunities for generosity, self-surrender, emotional healing, and profound learning that come through intimate (not necessarily sexual) contact will not do.

    You are meant to proceed in practice as a complete person, including human darkness and vast uncertainty.

    >"Human beings' shared sickness is anxiety with emptiness."
    –Third Patriarch of Zen, Seng-T'san

    Our courageous challenge is to step toward and into this emptiness, this uncertainty and boundlessness. If we suppress one manifestation this anxiety, another manifestation will appear. You may temporarily avoid it, sweep it under the rug, lock it in the basement, but it will come up from the shadows and control you — only it will then be unconscious, and far more difficult to transform.

    We use sex, as we do in addiction to many other powers, in habitual ways to gain a sense of control. We want to manipulate our mood, establish dominion over our energy. We want to feel powerful, or we want to pull down the blinds on the outer world for a while — all our problems and responsibilities. Opening to unpredictable feelings that sweep over us without discernable boundary is part of our growing up. Healing of any addiction, including the spiritual addiction to self-identity, requires making friends with uncertainty and boundlessness — resting with stability in the great emptiness, the great rich boundlessness of life. (Sex is then a powerful two-sided entity, both threatening to overcome us and providing a gateway to our boundless nature and our brilliant radiant life and love.)

    My sense is that you need some help anchoring yourself. Not resisting, not shutting down or blocking out, but becoming stable. This is not something that you do by yourself. You need appropriate guides and appropriate situations for both healing and spiritual practice.

    A therapist or counselor can provide the compassionate environment for you to re-process the emotional wounds that have led to your habit and distress; and they can offer structure, perspective, and knowledge for you to help yourself mature beyond your current stuck cycle.

    A proper spiritual teacher can provide the consistent view beyond struggling, reactive self — the view of your original mind, a view which as yet is not established in you. And they can offer structure, perspective, and knowledge about how to go about the proper meditative and lifestyle aspects of growing up spiritually.

    The urges are not destroyed. They are either integrated with awareness and compassion (and eventually, gradually calmed somewhat), or they are pushed into the shadows from where they take greater control. We start from a place of disconnection and perceived weakness; so of course our being reaches out for contact, bliss, power, and disappearance. We long to merge. Let us find the way to merge that will not harm us or others. For this, we need the guidance of those who have already done it.

    ~
