    I write a lot of haiku. This phrase I found echoes my sentiment.

    I can understand the puzzlement, if that's what you think of as the defining characteristic of haiku. What's the big deal, indeed?

    I find that a lot of people know the name haiku, and maybe have been taught that the syllable count is a necessity, but otherwise have no clue of the sophistication and depth of humanity implicit in the art. It's a bit like a colonialist assuming the 'natives' are all ignorant heathens because he/she hasn't been willing or able to perceive the depth of their culture.

    I agree that the syllable count is ridiculous and arbitrary in English. It is a framework meant for a completely different language and culture. The rhythm and density of Japanese language allows much more to be conveyed, with many more options, than English. Joining and cutting words, not present in our English tongue, contribute poignant meaning very concisely. And furthermore, the Japanese as a society are much more conscious of their nature, history, and geography; so the mere mention of a locale or seasonal event will elicit a stream of associations that would take many more words to convey in American or European contexts.

    So the syllable count makes a lot more sense in Japanese. The origin and reasons for the strict syllable count is the subject of many essays; but in short, it is a framework of rigor requiring and encouraging the flash of brilliant spontaneity. Like the rules in a sporting event, the framework becomes the setting for inspiration. Lax or absent rules engender perhaps a different kind of creativity, or perhaps more often a lazy, uninspired effort.

    Some forms of haiku may depart from a strict syllable count per line; and certainly many, if not most, non-Japanese modern forms do away with the syllable count altogether.

    But this is just a peripheral part of haiku. The central point is yet more vibrant and meaningful. Haiku is predicated on a flash of direct experience, both in the author and in the audience. An actual moment of real life is conveyed in a way that requires the reader to fill in a gap — to complete the experience with their own leap of understanding or connection. Life meets life, each side stepping forth in intimacy to illuminate an everyday moment. So illuminated, the everyday moment shines as something more: it is plain and uncontrived, yet reveals its eternal aspect.

    Here is a haiku I flipped to randomly, translated from one of the great masters, Buson:

    >old farmhouses, two or three—

    >a cat calls for his mate,

    >but no mate comes

    ~

    In the first line, the scene is set. There's already a certain feeling, to which most people can relate who have ever lived in or traveled to the countryside: earthy, rustic, perhaps comfortable (depending on your personal experiences of such places) and somewhat empty or lonely. There's something particularly lonely about an old farmhouse, and here we have two or three. The "two or three" is an interesting phrase in itself. The indistinct quality allows the mind to wander across its own landscape, weaving its own version.

    Within this scene, there is a very clear and specific event. This is a hallmark of haiku: it is very grounded in reality. It is not about concepts or poetic techniques. There is no rhyme, but rather space. It is the space that allows things to speak for themselves. Nouns figure prominently, rooting the statement in a real-life moment. Here, Buson's very sense-oriented art emerges through sound: a cat calls. It's not a pointless, meaningless call, but a call "for his mate". Here is where for many readers the haiku moves from external event to something very intimate. In the calling we find our own calling — that part of us that seeks and yearns, whether for a mate or for a more sublime completion. The cat's calling becomes the poem's calling, which originally is our own calling.

    And finally, the completion: "no mate comes." It is the universal condition of beings, to call and to miss. The vulnerability of a village cat is really speaking something very universally human, and ultimately just universally alive: the yearning of all beings. And in this conclusion, although we feel the sadness of aloneness we also realize that the poem has brought us to the place of great community, great connection. We share this sadness with all beings.

    ~

    The haiku author must have mastery of both simplicity and vulnerability. He or she must have the knack of being struck deeply by a real, momentary experience, and then expressing it in a way that allows the reader to participate. An overly conceptual poem, using symbols, allegories, or overtly naming lofty concepts like 'truth' or 'love' is too ham-handed; it crams ideas into the poetic space and robs the reader of the chance to experience the same moment for themselves.

    The practice of haiku — both writing and reading — is a practice of intimacy. One musters the courage to be open to our shared dilemma, that we are both alone and together — together in aloneness. To practice haiku deeply is to participate in universal truths, but in a very plain, simple, and real way.
