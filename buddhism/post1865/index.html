    Anatomical mechanism that causes sore back after extended sitting?

    The mental aspect applies because of the tensions it creates while sitting. They are still real tensions, with real aftermath. Fidgeting indicates an unsettled approach, a resistance. It is very common among newcomers, as they don't yet know the process of meditation and haven't yet clarified the approach.

    I'm not saying it's the only cause, but the mental approach can be a huge factor in pain, both in the generation of tension that leads to pain, and in the magnification of pain through resistance. For someone used to getting things done through force the problem could be more significant.

    When there's a gaining attitude, a mind that sets out to get something in particular from meditation, there will always be a dualism -- 'me' and 'my meditation'. At a subtle level, one sees the goal as positive and judges everything else as either a good sign or a bad obstacle. If one doesn't experience hoped-for states, one deems the meditation a failure our the moment a problem. Resistance builds, and arises in the body as divisive tension: the body itself tries to organize the duality between the ideal being held and the real situation being experienced. The correct approach is non-dual; it is completely open-handed to whatever arises, as meditation is actively open presence, without judgment about the content of the present moment. I think all meditators will at some point (and likely repeatedly) have the experience of pain and discomfort disappearing as they let go of resistance.

    I'm just letting you know the role of attitude in the experience of pain. I'm not saying this is necessary the cause of the pain you describe, though it may contribute.

    Pain near the waist involves the insertion of muscles that connect from, and through, the pelvis. The psoas in particular is involved. So your pain may indicate an imbalance in the way the pelvis & hips are aligned and bearing weight.

    The pelvis should be balanced. Often there's the instruction to push the lower back in or create a curve in the lower back, but this is not appropriate for some bodies and in others it is overdone. You should take note of whether the lower back and waist are collapsing forward or back. Use cushions as necessary to ensure that your knees are firm and comfortable on the mat, providing a very stable base. The stability and alignment of the skeleton reduce the need for muscular tension to do the work of holding you up.

    If the pain were originating higher up between the shoulder blades or the tops of the shoulders, we'd look at how the head and arms are being held. From what you describe, this seems like a tension that starts lower and creeps upward, but you may nevertheless take account of how the upper body is settled over its base. For instance, many people hold the head too forward, straining the upper back. An over-eager, grasping attitude sometimes goes along with this posture.

    In most cases, the somewhat triangular sit-bones should be fairly vertical on their cushion, not tilted forward or back. But accommodation to an imposed form is limited; the best way is to become sensitive to how changes in posture create or relieve tension.

    I recommend doing some separate sittings for the sake of understanding posture. Do a practice as usual, but with the self-permission to adjust and investigate posture. Make small incremental adjustments; sitters often overdo it and force things in the other direction when making corrections.

    Remember that you're seeking a neutral and easy balance -- which may differ from day to day and sitting to sitting -- rather than putting your body into some ideal static position handed down by someone else (though some traditions approach it that way, and expect you to fit in and shut up).
