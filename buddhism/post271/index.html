    Best is to learn teachings about life, death, and spiritual practice gradually and fully from a Lama (a Vajrayana master). In addition, or in the meantime...

    One of the best, most accessible and well-organized books on death and after-death in the Tibetan Buddhist tradition is the classic The Tibetan Book Of Living And Dying, nominally by Sogyal Rinpoche but apparently written by Andrew Harvey. It includes discussions of practices that prepare for death and use awareness of death to shine meaningful light on life, teachings on continuation and transference of consciousness at the time of death, and broad treatment of the 6 realms and the bardos.

    Trichiliocosm refers to the Buddhist cosmological view that there are countless worlds in vast numbers of universes.

    The Wheel Of Life is a very pithy teaching tool whose lessons encapsulate many of the basic teachings of Buddhism. You could spend a significant amount of time learning about each aspect in depth. Again, best would be to learn from a living Vajrayana master.

    There's a big danger in gaining too much information without sufficient organization and direction. In our society, our habit is to leap and grab as much as we can get; we don't question how it fits in or what its correct purpose is. So all kinds of wrong thinking develops, because the learning is based on people's old, ignorant understanding rather than on the clear insight of a master. But when you have correct direction you can use even the smallest tool to great benefit; and when you are armed with clear organizational principles you can synthesize broad and diverse teachings.

    Holding too many ideas before gaining the basic foundations can cause misunderstanding, confusion, arrogance, attachment to concept, and bad habits in practice. In Vajrayana training, compassionate direction is the all-important bottom line. The motivation is trained first and foremeost; because without correct motivation, all teachings send you to hell, but with correct motivation, even mistakes are soon transformed into benefits. Clarifying direction is of primary importance in all Buddhist traditions, and in Vajrayana it is firmly embedded as the very basis of all study and training. There is no such thing as gaining knowledge for its own sake, absent of the bottom-line motivation to awaken and save all beings from suffering — at least, not in authentic Vajrayana.

    Studying with a teacher is ideal, and beyond a rudimentary level it is absolutely necessary in Vajrayana because of the requirements of practice. But even if you're not studying with a teacher, take care to attend to first things first: why study? Why practice? Why do anything in life? These are questions for clarifying your clear direction.

    Coincidentally, the teachings on death and dying include widespread and beloved practices for clarifying direction: the Four Thoughts That Turn The Mind, and the Nine-Point or Nine-Round Meditation On Death (which are basically the same practice, organized slightly differently). These are more commonly taught and adopted as practices that establish the right foundations for more advanced and arcane methods. Even when students graduate to higher and more secret practices, these direction-clarifying meditations are continued. (Much of the material I've mentioned is touched on in this article. The topics are also all outlined in The Tibetan Book Of Living And Dying.) I would greatly recommend practicing these and learning the basic teachings of Buddhism before delving into the Tibetan Book Of The Dead/Bardo Thodol.
