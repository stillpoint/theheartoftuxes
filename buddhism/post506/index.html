    title: Beginner's problem: feeling groggy and sleepy after meditation summary: 7.0 authors: TheHeartofTuxes date: 2013-10-05 21:56:53 some_url:

    This is common.

    First question: are you getting enough sleep and rest?

    Next question: are you getting enough exercise, and do you have an otherwise healthy lifestyle?

    Meditation tends to reveal what's really there in the background, so if you're in sleep deficit meditation can make that more apparent.

    Another possible cause is stagnation of energy. If your constitution doesn't already have a strong circulation, sitting still for extended periods can make the whole bodymind sluggish. The cures for this are (A) to move, and (B) to strengthen the circulation of energy, blood, and bodily functions. This is is one of the reasons why regimens like yoga and taiji are such good companions to meditation practice.

    One of the circulating functions to be aware of is digestion. The digestion in particular does better with movement. Not that you necessarily go for a run after you eat, but if you eat too much, or too much hard-to-digest food, or if you don't move enough to assist your digestion, energy can stagnate in the belly and make you drowsy. Monks and experienced meditators on retreat know that if they eat too much their day won't go well.

    Also, it is difficult to overstate the influence diet has on the mind. Dull, dead foods make the mind dull. Fiery foods overstimulate the mind and can make it dull from the resulting exhaustion. Live and balanced foods help the mind be lively and balanced.

    In the morning, the body is just gearing up for activity, so in addition to doing sitting meditation it is both helpful and healthy to do some vigorous exercise. Doing bowing practice before sitting can dramatically change your energy, clarity of mind, and approach to meditation.

    One traditional practice is to do at least 108 full prostrations every morning, first thing upon rising; but you can also do 30 or so, or do yoga or qigong, workout, or run or bike before sitting.

    Waking early is also healthy, given that you get enough sleep, and can promote a brighter energy during the day. One should aim to arise before dawn. The moment you wake -- alarm or not -- you should move with brightness and a sense of purpose, splashing water on your face, brushing teeth, and starting practice.

    A third possible cause of sluggishness associated with meditation is perhaps more insidious, but also quite common: emotional resistance. Sometimes even despite our best intent and surface mood, there is a deep resistance to opening, to letting-be, and all that may entail. As we approach a more boundless being the ego cowers and struggles for security. As a result, armor goes up, awareness drifts or dulls.

    Many of us enter completely new territory in meditation by becoming more intimate with our inner life. For those who grew up in families where emotions were either suppressed or overblown, opening to sensitive and tender experiences can be terrifying. It doesn't necessarily appear as outward terror, but something deep within shuts down to avoid facing the moment naked. This is pretty much the same tiredness that appears when you have to have a difficult talk with your romantic partner: “Honey, we need to talk...," and suddenly “Whew! I'm just wiped out. Let's leave it 'till later."

    The cure for this is to keep practicing devoutly. Keep returning to the method of meditation, keep applying open awareness to the moment, keep showing up on the meditation mat and in life. Practice non-avoidance. After a time, the shutting-down pattern will dissolve.

    I encountered sleepiness nearly every sitting, for years; but I kept trying. I asked many teachers how to overcome it, and received many suggestions; but then one time a teacher leaned in close to my face, looked me in the eyes, and said in all sincerity “Interesting! What is that like?", and from then on it was not a frequent issue for me.

    Just don't let anything keep you from the mat. Keep showing up.

    If you keep chopping, even the dullest axe will fell the tree.
