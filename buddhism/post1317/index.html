    >would it be better to feel comfortable and honestly be attached to what you are and what you aren't

    What are you?

    We don't know; we only have an idea. So the idea is what we attach to; and no, it's not beneficial to attach to ideas. It limits us to living in unreality, being unable to accept or live harmoniously with things as they are, and never being completely content or fulfilled (and thus unable to share contentment and fulfillment with others).

    Is it better to feel comfortable? Yes, but where does comfort come from. We have to look deeply at the activity of our minds and our behavior, so we can see that — despite our very common and ingrained habit — comfort doesn't actually come from external situations, but from how our mind embraces situations.

    Attachment itself is not comfort, not ease; it is trying to get, avoid, or control. Our job is to put that down and return to our original comfort, our original complete mind.

    >...merely let things run their course until you understand why it is that you decide what you do desire?

    This is a frequent strategy among people who think too much about spiritual practice rather than doing it. It is also a strategy among many artists who think they need to suffer in order to manifest something interesting or worthwhile. It is a twisted view.

    Yes, suffering can prompt insights; and for many of us, suffering is the only thing that causes us to wake up. But that is because we 'only go along'. We continue living in the same way, making the same negative causes, and getting the same negative results, until we achieve a critical mass of pain and loss that wakes us up.

    This is the common person's path of evolution, so to speak. But it goes around and around, only getting relatively better and then relatively worse. It doesn't end, because although one puts stock in the (supposed) light at the end of the tunnel, the 'insight' at the end of all that mistaken, harmful activity and suffering, it is only one point along that whole line of cause and effect. All the rest of the story has been 'going along' as usual, deepening negative habits, creating countless new negative causes that will eventually ripen into results, and harming ourselves and countless others in the meantime.

    The point is that suffering is not necessary. The waking up can come not only as a result of hitting rock-bottom. And with the right motivation, active effort, and conditions, waking up can continue until it is more than just temporary and relative; it can overturn the whole vicious cycle.

    Once we are stable without regressing into Samsara, then we can be comfortable with regular life, just as it is.

    >Do you think it makes sense to find out what you are not to see what you are?

    It sounds like you have an idea here. That's fine; but seeing is the main point, not the idea. A correct practice that leads to seeing is necessary. No idea is as good. No idea is nearly as good. The very nature of ideas is limited and stagnant; they'd be useless as ideas if they didn't have boundary and set meaning. But actual insight is not limited in this way. So it's not that ideas are never useful; but beyond a certain point, they are not useful in attaining true self (whatever that is, beyond the words).

    Even having heard this numerous times, as most people who visit this forum have, a great many people still shunt that point aside and continue to put faith in their own fabrications, their own opinion, their own invented practices. Rather than gathering the conditions that lead to awakening, they continue to put stock in the same old around-around cycle. It just has a 'spiritual' or 'Buddhist' tag on it now, but it's the same grasping, avoiding, manipulating mind-habit that continues the cycle of suffering. Same cause makes same result.

    >Is questioning something what one must practice, or should the question-ee let the questioning come up?

    Don't ask these questions so that you can avoid proper teaching and proper practice. Don't make me create the very negative karma of encouraging you to trust thinking rather than seeing, or of not encouraging you to hurry to an awakened teacher.

    Don't try to invent your own practice. Your own blind spots will be built into the practice, and what results will have the same patterns as always. Access someone who can hold the view beyond ego.

    Small question makes the mind busy. Big, boundless question clears the mind. If you are interested in questioning, make the question something big; and don't attach to answers, don't engage in object consciousness.

    A good teacher will teach you how to use questioning to clarify your life.
