    First things first.

    Step by step.

    Yes, find a teacher and practice group. Then just take the next step. You don't yet know if you will maintain a beginner's practice, much less whether monastic life is for you.

    Buddhadharma is meant to help you purify your own character so you can be fulfilled with life as-it-is (rather than having to manipulate or escape it). In the meantime, you practice generosity and compassion, helping all others to be fulfilled. This goes hand-in-hand with self-purification and non-escape: your benefit and the benefit of others are completely bound together.

    So the course of training is a gradual increase in responsibility for others, rather than an increase in solitude or getting your own ideal situation. You will gain tools, you will gain support, but you will be required to show up for others more and more, so you can discover your boundless well of inner resource -- that is: no outer situation can save you; only learning and practicing how to show up unconditionally can save you.

    Seeing the futility in mundane life is very good. Turning to Dharma is very good. But many people then develop the expectation that they can somehow escape life's troubles. It's not like that. There will still be bullshit. There will still be difficulty. There will still be the things you carry in your own mind, everywhere you go.

    It's just that you can encounter these difficult aspects with a clear direction, a clear, compassionate, sincere intent to digest it all as part of waking-up. That makes all the difference. Outside, form is always imperfect. Inside, you can keep your clear life direction and turn everything toward awakening.

    In the Seven Points Of Mind Training, this is called "Driving all blames into one" or "Bringing all problems back to their one source". It is not the outer situation that's the problem; it's our ignorance -- our self-centered view and self-cherishing motive.
