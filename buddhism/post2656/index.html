    Quick (maybe) question on meditation

    >Where does loving kindness meditation fall? Is this only contemplation?

    No, far from it. I suppose you could say that it is an object-related meditation, but not in the usual way. It also trains concentration, but not in the usual way.

    Loving-kindness, sometimes called "tender regard", is known as Metta in the Pali language and Maitri in Sanskrit. It is one of the four Brahmaviharas: "Divine Abidings", a.k.a. "best homes" or "sublime attitudes" that represent aspects of the enlightened heart (the others being Karuna/Compassion, Mudita/Mutual Joy, and Upekkha/Equanimity).

    Metta practice, and practice of the Brahmaviharas in general is an expression of your enlightened nature, and is also considered to be a pathway to it. In the practice, you offer loving-kindness in a comprehensive way, leaving nothing out of your offering. So this offering is universal and unconditional; it doesn't depend on liking and not liking, it doesn't depend on your usual mind habit.

    This is a lovely and immediate expression of your innate, inexhaustible love. Because of our attachment to an illusory separate self, usually we think we have to portion out our love so that we won't be depleted. Humans tend to live in a half-assed way with love. Metta practice, though, doesn't say "let's build up your capacity so that some day you can express your enlightened nature"; it says "you are that â€” you are enlightened nature, so you can express it this very moment." And it develops your capacity as well along the way.

    Brahmavihara practice shares this outward-radiating assumption of enlightened nature with the Tibetan Tonglen practice. This should actually be implicit in all Buddhist practices, and ideally in all spiritual practices of any kind, although it is not an overt part of some religions and traditions.

    The symbol of embodied loving kindness in Buddhism is Maitreya, who is the Bodhisattva known for his perfection of Maitri/Metta. He is also regarded as the next Buddha for our world. If you see images of him you can almost sense the radiant quality of his practice. It's not a "practice" any more, it's fully culminated in him without separation: only love radiating everywhere non-stop, regardless of his other activity. Can you imagine a life like that? Maitreya is just like you and me, he just went all the way offering love.

    As you practice Metta, your unconditional nature is expressed. The more you practice, the more it comes to the fore. This has a very interesting relatonship with concentration. The concentration is in part trained by your effort to return to the practice upon being distracted, but there is another very interesting effect: as you practice, your relationship to the whole world of distracting objects changes. The conditioned mind that is moved by objects recedes and the stable, peaceful unconditioned mind emerges. It is a very skillful method that benefits the meditator and all beings at the same time. As such, it is an immediate embodiment of the truth that there is only one universal mind/heart, within which all of life unfolds. When you wake up, everything wakes up together. You sit as Maitreya, you sit as the heart of all of life.

    If you try Metta meditation you'll almost inevitably feel good about it, because your own innate goodness is emerging.
