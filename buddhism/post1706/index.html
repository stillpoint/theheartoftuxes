    Practicing a Koan without a Master during work

    There is a difference between a question arbitrarily applied that you try to jimmy into your life, and a question that arises naturally out of your life situation.

    The big problem with studying a koan on your own is that you have not yet attained the koan, so you don't know what it is you are studying. Even if you are able to answer the koan "correctly", there may be a hell of a lot of territory to cross before you can claim to have attained it. So there is the great possibility that you will be practicing under your own ignorance, reasonable though it may be.

    There may be both harm and benefit; that's life. So one can't say "yes, do it" or "no, don't". The movement of your life has a wisdom of its own, far and beyond your figuring and understanding. What is the koan? How are you practicing with it? These are very significant, life-altering issues.

    "To get rid of negative thoughts" is a dualistic approach — outer path. Furthermore, what new concepts and what new karma are arising because of your work with this koan? The ways you relate to your mind and try to get it to work certain ways... the habits that are ingrained, for better or worse, about how to approach practice: there are countless ways to drop off the path; and that is why having a guide who can rise above those diversions is so vital.

    But really, you already work with innumerable koans. Every day you have: How do I deal with the mess? What's my motivation for going to work? How do I negotiate space on the crowded subway? What do the children really need? Should I buy a new shirt or save for a vacation? What is correct practice right now? Why live? What about death? And so on....

    Moment to moment, you are in dokusan (koan interview) with life. The question faces you, and you are on your own in responding. So there's no such thing really as not working on a koan. It's just that if you press your mind to an artificial task (a koan applied from the outside) without a wisdom view — a view that can transcend self-attachment — then you will get artificial and erratic results.

    If, however, you have a clear direction — if you are well established in practice and know what constitutes correct relationship with your mind — then you can take whatever speaks to you and practice with it. If this were the case, I would expect you to understand the essence of hwadu practice, and also why it might be recommended not to practice it outside of retreat. Ironically perhaps, hwadu is correct koan practice. You should not attach your thinking to a phrase or try to force other thinking away. "The mind that alights nowhere": this is correct koan practice. The koan is already there; the ungrasping and unknowing are what clarifies it.

    In the old days, most of the world had not heard of the Dharma, and coming across a teacher was rare indeed. Someone who was driven to clarify life might travel for weeks to a remote temple, have a single meeting with a Zen Master (if fortunate enough) and receive one gesture or response, travel back home, and wrestle with the experience for maybe years without further instruction. Trial and error, extreme determination and perseverance, and common-sense wisdom might be all the practitioner would have to go on.

    Nowadays, both profound teaching and a deluge of misinformation are a mouse-click away, and the complex enmeshment of modern society doesn't provide the same kind of spacious field for solitary exploratory practice. We don't start with our feet on the ground these days, and common sense is up for debate 24-7. It's just not the same setting for working out a koan on your own. Even so, as I wrote above we can't simply say it's right or it's wrong to try. My sense, though, is that you need to be more clear on the direction of practice for koan work to be of value to you; and that almost certainly means more study with a good Zen teacher.

    >To hit the mark completely means there is no new nest in which to settle.

    Although some zen schools do teach a form where the koan is grasped and held and pushed through, and although that has proven effective, it is not the primary way and not the pure way of zen. Koan, hwadu, shikantaza, self-inquiry — in all of these practices, it is the ? before the "who?" that is important.
