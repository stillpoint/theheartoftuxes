    How come the Buddhist ultimate truth (all is impermanent) different than traditional Hindu ultimate truth (all is one)?

    Check the first lines of the Tao Te Ching (which is not a Buddhist text, by the way, for those unfamiliar with it): >The Way that can be spoken of is not the true Way.

    (Or also translated "...not the ultimate Way" or "...not the eternal Way".)

    It is completely wrong to assume that either of the sayings you present states the ultimate truth of Buddhism or Hinduism. Or at all: surely the ultimate truth of any religion is simply the actual truth, religion or not. No need to add the idea 'ultimate'.

    If words are used to sum it up, then that is not and cannot be the ultimate truth. The very nature of words and thoughts is limiting â€” that is what makes them useful for communicating about things and useless for communicating about the ultimate. In fact, words and thoughts are what make 'things' out of 'oneness'. With our concepts we separate out phenomena that are not really separate, and fashion a world of objects. We only think those objects are separate. If you look closely, you can see that there is really no separation. So words can't convey the ultimate truth, and thoughts can't grasp ultimate truth.

    But this also points to the common ground in how Buddhism and Hinduism speak of the ultimate truth. Originally there are no separate things, and all is one. That oneness never goes away. The things that we create with our thinking all overlap each other, and depend on each other, and create and destroy and disappear into each other: if one thing changes, everything changes. That first appearance of a thought-object means a universe of arising and passing away. So although originally there is 'one', once you make a thing with your thinking you then have relationship and movement, so the nature of that world is impermanent.

    Perhaps the two religions could agree on this: The nature of the (unreal) world of things is impermanent, illusory; before that arises, there is only a nameless, wordless non-separation.

    As bobbaphet correctly points out, using the word "one" is essentially incorrect. So don't attach to the words you use. "Before one" might be a better concept, but it's still a concept, which is not at all the reality. It's just that the word "one" is used as a sort of short-form code meaning "not many" or "not separate". No word will ever convey the reality, so we might as well use the word "one" as long as it's useful.

    What "one" really signifies must be attained by a means that is utterly free, not depending on words and concepts. That is why Buddhism stresses experience. Taste it for yourself; don't rely on what you hear or think about it.

    Boundlessgravity uses words well: >The crab-apples sure are pink this year.

    This embraces both the "one" and the world of objects. (Though the crab-apples don't have anything to do with the present situation, which is our activity on Reddit, so the line is still somewhat conceptual and not reflective of the living reality.)
