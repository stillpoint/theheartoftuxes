    TheHeartofTuxes

    The notable Zen teacher and scholar Daisetz Suzuki offered a very wonderful expression of this predicament:

    > Freedom is that the elbow doesn't bend backwards.

    Once we realize how dependent we are on form and how trapped we are by karma, we may take an antagonistic stance â€” we may see form as the enemy, and emptiness as the only relief. But form and emptiness are together, inseparable; both are 'no problem'.

    If you have your correct life direction, everything that now limits you becomes a benefit. Your karma becomes your Dharma.

    Weakness becomes the place of compassion. Suffering becomes art and connection. Problem becomes wisdom. Your individual unfolding becomes your gift to the world.

    Our usual way of thinking of freedom is that we want to be unlimited. But as long as there is form, there is limitation. This is the First Noble Truth of Buddha's teaching.

    If you have correct life direction, however, this whole dynamic gets turned on its head. Freedom isn't having an elbow that is unnaturally, magically bendy in every direction; freedom is just the elbow as-it-is. Everything is already complete; everything is already truth.

    'Correct life direction' means your life is not just for yourself; it is not a life of seeking magical elbows and fantasy situations. It means you express yourself completely within form. Picking up a shirt from the floor is complete expression. The smile offered to a friend or stranger is complete expression. Waiting in line at the bank is complete expression. In this way, your life is for the sake of all beings, for the sake of all of it.

    The student of another Suzuki, Zen Master Shunryu Suzuki, talked about the freedom of standing at a barren street corner in San Francisco at 3:00am, waiting for the light to turn green. This is another expression of freedom. Although it's not a formula: freedom could also sometimes mean crossing on a red or breaking some other rule. But the point is that one is complete in the moment, not seeking something extra from outside; and thus one offers one's very presence to life.

    So clinging and resisting clinging are beside the point. Just now, what are you doing? That is where eternity appears.

    ~

    So just to cap it off in real-world terms: When someone hits you in the face, how can you be free?

    If you adhere to a strategy, that is not freedom; that is adhering. If you react by hitting back or if you suppress your response, both are a kind of violence. There is no freedom anywhere but in the momentary truth. So what can you do?
