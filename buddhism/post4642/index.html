    If you must let go of everything, what is the point of being alive?

    Alive' is letting go of everything. There is no life that gets stuck in one place, in one posture or relationship, holding on to one thing. For life to be alive it has to move.

    You are looking for 'sense', for a meaning to the senses and the sense objects; but you don't yet understand where senses and sense objects come from. Buddhadharma doesn't say that life can only offer you suffering. It says that as long as you cling, you will have suffering; and when you let go of clinging, you will overcome suffering. That's a very different message!

    You are hitting a road block in your meditation regarding letting go. It is a phase. You are reaching the place where the issue of letting go is no longer just an idea, but an issue that addresses attachments deeply wired into you. You are reaching the point where letting go threatens the old order of things, and the old order of things wants to stay put (so the analyzing ego looks for new issues to analyze).

    The idea that you will be less conscious if you think less is attachment to thinking. I wrote about this in my recent post on checking. Our checking habit is so strong that we only trust checking, we don't trust ourselves. Meditation means trusting yourself. So you have to let go. If you want to avoid falling into a stupor, then pay attention. It is that simple: do the practice.

    Like many other meditators I've spoken to, you may be at a point where further understanding has a diminishing return. You need to address the conditions that support your practice rather than relying always on thinking, thinking, thinking. I've written about some of these conditions here. The most significant conditions for you will likely be finding a teacher and practicing with a group.

    You are afraid to let go because you don't yet have evidence that it's okay to let go. You don't yet have sufficient experience in the peace, the ease, the flow, the joy of trusting yourself. So what is the solution? You have to get that experience. There is no way to convince yourself through thinking that letting go of thinking is okay. You just have to do it and then get the result. Logically, you can see it. The teachings and role models you've encountered point to it. But you yourself have to leap off this cliff in order to get the result of trusting yourself.

    Your idea of meaning makes your life meaningless. But if you meet and spend time with a wonderfully clear and compassionate teacher you will experience meaning in a whole new way. If you practice side by side with good, sincere people you will experience meaning in a whole new way. Advanced practitioners are the most vibrant, unique, engaged, fulfilled people I know. There's a clear cause-and-effect relationship.

    You should also be aware that for the depressive or addictive constitution, meditation can be co-opted into a way of avoiding one's inner life. It may very well be that you fall into a stupor habitually â€” not because that's the function of meditation, but because you habitually shut off certain experiences. If this is the case, it's another sign that you need a proper structure of meditation and a guide who can watch for this habit and lead you out of it.

    Sometimes these kinds of issues aren't really about our meditation practice. Meditation just happens to be the focus of our speculating, analyzing thought. In my experience this sort of trepidation often arises together with sadness, depression, or a general lack of flow in life. In some cases it may be helpful to cry, or to otherwise feel and express emotional backlog. In other cases physical movement both in terms of exercise/dance and in terms of getting out and about or traveling may help a natural flow to reappear. When her students have these kinds of blocks, one teacher will inquire about the basics of their life: "How are you eating? Are you working? How is that going? How is your girlfriend doing?" Often the 'issues' that appear in meditation are just phantoms, ways we distract ourselves from getting our life in order.
