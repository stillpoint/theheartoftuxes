    The intent to alleviate short term suffering must be illuminated by awareness of the causes of long-term (perhaps future life) suffering. The value of even painful life must be recognized, the value of the opportunity to experience, purify, and move beyond the fruits of one's karma. If the opportunity is cut short, there is no guarantee that the future ripening of the karmic fruits will be any more clear or less painful. Let's not fall prey to what Pema Chodron calls â€œidiot compassion".

    In essence, one balances the motivation to ease the current pain with the great vow to ferry all beings to enlightenment. Sometimes holding peaceful space while the other goes through their trouble is the appropriate response. (And other times, quick and complete relief of pain may be.)

    When dealing with animals we also need to be aware of the difference between the way they digest pain and the way we do. Animals have, in general, substantially more simple and momentary relationship to life as a whole, and to pain in particular. They're more okay than we tend to give them credit for. The same can be said for children too.

    We also need to see how we may project our own unease and anxiety about pain, death, and helplessness. It is known that we humans tend to experience our own anxiety unconsciously, and take it for the suffering of the other. Thus we may act to cut the experience short, not because the other being can't handle it but because we ourselves can't handle it.

    A similar dynamic leads to us taking death too seriously (and paradoxically, not seriously enough; both, because it is encountered as a story or dream rather than a reality). We tend to understand it only from the perspective of a separate, doomed being, without the content, unified, eternal perspective that is in essence more real.

    On the other hand, this stuck solitary perspective is also responsible for our often utter failure at compassion. Many, perhaps most, of us can only conceptualize compassion, knowing somehow it's right but not actually feeling it and embodying it. In general we would rather play God with kill-or-not decisions than truly put ourselves in the other's place or make substantial sacrifices for their sake. (Clickstation's question of nursing the mole from OP's example back to health is a good challenge in this regard.)

    There is the story of a monk who was caring for an orphaned fawn. He would walk into town pleading "milk... I am hungry, I want milk...." The townspeople found it scandalous that a monk should act without reserve in this way, so they mostly shunned him; and when the few who were still making offerings found out the milk was not even for him but for a wild animal, they cut him off. They didn't understand that he was not acting for his own satisfaction, nor even out of pity, but that his mind had become one with the fawn's hunger. The fawn's mind-and-heart was being voiced through the monk.

    Our job is to become that compassionately open before we go making rules of thumb for situations where others are in trouble.

    Another exemplary story comes from Tom Brown. At the conclusion of his first assigned tracking and hunting of a deer (which he killed with only a knife, I believe) he threw the still-warm body at the feet of his teacher Stalking Wolf in a gesture of rage and disgust at the act of taking a life. Stalking Wolf responded that when he could feel the same pain at harming a single blade of grass, he will have learned the compassion needed to live in harmony.

    We have a long way to go, beyond armchair discussions.
