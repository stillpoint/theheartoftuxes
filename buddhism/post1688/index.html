    Help with Buddhism

    You rightly mistrust a path based on mere faith without verification. That is not what Buddhadharma teaches.

    What many religious people call 'faith' is really belief -- it's blindly holding an opinion because they feel part of a certain group or want to associate with a certain self-image or outcome. (And let's be honest and acknowledge that many in the fields of science, politics, etc. fall into this category of clinging to opinion as well.)

    Faith is quite the opposite: it's the willingness to let go of opinion, and to see. Sometimes it means wait and see; sometimes it means see directly, not relying on thinking that is conditioned by the past -- by culture, upbringing, peer pressure, and so on. In essence, it emerges as trust in oneself and trust in life. You begin to put your trust in your actual experience rather than your long-held idea.

    This is faith: life is basically good; you are basically good; and when your conditioned thinking and reactivity calms down, the natural response to life that emerges is wise, compassionate, appropriate. When the crap that is not who you really are gets cleared away, you can trust your innate activity.

    But this is not something you are expected to accept without cause or reason. On the other hand, clinging stubbornly or fearfully to old views won't do either, because these views are what keep you limited and cause you and others to suffer. So the approach is a reasonable surrender, based on gradual growth of understanding, which is all based on your actual experience. No one else's experience can suffice; you have to go through the steps and see the results for yourself.

    So at first you may have enough faith to ask a question of a practitioner or go to a Dharma talk. Then when you see that what is communicated accords with reality, you may have enough faith to try practicing. When you get certain results in practice, you may have the faith to give more commitment to a teacher and practice group; and when, each step of the way, the sincerity and veracity of the path is confirmed you may have the faith to give yourself over to the path, and be led by a wisdom that you have confirmed to be greater than your own.

    It is taught that there are two kinds of faith. Conditional faith grows with practice, because you confirm it step by step. Absolute faith is always there, because your true nature is never for a moment separate from the true teaching: who you really are is already Dharma.
