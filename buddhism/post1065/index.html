    That is very wonderful that you tried feet-on-the-ground! A lot of people read and think and discuss, but they don't actually do. Your trying and doing gave you your own real-life experience, beyond what anyone can say about it. Wonderful!

    If you keep trying the practice you will, I'm sure, get more benefit and more real-life experience that will become your wisdom.

    >Can I ask you what did you mean with "No other special technique or state is necessary"...

    When you have a true moment, that moment is complete and nothing else is necessary. Nothing else to get, nothing to avoid, no-one in particular to be. Only the truth of the moment. That is enough.

    Clear sky, rain, snow... high sun, dusk, night, twilight -- every situation is already complete.

    Human's bad habit is to try to wrestle reality into our favorite; therefore we always fight with reality and we disrespect the environment, harm animals, contend with each other, and even fight with ourselves!

    Feet-on-the-ground means asserting that you and this moment are okay. You can be with truth, as-is, rather than trying to force or escape a situation.

    This doesn't mean you can't have influence. Actually, it means you have more influence! As you discovered, you were able to be more present, more yourself even when faced with difficulty. So with practice your clarity and stability -- your mind's light -- will have an impact on others and on your life.

    Even in a relative sense, groundedness is its own benefit. When you come into your feet, you come into now. The mind becomes that much more stable, not needing to chase after things that you like or avoid things you don't like. You can be stable within the ever-changing scene.

    With practice, gradually your peace increases, your equanimity increases. You become more able to show up fully in a situation, because you are grounded rather than fighting to make the situation into something. And -- very precious -- you become more able to show up for others and offer them compassion, because you're not being thrown around by wanting, avoiding, or ignoring.

    >...and where can I read something about that practice?

    I don't know that you can. I haven't seen this practice written about in a book. Maybe some masters who work with various forms of walking meditation might teach this practice. You could try Thich Nhat Hanh's Peace Is Every Step. (What a great, teaching title!)
